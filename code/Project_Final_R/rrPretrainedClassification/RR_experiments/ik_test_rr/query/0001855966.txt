RatioOfTheDecision	The question whether a given transaction is a mortgage by conditional sale or a sale outright with a condition of repurchase is a vexed one which invariably gives rise to trouble and litigation.
RatioOfTheDecision	There are numerous decisions on the point and much industry has been expended in some of <ORG> in collating and analysing them.
RatioOfTheDecision	We think that is a fruitless task because two documents are seldom expressed in identical terms and when it is necessary to consider the attendant circumstances the imponderable variables which that brings in its train make it impossible to compare one case with another.
RatioOfTheDecision	Each must be decided on its own facts.
RatioOfTheDecision	But certain broad principles remain.
Precedent	The first is that the intention of the parties is the determining factor: see <NAME> (1).
Precedent	But there is nothing special about that in this class of cas es and here, as in every other case where a document has to be construed the intention must be gathered, in the first place, from the document itself.
RatioOfTheDecision	If the words are express and clear., effect must be given to them and any extraneous enquiry into what was thought or intended is ruled out.
RatioOfTheDecision	the real question in such a case is not what the parties intended or meant but what is the legal effect of the words which they used.
RatioOfTheDecision	If, however, there is ambiguity in the language employed, then it is permissible to look to the surrounding circumstances to determine what was intended.
Precedent	As Lord <NAME> said in A Aderson v. White (2) : "The rule of law on this subject is one dictated by commonsense; that prima facie an absolute conveyance, containing nothing to show that the relation of debtor and creditor is to exist between the parties, does not cease to be an absolute conveyance and become a mortgage merely because the vendor stipulates that he shall have a right to repurchase...............
Precedent	In every such case the ques tion is, what, upon a fair construction, is the meaning of the instruments?
Precedent	Their Lord-ships of <ORG> applied this rule to India in Bhagwan Sahai v. Bhagwan Din (3) and in <CITATION_386205> (4).

RatioOfTheDecision	Difficulty only arises in the border line cases where there is ambiguity.
RatioOfTheDecision	Unfortunately, they form the bulk of this kind of transaction.
RatioOfTheDecision	Because of the welter of confusion caused by a multitude of conflicting decisions the <ORG> stepped in and amended section 58(c) of the Transfer of Property Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	Unfortunately that brought in its train a further conflict of authority.
RatioOfTheDecision	But this much is now clear.
RatioOfTheDecision	If the sale and agreement to repurchase are embodied in separate documents, then the transaction cannot be  a mortgage whether the documents are, contemporaneously executed or not.
RatioOfTheDecision	But the converse does not hold good, that is to say, the mere fact that there is only one document does not necessarily mean that it must be a mortgage and cannot be a sale.
RatioOfTheDecision	If the condition of repurchase is embodied in the document that effects or purports to effect the -sale, then it is a matter for construction which was meant.
RatioOfTheDecision	The <ORG> has made a clear cut classification and excluded transactions embodied in more than one document from the category of mortgages, therefore it is reasonable to suppose that persons who, after the amendment, choose not to use two documents, do not intend the transaction to be a sale, unless they displace that presumption by clear and express words; and if the conditions of section 58(c) are fulfilled, then we are of opinion that the deed should be construed as a mortgage.

RatioOfTheDecision	181  The next step is to see whether the document is covered by section 58(c) of the Transfer of Property Act, for- if it is not, then it cannot be a mortgage by conditional sale.

RatioOfTheDecision	That means a transaction which takes the outward form of a sale, for the essence of a mortgage by conditional sale is that though in substance it is a mortgage it is couched in the form of a sale with certain conditions attached.

RatioOfTheDecision	The ones relevant to the present purpose are contained in clauses (6) and (7).
RatioOfTheDecision	Both are ambiguous, but we have already said that on a fair construction clause (6) means that if the money is paid within the two years then the possession will revert to the executants with the result that the title which is already in them will continue to reside there.
RatioOfTheDecision	The necessary consequence of that is that the ostensible sale becomes void.
RatioOfTheDecision	Similarly, clause- (7), though clumsily worded, can only mean that if the money is not paid, then the sale shall become absolute.

RatioOfTheDecision	If that is what they mean, as we hold  they do, then the matter falls squarely within the ambit of section 58(c) .
RatioOfTheDecision	Now, as we have already said, once a transaction is embodied in One document and not two and once its terms are covered by section 58(c) then it must be taken to be a mortgage by conditional sale unless there are express words to indicate the contrary, or, in a case of ambiguity, the attendant circumstances necessarily lead to the opposite conclusion.
