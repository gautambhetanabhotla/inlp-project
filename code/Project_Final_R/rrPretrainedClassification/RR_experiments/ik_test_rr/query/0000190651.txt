RatioOfTheDecision	The history of this unnecessarily protracted litigation appears to be as follows.
RatioOfTheDecision	At the time when the proceeding Under Section 145 of the Code of Criminal Procedure was pending before the learned Magistrate, the parties had filed a large number of documents including a large number of affidavits of many persons.
RatioOfTheDecision	Under the amended Section 145 of the Co de of Criminal Procedure,' the learned Magistrate had to peruse the statements of the parties, the documents and affidavits filed by them, hear the parties and conclude the enquiry by deciding the question as to which of the parties was in possession.
RatioOfTheDecision	The order of the learned Magistrate in this case, dated the Seth of February, 1959, hardly indicates that the learned Magistrate took the trouble of coming to his decision, upon considering the materials before him.
RatioOfTheDecision	The learned Magistrate has stated thus;  Both the parties are armed with volumes of documents which are contradictory and highly confusing and do not lead to definite finding on fact of possession.
RatioOfTheDecision	Both the parties have also filed affidavits of many persons which appear to be oaths against oaths and does not help in determining the fact of possession.
RatioOfTheDecision	So I am unable to decide as to which of the parties is in possession over the disputed lands and so I consider It necessary to refer the matter to <ORG> Under Section 146 CrIPC to give a findi ng on the fact of possession.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is apparent to me that the learned Magistrate made no serious effort to consider the affidavits mentioned by him in his order.
RatioOfTheDecision	Disposal of cases Under Section 145 of the Code of Criminal ProceduTe, where affidavits have been filed by both the parties, stating that these affidavits appear to be oaths against oaths, has not been approved by this <ORG> in a large number of decisions.
RatioOfTheDecision	Reference may be made to a decision of this <ORG>, in the case of <CITATION_300099> .
Precedent	It was stated in this case that the Magistrate should consider the affidavit of each deponent and should give his reasons for accepting or not accepting it.
Precedent	The order in this case was set aside on the ground that the Judgment of the learned Magistrate was vitiated for the consideration of a the affidavits fifed on behalf of the parties.
Precedent	Again, in the case of <NAME> v. <NAME> , the judgment the learned Magistrate was set aside on the ground that he had not considered the affidavits filed on behalf of the parties  at all.
Precedent	It was mentioned in this decision that the affidavits cannot be brushed aside by saying that they are oath against oath.
Precedent	It was held that the Magistrate should consider the affidavits in the same manner in which oak evidence is considered.
Precedent	In another decision of this Court, in the case of <CITATION_34509> , it was again held that the Magistrate had to consider the affidavits and give reasons for accepting the affidavits of one or the other of the parties.
Precedent	Our attention has also been drawn to a recent decision of <ORG> of this Court in the case of <NAME>, Criminal Revn.
Precedent	No.
Precedent	625 of 1958 D/-17-10-1960 (Pat), in which it was stated thus:  Sub-section (1) of Section 146 lays down that a Magistrate can make a reference to <ORG>, (1) if he is of opinion that none of the parties was in possession of the subject of dispute on the date of the proceeding, or (2) if he is unable to decide as to which of then was then in such possession.
RatioOfTheDecision	As I have already said,1 the Magi strate did not, in this case, make any attempt whatever to consider or discuss the evidence in order to find whether one or the other party was in possession or none of the parties was in possession.
RatioOfTheDecision	A Magistrate cannot take recourse to Section 146 (1) merely for the purpose of shifting his own responsibility.
