Fact	The Judgment of the Court was delivered by <NAME> J-The point for decision in this appeal is whether a Resolution of the Government of <ORG> Provinces and Berar, now Madhya Pradesh, dated 16th September, 1948, fixing a -scale of dearness allowance to be paid to its servants is repugnant to article 14 of the Constitution.
Fact	The circumstances under which the above Resolution came to be adopted may be briefly mentioned.
Fact	Consequent on the war, there was a phenomenal rise in the price of foodstuffs and of other essential commodities, and among the persons worst hit by it were the Government servants.
Fact	As a measure of relief to them, the <ORG> and <ORG> sanctioned a grant of grain allowances to them under various Resolutions passed in  1940.
Fact	The scheme adopted by <ORG> was that its employees stationed in various Provinces received the same benefit as the respective <ORG> employees.
Fact	But this scheme was found to be unsuitable for employees of <ORG>, as the allowances granted by the <ORG>s were not uniform.
Fact	On 10th May, 1946, <ORG> appointed <ORG>, hereinafter referred to as the <ORG>, to enquire into and   -report on the conditions of service of its employees with particular reference to I 'the structure of their pay scales 'and standards of remuneration with the object of achieving a rationalisation, simplification and uniformity to the fullest degree possible."
Fact	The <ORG>, which was presided over by Sir <NAME>, recommended by its report dated 3rd May, 1947, the grant of dearness allowance on a specified scale.
Fact	On 27th May, 1947, <ORG> appointed <ORG>, hereinafter referred  to as the <ORG>, "to examine the recommendations of <ORG> and to report the extent to which and the modifications subject to which these recommendations should be accepted by <ORG>, so far as <ORG> servants under its rule- making control are concerned."
Fact	By its report dated 22nd June, 1948, the <ORG> recommended the grant of dearness allowance on a scale which, though practically identical with that adopted by the <ORG> in respect of salaries above Rs.
Fact	400 per mensem, was less than it as regards salaries of Rs.
Fact	400 per mensem or less.
Fact	These recommendations were accepted by the <ORG> by its Resolution dated 16th September, 1948.
Argument	This difference in the result between the two scales not unnaturally caused considerable dissatisfaction among the employees concerned, and after unsuccessful attempts to get redress on the executive side, they filed through their representative, the respondent, the present application under article 226 of the Constitution.
Argument	In the petition it was alleged that "<ORG> should have uniformly adopted <ORG> rates for all its servants and the discrimination in making the two-fold slab and accepting <ORG> rates for one slab, i.e., for servants receiving salary over Rs.
Argument	400, and not accepting them in respect of the other slab, i.e., of servants drawing below Rs.

Argument	The petitioner then prayed:  "That declaring that all ministerial servants are entitled to <ORG> rates of Dearness Allowance or in any case adequate Dearness Allowance, <ORG> should be directed by a  writ of mandamus or by any other suitable writ or direction to cancel the discriminatory rules of Dearness Allowance and adopt the Government of India rates to all servants without discrimination or in any case, to provide with adequate rates of Dearness Allowance sufficient to provide reasonable subsistence for them."
Argument	The Government contested the petition on the grounds, firstly, that the claim for dearness allowance was not justiciable, and secondly, that the difference in the scales of dearness allowance adopted by the <ORG> and by the <ORG> did not violate article 14.
Argument	The learned Judges (<NAME> and <NAME>) held that under the rules dearness allowance was placed on the same footing as pay, and that the claim relating thereto was therefore justiciable; and that the differentiation made between the employees of <ORG> and of <ORG> in the matter of the grant of dearness allowance rested on "no intelligible and reasonable basis," and that the Resolution dated 16th  September, 1948, was therefore bad.
Argument	They accordingly issued a direction to <ORG> that they do reconsider the question of dearness allowance payable to the employees concerned.
Argument	It is against this judgment that the present appeal has been preferred by <ORG> on a certificate granted under article 132(1) of the Constitution.
Argument	It is argued on behalf of the appellant firstly that grant of dearness allowance is a matter ex gratia and not justiciable, and that neither a writ of mandamus nor any direction could be issued with reference thereto, and secondly, that the Resolution dated 16th September, 1948, is not hit by article 14 of the Constitution.
RatioOfTheDecision	In our opinion, both these contention are well founded On the first question, Rule 44 of the Fundamental Rules runs as follow: "Subject to any restrictions which the Secretary of <ORG> in <ORG> may by order impose upon the powers of the Governor-General in <ORG> or the Governor in <ORG>, as the case may be, and to the general rule that  the amount of a compensatory allowance should be so regulated that the allowance is not on the whole a source of profit to the recipient, <ORG> may grant such allowance to any Government servant under its control and may make rules prescribing their amounts and the conditions under which they may be drawn."
RatioOfTheDecision	Under this provision, it is a matter of discretion with the local Government whether it will grant dearness allowance and if so, how much.
RatioOfTheDecision	That being so, the prayer for mandamus is clearly misconceived, as that could be granted only when there is in the applicant a right to compel the performance of some duty cast on the opponent.
RatioOfTheDecision	Rule 44 of the Fundamental Rules confers no right on the Government servants to the grant of dearness allowance; it imposes no duty on the <ORG> to grant it.
RatioOfTheDecision	It merely confers a power on the <ORG> to grant compassionate allowance at its own discretion, and no mandamus can issue to compel the exercise of such a power.
RatioOfTheDecision	Nor, indeed, could any other writ or direction be  issued in respect of it, as there is no right in the applicant which is capable of being protected or enforced.
RatioOfTheDecision	The learned Judges of <ORG> relied on certain rules which put dearness allowance on the, same footing as pay for certain purposes, and held on the authority of the decision in The Punjab Province v. pandit <NAME>) that the present claim was justiciable.

RatioOfTheDecision	Mr. <NAME>, the learned counsel for the respondent, did not dispute the correctness of this position.
RatioOfTheDecision	But he argued that when once the Government passed 'a Resolution fixing a scale of allowance under Rule 44, that would be law as defined in article 13(3)(a) of the Constitution, and if that law infringed, article 14 , it could be declared void.
RatioOfTheDecision	That is a contention which is clearly open to him, and the question therefore that falls to be decided is whether the Resolution dated 26th September, 1948, is bad as infringing article 14.

RatioOfTheDecision	No contention is raised that fixing different rates of dearness allowance for differen t slabs of pay is obnoxious to article  14.
RatioOfTheDecision	Secondly, within any given slab, the scheme places all the employees in the same position, except that in the lowest ranks a slightly higher rate is fixed for residents in the cities of Nagpur and Jubbulpore, which again has not been attacked as discriminatory.

Argument	Mr. <NAME> does, not contend that there is anything in the scheme or in the Resolution adopting it, which bring s it within the prohibition enacted in article 14.
Argument	His contention is that the <ORG> whose recommendations were accepted by the <ORG> adopted the rates suggested in the report of the <ORG> as regards <ORG> servants who drew a monthly salary of.
Fact	over Rs.
Fact	400, but when they came to those employees who drew a monthly salary of Rs.
Fact	400 or less, they discarded the rates fixed by the <ORG>, and, instead, adopted different and lower rates, and that this was discrimin ation hit by article 14.
RatioOfTheDecision	In other words, the impugned Resolution, though valid in itself as not infringing article 14 , becomes void under that provision when it is taken in conjunction with the report of the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	We do not find anything in article 14 which supports this somewhat startling contention.
RatioOfTheDecision	Under the Constitution, the <ORG> and the <ORG> are distinct entities, each having its own executive and <ORG>, with their powers well-defined.
RatioOfTheDecision	Article 12 defines "the <ORG>" as including the Government and the <ORG> of each of the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	Article 13(2) enacts that the <ORG> shall not make any laws taking away, or abridging the rights conferred by Part III, and article 14 enacts that, "The <ORG> shall not deny to any person equality before the law or the equal protection of the laws within the territory of India."
RatioOfTheDecision	On these provisions, the position is that when a law is impugned under article 13 , what the Court has to decide is whether that law contravenes any of the provisions of Part III.
RatioOfTheDecision	If it-decides that it does, it has to declare it void; if it decides that it does not, it has to uphold it.
RatioOfTheDecision	The power of the <ORG> to declare a law void under article 13 has to be exercised with reference to the specific legislation which is impugned.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is conceivable that when the same <ORG> enacts two different laws but in substance they form one legislation, it might be open to the <ORG> to disregard the form and treat them as one law and strike it down, if in their conjunction they result in discrimination.
RatioOfTheDecision	But such a course is not open where, as here, the two laws sought to be read in conjunction are by different <ORG> and by different <ORG>s.
RatioOfTheDecision	Article 14 does not authorise the striking down of a law of one <ORG> on the ground that in contrast with a law of another <ORG> on the same subject its provisions are discriminatory.
RatioOfTheDecision	Nor does it contemplate a law of the <ORG> or of the <ORG> dealing with similar subjects being held to be unconstitutional by a process of comparative study of the  provisions of the two enactments.
RatioOfTheDecision	The sources of authority for the two statutes being different, article 14 can have no application.
RatioOfTheDecision	The result, therefore, is that the scale of dearness allowance recommended by the <ORG> and sanctioned by <ORG> can furnish no ground for holding that the scale of dearness allowance recommended by the <ORG> and adopted by the appellant is repugnant to article 14.
RatioOfTheDecision	It may no doubt ,sound hard that Government servants doing work of a similar kind and working, it may be, even in the same place, should receive different allowances; but the rights of the parties have to be decided on legal considerations, and it is impossible to hold that the Resolution in question is bad under article 14.
RatioOfTheDecision	It was argued on behalf of the appellant that the assumption underlying the argument of the respondent -with reference to article 14 that the <ORG> had adopted the Report of the <ORG> in part and rejected it in part was itself without foundation.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the view we h ave taken on the applicability of article 14 , this question has no practical importance; but as all the materials have been placed before us, we may briefly express our opinion thereon.
RulingByLowerCourt	In paragraph 80 of the Report the <ORG> observed that while the <ORG> based its scale on the cost of living index, they themselves adopted the current level of prices as the basis for fixation of dearness allowance.
RulingByLowerCourt	In paragraph 83 they further observed that in fixing the scale on the basis of the cost of living index the element of pay had also been taken into account, but that as they had revised the scale of basic pay, they were not including it in fixing the dearness allowance.
RulingByLowerCourt	In paragraph 31, they observed that unlike the <ORG> they were taking into consideration the financial resources of the <ORG> in fixing the scale.
RulingByLowerCourt	Thus, the <ORG> approached the problem from a different angle, and applied different principles in fixing the scale of dearness allowance; and if the two schemes produced the same resul ts at some stages, that was due to coincidence and not to adoption of the report of the <ORG> by the '<ORG>.
RulingByLowerCourt	Mr. <NAME> also referred us to two Resolutions of the appellant dated 4th January, 195 1, and 6th October, 195 1, adopting the scale fixed by the <ORG> in respect of certain other categories.
Fact	That has no bearing on the question whether the <ORG> whose recommendations were approved by the Government had adopted in part the Report of the <ORG> so as to result in discrimination.
RatioOfTheDecision	The facts stated above show that the <ORG> went into the matter independently, and viewed the question from a different standpoint; and in formulating the scheme which they did, they did not adopt the Report of the <ORG>, though they derived considerable assistance from it.
RulingByPresentCourt	In the result, this appeal must be allowed and the petition of the respondent dismissed; but in the circumstances, there will be no order as to costs either here or in the Court below.
