Statute	The rule was pressed on two grounds: (1) The order itself is illegal.

RatioOfTheDecision	In my opinion, it is necessary to distinguish carefully between the jurisdiction of the Magistrate to make an order and a possible practical difficulty in showing that it has been disobeyed.
RatioOfTheDecision	It does not follow that because it is difficult for the Crown to secure a conviction that the order itself was made without jurisdiction.
RatioOfTheDecision	If we apply the test laid down by the learned Judges in those two Bombay cases, it would be very difficult to say where a place ends and an area begins.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is obvious that a line would have to be drawn somewhere and for my part I shall find it very diff icult to draw such a line.
RatioOfTheDecision	Nor is the matter of much practical importance: for example, if an area may be said to contain 150 places, She Magistrate could pass 150 orders in identical terms and the result would be exactly the same.
RatioOfTheDecision	In our opinion, the order is a definite order and it does not contravene the provisions of Section 144 .

RatioOfTheDecision	Then in the second place the order itself is not very happ ily worded.
RatioOfTheDecision	It does not clearly forbid attendance at a meeting or making speeches at a meeting.
RatioOfTheDecision	The use of the words 'no public meeting shall be held' seems to suggest something in connexion with the organization of a meeting.
RatioOfTheDecision	From the evidence it appears that the petitioner, <NAME> did nothing more than behave like a Hyde Park orator.
RatioOfTheDecision	The actual order is capable of more interpretation than one.
RatioOfTheDecision	Before it can be said that the petitioners had knowledge of the order, it must be shown that its terms were communicated to them.
RatioOfTheDecision	Instead of doing that, the Sub-Inspector merely gave his own interpretation of it, which is quite a different thing.
RatioOfTheDecision	We must accordingly accept the contention raised in the second ground that there is no evidence upon which it can be held that the petitioners had any knowledge of the order.
RulingByPresentCourt	The rule is accordingly made absolute, the convictions and sentences are set aside and the petitioners are discharged from their bail.
