Fact	5.
Fact	Only question which needs to be considered is the alleged exercise of right of private defence.
Fact	Section 96 , IPC provides that nothing is an offence which is done in the exercise of the right of private defence.
RatioOfTheDecision	The Section does not define the expression `right of private defence'.
RatioOfTheDecision	It merely indicates that nothing is an offence which is done in the exercise of such right.
RatioOfTheDecision	Wh ether in a particular set of circumstances, a person legitimately acted in the exercise of the right of private defence is a question of fact to be determined on the facts and circumstances of each case.
RatioOfTheDecision	No test in the abstract for determining such a question can be laid down.
RatioOfTheDecision	In determining this question of fact, the <ORG> must consider all the surrounding circumstances.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is not necessary for the accused to plead in so many words that he acted in self-defence.
RatioOfTheDecision	If the circumstances show that the right of private defence was legitimately exercised, it is open to the <ORG> to consider such a plea.
RatioOfTheDecision	In a given case the <ORG> can consider it even if the accused has not taken it, if the same is available to be considered from the material on record.
Precedent	Under Section 105 of the Indian Evidence Act, 1872 (in short `the Evidence Act '), the burden of proof is on the accused, who sets up the plea of self-defence, and, in the absence of proof, it is not possible for the <ORG> to presume the truth of the plea of self-defe nce.
Precedent	The Court shall presume the absence of such circumstances.
Precedent	It is for the accused to place necessary material on record either by himself adducing positive evidence or by eliciting necessary facts from the witnesses examined for the prosecution.
Precedent	An accused taking the plea of the right of private defence is not necessarily required to call evidence; he can establish his plea by reference to circumstances transpiring from the prosecution evidence itself.
Precedent	The question in such a case would be a question of assessing the true effect of the prosecution evidence, and not a question of the accused discharging any burden.
Precedent	Where the right of private defence is pleaded, the defence must be a reasonable and probable version satisfying the Court that the harm caused by the accused was necessary for either warding off the attack or for forestalling the further reasonable apprehension from the side of the accused.
Precedent	The burden of establishing the plea of self-defence is on the accused and the burden stands discharged by s howing preponderance of probabilities in favour of that plea on the basis of the material on record.
Precedent	( <CITATION_1479705> (AIR 1968 SC 702), <CITATION_1660698> (AIR 1975 SC 1478), <CITATION_853570> (AIR 1977 SC 2226), and <CITATION_153251> (AIR 1979 SC  577).
Precedent	Sections 100 to 101 define the extent of the right of private defence of body.
Precedent	If a person has a right of private defence of body under Section 97 , that right extends under Section 100 to causing death if there is reasonable apprehension that death or grievous hurt would be the consequence of the assault.
Precedent	The oft quoted observation of this <ORG> in <CITATION_1344060> .
Precedent	(AIR 1979 SC 391), runs as follows:    "It is true that the burden on an accused person to establish the plea of self-defence is not as onerous as the one which lies on the prosecution and that, while the prosecution is required to prove its case beyond reasonable doubt, the accused need not establish the plea to the hilt and may discharge his onus by establishing a mere preponderance of  probabilities either by laying basis for that plea in the cross-examination of the prosecution witnesses or by adducing defence evidence."
RatioOfTheDecision	The accused need not prove the existence of the right of private defence beyond reasonable doubt.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is enough for him to show as in a civil case that the preponderance of probabilities is in favour of his plea.
RatioOfTheDecision	6.
RatioOfTheDecision	A plea of right of private defence cannot be based on surmises and speculation.
RatioOfTheDecision	While considering whether the right of private defence is available to an accused, it is not relevant whether he may have a chance to inflict severe and mortal injury on the aggressor.
RatioOfTheDecision	In order to find whether the right of private defence is available to an accused, the entire incident must be examined with care and viewed in its proper setting.
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 97 deals with the subject matter of right of private defence.
RatioOfTheDecision	The plea of right comprises the body or property (i) of the person exercising the right; or (ii) of any other person; and the right may be exercised in the case of any  offence against the body, and in the case of offences of theft, robbery, mischief or criminal trespass, and attempts at such offences in relation to property.
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 99 lays down the limits of the right of private defence.
RatioOfTheDecision	Sections 96 and 98 give a right of private defence against certain offences and acts.
RatioOfTheDecision	The right given under Sections 96 to 98 and 100 to 106 is controlled by Section 99 .
RatioOfTheDecision	To claim a right of private defence extending to voluntary causing of death, the accused must show that there were circumstances giving rise to reasonable grounds for apprehending that either death or grievous hurt would be caused to him.
RatioOfTheDecision	The burden is on the accused to show that he had a right of private defence which extended to causing of death.
RatioOfTheDecision	Sections 100 and 101 , IPC define the limit and extent of right of private defence.
RatioOfTheDecision	7.
RatioOfTheDecision	Sections 102 and 105 , IPC deal with commencement and continuance of the right of private defence of body and property respectively.
RatioOfTheDecision	The right commences, as soon as a reasonable apprehensi on of danger to the body arises from an attempt, or threat, to commit the offence, although the offence may not have been committed but not until there is that reasonable apprehension.
RatioOfTheDecision	The right lasts so long as the reasonable apprehension of the danger to the body continues.
Precedent	In <ORG> State of Punjab (AIR 1963 SC 612), it was observed that as soon as the cause for reasonable apprehension disappears and the threat has either been destroyed or has been put to route, there can be no occasion to exercise the right of private defence.

Precedent	9.
RatioOfTheDecision	As noted in <CITATION_801090> (AIR 1991 SC 1316), a person who is apprehending death or bodily injury cannot weigh in golden scales in the spur of moment and in the heat of circumstances, the number of injuries required to disarm the assailants who were armed with weapons.
RatioOfTheDecision	In moments of excitement and disturbed mental equilibrium it is often difficult to expect the parties to preserve composure and use exactly only so much force in retaliation commensurate with the danger apprehended to him where assault is imminent by use of force, it would be lawful to repel the force in self-defence and the right of private-defence commences, as soon as the threat becomes so imminent.
RatioOfTheDecision	Such situations have to be pragmatically viewed and not with high-powered spectacles or microscopes to detect slight or even marginal overstepping.
RatioOfTheDecision	Due weightage has to be given to, and hyper technical approach has to be avoided in considering what happens on the spur of the moment on the spot and  keeping in view normal human reaction and conduct, where self-preservation is the paramount consideration.
RatioOfTheDecision	But, if the fact situation shows that in the guise of self-preservation, what really has been done is to assault the original aggressor, even after the cause of reasonable apprehension has disappeared, the plea of right of private-defence can legitimately be negatived.
RatioOfTheDecision	The Court dealing with the plea has to weigh the material to conclude whether the plea is acceptable.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is essentially, as noted above, a finding of fact.
RatioOfTheDecision	10.
RatioOfTheDecision	The right of self-defence is a very valuable right, serving a social purpose and should not be construed narrowly.
RatioOfTheDecision	( <CITATION_1636646> .
RatioOfTheDecision	(AIR 1971 SC 1857).
RatioOfTheDecision	Situations have to be judged from the subjective point of view of the accused concerned in the surrounding excitement and confusion of the moment, confronted with a situation of peril and not by any microscopic and pedantic scrutiny.
RatioOfTheDecision	In adjudging the question as to whether more force than was necessary was used in the preva iling circumstances on the spot it would be inappropriate, as held by this <ORG>, to adopt tests by detached objectivity which would be so natural in a <ORG> room, or that which would seem absolutely necessary to a perfectly cool bystander.
RatioOfTheDecision	The person facing a reasonable apprehension of threat to himself cannot be expected to modulate his defence step by step with any arithmetical exactitude of only that much which is required in the thinking of a man in ordinary times or under normal circumstances.
RatioOfTheDecision	11.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the illuminating words of <NAME> (<NAME> on Crime, 11th Edition Volume I at page 49):    "....a man is justified in resisting by force anyone who manifestly intends and endeavours by violence or surprise to commit a known felony against either his person, habitation or property.
Precedent	In these cases, he is not obliged to retreat, and may not merely resist the attack where he stands but may indeed pursue his adversary until the danger is ended and if in a conflict between them he happens to kill his attacker, suc h killing is justifiable."
Precedent	12.
Precedent	The right of private defence is essentially a defensive right circumscribed by the governing statute i.e.
Precedent	the <ORG> , available only when the circumstances clearly justify it.
Precedent	It should not be allowed to be pleaded or availed as a pretext for a vindictive, aggressive or retributive purpose of offence.

Precedent	A right to defend does not include a right to launch an offensive, particularly when the need to defend no longer survived.
