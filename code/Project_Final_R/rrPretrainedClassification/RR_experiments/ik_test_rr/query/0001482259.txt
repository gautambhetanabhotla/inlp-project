RatioOfTheDecision	The next question tha t arises for considerion is, whether, even assuming that the terms of s. 3(3) are applicable, its terms are wide enough to take in not merely the provisions of the Central Excises and & Salt Act , 1944 and, in particular its definition clauses, as they stood in 1957 on the date when the 1957 Act came into force but also the amendments effected therein from time to time.
RatioOfTheDecision	The answer to this question depends upon the general principles applicable to what is described as 'referential legislation' of which this is an instance.
RatioOfTheDecision	Legislatures sometimes take a short cut and try to reduce the length of statutes by omitting elaborate provisions where such provisions have already been enacted earlier and can be adopted for the purpose on hand.
RatioOfTheDecision	While, on the one hand, the prolixity of modern statutes and the necessity to have more legislation then one on the same or allied topics render such a course useful and desirable, the attempt to legislate by reference is sometimes everdone and previty is achieved at the expense of  lucidity.
RatioOfTheDecision	However, this legislative device is quite well known and the principles applicable to it fairly well settled.
RatioOfTheDecision	Referential legislation is of two types.
RatioOfTheDecision	One is where an earlier Act or some of its provisions are incorporated by reference into a later Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	In this event, the provisions of the earlier Act or those so incorporated, as they stand in the earlier Act at the time of incorporation, will be read into the later Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	Subsequent changes in thc earlier Act or the incorporated provisions will have to be ignored because, for all practical purposes, the existing provisions of the earlier Act have been re-enacted by such reference into the later one, rendering irrelevant what happens to the earlier statute thereafter.
RatioOfTheDecision	Examples of this can be seen in <CITATION_1334697> , AIR 1931 P.C.
RatioOfTheDecision	149; <CITATION_1370552> , AIR 1975 S.C. 17 and <CITATION_1000517> , AIR 1979 S.C.798.
RatioOfTheDecision	On the other hand, the later statute may not incorporate the earlier provisions.
RatioOfTheDecision	It may only make a reference of a broad nature as to  thc law on a subject generally, as in <NAME> v. <NAME>.
RatioOfTheDecision	3 SCR 561; or contain a general reference to the terms of an earlier statute which are to be made applicable.
RatioOfTheDecision	In this case any modification, repeal or re-enactment of the earlier statute will also be carried into in the later, for here, the idea is that certain provisions of an earlier statute which become applicable in certain circumstances are to be made use of for the purpose of the latter Act also.
RatioOfTheDecision	Examples of PG NO 824 this type of legislation are to be seen in <CITATION_1193965> ,  3 SCR 786; <CITATION_838980> ,  2 SCR 92 and Special Land Acquisition Officer v. <ORG>,  1 SCR 569.
RatioOfTheDecision	Whether a particular statute falls into the first or second category is always a question of construction.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the present case, in my view, the legislation falls into the second category.
RatioOfTheDecision	S. 3(3) of the l957 Act does not incorporate into the 1957 Act any specific provisions of the 1944 Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	It only declares generally that the provisions of the 1944 Ac t shall apply "so far as may be", that is, to the extent necessary and practical, for the purposes of the 1957 Act as well.
RatioOfTheDecision	That apart, it has been held, even when a specific provision is incorporated and the case apparently falls in the first of the above categories, that the rule that repeals, modifications or amendments of the earlier Act will have to be ignored is not adhered to incertain situations.
RatioOfTheDecision	These have been set out in <CITATION_1721191> .

RatioOfTheDecision	Answering thc (question in the affirmative, the <ORG> outlined the following proposition:  "Where a subsequent Act incorporates provisions of a previous Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	then the borrowed provisions become an  integral and independent part of the subsequent <ORG> and are totally uneffected by any repeal or amendment in the previous <ORG> This principle.
RatioOfTheDecision	however will not apply in the following, cases:  (a) where the subsequent <ORG> and the previous <ORG> are supplemental to each other;  (b) where the two <ORG>s are in pari materia:  (c) where the amendment in the previous <ORG>, if not imported into the subsequent <ORG> also, would render the subsequent <ORG> wholly unworkable and uneffectual; and  (d) where the amendment of the previous <ORG>, either PG NO 825 expressly or by necessary intendment, applies the said provisions to the subsequent <ORG>. "
RatioOfTheDecision	The present case falls within the scope of these exceptions, even if s. 3(3) is construed as incorporating certain specific provisions of the 1944 into itself.

RatioOfTheDecision	It is also merely supplemental.
RatioOfTheDecision	While the 1944 <ORG> imposes a general levy of excise duty on all goods manufactured and produced, and aim of the  present Act is to supplement the levy by an additional duty of the same nature on certain goods.
RatioOfTheDecision	The duration of the applicability is undefined but the statute is clearly enforceable as long as it is in the statute book side by side with the normal excise duties.
RatioOfTheDecision	The clear intention is that the same provisions shall govern both the levies except that the duty under the later Act is confined to certain goods only and its distributability among the States may perhaps follow a different pattern from the principal duty.
RatioOfTheDecision	There is no reason or logic why all the incidents attaching under the earlier legislation, in so &r as they are not clearly inconsistent with the later one should not be extended to the later legislation as well.
RatioOfTheDecision	As has been pointed out earlier, the Finance Act s which levied special or regular or additional excise duties contained in themselves all the elements of charge of duty.
