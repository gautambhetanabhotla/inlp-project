Argument	JUDGMENT <NAME>, J.
Argument	1.
Argument	This is a petition under Article 226 of the Constitution arising out of proceedings under the U. P. Consolidation of Holdings Act (hereinafter referred to as the Act).
Argument	2.
Fact	One <NAME> died leaving some grove and agricultural plots which she had inherited from her <NAME>.
Fact	She had acquired Bhumidhari rights in respect of the agricultural plots before her death.
Fact	During the consolidation proceedings <ORG>, <NAME> moved an application before <ORG> claiming that as Smt.
Fact	<NAME> had died heirless her entire holding vested in <ORG>.
Fact	The original petitioners <NAME> and <NAME> filed objection laying claim to the estate of the deceased widow on the basis of:--  (i) inheritance being her <NAME>'s brother's son's sons:  (ii) Will executed by Smt.
Fact	<NAME> on 12-10-1962 in favour of <NAME>:  (iii) Registered sale-deed dated 4-4-1963 executed by <NAME> in respect of her entire holding including groves in favour  of the petitioners.
Fact	The petitioners' objection was rejected and the claim of <ORG> was upheld.
Fact	The appeal filed by the petitioners was dismissed by the Settlement Officer (Consolidation) and their revision under Section 48 of the Act was also dismissed by the Deputy Director of Consolidation by his order dated 26-3-1966.
Fact	The petitioners then moved the present writ Petition.
Argument	3.
Argument	When the Writ Petition came up for hearing before <NAME>, he noticed that one of the questions that arose in the case was as to whether the widow was competent to transfer the whole of her agricultural holding without the prior permission of the Settlement Officer (Consolidation) as required by Section 5 (1) (c) (ii) of the Act.
Argument	4.
Argument	Section 5 (1) (c) (ii) of the Act enacts that no tenure-holder, except with the permission in writing of the Settlement Officer (Consolidation) previously obtained shall transfer by way of sale, gift or exchange any part of his holding in the consolidation area.
Argument	Full Bench of this court i n <CITATION_1596363> at Hardoi, AIR 1971 All 87 (FB), has taken the view that the expression "any part of his holding" did not include the entire holding so that the ban applied only where a part of holding was transferred and not when the holding was transferred as a whole.
Argument	The learned Judge noticed the Hindi version of Section 5 (1) (c) (ii) of the Act which reads:  "NA CHAKBANDI KSHETTRA ME SAMMILIT APKI JOT ATHWA USKE KISHI BHAG KO VIKRAYA DAN ATHWA BINIMAY DWARA HASTAN-TRIT KAREGA."
Argument	According to the Hindi version the ban would apply as much to the transfer of a part of the holding as to the whole.
Argument	In his opinion the words in the English version were not clear and it was possible to interpret them in accordance with the Hindi version of the enactment.
Argument	He accordingly referred the matter to <ORG> and that is how this petition has come up before us.
Argument	5.
Argument	To begin with, we might state that although the learned Judge has not indicated as to whether the entire case has to be decided by <ORG> or only  the question relating to the interpretation of Section 5 (1) (c) (ii) of the Act, yet having regard to the fact and the circumstances of the case the intention of the learned Judge was to refer only the limited question relating to the interpretation of Section 5 (1) (c) (ii) and not the whole case, because even if the petitioners fail on this ground their alternative pleas will have to be examined.
RatioOfTheDecision	We shall accordingly restrict ourselves to the short question as to whether the ban incorporated in Section 5 (1) (c) (ii) applies to the transfer of an agricultural holding as a whole or applies only when a part of the holding is transferred.
Statute	Clause (c) of Section 5 (1) was added in the Act by Section 4 of the U. P. Consolidation of Holdings (Amendment) Act, 1958 (U. P. Act XXXVIII of 1958) and reads as under:--  "(c) Notwithstanding anything contained in the U. P. Zamindari Abolition and Land Reforms Act, 1950, no tenure-holder, except with the permission in writing of the Settlement Officer Consolidation previo usly obtained shall-  (i) Use his holdings or any part thereof for purpose not connected with agriculture, horticulture or animal husbandry including pisciculture and poultry farming; or  (ii) transfer by way of sale, gift or exchange any part of his holding in the consolidation area;  Provided that a tenure-holder may continue to use his holding, or any part thereof, for any purpose for which it was in use prior to the date specified in the notification issued under Sub-section (2) of Section 4 ."
RatioOfTheDecision	It may at once be noticed that Clause (ii) is flanked by Clause (i) and the proviso at the end of Clause (ii).
RatioOfTheDecision	Clause (i) and the proviso deal with the ban on non-agricultural use of a holding while Clause (ii) imposes a ban on the transfer of a holding.
RatioOfTheDecision	In Clause (i) the expression used is "holding or any part thereof".
RatioOfTheDecision	The same expression is used in the proviso, which in fact, is a proviso to Clause (i) so that ban on the user of agricultural holding for non-agricultural purpose extends to the holding as a whole  as also to a part thereof.
RatioOfTheDecision	But when we come to Clause (ii) the expression used is "any part of his holding".
RatioOfTheDecision	Obviously the ban contemplated under Clause (i) is not co-extensive with the ban contained in Clause (ii) otherwise there is no reason why the expression "holding or any part thereof" should not have been used in Clause (ii) also.
RatioOfTheDecision	The expression "holding or any part thereof" is not synonymous with the expression "any part of the holding" if regard is had to the jurisdiction of Clause (ii).
RatioOfTheDecision	If Clause (ii) had stood alone it could be possible to interpret the expression "any part of the holding" as meaning every part of the holding.
RatioOfTheDecision	But having regard to the terminology employed in the preceding clause and the succeeding proviso, no other meaning can be assigned to it except that it means "a part" as distinguished from 'the whole': It is true that grammatically, the expression "a part" would have been more apt than the expression "any part".
RatioOfTheDecision	But when the intention of the <ORG> is clear, nothing wil l turn upon this grammatical error.
RatioOfTheDecision	According to the dictionary meaning also "any" is sometimes used for the adjective "a".
RatioOfTheDecision	It is not necessary, however, to dwell upon this point any further because the question has been the subject-matter of previous decisions of this court which have unanimously interpreted the expression "any part" to mean "a part".
Precedent	The first decision is of <NAME>, in <CITATION_56603> , 1966 All WR (HC) 818 His Lordship held that the prohibition in Clause (ii) of Section 5 (1) (c) extended to the transfer of a part of the holding and not to the whole holding so that the whole holding could be transferred by a tenure-holder without the permission of the Settlement Officer (Consolidation).
Precedent	This decision has been affirmed on special appeal by <NAME>, <NAME> and <NAME>, J., in <NAME> v. <NAME>, 1968 All LJ 223.
Precedent	The question was again considered by another Full Bench of which two of us were members in the case Smt.
Precedent	<NAME>, AIR 1971 All 87 (Supra) w hen the view taken in the earlier cases was affirmed.
Precedent	There is yet another decision of <ORG> of this court consisting of <NAME>, <NAME>, and <NAME>, JJ., in <CITATION_777513> , 1970 RD 130.
RatioOfTheDecision	<ORG> agreed with the view taken by the earlier <ORG> in the case of <NAME> (Supra).
RatioOfTheDecision	Thus so far this court is concerned, the matter stands concluded.
RatioOfTheDecision	6.
RatioOfTheDecision	If one has regard to the object underlying the provisions in question it becomes quite clear as to why the ban in Clauses (i) and (ii) is not co-extensive.
RatioOfTheDecision	The object underlying Clause (i) is to preserve the land for agricultural purposes and that is why a complete ban has been placed on the use of the land for non-agricultural purposes.
RatioOfTheDecision	A ban on a part of the land only would not have served the purpose.
RatioOfTheDecision	Under Clause (ii) the ban was intended to prevent the fragmentation of holdings and, as such, it was placed only on a transfer of a part of the holding.
RatioOfTheDecision	There could be no objection to the transfer of the holding as a wh ole because if would not involve fragmentation but would involve only a change in ownership.
RatioOfTheDecision	The scheme underlying the Act was to consolidate agricultural holdings and to prevent their further fragmentation and also to preserve the land for agricultural purpose.
RatioOfTheDecision	It was not the intention to restrict the right of an owner to deal with his property by way of sale, exchange or transfer except to the extent that was necessary to carry out the objects of the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	That is why a ban of a limited nature has been placed on transfers while a complete ban has been placed on the user of agricultural land for non-agricultural purposes.
RatioOfTheDecision	Having regard to all these circumstances it is not possible to take a different view.
Argument	7.
Argument	The learned counsel for the petitioners submitted that the law having been settled by a series of decisions of this court it should not be unsettled by taking a different view as it would lead to a lot of confusion and give rise to a spate of litigation.
Argument	For this he relied upon the decision of the Supre me Court in <CITATION_857581> , AIR 1973 SC 291 where it has been observed:  "............ to take a contrary view from the law laid down would have the effect of unsettling the law established for a number of years.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the matter of interpretation of a local statute the view taken by <ORG> over a number of years should normally be adhered to and not disturbed.
RatioOfTheDecision	A different view would not only introduce an element of an uncertainty and confusion, it would also have the effect of unsettling transactions which might have been entered on the faith of those decisions.
RatioOfTheDecision	The doctrine of stare decisis can be invoked in such a situation."
RatioOfTheDecision	There is a lot of force in this contention also but it is not necessary to invoke the doctrine of stare decisis, because we are of the firm view that there is no ambiguity whatsoever in the language in which this provision is couched and having regard to all the facts and the circumstances of the case it is not possible to interpret it differently.
RatioOfTheDecision	8.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is no doubt true th at the Hindi version is in conflict with the English version.
RatioOfTheDecision	The Hindi version expressly says that the ban shall apply to the holding as a whole as also to a part thereof.
RatioOfTheDecision	The question arises as to whether in such a situation we should give preference to the English version or to the Hindi version.
RatioOfTheDecision	For this purpose we have to turn to Article 348 of the Constitution.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is necessary to reproduce that Article.
Statute	It enacts:--  "348 (1) Notwithstanding anything in the foregoing provisions of this Part, until <ORG> by law otherwise provides-  (a) all proceedings in <ORG> and <ORG>,  (b) the authoritative texts-  (i) of all Bills to be introduced or amendments thereto to be moved in either <ORG> of <ORG> or in the <ORG> or either <ORG> of the Legislature of a State.
Statute	(ii) of all Acts passed by <ORG> or the Legislature of a State and of all ordinances promulgated by the President or the Governor of a State, and (iii) of all orders, rules, regulations and bye-laws issued under thi s Constitution or under any law made by <ORG> or the <ORG> of a <ORG>, shall be in the English language.
Statute	(2) Notwithstanding anything in Sub-clause (a) of Clause (1) the Governor of a <ORG> may, with the previous consent of the President, authorise the use of the Hindi language, or any other language used for any official purpose of the <ORG>, in proceedings in <ORG> having its principal seat in that <ORG>: Provided that nothing in this Clause shall apply to any judgment, decree or order passed or made by such High Court.
Statute	(3) Notwithstanding anything in Sub-clause (b) of Clause (1) where the <ORG> of a state has prescribed any language other than the English language for use in Bills introduced in, or Acts passed by, the <ORG> of the <ORG> or in Ordinances promulgated of the Governor of the state or in any order, rule, regulation or bye-law referred to in paragraph (iii) of that sub-clause, a translation of the same in the English language published under the authority of the  Governor of the State in the official Gazette of that State shall be deemed to be the authoritative text thereof in the English language under this Article."
RatioOfTheDecision	Under this Article two things have been provided.
RatioOfTheDecision	Firstly all proceedings in <ORG> and in every High Court have to be in the English language except when under Clause (2) the Governor of a State with the previous consent of the President authorises the use of the Hindi language or any other local language in proceedings in <ORG> of that State.
RatioOfTheDecision	We are not concerned with that question in this case.
RatioOfTheDecision	Secondly the authoritative text of all Bills and Acts of the <ORG> and of <ORG> has to be in the English language.
RatioOfTheDecision	However, <ORG>s are authorised to prescribe any language other than the English language for this purpose, but, in such an event Clause (3) provides that a translation in the English language of any Bill introduced in or Act passed by <ORG> in a language other than the English  language has to be published under the authority of the Governor of the State in <ORG> state and such translation shall be deemed to be the authoritative text of such a Bill and Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	Whenever a question arises as to what is the authoritative text of a particular Act or an Ordinance etc., of <ORG> one has to turn to its English translation if ii was enacted in a language other than the English language.
RatioOfTheDecision	The authoritative text of a Bill, Act or Ordinance of <ORG> cannot be in a language other than the English language unless the <ORG> by law otherwise provides.
RatioOfTheDecision	Thus the power to declare that the authoritative text of any Ordinance, Act etc., of a State Legislative shall, be in a language other than the English language has been vested exclusively in the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	The <ORG> has not made any such provision so far.
RatioOfTheDecision	The official language of the State of Uttar Pradesh is Hindi, so that the <ORG> of this State can pass Ordinance, Acts etc., in the H indi Language.
RatioOfTheDecision	Thus even though the U. P. Consolidation of Holdings Act was passed by <ORG> in Hindi, yet its translation in the English Language shall be regarded its authoritative text and shall prevail over its Hindi version.
RatioOfTheDecision	9.
RatioOfTheDecision	The learned Counsel for the respondents referred to Articles 345, 346 and 347 of the Constitution.
RatioOfTheDecision	Article 345 makes a provision for official language of a <ORG> and provides that <ORG> may by law adopt any one or more of the languages in use in the <ORG> or the Hindi as the language or languages to be used for all or any of the official purposes.
RatioOfTheDecision	Article 346 makes a provision about the official language for communication between one <ORG> and another or between a <ORG> and the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	In Article 347 a provision has been made that on a demand being made the President may direct that a language other than the English language shall be officially recognised in a <ORG> or any part thereof for such purposes as may be specified.
RatioOfTheDecision	These Articles have no bea ring upon the question before us.
RatioOfTheDecision	We are not concerned with the official language of a <ORG> but are concerned with the question of the language in which the Bills and Acts have to be passed by a <ORG> <ORG> and that subject has been specifically dealt with in Article 348 , which provides that notwithstanding anything in the foregoing provisions of this part of the Constitution, all Acts etc., shall be in the English language.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is thus clear that the provisions contained in Articles 345, 346 and 347 are subject to the provisions contained in Article 348.
RatioOfTheDecision	10.
RatioOfTheDecision	The other argument is that in Clause (3) of Article 348 the use of words "notwithstanding anything in Sub-clause (b) of Clause (1)" suggests that this clause will override Clause (1).
RatioOfTheDecision	This interpretation is not correct.
RatioOfTheDecision	It only means that a <ORG> <ORG> may prescribe any language other than the English Language for use in Bills introduced in or Acts passed by the <ORG> <ORG> and that Clause (1) (b) shall not create an impediment in it s way.
RatioOfTheDecision	As we have already indicated above, when a Bill is introduced or an Act is passed in a language other than the English language by <ORG>, an authoritative translation thereof in the English language has to be provided and that translation shall for the purposes of Clause (1) (b) be deemed to be the authoritative text thereof.
RatioOfTheDecision	Indeed, it will be beyond the competence of <ORG> to provide that the authoritative text of its Act and Ordinance etc., shall be in a language other than the English language, because such a power vests only in the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	Thus, when there is a conflict between the English version of a Statute of <ORG> and its version in a local language, the version in English language will prevail over the version in the local language.
Precedent	<ORG> of this court in <CITATION_1822801> ., AIR 1954 All 257 while referring to Article 348 of the Constitution has at page 278 in paragraph 83 observed:--  "In view of this provision of the Constitution t he notification appearing in English must prevail over the notification appearing in Hindi."
Precedent	A <ORG> of this Court in <CITATION_973037> , AIR 1962 AH 240 (FB) has held that in U. P. after the passing of the U. P. Act s No.
RatioOfTheDecision	1 of 1950 (U. P. Language Bills Act) and 26 of 1951 (U. P. Official Language Act) <ORG> has prescribed Hindi as the language for the official use in the <ORG>, and both, the Hindi version as also the English translation of a Bill or Act etc., published in <ORG> are valid and authorised and both of them can be looked into and put to official use.
RatioOfTheDecision	There is no competition between the two.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is only in case of conflict or divergence between the two versions that the English version may reign supreme and supersede the Hindi one.
RatioOfTheDecision	Following this <ORG> decision a learned Single Judge in <CITATION_1365275> , AIR 1965 All 170 has held that although Hindi was the official language of the <ORG> of U. P., in case of divergence between the Hindi and the English  versions, of the official gazette the English version reigned supreme and superseded the Hindi version.
RatioOfTheDecision	The same view has been taken by <ORG> in <CITATION_1297969> , AIR 1966 Raj 142 and <ORG> in Govindram Ram Pra-sad v. Assessing Authority (Sales Tax), AIR 1958 Madh Pra 16.
RatioOfTheDecision	11.
RatioOfTheDecision	This being the position we are clearly of the opinion that in the present case it is the English text which shall prevail over the Hindi version and according to the English text the expression "any holding" occurring in Clause (ii) of Section 5 (1) (c) of the Act does not include the "Whole holding" so that it is not necessary to obtain the permission of the Settlement Officer (Consolidation) for the transfer of the holding as a whole.
RatioOfTheDecision	12.
RulingByPresentCourt	With this answer, we direct the writ Petition to be placed before a learned Single Judge for disposal.
