RatioOfTheDecision	17.
RatioOfTheDecision	The above general consideration apart, there are other indications in the statute which show that the Act does not prohibit the export of imported goods.
RatioOfTheDecision	The Act provides that goods which are cleared from the customs area for warehousing can be cleared from the warehouse for home consumptio n (S. 68) or exportation (S. 69).
RatioOfTheDecision	At first blush, this may seem to support the <ORG>'s interpretation that clearance for exportation and clearance for home consumption are two different things.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is indeed suggested by <ORG> counsel that, if an importer intends to export the imported goods, he should clear them for warehousing and then proceed in terms of Section 69 .
RatioOfTheDecision	But a little thought would show this interpretation cannot be correct.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the first place, where an importer, even at the time of the import purchase has decided to sell the goods in another country (as in the present case), he may, as pointed out earlier, easily ask the goods to be transitted or transhipped to the country of sale and thus avoid any necessity for their being at all cleared in India.
RatioOfTheDecision	But where, for one reason or other, he wants to import the goods into India and then sell them to the foreign country or where the importer decides on an export sale only after he has arranged for the import of the goods into India, the Act prescr ibes no form of a Bill of Entry under which he can clear such goods intended for re-export.
RatioOfTheDecision	It would not be correct to insist that he must clear them for warehousing and then export them by clearing from the warehouse.
RatioOfTheDecision	Whether to deposit the goods in warehouse or not is an option given to the importer.
RatioOfTheDecision	If he is able to pay the import duties and has his own place to stock the goods, he is entitled to take them away.
RatioOfTheDecision	But, where he has either some difficulty in payment of the duties or where he has no ready place to stock the goods before use or sale, he cannot clear the goods from the customs area.
RatioOfTheDecision	The warehouse is only a place which the importer, on payment of prescribed charges, is permitted to utilise for keeping the goods where he is not able to take the goods straightaway outside the customs area.
RatioOfTheDecision	There is nothing in the provisions of the Act to compel an importer even before or when importing the goods, to make up his mind whether he is going to use or sell them in India or whether he proposes to re-expor t them.
RatioOfTheDecision	Again, there may be cases where he has imported the goods for use or sale in India but subsequently receives an attractive offer which necessitates an export.
RulingByLowerCourt	It would make export trade difficult to say that he cannot accept the export offer as the goods, when imported, had been cleared for home consumption.
RulingByLowerCourt	Section 69 , therefore, should be only read as a provision setting out the procedure for export of warehoused goods and not as a provision which makes warehousing an imperative pre-condition for exporting the imported goods.
RulingByLowerCourt	The second reason for not reading Sections 68 and 69 as supporting the <ORG>'s interpretation is even more weighty.
RulingByLowerCourt	That interpretation would mean that imported goods can be re-exported after being warehoused for sometime (even a day or a few hours) but that they cannot be exported otherwise.
RatioOfTheDecision	Such an interpretation has no basis in logic or sense and makes mincemeat of the broader principle contended for by the <ORG> that imports are intended for use in the country and not  for export.
RatioOfTheDecision	Incidentally, we may observe that even this principle contended for by <ORG> may itself be of doubtful validity as it is based on an erroneous assumption that a re-export of imported goods will always be detrimental to the country.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is true that, in the present case, the appellant has been criticised for having utilised valuable hard currency for the purchases and reselling the goods only for rupee consideration.
RatioOfTheDecision	But, conceivably, there may be cases where an importer is able to import goods from soft-currency area and sell them in a hard-currency area earning foreign exchange for the country.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is also possible to think of cases where, though economically unremunerative, the re-exports can be justified on considerations of international amity and goodwill such as for example, where the goods are exported to a country which is in a dire need of help and assistance.
RatioOfTheDecision	The principle is also non-acceptable on the ground of vagueness as to the extent of its application to exports made after an inter val or after changing several hands inside the country by way of sale.
RatioOfTheDecision	We are, therefore, unable to read Sections 68 and 69 as supporting the <ORG>'s contention.

RatioOfTheDecision	The question raised by the <ORG> in the present case, are also answered by <ORG> as can be clearly noted.
