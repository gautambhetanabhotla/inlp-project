RatioOfTheDecision	Before we enter into the merits of this case on the questions as addressed by the learned Advocates of both sides, let Us Iook into the admitted facts of this case .

RatioOfTheDecision	8.
RatioOfTheDecision	Be it stated here at the very outset that in a case of concurrent findings of fact by both the courts below, the scope of interference  by <ORG> is very limited and <ORG> should not interfere with the concurrent findings of facts of the <ORG> below on the ground of perversity unless the court concerned misdirected itself In coming to its finding on the question of fact.
RatioOfTheDecision	Such concurrent finding of fact, however, as observed by <ORG> can be interfered with only where it is manifestly unjust or where the essential ingredients for such a finding of fact have not been found by the <ORG> below.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is true, in an ejectment suit a finding on the question whether the defendant is a tenant or a licensee, the finding of the lower appellate Court on a consideration of the evidence is a finding of fact.
RatioOfTheDecision	Reference may be made in this connection to the decision of <ORG> .
RatioOfTheDecision	However, it has also been observed by <ORG> in another decision reported in AIR 1989 SC 1 that the question whether there is a tenancy or sub-tenancy or licence or parting with possession in any particular case, must depend upon the  quality of occupation given to the licensee or the transferee.
RatioOfTheDecision	Of course, in the case referred to, dispute arose regarding the occupation of a holder to dis-  play his holding and as such the vital question in that case was whether occupation itself will amount to a question of tenancy or that of a licence.
RatioOfTheDecision	Mere occupation is not sufficient to infer either sub-tenancy or parting with the possession.
RatioOfTheDecision	Facts of the instant case are somewhat different from the one referred to, as because parting of possession in favour of the defendant/appellant is an undisputed fact, though it is claimed on behalf of the plaintiff/ respondent that the respondent was in actual legal possession of the suit property.
RatioOfTheDecision	In other words it is sought to be urged and emphasised that the possession of the defendant/appellant was not exclusive possession in this case.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is, therefore, urged that although the defendant/appellant was permitted to occupy a room, it was the possession of a licensee.
RatioOfTheDecision	9.
RatioOfTheDecision	In view of the contentions of the learne d Advocate for the defendant/appellant, it will be necessary to see as to whether the possession which was given to the defendant/appellant was that of a licensee or that of a tenant.
RatioOfTheDecision	Further, in view of the points raised by the learned Advocate for the defendant/appellant, the entire relevant evidence on record is to be considered to see that the <ORG>s below considered all such important evidence having direct bearing on the disputed issue.
RatioOfTheDecision	If, on such examination we find that the courts' below made such a mistake, then in that case this <ORG> is fully authorised to set aside such finding.
RatioOfTheDecision	To make it clear once again, it is true that High <ORG>, while hearing second appeal under Sec.
RatioOfTheDecision	100, C.P.
RatioOfTheDecision	Code, has not the jurisdiction to examine the evidence and reverse or reject the conclusion reached by the first appellate <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	But we should also bear in mind that it has the power to interfere with such finding, when the lower appellate <ORG> made a mistake of the nature as stated, and can decide the issue, treati ng such finding as unwarranted and then it can be looked into as a substantial question of law.
RatioOfTheDecision	If any authority is needed on this point even after the amendment of 1976, we may refer to the decision , <CITATION_206819> and also the decision of <ORG> , <CITATION_1748770> .
RatioOfTheDecision	10.In the case of this nature where the plaintiff alleges that the defendant/appellant is a licensee and where the defendant/appellant asserts that he is a tenant, the initial onus is upon the plaintiff/landlord to prove that the defendant was inducted as a licensee.

RatioOfTheDecision	Once the plaintiff/respondent tenders evidence showing that the defendant was inducted as a licensee, onus shifted upon the defendant to show that his status is that of a tenant and not a licensee as claimed by him.
