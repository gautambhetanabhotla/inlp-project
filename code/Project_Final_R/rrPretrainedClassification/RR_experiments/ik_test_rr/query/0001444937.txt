RatioOfTheDecision	The basic principles of the doctrine of estoppel may be summarised.
RatioOfTheDecision	Where a promise is made with the intention that the other party should act upon it, the promisor can be compelled to keep his word, if the promisee has acted upon the promise.
RatioOfTheDecision	The promise need not prove that, acting on the promise, he suffered prejudice, detriment or damage, <CITATION_871220> .,  : (AIR 1979 SC 621).
RatioOfTheDecision	However, the indispensable requirement o f the rule of estoppel is that the promise altered his position <CITATION_1348961> , AIR 1987 SC 2414.
RatioOfTheDecision	The doctrine applies to all public authorities including public corporations.
RatioOfTheDecision	Such corporation can be compelled to perform their obligations arising from a promise made by them.
RatioOfTheDecision	<CITATION_1635654> ., AIR 1983 SC 848.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the application of the doctrine of estoppel the limitations of its application cannot be lost sight of.
RatioOfTheDecision	The doctrine is not applicable irrespective of the circumstances in which the promise was made and the situations which it was intended to meet.
RatioOfTheDecision	In public law the most obvious limitation on the doctrine of estoppel is that it cannot be invoked so as to give an authority powers which it does not, in law, possess.
Precedent	In other words, no estoppel can legitimise action which is ultra vires Administrative Law --H.W.R.
Precedent	Wade, Fifth Edition, Page 233.
Precedent	For instance, where an electricity authority, by misreading a meter, undercharged its customer, it was held that the accounts it delivered did not estop  it from demanding payment in full.
Precedent	The reason is that the authority had statutory duty to collect the full amount and had no power to release the customer, expressly or otherwise.
Precedent	Administrative Law -- H.W.R.
Precedent	Wade, Fifth Edition, page 233.
Precedent	Secondly, the promisor cannot be compelled to perform his promise if it is inequitable to do so.
Precedent	<CITATION_871220> ., (1979)2 SCC 409 : (AIR 1979 SC 621).
Precedent	Since the doctrine is an equitable doctrine the promisor will not be bound to fulfil the promise if it is shown that equity lies in his favour <CITATION_871220> .,  : (AIR 1979 SC 621).
Precedent	Thirdly, promisory estoppel cannot be used to compel the <ORG> or a public authority to carry out a representation or promise which is prohibited by law or which was devoid of the authority or power in the officer of the <ORG> or the public authority to make <CITATION_871220> .,  : (AIR 1979 SC 621); (b) <NAME> <CITATION_517737> , AIR 1991 SC 14).
RatioOfTheDecision	A fifth limitation arises where <ORG>'s policy is concerned.
RatioOfTheDecision	Where Go vernment changes its policy resulting in refusal to fulfil the promise, no estoppel arises.
RatioOfTheDecision	Many people may be victims of such reversal of policy.
RatioOfTheDecision	But estoppel cannot be allowed to hinder the formation of policy, <ORG> v. <ORG>, (1977 QB 643, <ORG>).

RatioOfTheDecision	10.
RatioOfTheDecision	In view of the two facts set out in paragraph 9 above, the promise by the <ORG> becomes without authority and estoppel cannot be in voked to legitimate action which is ultra vires.
Precedent	Realising that the <ORG> had no legal existence after 1st October, 1988 making its promise unauthorised, learned counsel for the petitioner called in aid the judgment in <NAME> v. <ORG> (1949) 1 KB 227.
Precedent	The circumstances in which the doctrine of estoppel was applied explain the rationale of the judgment.
Precedent	<NAME>, a colonel in the <ORG> suffered a permanent physical disability during the war in 1940.
Fact	Two departments of the <ORG>, <ORG> and <ORG>, were concerned with the grant of pension to <NAME>.
Fact	<NAME> based his claim on the certificate of disability issued by the <ORG> Medical Board.
Fact	The Director of Personnel Services of <ORG>, on 8th April, 1941 wrote to <NAME> that "your case has been duly considered and your disability has been accepted as attributable to military service."
Fact	The acceptance of attribut-ability of the disability to military service entitled <NAME> to pension.
Fact	But the let ter dated 8th April, 1941 was written without even consulting <ORG> who declined to pay.
Fact	If the war office were to hold that the disability was not attributable to war service, <NAME> would have obtained independent medical opinion which he did not do.
Fact	He thus altered his position, acting on the letter from the Director of Personnel Services.
Fact	<NAME>, J. made these findings:  (i) <NAME>, an officer in the <ORG> who naturally dealt with the war office was entitled to assume that the war office had issued the letter after consulting <NAME>.
RatioOfTheDecision	(ii) The war office assumed authority over the matter and made the assurance contained in the letter dated 8th April, 1941, which, in the circumstances, was authoritative decision intended to be binding and intended to be acted upon.
RatioOfTheDecision	(iii) The citizen is not expected to know the limits of the authority to make the promise.
RatioOfTheDecision	(iv) If <ORG> in its dealings with a subject takes upon itself to assume authority upon a  matter with which he is concerned, he is entitled to rely upon it.
Fact	(v) The war office is an agent of the <ORG>.
Fact	Its letter binds the <ORG> through whom it also binds <NAME>.
RatioOfTheDecision	11.
RatioOfTheDecision	There are clear features which distinguish <NAME>'s case with the case which I am called upon to decide.
RatioOfTheDecision	The assurance by the war office was held authoritative, as <NAME>, an officer in <ORG> naturally looked to the war office to whom he was subordinate.
RatioOfTheDecision	The relation was of employer and employee.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the present case, there is no such relationship between the petitioner and the <ORG> Secondly, the <ORG> was held bound by the promise by <NAME>, irrespective of whether the latter acted within his authority.
RatioOfTheDecision	In this case <ORG> which provides the funds for disbursement of the subsidy is not bound by the sanction by the <ORG> unless it acts within the scheme.
RatioOfTheDecision	Thirdly, it has not been held in <NAME> that a promise by a department who lacks the authority to certify at tributability to military service, by itself estops the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	<NAME>, J. construed the circumstances such as the relationship between the Minister of War, and <NAME> or.
RatioOfTheDecision	the one hand, and the <ORG> on the other, and read in the letter an authority in the war office to make the promise.
RatioOfTheDecision	A fourth distinguishing feature is that whether <ORG> had authorised it or not, the <ORG> was held bound by what the war office promised.
RatioOfTheDecision	While these distinguishing features are sufficient to make, <NAME>, <NAME> v. Minister of Pensions, (1949) 1 KB 227 inapplicable to the facts of the case before me they do not end the matter.
RatioOfTheDecision	The question of authority of the promisor continues to raise its head and demands an answer.
RatioOfTheDecision	The assumption of authority by <NAME> of the war office, was the dominant feature of the case.
RatioOfTheDecision	By analogy, it has been urged that <ORG> too assumed such authority when it sanctioned the subsidy.
RatioOfTheDecision	But this argument is not tenable.
RatioOfTheDecision	The war off ice has continued authority to deal with cases of the members of the armed forces.
RatioOfTheDecision	Its authority did not flow from a temporary scheme nor was It limited by time, as in this case.
RatioOfTheDecision	Secondly, the war office was not altogether without authority to determine the cases of pensions of the army personnel.
RatioOfTheDecision	Its letter would have been authoritative if <ORG> had concurred with it.
RatioOfTheDecision	In <NAME>, the question of authority arose because the letter was written without the concurrence of or consultation with <ORG> who was concerned with the payment of pensions.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the case of <ORG>, it cannot be said that it would have acquired the authority to sanction the subsidy, if it had consulted any other department of the Government.
RatioOfTheDecision	A close scrutiny of the case of <NAME> and this original petition shows that the rule laid down in <NAME> is not applicable, to the circumstances of this case.
RatioOfTheDecision	12.
Fact	I will now examine the validity of the principle laid down in <NAME>, <NAME> v. Minister o f Pensions, (1949) 1 KB 227.
Fact	For this purpose I will examine whether the principle is accepted in England.
Precedent	The basic postulate of <NAME> was that where <ORG> wrongfully assumes authority to perform some legal act, the citizen is entitled to assume that it has that authority Adminsitrative Law -- H.W.R.
Precedent	Wade, Fifth Edition Page 342.
Argument	The British judicial decisions have not accepted this proposition.
Argument	When the same proposition was repeated by <NAME>, <NAME> in a later case, <ORG> repudiated it in these words:  ".....
RatioOfTheDecision	I know of no such principle in our law nor was any authority for it cited.
Precedent	The illegality of an act is the same whether, or not the actor has been misled by an assumption of authority on the part of the Government officer however high or low in the hierarchy"  <NAME> v. <ORG>,(1951 AC 837).
Precedent	If the promise by an official who has no legal power is given the force of law, ultra vires acts will gain legitimacy which is a negation of the fun damental canons of administrative law Administrative Law -- H.W.R.
Precedent	Wade, Fifth Edition, Page 345.
Precedent	Where a public authority without having real or ostensible authority sold the property of the <ORG>, it was held that the <ORG> was not bound by the sale.
Precedent	The burden of ascertaining whether the officer has such authority is on the purchaser A.G. For Ceylon v. <NAME>, (1953 AC 461 at page 480).
Precedent	In <ORG>, <ORG> Ltd. v. Penwith D.C., ((1981) 2 All ER 204), the planning officer without having the authority, stated that <ORG> had the existing user right in respect of the site in question and that the District council who had the statutory authority would grant planning permission.
Precedent	Acting on the planning officer's statement <ORG> proceeded to renovate and rebuild the old buildings.
Precedent	<ORG> in exercise of its statutory authority rejected the applications for established use certificates and issued stop notices.
Precedent	It was held that since there  was nothing apart from the position held by the Planning Officer on which <ORG> could have assumed that the officer could bind the council, the council was not estopped by anything the planning officer had said, from refusing the applications by <ORG>.
Precedent	13.
Precedent	The analysis of the law in England made in the last paragraph may be summed up.
Precedent	<ORG> in <NAME>, <NAME> v. <ORG>, 1951 AC 837, expressly and emphatically rejected the proposition laid down in <NAME>, <NAME> v. Minister of Pensions, (1949) 1 KB 227.
Precedent	In A.G. for Ceylon, A.G. For Ceylon v. <NAME>, 1953 AC 461 at page 480, the sale of Government's property by an officer having no authority was held not binding on the Government.
RatioOfTheDecision	This judgment is a rejection of the claim of legitimacy by ultra vires acts.
RatioOfTheDecision	<ORG>, <ORG> Ltd. v. <ORG>, (1981) 2 All ER 204 also demonstrates that unauthorised promises do not create estoppel.
RatioOfTheDecision	<NAME>, J.  assumed that the citizen is not expected to know the limits of the authority to make promises.
RatioOfTheDecision	Considerations of hardship to the citizen led him to this conclusion.
Precedent	But in A.G. for Ceylon, A.G. For Ceylon v. A.D. Silva, 1953 AC 461 at page 480 this very proposition was rejected.
RatioOfTheDecision	I therefore conclude that <NAME>, <NAME> v. Minister of Pensions, (1949) I KB 227 which has been rejected in England does not lay down the correct law.
RatioOfTheDecision	Its assumption that an unauthorised promise creates estoppel is contrary to the fundamental principle of administrative law that estoppel cannot be invoked to legitimise ultra vires actions.
RatioOfTheDecision	Again the proposition that a citizen is not expected to know the limits of the authority of an officer to make the promise tends to legitimise unauthorised promises.
RatioOfTheDecision	To hold that a citizen, acting on an unauthorised promise without enquiring into the limits of such authority, can bind a public authority, is to create a power which does not exist.
RatioOfTheDecision	14.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the present case the <ORG> wrote the  letter dated 28-9-1989 (Ext.
RatioOfTheDecision	P2) at a time when the scheme which created the <ORG> was not in force.
RatioOfTheDecision	The termination of the scheme with effect from 1-10-1988, automatically terminated the <ORG> The "promise" or "sanction" contained in the letter dated 28-9-1989 (Ext.
RatioOfTheDecision	P2) was therefore an assurance by a non-existent body.
RatioOfTheDecision	Even if the existence of the <ORG> is assumed, the letter dated 28-9-1989 (Ext.
RatioOfTheDecision	P2) was without the authority of the scheme.
RatioOfTheDecision	The "sanction" or "promise" was ultra vires of the authority of the <ORG> and therefore no estoppel arises out of such a promise.
RatioOfTheDecision	The submission of the petitioner that the respondents are estpped from denying the payment of the subsidy is without merit.
