Precedent	Strong reliance was placed on the decision of this Court in <CITATION_169580> (AIR 1995 SC 1601) to contend that same was fatal to the prosecution case.
Precedent	In the statement under Section 313 of the .

RatioOfTheDecision	In response, learned counsel for the <ORG> submitted that three eye-witnesses specifically deposed regarding the place of occurrence, the manner of assault and gave detailed description of the entire scenario.

RatioOfTheDecision	We shall first deal with the question regarding non-explanation of injuries on the accused.
RatioOfTheDecision	Issue is if there is no such explanation what would be its effect?
RatioOfTheDecision	We are not prepared to agree with the learned counsel for the defence that i n each and every case where prosecution fails to explain the injuries found on some of the accused, the prosecution case should automatically be rejected, without any further probe.
RatioOfTheDecision	<CITATION_1935279> (1968 (3) SCR  525), it was observed:  "...In our judgment, the failure of the prosecution to offer any explanation in that regard shows that evidence of the prosecution witnesses relating to the incident is not true or at any rate not wholly true.
RatioOfTheDecision	Further those injuries probabilise the plea taken by the appellants."
Precedent	In another important case <CITATION_807023> (1976 (4) SCC 394), after referring to the ratio laid down in <NAME> case (supra), this Court observed:  "Where the prosecution fails to explain the injuries on the accused, two results follow:  (1) that the evidence of the prosecution witnesses is untrue; and (2) that the injuries probabilise the plea taken by the appellants."
Precedent	It was further observed that:  "In a murder case, the non-explanation of the injuries sustained by the accused at about the  time of the occurrence or in the course of altercation is a very important circumstance from which the Court can draw the following inferences:  (1) that the prosecution has suppressed the genesis and the origin of the occurrence and has thus not presented the true version;  (2) that the witnesses who have denied the presence of the injuries on the person of the accused are lying on a most material point and, therefore, their evidence is unreliable;  (3) that in case there is a defence version which explains the injuries on the person of the accused assumes much greater importance where the evidence consists of interested or inimical witnesses or where the defence gives a version which competes in probability with that of the prosecution one."
RatioOfTheDecision	In <NAME> case (supra) it is made clear that failure of the prosecution to offer any explanation regarding the injuries found on the accused may show that the evidence related to the incident is not true or at any rate not wholly true.
RatioOfTheDecision	Likewise in <NAME>  case (supra) it is observed that any non-explanation of the injuries on the accused by the prosecution may affect the prosecution case.
RatioOfTheDecision	But such a non- explanation may assume greater importance where the defence gives a version which competes in probability with that of the prosecution.
RatioOfTheDecision	But where the evidence is clear, cogent and creditworthy and where the <ORG> can distinguish the truth from falsehood the mere fact that the injuries are not explained by the prosecution cannot by itself be a sole basis to reject such evidence, and consequently the whole case.
RatioOfTheDecision	Much depends on the facts and circumstances of each case.
Precedent	These aspects were highlighted by this <ORG> in <CITATION_1060754> .
Precedent	(AIR 1990 SC 1459).
Precedent	Non-explanation of injuries by the prosecution will not affect prosecution case where injuries sustained by the accused are minor and superficial or where the evidence is so clear and cogent, so independent and disinterested, so probable, consistent and creditworthy, that it outweighs the effect of the omiss ion on the part of prosecution to explain the injuries.
Precedent	As observed by this <ORG> in <CITATION_900679> (AIR 1972 SC 2593) prosecution is not called upon in all cases to explain the injuries received by the accused persons.
Precedent	It is for the defence to put questions to the prosecution witnesses regarding the injuries of the accused persons.
Precedent	When that is not done, there is no occasion for the prosecution witnesses to explain any injury on the person of an accused.
Precedent	In <NAME> and Ors.
Precedent	v. State of Bihar (AIR 1988 SC 863), it was observed that the obligation of the prosecution to explain the injuries sustained by the accused in the same occurrence may not arise in each and every case.
RatioOfTheDecision	In other words, it is not an invariable rule that the prosecution has to explain the injuries sustained by the accused in the same occurrence.
RatioOfTheDecision	If the witnesses examined on behalf of the prosecution are believed by the <ORG> in proof of guilt of the accused beyond reasonable doubt, question of obligation of prosecution to expla in injuries sustained by the accused will not arise.
RatioOfTheDecision	When the prosecution comes with a definite case that the offence has been committed by the accused and proves its case beyond any reasonable doubt, it becomes hardly necessary for the prosecution to again explain how and under what circumstances injuries have been inflicted on the person of the accused.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is more so when the injuries are simple or superficial in nature.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the case at hand, trifle and superficial injuries on accused are of little assistance to them to throw doubt on veracity of prosecution case.
RatioOfTheDecision	So far as the non seizure of blood from the cot is concerned, the investigating officer has stated that he found blood stained earth at the place of occurrence and had seized it.
RatioOfTheDecision	Merely because it was not sent for chemical examination, it may be a defect in the investigation but does not corrode the evidentiary value of the eye-witnesses.
RatioOfTheDecision	The investigating officer did not find presence of blood on the cot.
RatioOfTheDecision	<ORG> and <ORG> have a nalysed this aspect.
RatioOfTheDecision	It has been found that after receiving the bullet injury the deceased leaned forward and whatever blood was profusing spilled over to the earth.
RatioOfTheDecision	So far as the effect of the bullet being not sent for chemical examination, it has to be noted that <NAME> case (supra) is not an authority for the proposition as submitted that whenever a bullet is not sent for chemical examination the prosecution has to fail.
RatioOfTheDecision	In that case one of the factors which weighed with this Court for not finding the accused guilty was the prosecution's failure to send the weapon and the bullet for ballistic examination.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the instant case, the weapon was not seized.
RatioOfTheDecision	That makes a significant factual difference between <NAME> case (supra) and the present case.
