RatioOfTheDecision	With respect, we are unable to agree with the view taken by <ORG>.
Precedent	As held by this <ORG> in <CITATION_1642416> .,  4 SCC 183, whereas in England the rights of the spouses to the matrimonial home are governed by the Matrimonial Homes Act, 1967, no such right exists in India.
Precedent	In the same decision it was observed "it may be that with change of situation and complex problems arising it is high time to give the wife or the <NAME> a right of occupation in a truly matrimonial home, in case of the marriage breaking up or in case of strained relationship between the <NAME> and the wife."
RatioOfTheDecision	In our opinion, the above observation is merely an expression of hope and it does not lay down any law.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is only the legislature which can create a law and not the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	The courts do not legislate, and whatever may be the personal view of a Judge, he cannot create or amend the law, and must maintain judicial restraint.
Argument	There is no such law in India, like the British Matrimonial Homes Act, 1967, and in any  case, the rights which may be available under any law can only be as against the <NAME> and not against the <NAME>-in-law or mother- in-law.
Argument	Here, the house in question belongs to the mother-in-law of Smt.
Argument	<NAME> and it does not belong to her <NAME> <NAME>.
Argument	Hence, Smt.
Argument	<NAME> cannot claim any right to live in the said house.
Fact	Appellant No.
Fact	2, the mother-in-law of Smt.
Fact	<NAME> has stated that she had taken a loan for acquiring the house and it is not a joint family property.
RatioOfTheDecision	We see no reason to disbelieve this statement.
Precedent	Learned counsel for the respondent then relied upon the Protection of Women from Domestic Violence Act , 2005.
Precedent	He stated that in view of the said Act respondent Smt.
Precedent	<NAME> cannot be dispossessed from the second floor of the property in question.
Precedent	It may be noticed that the finding of the learned Senior Civil Judge that in fact Smt.
Precedent	<NAME> was not residing in the premises in question is a finding of fact which cannot be interfered with either under Article  226 or 227 of the Constitution.
Precedent	Hence, Smt.
Precedent	<NAME> cannot claim any injunction restraining the appellants from dispossessing her from the property in question for the simple reason that she was not in possession at all of the said property and hence the question of dispossession does not arise.
Precedent	Apart from the above, we are of the opinion that the house in question cannot be said to be a `shared household' within the meaning of Section 2(s) of the Protection of Women from Domestic Violence Act , 2005 (hereinafter referred to as the 'Act').
Precedent	Section 2(s) states:  "`shared household` means a household where the person aggrieved lives or at any stage has lived in a domestic relationship either singly or along with the respondent and includes such a household whether owned or tenanted either jointly by the aggrieved person and the respondent, or owned or tenanted by either of them in respect of which either the aggrieved person or the respondent or both jointly or singly have any right, title, interest or e quity and includes such a household which may belong to the joint family of which the respondent is a member, irrespective of whether the respondent or the aggrieved person has any right, title or interest in the shared household".
Precedent	Learned counsel for the respondent Smt.
Precedent	<NAME> has relied upon Sections 17 and 19(1) of the aforesaid Act, which state:  "17.
Precedent	(1)Notwithstanding anything contained in any other law for the time being in force, every woman in a domestic relationship shall have the right to reside in the shared household, whether or not she has any right, title or beneficial interest in the same.
Precedent	(2) The aggrieved person shall not be evicted or excluded from the shared household or any part of it by the respondent save in accordance with the procedure established by law.
Precedent	19.
Precedent	(1) While disposing of an application under sub-section (1) of section 12 , the Magistrate may, on being satisfied that domestic violence has taken place, pass a residence order--  (a) restraining the respondent from dis possessing or in any other manner disturbing the possession of the aggrieved person from the shared household, whether or not the respondent has a legal or equitable interest in the shared household;  (b) directing the respondent to remove himself from the shared household;  (c) restraining the respondent or any of his relatives from entering any portion of the shared household in which the aggrieved person resides;  (d) restraining the respondent from alienating or disposing off the shared household or encumbering the same;  (e) restraining the respondent from renouncing his rights in the shared household except with the leave of the Magistrate; or  (f) directing the respondent to secure same level of alternate accommodation for the aggrieved person as enjoyed by her in the shared household or to pay rent for the same, if the circumstances so require:  Provided that no order under clause (b) shall be passed against any person who is a woman".
Precedent	Learned counsel for the respondent Smt.
Argument	<NAME> stated that  the definition of shared household includes a household where the person aggrieved lives or at any stage had lived in a domestic relationship.
Argument	He contended that since admittedly the respondent had lived in the property in question in the past, hence the said property is her shared household.
RatioOfTheDecision	We cannot agree with this submission.
RatioOfTheDecision	If the aforesaid submission is accepted, then it will mean that wherever the <NAME> and wife lived together in the past that property becomes a shared household.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is quite possible that the <NAME> and wife may have lived together in dozens of places e.g.
RatioOfTheDecision	with the <NAME>'s <NAME>, <NAME>'s paternal grand parents, his maternal parents, uncles, aunts, brothers, sisters, nephews, nieces etc.
RatioOfTheDecision	If the interpretation canvassed by the learned counsel for the respondent is accepted, all these houses of the <NAME>'s relatives will be shared households and the wife can well insist in living in the all these houses of her <NAME>'s relatives merely because she had stayed with her <NAME>  for some time in those houses in the past.
RatioOfTheDecision	Such a view would lead to chaos and would be absurd.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is well settled that any interpretation which leads to absurdity should not be accepted.
RatioOfTheDecision	Learned counsel for the respondent <NAME> has relied upon Section 19(1)(f) of the Act and claimed that she should be given an alternative accommodation.
RatioOfTheDecision	In our opinion, the claim for alternative accommodation can only be made against the <NAME> and not against the <NAME>'s in-laws or other relatives.
RatioOfTheDecision	As regards Section 17(1) of the Act, in our opinion the wife is only entitled to claim a right to residence in a shared household, and a `shared household' would only mean the house belonging to or taken on rent by the <NAME>, or the house which belongs to the joint family of which the <NAME> is a member.
RatioOfTheDecision	The property in question in the present case neither belongs to <NAME> nor was it taken on rent by him nor is it a joint family property of which the <NAME> <NAME> is a member.

RatioOfTheDecision	No doubt, the definition of `shared household' in Section 2(s) of the Act is not very happily worded, and appears to be the result of clumsy drafting, but we have to give it an interpretation which is sensible and which does not lead to chaos in society.
