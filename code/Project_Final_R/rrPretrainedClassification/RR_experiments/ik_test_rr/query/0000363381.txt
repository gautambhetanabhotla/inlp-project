RatioOfTheDecision	10.
RatioOfTheDecision	The question for our consideration, then, is whether it is a necessary requirement of the law that owners of land should receive individual notices from the Land Acquisition Collector of the Government's intention to acquire the land for a public purpose, so as to enable them, if they so chose, to object to the acqu isition.
RatioOfTheDecision	The further question is, whether the acquisition proceedings would be invalid if such individual notices were not given to the persons interested in the land.
RatioOfTheDecision	The answer to these questions must be found, first, in the provisions of the Land Acquisition Act , 1894.
Statute	Section 4(1) of the Act, which is the pertinent provision in this regard, is in the following terms:  Whenever it appears to the appropriate Government that land in any locality is needed or is likely to be needed for a public purpose a notification to that effect shall be published in <ORG>, and the Collector shall cause public notice of the substance of such notification to be given at convenient places in the locality.
Statute	11.
Statute	The section deals with the genesis of the proceedings for compulsory acquisition.
RatioOfTheDecision	The appropriate Government must first be satisfied that any land in a given locality is needed, actually or potentially, for a public purpose.
RatioOfTheDecision	If the Government is so satisfied, then a notification to that effect has to be  published in the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	The section does not require that even for the initial formation of its opinion that the land is needed for a public purpose, the <ORG> should send notices to the owners of the land, convene them, and listen to their objections.
RatioOfTheDecision	The satisfaction of the <ORG> both as to the needs of public purpose and as to the particular land that would satisfy those needs is purely a subjective satisfaction.
Statute	The <ORG> notification is to be issued solely on the basis of this subjective opinion of the <ORG>.
Statute	What should follow the gazette notification is also laid down in the section.
Statute	It provides that the Collector should cause public notice of the gist of the <ORG>'s notification in convenient places in the locality.
Statute	The marginal note to the section sums up the procedure correctly when it refers to "publication of preliminary notification."
Statute	Public notice by the Collector is mandatory, as might be seen from the use of the word "shall" in the section.
Argument	He would be failing in his d uty if he did not cause public notice to be displayed in convenient places in the locality.
Argument	Failure or omission to comply with this step in the procedure for acquisition would invalidate subsequent proceedings, and any person aggrieved would have a right to move the <ORG> under Article 226 of the Constitution to have them quashed, vide observations of the Supreme <ORG> in <CITATION_892303> , wherein this position is clearly laid down, although the person aggrieved in that case was not allowed to challenge the proceedings on this ground, on account of unreasonable delay.
Argument	12.
Argument	In the present case, as we had earlier pointed out, the writ petitioners do not seek to challenge the acquisition proceedings on the score that no public notice was given as required by Section 4(1) , or even on the ground that the public notice as actually given by the Collector fell short of what is contemplated by the section.
Argument	Their grievance is that the Collector did not personally inform them about the <ORG> notification by issuing  to them individual notices containing the gist of the Government's decision to acquire the lands in question for a public purpose.
Argument	This argument, manifestly, could not be founded on the words of Section 4(1) .
Statute	This is because the section, while being positive about the Collector's duty to give public notice in that locality stops short with that provision.
Statute	It does not impose on the Collector any other positive duty in regard to notice of the Government's <ORG> notification.
Statute	Sub-section (2) of Section 4 deals with the next step in the procedure for compulsory acquisition, and it begins with these words, "Thereupon, it shall be lawful for any officer, etc."
Statute	These words are an indication that the issue of <ORG> notification and publicising of the <ORG> notification by display of public notices in the locality are both intended to exhaust the process of notice-giving at the preliminary stage.
Statute	There are other indications, too, in Section 4(2) to the same effect.
Statute	The sub-Section provides that on the publicat ion of the <ORG>'s preliminary notification in the <ORG> and the Collector's public notices in the locality, the <ORG> may authorise any of its officers to enter upon and purvey the lands in the locality and to do any acts necessary to ascertain whether the land notified for acquisition for the avowed public purpose is suitable for that purpose.
Statute	The <ORG> might, under this sub-section authorise its officers to enter any building or dwelling-house or any enclosed Court or garden attached thereto.
RatioOfTheDecision	But the proviso to Section 4(2) clearly enacts that this power of entry of residential buildings etc., cannot be exercised by the officers without previously giving the occupier of the premises at least seven days' notice in writing of his intention to do so.
RatioOfTheDecision	The reference to notice in this proviso is specific and detailed.
RatioOfTheDecision	It requires that the notice should be in writing, it should be addressed to the occupier, it should be served on him personally, and it should give him seven days' time.
RatioOfTheDecision	The provis o, no doubt, dispenses with notice as unnecessary wherever the occupier himself consents to the inspection of the premises.
RatioOfTheDecision	But, short of obtaining the occupier's consent, the power of entry can be exercised by the officers authorized in that behalf only after service of individual notices on the occupier.
Statute	How the notice on the occupier is to be served is dealt with by Section 45(1) , which provides that in the case of a notice under Section 4 it shall be effected by "the officer therein mentioned" by delivering or tendering a copy thereof signed by him to the person intended to be served with the notice.
Statute	Section 45(1) in terms, only refers to " Section 4 ", but it is obvious that this reference cannot appertain to any notice under Sub-section (1) of that section; it can only refer to the notice of intention to enter the premises issued to the owner or occupier of a residential house by officers generally or specially authorized in that behalf by the <ORG>, in connection with the proceedings contemplated  by Section 4(2) and the proviso thereto.
RatioOfTheDecision	13.
RatioOfTheDecision	On the terms of Section 4(1) , therefore, there can be no scope for assuming that the Collector's statutory obligation to publicise the <ORG>'s preliminary Gazette notification for acquisition extends to the giving of individual notices to the persons interested in the lands notified for acquisition.
RatioOfTheDecision	14.
Argument	Mr.<NAME>, learned Counsel for the writ petitioners, however referred to Section 5-A of the Act as an aid to the understanding of the true scope and intendment of the statute regarding notice of the acquisition even at the preliminary stage.
Argument	In this case, the procedure under Section 5-A had not been dispensed with by any direction made by the <ORG> in exercise of its powers under Section 17(4) .
Argument	Section 5-A would, accordingly have full force and effect.

Argument	According to learned Counsel, it was implicit in <NAME>ction 5-A that every one who had a stake in the land should be individually served with the notice of the acquisition.
Argument	learned Counsel referred, in this connection, to an unreported judgment of <NAME>, in <NAME> v. The Land Acquisition Officer, Omalur, Salem District W.P.No.
Argument	1686 of 1971, dated 31st January, 1973.

Statute	After giving such a full hearing, as contemplated under Section 5-A (2), <ORG> shall make his recommendations and submit the case for decision to the appropriate authority.
Statute	15.
Statute	It seems to us that the learned Judge has read more into the section than its language can bear.
Statute	Section 5-A has three sub-sections.
Statute	Under Sub-section (1) any person interested in any land which has been notified under Section 4(1) is at liberty to object to the acquisition of the land within 30 days after the issue of <ORG> notification.
Statute	Sub-section (2) requires that objection shall be made to the Collector in writing.

Statute	Apart from hearing the objections, the Collector is also empowered to institute further inquiries.

Statute	Any decision that the Government might take on the objections is declared by this sub-section to be final.
RatioOfTheDecision	Sub-section (3) of Section 5-A merely lays down the locus standi of the objectors by inserting a special definition of the expression "person interested ."
RatioOfTheDecision	.
RatioOfTheDecision	But this definition is more or less a repetition of the definition in the interpretation clause contained in <CITATION_22955350> of the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	According to these provisions, a person shall be deemed to be interested in the land who would be entitled to claim an interest in compensatio n if the land were acquired under the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	16.
RatioOfTheDecision	The foregoing analysis of Section 5-A of the Act would show that the section deals not with the stage of notice of acquisition, but with a subsequent stage.
RatioOfTheDecision	We have earlier described Section 4(1) as marking the very beginning of the acquisition proceedings under the Land Acquisition Act .
Statute	Section 5-A , no doubt, confers on the persons interested a right to object to the acquisition, but under Sub-section (1) this right has to be exercised by them within 30 days after the preliminary notification under Section 4(1) .
Statute	The prescription of a time-limit in Section 5-A (1) and its computation as of a fixed period after the notification under Section 4(1) clearly show that the provision deals with a stage subsequent to the stage of notification of the acquisition.
Statute	The right to object, under Section 5-A , begins to operate from the date of the notification.
Statute	The person objecting may file his objection on the very next day after the <ORG> publication or on any day falli ng within a period of 30 days.
Statute	By that time, the Collector may or may not have given public notices in prominent places in the locality in accordance with Section 4(1) .
Statute	Irrespective of the date of his public notice the Collector has to entertain the objections, provided they are filed within 30 days after the <ORG> publication.
RatioOfTheDecision	This, again, shows that the right conferred by Section 5-A (1) has nothing whatever to do with the mechanics of notice which the statute contemplates for publicising the notification of the <ORG> under Section 4(1) .
RatioOfTheDecision	We are satisfied that the right under Section 5-A (1), in content as well as in chronology, is a bare right to object to the acquisition.
RatioOfTheDecision	The section does not deal with any right, properly so-called, to notice of the acquisition itself.
RatioOfTheDecision	17.
RatioOfTheDecision	Of what use, it was asked in argument, is a right to object, if the person concerned does not know, and has no means of knowing, that his land has been notified for acquisition?
RatioOfTheDecision	This, however, is mere rhetoric, of a kind that a  law reformer may properly employ to some purpose on the floor of the <ORG> or in his published writings.
RatioOfTheDecision	But for lawyers and <ORG> whose task it is to interpret the legislative enactment, the answer must only be found in the words of the section or not at all.
RatioOfTheDecision	<NAME>, J., had taken the view that it is implicit in Section 5-A that notices ought to go to all the individuals interested in the land.
RatioOfTheDecision	In our view, however, the language of Section 5-A , when read in juxtaposition to Section 4(1) , does not bear any such implication being read into it.
RatioOfTheDecision	18.
Statute	Mr. <NAME> made a reference to the statement of Objects and Reasons set out in the Bill which introduced Section 5-A in the Act in the year 1923.
Statute	According to this statement:  The object of the Bill is to provide that a local Government shall not declare under Section 6 of the Act that any land is needed for a public purpose unless time has been allowed after the notification under Section 4 for persons interested in the land to put in objecti on and for such objections to be considered by the local Government.
RatioOfTheDecision	19.
RatioOfTheDecision	We do not find even in this statement of Objects and Reasons any indication of an intention to enlarge the scope of the notice.
RatioOfTheDecision	It may be observed that while Section 5-A was newly introduced by the Land Acquisition Amendment Act (XXXVIII of 1923), the provisions of Section 4(1) relating to notice were not subjected to any amendment whatever.
RatioOfTheDecision	Indeed, right from 1894, upto the present day Section 4(1) has remained the same, in this regard.
Statute	<ORG> in its 10th Report, dated 26th September, 1958, had suggested an amendment of Section 4(1) in the direction of liberalising the procedure for notice.
Statute	Under <ORG> proposals.
Statute	the Collector shall cause--  (a) public notice of the substance of such notification to be given at convenient places in the locality in which the land is situated, and  (b) a copy of such notification to be served on the owner, and where the owner is not the occupier, also on the occupier, of the l and.
RatioOfTheDecision	20.
RatioOfTheDecision	These amendments, especially Clause (b), are yet to be written into the statute book.
RatioOfTheDecision	Till that time comes, we must construe and persist in construing Section 4(1) only on its present terms.
RatioOfTheDecision	We would not be justified in writing into the section what properly is the function of future legislation.
RatioOfTheDecision	The canons of statutory construction do not include anticipatory interpretation.
RatioOfTheDecision	21.
Argument	Reference was then made to Rule 1 of the Madras Land Acquisition Rules framed by the State Government in exercise of its rule-making power under Section 55(1) of the Act.
Statute	The said Rule is in the following terms:  Rule 1: "Immediately after the publication of the notification under Section 4(1) , the Collector shall issue a notice stating that the land is needed, or is likely to be needed, as the case may be, for a public purpose and requiring all persons interested in the land to lodge before the Collector within 30 days after the issue of the notification, a statement in writing of their objections if any, to the proposed  acquisition.
Statute	This notice should be published at convenient places in the said locality, and copies thereof fixed up in the office of the Collector, the <NAME>, and in the nearest Police Station.
RatioOfTheDecision	22.
RatioOfTheDecision	This Rule does not so much as whisper anything about individual notices to the persons interested in the land.
RatioOfTheDecision	All it does is to repeat the provisions of Section 4(1) and, by way of elucidation, make mention of the places where copies of the public notice should be exhibited.
RatioOfTheDecision	23.
RatioOfTheDecision	It would appear that different States have different Rules on the subject of notice.
RatioOfTheDecision	<CITATION_879806> , <ORG> of <ORG> had occasion to construe the relevant rule in force in the State of Maharashtra .
RatioOfTheDecision	In the course of their judgment, the learned Judges observed that "Neither Section 4 nor Section 5-A , contains any provision for the service of individual notice on any person interested in the land sought to be acquired by a notification under Section 4 ."
RatioOfTheDecision	They were, however, referred to Rule 1 framed by t he Government of Maharashtra.
Precedent	It was to the following effect:  Whenever any notification under Section 4 has been published but the provisions of the section have not been applied and the Collector has under Section 4(1) issued notice to the parties interested, and on or before the last day fixed by the Collector in those notices in this behalf any objection is lodged under Section 5-A(2) : Firstly the Collector shall record the objection in his proceedings, secondly, the Collector shall consider whether the objection is admissible according to these rules.
Precedent	24.
Precedent	Before the learned Judges, there was no dispute that this rule formed part of the statutory rules made by <ORG> in exercise of its powers under Section 55(1) of the Act and hence it had the force of law.
Precedent	Proceeding to construe the rule, the learned Judges held that the words "the Collector has, under Section 4(1) , issued notices to the parties interested" clearly indicated that individual notice should be given to all the parties inter ested in the land.

Precedent	25.
RatioOfTheDecision	Whether or not the conclusion of the learned Judges is justified on the construction of the words of Rule 1 of the Maharashtra Rules is not for us to say.
RatioOfTheDecision	So far as the relevant Rule in force in this State is concerned we have no doubt whatever that it does not envisage or contemplate, much less expressly provide, that individual notices shall be addressed to all the persons interested in the land, in addition to the publication of notices in prominent places in the locality.
RatioOfTheDecision	26. learned Counsel referred us, in this connection, to a decision of <ORG> in <CITATION_892303> .
Fact	<ORG> in that case was concerned with the question of validity of Rule 3(b) of the Madras Land Acquisition Rules.
Fact	This Rule provided that the Collector, while conducting an inquiry under Section 5-A (2) of the Act, must also issue notice of the hearing to the <ORG> at whose  instance the land was proposed to be acquired.
Fact	In the case before <ORG> the Collector did not give notice to <ORG>, which was the concerned <ORG> interested in that acquisition.
Argument	It was argued that Rule 3(b) was not mandatory, and, if it was, it would be against the provisions of Section 5-A (2) which invested the Collector with a complete discretion in the matter of conducting the inquiry, his only statutory obligation being that he should serve the notice of the inquiry on the persons who had lodged their objections.
Argument	This argument was repelled by <ORG>.
Precedent	The learned Judges held that the concerned Rule 3(b) was not only intra vires the Act, but also mandatory in character.
Precedent	27.
RatioOfTheDecision	We do not see how this decision can be relied on as authority for the position that the persons interested in the land must be given individual notices of the intended acquisition.
RatioOfTheDecision	The provisions of Rule 3(b) and Section 5-A , with which <ORG> was concerned, deal with a stage i n the compulsory acquisition proceedings which, as we have shown earlier, is subsequent to the issue of the gazette notification by the <ORG> under Section 4(1) .
RatioOfTheDecision	While Section 4(1) and Rule 1 are obviously intended to facilitate objections to be lodged by the persons interested in the subject-matter of the acquisition, neither Section 4(1) nor Rule 1 contemplates that the Collector should serve individual notices on the persons interested in the lands.
RatioOfTheDecision	Under the scheme of Section 5-A the question of sending individual notices to persons hardly arises until the proceedings reach a stage when objections are actually lodged with the Collector and an inquiry into these objections has to be gone into by him.
RatioOfTheDecision	Besides, even at the stage when the Collector has taken the objections on file, his obligation under Section 5-A (2) to send notices of inquiry does not extend to all persons interested in the land, but is limited only to those of them who had lodged their objections before the Collector within 30 days o f the gazette notification.
RatioOfTheDecision	It follows, therefore, that for the purposes of seeing whether or not the initial notices of acquisition should be served individually on all the persons interested in the land, we would not have to be searching the provisions of Section 5-A or of the relevant rules bearing on Section 5-A , but have to look only to Section 4(1) and Rule 1 for the purpose.
RatioOfTheDecision	The decision of <ORG> cited above is not addresed to any question touching the notice of acquisition, but is concerned rather with the subsequent notice of the inquiry which is taken up by the Collector after he receives the objections on file.

RatioOfTheDecision	Mr. <NAME> then pressed before us the contention that, notwithstanding the absence of any specific provision in the Act or in the Rules in force in this State for sending individual notices of acquisition to persons interested in the land at the stage of proceedings under Section 4(1) of the Act, such notices must be given even at that particular stage, since they are r equired to be given under executive instructions issued to the officers concerned by <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	He relied on a circular issued by <ORG> in G.O.
