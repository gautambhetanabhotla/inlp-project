Fact	<NAME>, J.
Fact	1.
Fact	The petitioner is a company incorporated under the Companies Act , 1956.
Fact	It has a factory at Baroda where it had been manufacturing extruded aluminium collapsible tubes.
Fact	The petitioner held a license for manufacturing extruded shapes and sections including extruded pipes and tubes.
Fact	The license was granted to it by the <ORG> under the <ORG>s and Salt Act , 1944 (hereinafter referred to as "the said Act" for the sake of brevity).
Fact	On 3rd April, 1970 the petitioner surrendered its license to <ORG> for cancellation.
Fact	The Assistant Collector of <ORG>, Baroda cancelled it and informed the petitioner accordingly on 21st April, 1970.
Fact	2.
Fact	Thereafter the petitioner started purchasing plain tubes and plain containers from the market and engaged itself in printing and lacquering them.
Fact	Printed and lacquered tubes and containers were sold by it in the market to their consumers.
Fact	On 25th February, 1971 the petitioner received from <ORG> intendent of <ORG>, Baroda, the letter dated 24th February, 1971 enquiring whether the petitioner had stopped all operations relating to the manufacture of extruded aluminium pipes and tubes including incidental and ancillary process of printing and lacquering them.
Argument	On 2nd March, 1971 the petitioner replied to the Superintendent of <ORG> and stated that no <ORG> license was necessary for printing and/or lacquering duty paid extruded shapes and sections or tubes and pipes purchased by it from the market.
Argument	It contended that no <ORG> duty was leviable on such printing and lacquering.
Argument	The petitioner however stated that if the Superintendent of <ORG> did not accept the aforesaid view of the petitioner, he should hear the petitioner before taking any decision in the matter.
Argument	On 22nd May, 1971, the petitioner received from the Superintendent of <ORG> notice dated 20th May, 1971 (hereinafter referred to 'the impugned notice') asking the petitioner to obtain <ORG> ral Excise L-4 license with immediate effect because according to him printing and/or lacquering was incidental to the process of manufacturing extruded shapes and sections or extruded tubes and pipes of aluminium.
Fact	The petitioner appealed against the impugned notice to the Assistant Collector of <ORG> Baroda on 15th June, 1971.
Fact	The appeal was heard on 16th June, 1971 (hereinafter referred to as the 'impugned order'), the Assistant Collector dismissed the appeal.
Fact	3.
Fact	It is against the impugned notice and the impugned order that this petition has been filed.
Fact	4.
Fact	Mr. <NAME> who appears for the petitioner has raised before us only one contention.
Fact	Though some other contentions have been raised in the petition by the petitioner they have not been convassed before us.
RatioOfTheDecision	The contention which Mr. <NAME> has raised is this.
RatioOfTheDecision	5.
Fact	Whether printing and lacquering of extruded shapes and sections or tubes and pipes of aluminium is liable to payment of duty under the said Act ?
Argument	6.
Argument	In the impugned notice it has been sta ted that extruded tubes by themselves are not marketable unless they are printed in a distinguishable form.
Argument	It is only printed extruded tubes which are according to the <ORG> authorities known to the market and consumed by the consumers.
Argument	It has also been stated that the printing and lacquering of extruded tubes and pipes is a process which is incidental to the process of manufacture of the extruded tubes of aluminium.
Argument	In the petition it has been denied that the plain unprinted and unlacquered tubes are not marketable.
Fact	At <ORG> to the petition, the petitioner has annexed details showing the sale of plan (unprinted and unlacquered) tubes.
Fact	At <ORG> to the petition there are some more details in that behalf.
Fact	At <ORG> there are still more details showing the sale by the petitioner of plain collapsible tubes of aluminium to different persons.
Fact	That plain collapsible tubes of aluminium are a marketable commodity and that they are purchased by consumers in the market have not been disputed before us.
Fact	We, ther efore, proceed to decide this petition on the footing that both plain collapsible tubes of aluminium as well as printed and lacquered tubes of aluminium are marketable commodities and that the consumers buy them.
Argument	7.
Argument	In order to examine the contention raised by Mr. <NAME> it is necessary to turn to Section 3 of the said Act which is a charging section.
Statute	It provides for levy and collection of duties of excise on all excisable goods other than salt and on salt manufactured in, or imported by land into any part of India at the rates set forth in the First Schedule to the said Act.
Statute	The expression "excisable goods" has been defined by Clause (d) of Section 2 in the following terms :  " 'Excisable goods' means goods specified in the First Schedule as being subject to a duty of excise and includes salt."
RatioOfTheDecision	If a person produces "excisable goods" within the aforesaid meaning of that expression he has got to obtain a licence under Section 6 of the said Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 3 , Sub-section (1) read with the definition of "excisabl e goods" given in Clause (d) of Section 2 , takes us to the First Schedule.
RatioOfTheDecision	Item 27 in the First Schedule specifies aluminium.
RatioOfTheDecision	Sub-item (e) specifies the following commodity :  "Extruded shapes and sections including extruded pipes and tubes."
RatioOfTheDecision	The contention raised on behalf of the respondents is that printing and lacquering of duty paid plain extruded collapsible pipes and tubes of aluminium are covered by sub-item (e) of Item 27.
RatioOfTheDecision	The respondents have placed no reliance upon any other sub-item of Item 27.
RatioOfTheDecision	On the other hand Mr. <NAME> has contended that sub-item (e) of Item 27 has no application to the printing and lacquering of duty paid extruded pipes and tubes of aluminium.
RatioOfTheDecision	8.
RatioOfTheDecision	Let us now see the connotation of sub-item (e) of Item 27.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is .
RatioOfTheDecision	not in dispute before us that the petitioners have been printing and lacquering only plain extruded tubes.
RulingByPresentCourt	We, therefore, examine sub-item (e) of Item 27 in that context.
RulingByPresentCourt	Sub-item (e) of Item 27 in the First Schedule read with Section 3(1) subjects to payment of exci se duty manufacture of extruded tubes of aluminium.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is not in dispute that the petitioner has been purchasing plain extruded tubes of aluminium from the market and has been printing and lacquering them for its customers.
RatioOfTheDecision	The plain extruded tubes which the petitioners have been purchasing are all duty paid.
RatioOfTheDecision	When we read sub-item (e) of Item 27 in the light of Section 3 , Sub-section .
RatioOfTheDecision	(1), there are two facts which clearly emerge thereform.
RatioOfTheDecision	The first fact is that there must be a manufacture of tubes of aluminium and the second is that they must have been manufactured by applying the process of extrusion.
RatioOfTheDecision	Plain extruded tubes which the petitioners have been purchasing from the market were already manufactured by other manufacturers.
RatioOfTheDecision	The process of extrusion was applied to their manufacture by other manufacturers before they were turned out as finished goods and placed in the market for sale.
RatioOfTheDecision	The question which therefore arises is this :  9.
RatioOfTheDecision	By printing and lacquering the plain extruded tubes of aluminium do t he petitioners firstly apply any further process of extrusion to them and do they thereby manufacture tubes ?
RatioOfTheDecision	10.
RatioOfTheDecision	Mr. <NAME> appearing for the respondents has not been able to tell us that the printing and lacquering of plain extruded tubes of aluminium require any application of the process of extrusion.
RatioOfTheDecision	Mr. <NAME> has on the other hand categorically stated to us that mere printing and lacquering of plain extruded tubes do not require application of any process of extrusion.
RatioOfTheDecision	So far as the tubes are concerned extrusion means process of forming the tube from a metal slug or dump; That is how the connotation of 'extrusion' has been explained in <ORG> (revised edition, October, 1966) published by the <ORG>s Institution.
RatioOfTheDecision	If extrusion means the process of forming a tube from a metal slug or dump, it was already applied when the manufacturer of a plain extruded aluminium tube manufactured it.
RatioOfTheDecision	Printing and lacquering do not involve the process of forming a tube from a metal slug or dump which ext rusion means.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is therefore clear that since printing and lacquering of plain extruded tubes do not require application of any further process of extrusion, it cannot be covered by sub-item (e) of Item 27.
RatioOfTheDecision	Secondly by printing and lacquering plain extruded tubes, can the petitioners be said to manufacture aluminium tubes ?
RatioOfTheDecision	This is the second aspect which emerges from the analysis of sub-item (e) of Item 27.
RatioOfTheDecision	"Manufacture" has been defined by Clause (f) of Section 2 in the following terms :  "Manufacture" includes any process incidental or ancillary to the completion of a manufactured product...."  The tubes which the petitioners have been printing and lacquering were already manufactured by other manufacturers.
RatioOfTheDecision	Is the process of printing and lacquering them incidental or ancillary to the completion of manufacture of such a tube ?
RatioOfTheDecision	The definition of 'manufacture' given in Clause (f) clearly suggests that any process before it is regarded an incidental or ancillary to the completion of a manufactured product mu st have some relation to the manufacture of a finished product.
RatioOfTheDecision	However, unessential that process may be if it is incidental or ancillary to the completion of a manufactured product then that process falls within the compass of the expression "manufacture".
RatioOfTheDecision	Printing and lacquering cannot be said to be incidental or ancillary to the completion of manufacture of the product.
RatioOfTheDecision	They have no relation to the completion of manufacture of tubes because unprinted and unlacquered tubes are by themselves a marketable commodity requiring nothing else for completing their manufacture.
RatioOfTheDecision	Printing and lacquering are therefore not even remotely connected with the completion of the manufacture of aluminium tubes.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is a process which is independent of the manufacture of aluminium tubes.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is applied for the purpose of enhancing their salability and not for completing their manufacture.
RatioOfTheDecision	If such a commodity is subjected to central excise duty after it has been once paid on the plain extruded tubes, it will not be a duty on the m anufacture of extruded iluminium tubes but it will be a duty on the sale of that commodity.
RatioOfTheDecision	We are therefore of the opinion that the printing and lacquering of plain extruded aluminium do not fall within the ambit of sub-item (e) of Item 27.
RatioOfTheDecision	This is our view on the plain reading of the said sub-item (e) of Item 27 in the light of the provisions of Sub-section (I) of Section 3 of the said Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	11.
RulingByPresentCourt	A few decisions have been cited before us in this behalf.
RulingByPresentCourt	We now turn to them.
RulingByPresentCourt	<CITATION_941355> , AIR 1968 SC 922 <ORG> was concerned with Item 14H in Schedule I to the said Act which specifies compressed liquefied or solidified gases more particularly described therein.
RatioOfTheDecision	It was a case where in the process .
RatioOfTheDecision	of manufacture of sugar by carbonisation process and in the process of manufacture of soda ash by solvay ammonia soda process, a manufacturer required carbon dioxide for the purpose of producing those two commodities.
RatioOfTheDecision	The manufacturer in that case had therefore set up a lime kiln which also generated  kiln gas.
RatioOfTheDecision	The question which arose was whether kiln gas which the lime kiln produced was subject to excise duty under Item 14H which inter alia particularised carbon dioxide and compressed carbon dioxide.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is in that connection that <ORG> examined the definition of the expression "manufacture" given in Section 2(f) of the said Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	It has laid down that word "manufacture" implies a change but every change in the raw material is not manufacture.
RatioOfTheDecision	There must be such a transformation that a new and different article must emerge having a distinctive name, character, or use.
RatioOfTheDecision	The said Act does not define goods.
RatioOfTheDecision	The <ORG> therefore must be taken to have used every word in its ordinary dictionary meaning.
RatioOfTheDecision	The dictionary meaning is that to become "goods" it must be something which can be ordinary come to the market to be bought and sold and is known to the market.
RatioOfTheDecision	In other words a new substance must be brought into existence from raw material.
RatioOfTheDecision	The test which <ORG> has therefore laid dow n is the test of distinctive name, character or use of the new substance which is brought into existence from materials.
RatioOfTheDecision	12.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the instant case if we apply the aforesaid test we come to the inescapable conclusion that a printed and lacquered extruded aluminium tube does not have in relation to a plain extruded tube any distinctive name, or character use.
RatioOfTheDecision	Both can be used for the same purpose.
RatioOfTheDecision	Both enjoy the same character.
RatioOfTheDecision	Both bear the same name.
RatioOfTheDecision	The printed and lacquered extruded tube cannot be said by any stretch of imagination to be a new substance distinguishable from plain extruded tube.
RatioOfTheDecision	The first test laid down by <ORG> in the aforesaid decision therefore produces an answer in favour of the petitioner.
RatioOfTheDecision	It has been laid down by <ORG> that <ORG> duty is a tax on manufacture and not on sale.
RatioOfTheDecision	Since in our opinion the printed and lacquered extruded tubes do not involve any process incidental or ancillary to the completion of the manufactured product, to uphold the contenti on raised by Mr. <NAME> will be to uphold the imposition of <ORG> duty not on the manufacture of extruded tubes on which duty is already paid once but on the sale of printed and lacquered extruded tubes.
RatioOfTheDecision	We are, therefore, of the opinion that by printing and lacquering plain extruded tubes no different tariff item is brought into existence by the petitioner.
RatioOfTheDecision	The petitioner is, therefore, not liable to take out a licence for printing and lacquering them because it has been for the aforesaid purpose purchasing plain extruded duty-paid tubes from the market.
RatioOfTheDecision	13.
RatioOfTheDecision	The next decision which has been cited before us is in <ORG> v. <NAME>) 1 SSC 7.
RatioOfTheDecision	A similar question in that case arose.
RatioOfTheDecision	While construing the expression 'manufacture'.
RatioOfTheDecision	it has been laid down by <ORG> that 'manufacture' implies a change but that every change is not manufacture though every change is not manufacture though every change in an article is the result of treatment, labour and manipu lation.
RatioOfTheDecision	In order to satisfy the test of 'manufacture' it is necessary that there must be transformation, that is to say, a new and different article must emerge having a distinctive name, character or use.
RatioOfTheDecision	The transformation which is necessary in order to satisfy the test of 'manufacture' must be of corporeal and substantial nature.
RatioOfTheDecision	The test laid down by <ORG> in the case of (supra) has been followed in the subsequent case of (supra).
RatioOfTheDecision	The tests laid down by these two decisions, for the reasons stated above, have not been satisfied in the instant case.
Precedent	14.
Precedent	The third decision is in <CITATION_193029735> ., AIR 1963 SC 791.
Precedent	In that case <ORG> was concerned with the chargeability of excise duty on the manufacture of Vanaspati under Items 12 and 13 of Schedule I to the said Act.
Precedent	It has been laid down in that decision that though manufacturing requires application of some sort of processes every processing cannot be equated to manufacture.
Precedent	Manufacture brings into existence, <ORG>  has observed, a new substance and does not merely produce some change in substance, however minors in consequence it may be.
Precedent	This distinction has been brought out by <ORG> by relying upon the elaborate meaning of the expression "manufacture" given in permanent Edition of Words and Phrases, Volume 26.
Precedent	The passage quoted by <ORG> in that decision is in the following terms : -  " 'Manufacture' implies a change but every change is not manufacture, and yet every change of an article is the result of treatment, labour and manipulation.
Precedent	But something more is necessary and there must be transformation; a new and different article must emerge having distinctive name, character of use."
Precedent	15.
Precedent	The printing and the lacquering of plain extruded tubes no doubt mean some change in the form of that article but they do not bring into existence a new article or substance altogether.
RatioOfTheDecision	The tube remains a tube and whether printed and lacquered or not, it is used for the purpose for which a plain extruded tu be is used.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore printing and lacquering change the form of the extruded tubes but they do not bring into existence a new substance altogether which can be put to a use different from one to which a plain extruded tube is put.
RatioOfTheDecision	It does not enjoy a plain extruded tube enjoys and it does not bear a name distinguishable from that of a plain extruded tube.
RatioOfTheDecision	16.
Precedent	The last decision which has been cited before us is in <CITATION_1605661> known as Ramlal Mansukhrai Rewari, AIR 1971 SC 2333.
Precedent	In that case <ORG> was concerned with Item 26A in the First Schedule to the said Act.
Precedent	It specifies copper and copper alloys in different forms and shapes.
Precedent	At the stage where copper billets were rolled into circles, the excise duty was levied because the manufacture of circles was complete.
Precedent	Therefore, in that case copper circles so manufactured became liable to excise duty at the rate mentioned against Item No.
Precedent	26A (2).
Precedent	It was contended by the respondents to that case that copper circles which were subjected to payme nt of excise duty were uncut circles manufactured by rolling mills after rolling copper billets.
Precedent	Later on these uncut circles were trimmed and were converted .into utensils and sold as such in the market.
Precedent	Now Item 26A(2) as it was in force then mentioned amongst other things the manufacture of circles in any form or size.
Precedent	<ORG>, therefore, held that the manufacture of circles cut or uncut, was liable to payment of excise duty and negatived the argument that uncut circles cannot be held to be circles specified in the said Item.
Precedent	\ While placing that construction upon sub-item (2) of Item 26A, it took into account the expression "in any form or size" which was applicable to the expression "circles".
RatioOfTheDecision	It was further argued that those uncut circles were trimmed and converted into utensils.
RatioOfTheDecision	The argument that only trimmed circles can be treated as circles and as finished product for the purpose of Item 26A was turned down on the strength of the expression "circles in any form or size" used in that Item.
RatioOfTheDecision	I t was held on the construction of the said <ORG> that the <ORG> was aware that billets were converted into circles and that excise duty should be leviable both at the stage of manufacture of billets and at the stage of manufacture of circles.
RatioOfTheDecision	The argument advanced on behalf of the manufacturer was turned down because <ORG> 26A clearly disclosed the intention of the <ORG> to levy central exciseduty at both stages - on billets as well as on circles.
RatioOfTheDecision	This decision cited by Mr. <NAME> latdoes not help him in establishing his contention because we are unable to read in <ORG> 27 any intention on the part of the <ORG> to levy central exciseduty both on the manufacture of plain extruded tubes and on the printing and lacquering of such extruded tubes.
RatioOfTheDecision	Whenever <ORG> wanted to spread a wider sweep it has done so.
RatioOfTheDecision	<ORG> 26A(2) furnishes one such instance by using the expression "in any form or size".
RatioOfTheDecision	<ORG> 27(b) also furnishes an illustration of this type where the <ORG> has used the expression "in  .
RatioOfTheDecision	any form or size" in respect of such manufactures as plates, sheets, circles and strips.
RatioOfTheDecision	Item 29A(3) used the expression "all sorts" in relation to parts of refrigerating and air-conditioning appliances and machinery.
RatioOfTheDecision	In a similar context sub-item (2) of Item 26B uses the expression "in any form or size".
RatioOfTheDecision	So also Item 26AA(ii) uses in a similar context the expression "all sorts".
RatioOfTheDecision	These are some of the illustrations which we have culled out from the first Schedule to the said Act in order to show that wherever the <ORG> wanted to cast a wide net in order to embrace a commodity or an article in all its conceivable forms it has done so.
RatioOfTheDecision	We do not find a similar attempt on the part of the <ORG> in Item 27.
RatioOfTheDecision	We are therefore of the opinion that only those goods which answer the description of extruded tubes of aluminium in the context of the present case are liable to payment of excise duty... We are therefore unable to uphold the argument advanced by Mr. <NAME> that the printed and lacquered extrude d tubes of aluminium are different commodities distinguishable from plain extruded tubes and therefore liable to payment of central excise duty.
RatioOfTheDecision	17.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the light of the view which we have expressed above, we are of the opinion that the petitioner is not liable to take out a license under Section 6 of the said Act for the purpose of carrying on its business activity of printing and lacquering plain extruded tubes of aluminium.
