Argument	Th e case of the appellant is that under the 2nd proviso to Art.
Argument	31-A(1 ), he is entitled to compensation because land under his personal cultivation is an estate, and land within the ceiling limit cannot be acquired without payment of compensation which is less than the market value of his land, notwithstanding any law enabling acquisition of land for the <ORG>.
Argument	The <ORG> contends that no land has been acquired because all lands continue to be recorded in the names of the owners in proportion to the area originally held by them as provided by rule 16(ii) and the lands are to be used for the benefit of the proprietors.
Argument	The appellant contends that this is acquisition all the same.
RatioOfTheDecision	A question thus arises : what is meant by 'acquisition' and 'to acquire' in the second proviso?
RatioOfTheDecision	To determine the correct meaning it is necessary to view Articles 31 and 31-A together.
RatioOfTheDecision	The <ORG> seeks to establish a contrast between acquisition and requisition and contends that "acquisition" means a total deprivation of the property  for all time and "requisition" means either a partial deprivation or deprivation for a time.
RatioOfTheDecision	It submits that by the former there is a change of ownership and by the latter a change in possession or enjoyment without a change in ownership.
RatioOfTheDecision	This contrast the <ORG> seeks to establish from the way in which the words "acquisition" and "requisition" and "acquired" and "requisitioned" are used in juxtaposition in the two Articles.
RatioOfTheDecision	We have, therefore, to examine the scheme of the two Articles to see if they throw any light upon the matter.
RatioOfTheDecision	Before this is done it may be admitted that the distinction between "acquisition" and "requisition" is a real one and legislative practice in the past has clearly provided separately for acquisition and requisition in Acts which were even named acquisition and requisition Acts.
RatioOfTheDecision	The distinction was also made by the Government of India Act, 1935.
RatioOfTheDecision	Legislative practice, however, uniform is not always conclusive.
RatioOfTheDecision	We must discover from the Constitution itself, how the words are to be u nderstood.
RatioOfTheDecision	What then are the indications in the Constitution?
Precedent	The first indication is in the legislative entry No.
Precedent	42 in List 3 (Seventh Schedule) which was added by the Constitution Seventh Amendment.
Precedent	Before the Amendment the entry read :  "42.
Precedent	Principles on which compensation for property acquired or requisitioned for the purposes of the <ORG> or of a <ORG> or for any other public purpose is to be determined, and the form and the manner in which such compensation is to be given."
Precedent	The entry now reads "Acquisition and requisitioning of property".
Precedent	These entries give an indication that the Constitution continues to make a distinction between the two terms.
Precedent	Next Art.
Precedent	31 begins by laying down in clause (1) that no person shall be deprived of his property save by authority of law and goes on to say in cl.
Precedent	(2) that no property shall be compulsorily acquired or requisitioned save for public purpose and save by authority of law which provides for compensation for the property so acquired or requisitioned and either  fixes the amount of compensation or specifies the principles on which and the manner in which, the compensation is to be determined and given.
Precedent	The words of the article refer to acquisition and requisition of property.
RatioOfTheDecision	Clause 2(A), which was added by the Fourth Amendment, uses different phraseology.
RatioOfTheDecision	This clause says that where a law does not provide for the transfer of ownership or right to possession of any property to the <ORG> or to a corporation owned or controlled by the <ORG>, it shall not be deemed to provide for the compulsory acquisition or requisitioning of property, notwithstanding that it deprives any person of his property.
RatioOfTheDecision	This means that property shall not be considered to be compulsorily acquired or requisitioned unless the law provides for the transfer of the ownership or right to possession to the <ORG> or to a corporation owned or controlled by the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	<ORG> is a local authority and by virtue of the definition of "<ORG>" in Art.
RatioOfTheDecision	12 stands included in that term.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, a  law providing for the transfer of ownership or right to possession to <ORG> is for the purposes of Art.
RatioOfTheDecision	31-A(1) and (2 ), a law providing for the compulsory acquisition or requisitioning of the property.
RatioOfTheDecision	The contention of the <ORG> is that in Art.
RatioOfTheDecision	31(2A) we get the :clue to the meanings of the words "acquisition" and "requisition" and that the former indicates the transfer of ownership and the latter the transfer of the right to possession.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is, therefore, submitted that the transfer of the use of the land to <ORG> with the ownership still in the cultivators (as is shown by the entry of their names as owners of shamlat deh) and use of the land for their benefit indicate a requisitioning of the lands, that is to 'Say, a transfer of the right to possession merely and not acquisition, that is to say, transfer of ownership.
RatioOfTheDecision	The conclusion is thus drawn that inasmuch as the second proviso speaks of acquisition and not requisition, it cannot apply to the case of the appellant and persons li ke him who are still regarded as owners of the lands although they may be deprived of the immediate right to possession by the handing ,over of land to the <ORG> for management.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is urged that such persons are not entitled to the advantage of the second proviso since their land is not acquired as contemplated therein.
RatioOfTheDecision	We ,cannot accept this argument.
RatioOfTheDecision	Article 31-A deals with a special subject, namely, the saving of laws providing for acquisition of 'estates'.
RatioOfTheDecision	This article saves any law from an attack under Arts.
RatioOfTheDecision	14, 19 and 31 provided it is for the acquisition by the <ORG> of an estate or of any rights therein or the extinguishment or modification of any such rights.
RatioOfTheDecision	It will be noticed that here the article does not refer to property as such, but speaks of an estate as defined in the Article and also of rights in the estate.
RatioOfTheDecision	Estate is defined to include, among other things, " any land held or let for purposes of agriculture or for purposes ancillary thereto, including waste land, forest land, land for pa sture or sites of buildings and other structures occupied by cultivators of land, agricultural labourers and village artisans".
RatioOfTheDecision	Applying the definition, the lands under cultivation must be regarded as 'estates'.
RatioOfTheDecision	Now the intention underlying Art.
RatioOfTheDecision	31-A is to give protection to <ORG> action against Arts.
RatioOfTheDecision	14, 19 and 31 so long as the acquisition is by the <ORG> of any estate or of any rights therein or the extinguishment or modification of any such rights.
RatioOfTheDecision	To this protection there is only one exception and that is to be found in the second proviso.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is that land under the personal cultivation of any estate holder of any kind which is within the ceiling limit applicable to such person, shall not be acquired, unless at least market value of the land is given as compensation.
RatioOfTheDecision	Such land can be acquired but only on compensation which is not less than the market value.
RatioOfTheDecision	The word "acquisition" used in the proviso must take its colour from the same word used earlier and not from the word as used in the earlier article i n juxtaposition with the 1 6 3 word requisition.
RatioOfTheDecision	The word must denote not only the acquisition of ownership, that is to say, the entire bundle of rights, but also acquisition of some rights particularly an acquisition which leaves the person an owner in name only.
RatioOfTheDecision	Article 31-A , it is submitted by the <ORG>, introduces two further concepts, viz., extinguishment of rights and modification of rights.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the case of extinguishment, if all the rights in the property are extinguished, the result would be nothing else than acquisition.
RatioOfTheDecision	For, no property can remain in suspense without the rights therein being vested in someone or the other.
RatioOfTheDecision	In this case the property goes to the <ORG> which is included in '<ORG>'.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the case of modification of rights all the rights of ownership remain in the owner except that they would be modified by some statutory provisions.
RatioOfTheDecision	In such a case the conception either of acquisition orrequisition may not apply.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the present case bits of properties are being taken from the lands be longing to the appellants and others and are thrown in a common lot.
RatioOfTheDecision	The ownership is supposed to be vested in a proprietary body consisting of several proprietors.
RatioOfTheDecision	The statute provides that though ownership is vested in the said proprietary body the management of the land would be with the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	The only obligation thrown on the <ORG> is that the income arising from such land should be utilized for the common benefit not only of the proprietors but also of non- proprietors in the <ORG> area.
RatioOfTheDecision	The result of these provisions is (1) that a proprietor is undoubtedly deprived of his property howsoever small a proportion it may be thereof; (2) the ownership in such a property is transferred to another body which under the statute is an entity different from the proprietor himself; (3) though the ownership is vested in such a proprietary body all rights withregard to the management and income thereof are vested in a<ORG>; and (4) the benefit of the income from such lands goes not to the proprietor b ut to all the proprietors as well as non-proprietors in the <ORG> area.
RatioOfTheDecision	Although the property is not actually vested either in <ORG> or the <ORG> a device is being made in the Act to escape the concept or ,acquisition' to avoid the payment of compensation required under the second proviso to Art.
RatioOfTheDecision	31A.
RatioOfTheDecision	In substance and in effect this is nothing but a colourable use of the provisions of Art.
RatioOfTheDecision	31-A bymaking out a case of modification of rights when there is in realityan acquisition, mainly for the sake of avoiding compensation.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, when the <ORG> acquires almost the entire bundle of rights, it is acquisition within the second proviso and compensation at market rates must be given.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is not at all difficult to determine this compensation.
RatioOfTheDecision	The total land of the holder must be assessed at market value and the value of the dimunition of the area determined proportionately.
RatioOfTheDecision	The appellant is thus entitled to compensation and he cannot be deprived of land within his ceiling without paymen t of compensation calculated in the manner indicated.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is admitted that his land has been reduced to something less than the ceiling applicable to him.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is contended that what is acquired is a small bit from each landholder and that is not of much significance.
RatioOfTheDecision	We do not know what rule is in contemplation.
RatioOfTheDecision	If it is the de minimis rule, we can only say that it would be a most unsatisfactory mode of avoidance of the constitutional provision.
RatioOfTheDecision	What is a small bit is a very vague and uncertain expression.
RatioOfTheDecision	The safe rule is that the Constitution means what it says, that is, land within the ceiling is not to be touched unless compensation at market rate is given.
RulingByPresentCourt	We would, therefore, reject the plea that we should ignore these small bits of land especially as they will be used for the general good and will confer some benefit also upon those who will lose them.
RulingByPresentCourt	We would accordingly allow the appeal with costs.
