Fact	1.
Fact	This is a Civil Revision Petition brought by <NAME> against an order made by the Chief Judge of <ORG> in the following circumstances.
Fact	On the 17th August 1926, the petitioner was nominated as a candidate for election as a divisional Councillor for the 29th Division of the City.
Fact	It is not in dispute that the nomination paper was regular on the face of it but it was said that the petitioner was disqualified for these reasons.
Fact	For some time past he had been an Honorary Presidency Magistrate of the city.
Fact	On the 11th of August he tendered his resignation having in mind no doubt Section 52 (1) (b) (iv) of the Madras City Municipal Act of 1919 which disqualifies a Presidency Magistrate which has been held to include an Honorary Magistrate for election as a Councillor.
Fact	That resignation reached <ORG> on the 14th of August, and <ORG> passed a G. O. in the following terms:  The Governor-in-Council withdraws the powers of Honorary Preside ncy Magistrate for the City of Madras conferred on the undermentioned gentleman (i.e., the petitioner) who has resigned his appointment.
Fact	2.
RulingByLowerCourt	On the 20th of August an objection was put forward by the respondents alleging that the withdrawal by the candidate from his appointment as Presidency Magistrate only took effect by virtue of <NAME> withdrawing his powers which was subsequent to his nomination and that, therefore, the nomination was bad.
RulingByLowerCourt	The objection was heard by the Commissioner acting under Rule 4 hereinafter to be referred to of the Rules made under the Act and a petition was put in for revision before the Chief Judge of <ORG> under the same rule.
RulingByLowerCourt	Both officers pronounced the nomination to be invalid and struck his name off the list of nominations in accordance with Rule 3.
Argument	Against that decision of the Chief Judge this revision petition is brought.
Argument	3.
Argument	Two main points have been argued before us: the first, which goes to the root of the whole matter, being that this Court has no  jurisdiction to entertain any such petition, on the ground that the Chief Judge of <ORG> is not a <ORG> subject to the machinery of the Code of Civil Procedure but a persona designata whose decison is not only not appealable but cannot be called up in revision.
Argument	It has been held by <ORG> of this <ORG>: <CITATION_809444> A. I. R. 1924 Mad.
Argument	561 that the fact that his decision is not appealable so far from being a ground for holding that revision will not lie points decisively in the opposite direction.
Argument	The appellant's contention as to the character in which the Chief Judge decided this matter depends partly upon a consideration of the relevant sections of the Municipal Act and the rules made thereunder, partly on the effect of certain authorities which were cited to us.
Argument	Section 59 (2) (b) of the Act procides that the Governor-in-Council may make rules which may provide for the adjudication by the <ORG> of Small Causes of disputes...... arising out of elections.
Argument	4.
RatioOfTheDecision	In our opinion "electio ns" within the meaning of that section means completed elections which have resulted in the creation of a councillor, and does not cover disputes arising before such election; but though Rule 4 with which we are primarily concerned purports to be made under Section 59 we are of opinion that it is rendered valid by a generality of the powers conferred on the Governor-in-Council by Section 347 of the Act, and that the rule is not vitiated by its description as having been made under a section which is not applicable.
RatioOfTheDecision	Rule 4 gives power to a registered voter to make an objection to the list of candidates.
Argument	Thereupon the Commissioner is to give his deeisicn on the objection in writing which decision shall be final unless......a petition for revision is put in before the Chief Judge.
Argument	and in such a case it is argued that the Chief Judge acting under Rule 4, is in effect <ORG> functioning through him alone, and that his decision is, therefore, subject to revision.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is said that this position is  confirmed by Order 1 (a), Rule 6, of the rules of the Small Cause <ORG>, 1912.
RatioOfTheDecision	That rule runs as follows:  The Judges may sit apart or together at any time and any one or more of the Judges so sitting apart shall have all the judicial authority which is given to the <ORG> by the principal Act or any other enactment for the time being in force, except in cases in which by such Act or other enactment or any rule or order having the force of law such powers are exercisable only by two or more Judges of the <ORG> or only by the Chief Judge.
Argument	It shall be competent to the Chief Judge to direct that any application, petition, or suit shall be heard by a Bench consisting of two or more Judges of the <ORG>.
Argument	5.
Argument	The argument is that if by any Act or Rule a matter shall be referred to the Chief Judge alone, he is nevertheless acting as the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	We are of opinion that this rule only applies to cases where the reference made by the Act or rule in question is clearly to the <ORG>, though functioning through the Chief Ju dge and that it leaves untouched the real question which we have to decide; namely, whether this power was conferred by Rule 4 made under the Municipal Act upon the Chief Judge as the Court functioning through himself or upon him as persona designata.
RatioOfTheDecision	Looking at the Municipal Act, it is clear that the draftsman of that Act was quite alive to the distinction between <ORG> (vide Section 59) and the Chief Judge of that Court (vide Section 54).
RatioOfTheDecision	It is difficult to resist the inference that when the Chief Judge is referred to in the Act he was meant to Act as a persona designata and not as representing the Court.
Precedent	The Full Bench decision in <CITATION_809444> A. I. R. 1924 Mad.
Precedent	561 was relied upon for the proposition that whenever jurisdiction is vested in the Judge of a Court it must be deemed to be vested in that Judge as representing that Court.
Precedent	In that case a vesting of jurisdiction in a District Judge was held to be equivalent to vesting the jurisdiction in <ORG>, thereby subject ing his decision to the revisional powers of <ORG>.
Precedent	6.
Precedent	It was pointed out in a judgment of <ORG> in <ORG> <ORG> v. M. A. Shakur A. I. R. 1926 Rang.
Precedent	25, reviewing all the authorities with which we respectfully agree, that though that reasoning may apply to a <ORG> which by its constitution has only one Judge it cannot govern a case where jurisdiction is conferred on a single member of a <ORG> consisting of three Judges.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is obvious that words might easily have been used to indicate that while jurisdiction was conferred on the Small Cause <ORG>, that <ORG> should only function through its Chief Judge.
RatioOfTheDecision	We think that that distinction drawn by <ORG> was correctly drawn and that nothing in the decision of <ORG> in <CITATION_809444> A. I. R. 1924 Mad.
RulingByPresentCourt	561 precludes us from holding, as we hold, that under the Municipal Act and the Rules the Chief Judge, for this purpose is persona designate and not merely a selected member of the <ORG>  chosen to represent it.
RulingByPresentCourt	That being so, we have no jurisdiction to entertain this revision petition, and it must be dismissed with costs.
RatioOfTheDecision	7.
RatioOfTheDecision	But we think it is desirable that we should say a few words on the second point which was elaborately argued before us.
RatioOfTheDecision	That point was that the Commissioner and the Chief Judge were confined to questions of form, and that it was entirely outside the ambit of their jurisdiction to enquire into grounds of disqualification which did not appear on the face of the nomination paper and would involve an enquiry into facts.
Precedent	The position of a returning officer in England is analogous generally to that of the Commissioner under the Madras Municipal Act, and it has been decided by a series of decisions in England, of which we need only cite <NAME> v. <ORG> [1888] 13 A. C. 241 and <NAME> v. <NAME> [1904] 1 K. B.
Precedent	74 that the returning officer is only to decide objections arising on the face of the nomination paper itself and not to go into questions relating to the pers onal qualifications of the candidate.
Precedent	Under Section 52 of the Madras Act, among the disqualifications enumerated are that the person concerned is of unsound mind, or is interested in a contract with the <ORG>.
Precedent	If the position be right that such allegations are to be investigated not after election but at the stage of candidature, the Commissioner might be faced with a series of elaborate enquiries relating to persons who might never reach the stage of election or appointment as a councillor, might indeed receive no votes at all.
Precedent	It is said that nevertheless the effect of the Madras Act is to throw the burden of conducting such enquiries upon the Commissioner with regard to every candidate at the stage of nomination.
RatioOfTheDecision	In view of our decision on the main point, it is unnecessary to pronounce upon this question.
RulingByPresentCourt	But we think it desirable to point out the doubts that have been raised as to the effect of the Madras Act and to suggest that it should be amended in a manner that shall make it clear whether it is  intended to invest the Commissioner with the wide powers suggested by the respondents or to confine him to the purely ministerial function with which alone he would he clothed if the principle of the English cases is to be applied.
RulingByPresentCourt	If the latter view is to prevail, there seems to be no good reason for granting an appeal to the Chief Judge or indeed to any other tribunal.
