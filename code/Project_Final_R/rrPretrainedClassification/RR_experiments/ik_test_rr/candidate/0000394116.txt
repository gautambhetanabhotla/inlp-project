Precedent	19.
Precedent	The provisions of Section 96 of the Motor Vehicles Act, 1939, are more or less based upon the corresponding provisions of the Road Traffic Act, 1934, in England which sought to protect the interests of third parties.
Precedent	In fact, we find that the provisions of the said Act sought to supplement the  provisions of the Road Traffic Act of 1930.
Precedent	We also find that the provisions of the above said two Acts have more or less been adopted not only in India in the Motor Vehicles Act , 1939, but also in Sri Lanka and also in other Commonwealth countries with minor local changes.
Precedent	It would be convenient to refer to the history of these provisions briefly.
Precedent	20.
Precedent	In the 1920s, motor cars on the roads were rapidly becoming a menace to life in England.
RatioOfTheDecision	The persons injured had no recourse against anyone except the driver or his principal (the owner of the vehicle).
RatioOfTheDecision	Sometimes, the owner was insured in full or in part against third party risks.
RatioOfTheDecision	Sometimes, he was not insured at all.
RatioOfTheDecision	In any case, the injured person had no right whatsoever to call upon the insurance company or on the moneys which were payable under the policies.
RatioOfTheDecision	The injured person's only recourse was against the negligent driver and he was often worth nothing or did not have enough money to pay the damages.
RatioOfTheDecision	In order to mitigate these evils, the British Parli <ORG> in 1930 passed two statutes within a few days of one another.
RatioOfTheDecision	The principal one was the Road Traffic Act, 1930.
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 35(1) of that Act corresponds to Section 94(1) of the Motor Vehicles Act, 1939, while Section 36 thereof corresponds to our Section 95 and Section 38 corresponds to Section 96(3) of our Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	Under the Road Traffic Act, 1930, it was compulsory for motorists to insure against third parties in respect of certain classes of liability.
RatioOfTheDecision	They were not bound to insure in respect of their employees or in respect of passengers in private cars.
RatioOfTheDecision	But, they were bound to insure for other classes of insurance.
RatioOfTheDecision	The other Act was the Third Party (Rights Against Insurers) Act, 1930.
RatioOfTheDecision	That applied to cases where the driver or the owner of the car became insolvent--even bankrupt or the company went into liquidation.
RatioOfTheDecision	The Act enables the insured person to go directly against the insurance company ; but only for the sum covered by the policy.
RatioOfTheDecision	The insurance company was not bound to pay any more than what the p olicy provided as stated in Section 1(4) of that Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	The result, therefore, was that under these two Acts, the insurance company could not be made liable beyond their contractual obligations.
RatioOfTheDecision	The insurance company could still say that they were bound under their own contract.
RatioOfTheDecision	So much so, the protection became illusory.
Precedent	In Zurich General Accident and Liability Insurance Co. Ltd. v. <ORG>72 Lloyd's Rep 167 ;  2 KB 53, Lord Justice <NAME> pointed out that a position had arisen which to a great extent nullified the protection which compulsory insurance was intended to afford.
