RatioOfTheDecision	(vi) The department's theory that Section 43A occupies the entire field cannot be accepted unless it is shown either that the sectio n confers for the first time a benefit which would not be otherwise available or that the section was intended not to confer a benefit but to curtail the existing benefits and allowances under the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	Neither of these premises is correct The first is incorrect because general principles of accountancy permit the incremental liability to be added to, or deducted from, the actual cost; the only controversy can be in relation to the year of adjustment of such increase or decrease, where it occurs in a later year.
RatioOfTheDecision	The second premise is incorrect as it runs counter to the tenor of the "notes on clauses" and the circular.
RatioOfTheDecision	(vii) If the department's contention were the correct one, the non obstante clause would have been inserted in Sub-section (2) and not in Sub-section (1): and Sub-section (2) would have read: "such increase or decrease in liability as is referred to in Sub-section (1) shall not be taken into account" and not "the provisions of Sub-section (1) will not be taken into account" as at present  17.
RatioOfTheDecision	Pl ausible and ingenious as this argument sounds, we are of opinion that it cannot be accepted.
RatioOfTheDecision	It puts too strained and artificial a construction on the clear language of Section 43-A .
RatioOfTheDecision	This section was specially introduced to provide for the treatment of the situation created by the devaluation of the rupee.
RatioOfTheDecision	It specifically enacts that the amount of increase or decrease in the liability due to exchange rate fluctuation should be adjusted against the actual cost or the capital expenditure or the cost of acquisition referred to in the five provisions of the statute mentioned earlier.
RatioOfTheDecision	Where the terms of Sub-section (1) are fulfilled in any case, it is mandatory to take the actual cost, capital expenditure or cost of acquisition at the higher or lower figure for the purposes of the provisions mentioned irrespective of whatever might have been the position independent of the section.
RatioOfTheDecision	The non obstante clause with which the section begins, indeed, makes it clear that, if the position had been different otherwise, it  cannot prevail after the introduction of this section.
RatioOfTheDecision	Equally, even if the position would have been the same otherwise, that would be no justification to ignore or disregard the enacted provision on the ground that a specific statutory provision was not at all necessary.
RatioOfTheDecision	Once the provision is there and its terms apply, it should be applied; it is idle to speculate on what the position would have been otherwise.
RatioOfTheDecision	18.
RatioOfTheDecision	The facts of the case undoubtedly fall within the terms of Section 43-A(1) .
RatioOfTheDecision	The assessee has acquired, for the purposes of its business, a capital asset from a country outside India by making payment in foreign currency.
RatioOfTheDecision	For this purpose it has borrowed monies in foreign currency from outside agencies and the liability in respect of such assets is outstanding.
RatioOfTheDecision	At a point of time after the acquisition of the asset, this liability has increased on account of the devaluation of our currency.
RatioOfTheDecision	These conditions being satisfied, the language of the sub-section is attracted.

RatioOfTheDecision	We find no merit in the contention of <NAME> that Sub-section (1) will come into operation only in respect of a year subsequent to the year in which the asset is acquired.

RatioOfTheDecision	There is noth ing in the present language of the sub-section which makes it inapplicable to a case where the change in the magnitude of the liability consequent on a change in the rate of exchange occurs during the very previous year in which the asset has been acquired.
RatioOfTheDecision	20.
RatioOfTheDecision	We also find it difficult to find substance in the second argument of Sri <NAME> that Sub-section (1) was inserted only to define the year in which the increase or decrease in liability has to be adjusted.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is no doubt true that, but for the new section, various kinds of arguments could have been raised regarding the year in which such liability should be adjusted.
RatioOfTheDecision	But, we think, arguments could also have been raised as to whether the actual cost calls for any adjustment at all in such a situation.
RatioOfTheDecision	It could have been contended that the actual cost can only be the original purchase price in the year of acquisition of the asset and that, even if there is any subsequent increase in the liability, it cannot be added to the actual cost at any stage and th at, for the purposes of all the statutory allowances, the amount of actual cost once determined would be final and conclusive.
RatioOfTheDecision	Also Section 43-A provides for a case in which, as in the present case, the assessee has completely paid for the plant or machinery in foreign currency prior to the date of devaluation but the variation of exchange rate affects the liability of the assessee (as expressed in Indian currency) for repayment of the whole or part of the monies borrowed by him from any person directly or indirectly in any foreign currency specifically for the purposes of acquiring the asset.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is a moot question as to whether in such a case, on general principles, the actual cost of the assessee's plant or machinery will be the revised liability or the original liability.
RatioOfTheDecision	This is also a situation which is specifically provided for in the section.
RatioOfTheDecision	It may not, therefore, be correct to base arguments on an assumption that the figure or actual cost has necessarily to be modified for purposes of development reb ate or depreciation or other allowances and that the only controversy that can arise will be as to the year in which such adjustment has to be made.
RatioOfTheDecision	In our opinion, we need not discuss or express any concluded opinion on either of these issues.
RatioOfTheDecision	As we said earlier, there is no need to speculate on all the problems that might have arisen if Section 43-A had not been there because the statute had resolved these problems.
Precedent	It lays down, firstly, that the increase or decrease in liability should be taken into account to modify the figure of actual cost and secondly that such adjustment should be made in the year in which the increase or decrease in liability arises on account of the fluctuation in the rate of exchange.

RatioOfTheDecision	The anxiety of the Government, which has been given effect to by the legislature, was only that, in the absence of a proper statutory provision, the assessee should not suffer by even normal allowances like depreciation and amortisation being denied to him for one reason or another.

RatioOfTheDecision	There is no basis for an assumption that increased development rebate was also intended to be provided and that an interpretation which would result in its non-availability would be unfair as it would result in the curtailment of an intended benefit.

RatioOfTheDecision	Counsel for the assessee points out that the departmental cou <ORG> has not been able to invite our attention to any specific reason spelt out anywhere as to why the legislature should deny development rebate on the basis of the revised cost though the other allowances and expenditure determined with reference to actual cost, capital expenditure or cost of acquisition will be calculated on the modified figure atleast subsequent to the change in exchange rate.

RatioOfTheDecision	There is no rationale for making any such distinction.
RatioOfTheDecision	Such discrimination would indeed be more meaningless than an interpretation which, at least consistently, denies development rebate in all cases of increase  in liability due to fluctuation in exchange rates.
RatioOfTheDecision	It cannot, therefore, be said that the interpretation contended for by the assessee is more reasonable or logical than the interpretation which follows from the clear language of the section.
RatioOfTheDecision	24.
RatioOfTheDecision	Nor is there any in-appropriateness of statutory language as urged.
RulingByPresentCourt	As we have discussed above, the provisions of Sub-section (1) apply to the present case and the increased liability should be taken as 'actual cost' within the meaning of Section 43A(1) .
RulingByPresentCourt	All allowances including development rebate or depreciation allowance or the other types of deductions referred to in the sub-section will therefore have to be based on such adjusted actual cost.

RatioOfTheDecision	Read thus, there is no d ifficulty in the application of the language of the section to the present case.
RatioOfTheDecision	There is no inappropriateness of language either in Sub-section (1) or in Sub-section (2).
RatioOfTheDecision	The language used is quite appropriate and meets the situation fully.
RatioOfTheDecision	25.
RatioOfTheDecision	For the reasons discussed above, we are of the opinion that the language of the provision is perfectly clear.
RatioOfTheDecision	It cannot be interpreted in a restrictive manner as contended for by the learned Counsel for the assessee.
RatioOfTheDecision	In our opinion, it is a clear requirement of the statute that, for purposes of development rebate, any increase or decrease in the actual cost consequent on fluctuations in exchange rate should not be taken into account.
RatioOfTheDecision	It may be that the legislature intended to give a different treatment to development rebate from depreciation and other allowances because the allowance of development rebate can result in an assessee claiming allowances exceeding the original cost.
RatioOfTheDecision	It may be that the legislature thought that, though development rebate was intended to pr omote development of industries, this could not be allowed at the cost of the foreign exchange resources of the country which are also depleted when there is an increase in liability due to devaluation of the currency.
