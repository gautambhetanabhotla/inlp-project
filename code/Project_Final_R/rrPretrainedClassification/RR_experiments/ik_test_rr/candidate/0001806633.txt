Argument	The Contentions It is contended on behalf of the <ORG> that they are secured creditors and as such entitled to exercise their rights under the mortgage as also the st atutory rights conferred on them by Section 29 of SFC Act without interference of courts.
Argument	Hence, it is urged that the <ORG> can sell the mortgaged and charged properties without reference to any court, much less <ORG>.
Precedent	Reliance is placed on the judgment of this <ORG> in <CITATION_1126538> ., AIR (1955) SC 604.
RatioOfTheDecision	That was a case arising under Section 232 of the Companies Act, 1913.
RatioOfTheDecision	This <ORG> was required to consider the meaning of the provision "any sale held without leave of the <ORG> of any of the properties" used in Section 232 (1) of the Companies Act , 1913 which rendered such sales void.
RatioOfTheDecision	It was held that these words refer only to sales held through the intervention of the <ORG> and not to sales 'effected by the secured creditor outside the winding up without intervention of the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	This <ORG> pointed out that the law in England, and under the provisions of the Companies Act in India, was the same, namely, that the secured creditor had the right of realizing his security by standing outside t he winding up, in which case he was not required to seek intervention of the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	The decision in <NAME> (supra) held the field for considerable period, both under the Companies Act , 1913 and the Companies Act , 1956.
RatioOfTheDecision	However, by amending Act 35 of 1985, amendments were carried out in section 529 and a new Section 529A was enacted.
RatioOfTheDecision	These developments in our view, brought about a qualitative change in the legal situation.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is important to notice that M.K.. <NAME> (supra) was decided under the Companies Act , 1913 which did not have any provision corresponding to the proviso to Section 529 or Section 529A of the Companies Act 1956.
RatioOfTheDecision	Obviously therefore, <NAME> could not have considered the impact of these amendments on the provisions of section 232 of the Companies Act, 1913 (corresponding to Section 537 of the Companies Act.
Argument	1956).
Argument	<ORG> of the Bombay High <ORG> has considered in detail the change in the legal situation brought about by these new legal provision in <CITATION_1 732313> , <ORG> (1993) Bombay 392.
Argument	As a result of the proviso added in Section 529 , the security of every secured creditor is deemed to be subject to a 'pari passu' charge in favour of the workmen to the extent of the workmen's dues (called 'workmen's portion, as defined in sub-section (3)(c) therein.
Argument	It is further provided that, where the secured creditor, instead of relinquishing its mortgage and proving his debt, opts to stand outside the winding up proceedings and realise his security, the Official.
Argument	<ORG> shall be entitled to represent the workman and enforce such charge and that any amount realised by enforcement of such charge shall be applied ratably by the Official <ORG> for the discharge of workmen's dues.
Argument	It is true that even the amended proviso does not give the <ORG> an independent right of enforcing the charge by selling the security against which such charge is created.
Argument	Nonetheless, it creates a 'pari passu' charge in favour of the workmen to the extent of their dues and makes the  Liquidator the representative of the workmen to enforce such a charge.
Argument	By reason of Clause  (c) of the newly added proviso , so much of the debt due to the secured creditor opting to realise security as could not be realize because of the specially created rights in favour of the workmen or the amount of the workmen's portion in the security, whichever is less , shall rank 'pari passu' with the workmen's dues under Section 529A .
Argument	Section 529A provides for overriding preferential payments of workmen's dues and unrealised portion of the secured creditors dues, as provided in clause (c) of the proviso to Section 529 .
Argument	Counsel for the <ORG> contended that there is a conflict between the provisions of Section 529 read with Section 529A of the Companies Act on the one hand and Section 29 of the SFC Act on the other.
Argument	It is urged that the provisions of the Companies Act being general law must yield to the provisions of the SFC Act which is special law.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the first place, we see no such conflict between the provisio ns of the Companies Act as amended in 1985 and the provisions of the SFC Act , 1951.
RatioOfTheDecision	In our view, the provisions of the SFC Act were merely intended to give an expeditious remedy to the <ORG> without having to go through the procedure of enforcing the mortgage under the Transfer of Properties Act, 1882.
RatioOfTheDecision	In fact, even under Section 69 of the Transfer of Property Act, under certain circumstances a mortgagee has the power to sell the mortgaged property in default of payment of mortgaged money without intervention of the court.
RatioOfTheDecision	Under the general law, the <ORG> would have to file a suit for realising their security unless they qualified under section 69 of the Transfer of Property Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	This meant considerable delay and holding up of the public monies due to the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	In public interest, therefore, special provisions were made by Section 29 , 30 , 31 and 32 enabling the <ORG> to take possession of the mortgaged assets and sell them without having to move a court of law.
RatioOfTheDecision	The provisions of Section 29 to 32 and the right s flowing thereon are exercisable under ordinary circumstances.
RatioOfTheDecision	However, when the debtor is a Company in winding up, the rights of the SFCs are affected by the provisions of the Companies Act , 1956.
RatioOfTheDecision	Looked at from this point of view, therefore, there is no conflict between the provisions of the SFC Act and the Companies Act .
RatioOfTheDecision	Assuming that there is conflict, then the judgment of this Court in <CITATION_106471> ,  7 SCC 291 clearly holds that the amendments made in Section 529 and 529A would override and control the rights under Section 29 of the SFC Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	Though the Companies Act may be general law, the provisions introduced therein in 1985, were intended to confer special rights on the workers and pro tanto must be treated as special law made by <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	Since the amendments to the Companies Act were made by a later Act of 1985, they would override the provisions of section 29 of SFC Act, 1951.
RatioOfTheDecision	We are unable to accept the contention that the view taken in <ORG> (supra) needs re consideration.
RatioOfTheDecision	Far from it, we are in agreement with the view expressed therein.
RatioOfTheDecision	The decision of <ORG> in <ORG> case (supra) gives weighty reasons as to why when the company is under winding up the SFC to which the assets of the company are charged cannot proceed to realise the security without intervention of <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	We have already noticed that as a result of the amendment to Section 529 a pari passu charge to the extent of the workmen's portion is created on the security of every secured creditor when he opts to realize a security by standing outside winding up.
RatioOfTheDecision	'Pari Passu' means "with equal steps, equally, without preference" (Jowitt's Dictionary, Vol.
RatioOfTheDecision	II, 1959 Edition 1294).
RatioOfTheDecision	<ORG>, 6th Edition, 115 defines it as 'By an equal progress... used especially of creditors who, in marshalling assets, are entitled to receive out of the same fund without any precedence over each other."
RatioOfTheDecision	It is also defined as "With equal steps, that is  to say, proceeding side by side at the same place" (<ORG>, Volume III, 1964 Edition, page 1217) The rights of the pari passu charge holders would run equally, temporally and potently, with the rights of the secured creditors.
RatioOfTheDecision	The Official Liquidator, as the representative of the workmen, to enforce such pari passu charge would have the right of representing the workmen equally with the rights of the secured creditors.
RatioOfTheDecision	Charge is defined under Section 100 of the Transfer of Properties Act thus:  " Section 100-Where immoveable property of one person is by act of parties or operation of law made security for the payment of money to another, and the transaction does not amount to a mortgage, the latter person is said to have a charge on the property; and all the provisions hereinbefore contained [which apply to a simple mortgage shall, so far as may be, apply to such charge.]
RatioOfTheDecision	Though the charge by itself may not amount to mortgage, all the provisions which apply to a simple mortgage, so far as  may be, apply to a charge.
RatioOfTheDecision	Thus, <ORG>, as the representative of the workmen's pari passu charge, would be in the position of a co-mortgagee.
RatioOfTheDecision	Though Section 29 hitherto enabled the <ORG> as a mortgagee to exercise its right thereunder by taking possession of the property and selling it in satisfaction of its debt, the situation has now changed.
RatioOfTheDecision	Because of the aforesaid statutory intervention, the <ORG> must necessarily contend with a pari passu charge holder who has equal rights.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is well established law that where there are co-mortgagees, one co-mortgagee cannot sell without consent of the comortgagee or institute any proceedings for sale of mortgaged property without joining the other co-mortgagees either as plaintiffs or as defendants.
RatioOfTheDecision	The <ORG>'s right under Section 29 of freely realising its security gets trammeled if it has to take on a pari passu charge holder.
RatioOfTheDecision	The realisation of the security can thereafter only be done either by satisfaction of the pari passu charge or by a suit in which  the pari passu charge holder would be a party defendant.
RatioOfTheDecision	Though when the <ORG> Act was enacted in 1951 it was intended that <ORG> could act unilaterally, the amendments made to the Companies Act in 1985 have introduced a pari passu charge holder as a co-helmsman of the ship of the <ORG>, who can neither be ignored nor overridden.
RatioOfTheDecision	In other words, the existence of the pari passu charge holder being represented by <ORG> would necessarily bring in supervision of <ORG> as <ORG> cannot act without directions from and supervision of <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	This is precisely the reason why the judgment of this Court in <ORG> (supra) holds that the statutory right of the <ORG>s to sell the property under Section 29 of the <ORG> Act is now subject to the provisions of Section 529 and Section 529 A of the Companies Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	The statutory right to sell the property under Section 29 of the Act has now to be exercised in tandem with the rights of pari passu charge in  favour of the workmen created by the proviso to Section 529 of the Companies Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	This Court observed in <ORG> (Supra):  " The Act of 1951 is a special Act for grant of financial assistance to industrial concerns with a view to boost up industrialization and also recovery of such financial assistance if it becomes bad and similarly the Companies Act deals with companies including winding up of such companies.
RatioOfTheDecision	The proviso to sub-section (1) of Section 529 and Section 529-A being a subsequent enactment, the non obstante clause in Section 529-A prevails over Section 29 of the Act of 1951 in view of the settled position of law.
RatioOfTheDecision	We are, therefore, of the opinion that the above proviso to sub-section (1) of Section 529 and Section 529-A will control Section 29 of the Act of 1951.
RatioOfTheDecision	In other words the statutory right to sell the property under Section 29 of the Act of 1951 has to be exercised with the rights of pari passu charge to the workmen created by the proviso to Section 529 of the Co mpanies Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	Under the proviso to sub-section (1) of Section 529 , the liquidator shall be entitled to represent the workmen and force (sic enforce) the above pari passu charge.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, <ORG> was fully justified in imposing the above conditions to enable the Official Liquidator to discharge his function properly under the supervision of <ORG> as the new Section 529-A of the Companies Act confers upon <ORG> the duty to ensure that the workmen's dues are paid in priority to all other debts in accordance with the provisions of the above section.
RatioOfTheDecision	The legislature has amended the Companies Act in 1985 with a social purpose viz.
RatioOfTheDecision	to protect dues of the workmen.
RatioOfTheDecision	If conditions are not imposed to protect the right of the workmen there is every possibility that the secured creditor may frustrate the above pari passu right of the workmen."
RatioOfTheDecision	Since the Official Liquidator is in the position of a comortgagee, the SFCs cannot act independently or by ignoring him for enforcing their securit y.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is established law that, in case of co-mortgagees, all of them should join in the suit for enforcing the security, but if some of them refuse to join, they have to be included as defendants, not merely as performa parties, but as necessary parties inasmuch as the mortgage right vests in them along with the plaintiffs mortgages.
RatioOfTheDecision	(See in this connection the judgment of <ORG> in <CITATION_1069183> , AIR (1919) PC 24).
RatioOfTheDecision	The same principle would be substantially true and applicable in the case of a mortgagee and a pari passu charge-holder over the same security for realising the security.
RatioOfTheDecision	The realization of the security can only be done by both the charge-holders joining and realising the security simultaneously.
RatioOfTheDecision	If a sale takes place, it can only be simultaneously for recovery of the claim of all pari passu charge holders and sale proceeds are required to be divided proportionately in the same proportion as their dues.
RatioOfTheDecision	In support of their respective contentions, parties have referred to and reli ed upon judgments of different <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	The view taken by <ORG> commands itself to us.
RatioOfTheDecision	<ORG> of the said <ORG> pointed out that, like a secured creditor, the official liquidator as a pari passu charge holder cannot independently bring the security to sale ignoring the secured creditor.
RatioOfTheDecision	He must, therefore, either obtain concurrence of the secured creditor for sale and take the <ORG>'s sanction, or he can apply for sanction of the <ORG> after notice to the secured creditor.
RatioOfTheDecision	In either event, the <ORG> while granting sanction may impose appropriate conditions and give directions regarding the conduct of the sale, the fixing of the reserve bid, acceptance of the bid, confirmation of sale and distribution of sale proceeds.
RatioOfTheDecision	We cannot be unmindful of the fact that every creditor is interested in realizing the security only for his benefit and to the extent necessary for recovery of his outstandings.
RatioOfTheDecision	Prior to 1985 it might have been possible for a secured creditor under section 5 29 of the Companies Act, 1956, or its predecessor, section 232 of the Companies Act, 1913 as interpreted by this <ORG> in <NAME> case (supra), to opt to stand outside the winding up and realise the security by bringing it to sale.
RatioOfTheDecision	This was possible because the secured creditor had unrestricted right of standing outside the winding up and proceeding against the property mortgaged to him.
Precedent	Or, to put it in the words Lord <NAME> in <ORG>, (1923) AC 647 A, [as quoted in para 15 of <NAME> case (supra)]:  "The phrase 'outside the winding up' is an intelligible phrase if used, as it often is, with reference to a secured creditor, say a mortgagee.
Precedent	The mortgagee of a company in liquidation is in a position to say "the mortgaged property is to the extent of the mortgage my property.
Precedent	It is immaterial to me whether my mortgage is in winding up or not.
RatioOfTheDecision	I remain outside the 'winding up' and shall enforce my rights as mortgagee".
RatioOfTheDecision	This is to be contrasted with the case in which such a c reditor prefers to assert his right, not as a mortgage, but as a creditor.
RatioOfTheDecision	He may say 'I will prove in respect of my debt'.
RatioOfTheDecision	If so, he comes into the winding up."
RatioOfTheDecision	Of course, even in such a situation, if the same property was mortgaged to more than one secured creditor, they had to either come to an agreement, or in the event of disagreement, there had to be a suit in which dissenting mortgagee had to be sued as a necessary party defendant.
RatioOfTheDecision	No doubt section 29 of the SFC Act was intended to place the <ORG> on a better footing .
RatioOfTheDecision	But, in our view, this better footing is available only so long as the debtor is not a company or is a going company.
RatioOfTheDecision	The moment a winding up order is made in respect of a debtor company, the provisions of section 529 and 529A come into play and whatever superior rights had been ensured to <ORG> under the provisions of the SFC Act are now subjected to and operate only in conjunction with the special rights given to the workmen, who as pari passu charge-holders are represented by <ORG> ial liquidator.
