Statute	(1) The levy and collection of excise duty on goods listed in the First Schedule to the Central Excise and Salt Act , 1944 (hereinafter) referred to as the Act) can be done only after the relevant requirements of the Act and the Rules are satisfied.
Statute	In the present case, the following requirements of section 3 and 4 of the Act and Rules 9 and 49 of the Rules framed there under are relevant:-  (1)The duty can be imposed only on "goods", i.e.
Statute	things which have an economic significance, that is value, utility, exchangeability, etc.
Statute	(2)Since the Calcium Carbide listed under entry 14AA of the Schedule is assessable to duty ad valorem, its value would be deemed to be the cash price for which an article of the like kind and quality is sold or capable of being sold.
Statute	(3)The value has to be determined at the time of the removal of the article chargeable to duty from the factory or any other premises of manufacture or production for delivery at the place of manufacture or production.
Statute	(4)"Factory"  is defined by section 2(e) to mean any premises, including the precincts thereof, wherein or in any part of which excisable goods are manufactured subject to any manufacturing process connected with their production.
RulingByLowerCourt	(2) We are concerned with the Act and the Rules as they existed from 1-3-1970 when the goods produced by the petitioner became liable to duty on the insertion of entry 14AA in the First Schedule to the Act to 30-9-1975 when the Act was amended.

RulingByLowerCourt	Similarly, the residuary entry 68 making assessable to duty all other goods not elsewhere specified, manufactured in a factory but not excluded by the said item is also not applicable.
RulingByLowerCourt	(3) The undisputed facts are that the petitioner used to manufacture Calcium Carbide and sell the same till 1967.

Fact	The Calcium Carbide produced in the petitioner's factory is tapped from the furnace in liquid form and is placed in small trolly trays, where it is allowed to cool and solidify.

Argument	(4) The petitioner contends that the calcium carbide manufactured by it is only of intermediary product, which is used for the generation of acctylene gas in the same factory.
RulingByLowerCourt	This intermediary product is not known to the market as calcium carbide for the following reasons:  1.It is not "goods" as known to the market.
RulingByLowerCourt	By its very properties, calcium carbide in its naked form, turns into dust if it comes into contact with air in the atmosphere due  to the reaction of the moisture in the air on it.
RulingByLowerCourt	It can become ''goods" only when it attains commercial purity and is put into containers as prescribed by the provisions of Chapter Iii of the Carbide of calcium Rules, 1937.
RulingByLowerCourt	The calcium carbide used by the petitioner for the generation of acetylene gas does not comply with these statutory provisions and is not, therefore, "goods".
RulingByLowerCourt	2.Since It is not marketable, its assessable value cannot be found under section 4 , because no such article of the like kind and quality is sold or is capable of being sold.
RulingByLowerCourt	3.It is not removed by the petitioner from its factory and hence the question of levy and collection of duty on it at the time of its removal does not arise.
RulingByLowerCourt	4.The calcium carbide plant and the plant for the production of acetylene gas are both situated in one factory of the petitioner.
RulingByLowerCourt	The passage of calcium carbide from the former plant to the latter plant is not, therefore, its removal from the factory to any other place.

Fact	(7) Let us consider the contentions of the petitioner vis-a-vis the legal requirements for the levy and collection of such duty set out above seriatim.
Fact	(1)Is the calcium carbide produced by the petitioner "goods"?
RatioOfTheDecision	The question has three aspects, material, economic and legal.
RatioOfTheDecision	Goods are matter.
RatioOfTheDecision	The material composition of goods shows whether the goods is in a form in which it has utility, value or exchangeability.
RatioOfTheDecision	Things which are in a raw or unfinished or an intermediate stage of formation will have to be examined with a view to see if they have reached a form of the goods as contemplated in the list of goods given in the First Schedule to the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	What is raw.
RatioOfTheDecision	unfinished or intermediate as distinguished from a finished product may often be a question of degree, but, as Justice <NAME> has said, many distinctions in law are often distinctions of degree.
RatioOfTheDecision	(8) Considerations of the material aspect of goods will be incomplete unless its economic aspect is examined.
Argument	It is only  if the term mentioned in the First Schedule is in a usable form that it can have economic significance.
Argument	It will then have utility and exchangeability.
Argument	In other words, it will have a meaning for the market.
RatioOfTheDecision	In economics market is no a place, but is the quality of goods which make them useful and exchangeable or saleable.
RatioOfTheDecision	(9) Lastly, the use and exchange of goods is often regulated by law.
RatioOfTheDecision	Unless the relevant rules are complied with, an article may not be useful or exchangeable and that may be an additional reason why it may not be "goods".
RatioOfTheDecision	(10) Applying these tests to the substance manufactured by the petitioner, we have to see if it is carbide of calcium within the meaning of entry 14AA of the Schedule.
Fact	As for the material composition of the substance manufactured by the petitioner, the Standard specification of calcium carbide are given in the Indian Standard specification for Calcium Carbide Technical issued by <ORG>.
Fact	The gas yield of Calcium Carbide is given at page 5 of the bo oklet issued by <ORG>.
Fact	The respondents point out that the Calcium Carbide produced in the molten form is solidified and broken into cakes for use in the acetylene gas plant.
Fact	The petitioner has four generators for acetylene gas, two of which are small and the other two are very big.
Argument	The small generators are fed with Calcium Carbide of 1-80 mm size, while the bigger ones are fed with Calcium Carbide of 80-250 mm size.
Argument	The petitioner points out in the rejoinder that on the pleadings of the respondents themselves 74 per cent of the production of the so-called Calcium Carbide of the petitioner is not commercially pure as per <ORG> specifications.
Argument	(11) This would mean that the substance produced by the petitioner is sub-standard judged from <ORG> specifications.
Argument	This would, however, not necessarily make it unmarketable.
Argument	The marketability would itself depend on the degree by which the product falls below the standard.
Argument	It is not possibl e to lay down a hard and fast line as to when a sub-standard product becomes marketable and when it falls below the line of marketability.
Argument	For instance, a car may be marketable if it or it may not have a roof, but it may not be marketable if it has no engine or no brakes.
Argument	Since the substance in the form of cakes cut to required sizes is usable by the petitioner in its acetylene gas plant, it would also be usable by someone else for the generation of acetylene gas.
Argument	It would then be saleable as being useful for that purpose.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the absence of actual evidence, it cannot, however, be said if the substance manufactured by the petitioner can be called in its material and economic senses Carbide of Calcium and whether it can be saleable as such.
RatioOfTheDecision	(12) What is decisive in the present case, however, is the law relating to the marketability of calcium carbide.
RatioOfTheDecision	Chapter Iii of the Carbide of Calcium Rules, 1937 made under the Petroleum Act , 1934, as applied to carbide of calcium prohibits the transport of Calcium Carbid e its storage, its delivery or dispatch and its sale, except after complying with the safety precautions prescribed by the rules and particularly by the provisions of Chapter Iii thereof.
RatioOfTheDecision	Most of these safety requirements do not have to be complied with by the petitioner because the petitioner is not storing, transporting, dispatching, deli- vering or selling Calcium Carbide.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is particularly notable that the petitioner does not have to ensure that the calcium carbide produced by it is "commercially pure"- The significance of these statutory rules is two-fold.
RatioOfTheDecision	Firstly, they create a legal bar to the marketability of Calcium Carbide except in accordance with the rules.
RatioOfTheDecision	Secondly, the use of the expression "commercially pure" in Rule 20 in inverted commas shows that even statutory rules recognise that the calcium carbide must attain the requisite degree of purity before it can be rendered commercial or marketable.
RatioOfTheDecision	This lends substance to the view that Calcium Carbide is not "goods" unless it is marketable.
RatioOfTheDecision	Sin ce the requirements of the <ORG> are not satisfied by the product of the petitioner because it has not attained the material form and purity required by the <ORG>, the product does not seem to be economical "goods" within the meaning of section 3 of the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	(13) It is significant that Chapter V of the Carbide of Calcium <ORG> separately deals with use of Calcium Carbide for the generation of acetylene gas.
RatioOfTheDecision	This shows that it was within the contemplation of the framers of these <ORG> that the Calcium Carbide which does not comply with the requirements of Chapter Iii of the <ORG> as to its marketability can nevertheless be used for the generation of acetylene gas.
RatioOfTheDecision	Calcium carbide is used as an intermediate product or material which may not be otherwise marketable for the generation of the acetylene gas.
RatioOfTheDecision	(2)The second question is whether an article of the like kind and quality, as the product of the petitioner, is sold or is capable of being sold within the meaning of section 4 .
RatioOfTheDecision	Before section 4 of the Act c an be applied to the product of the petitioner, it has to be seen that an article of the like kind and quality is sold or is capable being sold within the meaning of section 4 .
RatioOfTheDecision	Since the duty on this product is ad valorem, its value has to be found under s. 4 .
RatioOfTheDecision	In the present case, it cannot be done unless it is shown that the product is so or is capable of being sold.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is not disputed that after 1967 this product by the petitioner is not sold.
RatioOfTheDecision	The question is whether an article of the like kind or quality is sold or is capable of being sold.
RatioOfTheDecision	The words "of the like kind and quality" are important.
RatioOfTheDecision	They mean that it is not the calcium carbide which is actually marketable because it complies with the Carbide of Calcium Rules which is for consideration before us.
RatioOfTheDecision	On the contrary, the product under consideration is one which neither sold nor is capable of being sold- The reasons for its non marketability are already given above in dealing with the question whether it is "goods" within the meaning of section 3  .
Argument	The same reasons show that the product is not capable of being sold.
Argument	Firstly, it has not attamed the material form and composition which make it saleable calcium carbide.
Argument	Secondly, calcium carbide which does not attain the form and composition required by the Carbide of Calcium Rules for marketability is not marketable at all in view of the prohibition imposed by the Carbide of Calcium Rules.
Argument	This leads to the third result that the calcuirn carbide is not economic goods and is not, therefore, capable of being sold.
Argument	(3)The third question is as to whether assessable value of the product can be determined under section 4 .
Argument	The crucial requisite of section 4 is that the value has to be determined at the time of the removal of the article chargeable with duty from the factory.
Argument	The petitioner has alleged that the article manufactured by it is not removed from its factory, but is straightway used to generate acetylene gas by the transfer of the article from one plant to another in the same factory.
Argument	There is no de nial by the respondents of this assertion.
Argument	All that is said is that removal from one plant to another is removal within the meaning of section 4 .
Argument	But both the plants are situated in the same factory.
Argument	The calcium carbide plant takes the first step, while the acetylene gas plant takes the second step and the two combined produce the acetylene gas.
Argument	Since there is no traverse by the respondents of the pleading of the petitioner that this movement takes place within the factory, the point of time at which the value of the product is to be determined under section 4 is not reached.
Argument	Consequently, the question of levy and collection of the duty does not arise.
Statute	(4)The expression "factory" is defined in section 2(e) to mean any premises including the precincts thereof, wherein or in any part of which excisable goods are manufactured.
RatioOfTheDecision	The definition covers the present case because the calcium carbide is manufactured in one part of the factory while the acetylene gas is manufactured in another part thereof.
RatioOfTheDecision	The definit ion of "factory" makes it clear that the meaning of factory is not restricted to only the part in which the excisable goods are manufactured.
RatioOfTheDecision	On the other hand, it includes the whole of the premises in a part of which such goods are manufactured.
Argument	At any rate the case of the petitioner is that the whole of the premises which comprise both the plants making calcium carbide and acetylene gas are its factory.
Argument	It is not contended by the respondents that the calcium carbide plant constitutes a separate factory and the acetylene gas plant constitutes another factory.
Argument	It cannot be said, therefore, that the so-called calcium carbide made by the petitioner is removed from the factory in which it is made.
RulingByPresentCourt	A perusal of Rules 9 and 49 makes it clear that the question of collection of any excise duty cannot arise unless and until the goods are removed from the factory.
RulingByPresentCourt	(14) We may conclude, therefore, that even if it is assumed for the sake of argument that for some reason or the other the calcium carbide made by the peti tioner is "goods" and is, therefore, assessable to excise duty still the actual collection of the excise duty on these goods cannot be made unless and until these goods are removed from the factory of the petitioner.
RulingByPresentCourt	(15) For the above reasons, the writ petition succeeds, the impugned orders of the Superintendent, dated 5th January, 1972, of the appellate Collector, dated 25th November, 1972 and of <ORG>, dated 16th September, 1975 are quashed.
