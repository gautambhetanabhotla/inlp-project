Argument	44, Mr. <NAME>, Attorney- General, has appeared for the <ORG> and has argued that both the Tribunals below went wrong on principle in construing the notices dated August 23, 1953, and September 17, 1953, respectively.
Argument	According to him, the continued illegal stoppages of work, 'slow-down' tactics and strikes indulged in by the workmen despite the advice of their Union , left the <ORG> no alternative but to discharge the workmen, except in some essential departments, with effect from August 24, 1953, and the notice dated August 23, 1953, though it stated that the <ORG> declared a lock-out of the entire <ORG> except for some special shifts, really terminated the services of the respondents by discharging them with effect from August 24, 1953.
Argument	He has further submitted that the notice dated September 17, 1953, did not revoke the earlier order of discharge, but merely gave the respondents an opportunity of reemployment at the pleasure of the <ORG> on fulfilment of certain conditions.
Argument	The learned Attorney-General contends that if the notices are so construed, then the <ORG> below are wrong in holding that the respondents are entitled to be taken back in employment as of right.
Argument	He has further submitted that <ORG> was wrong in law in holding that there could not be a lock-out and discharge at the same time.
RatioOfTheDecision	In our view, the two notices in questio n are not capable of bearing the construction which the learned Attorney-General has pressed for our acceptance, apart altogether from the question if under the Industrial Disputes Act , 1947, there can be a simultaneous order of discharge and lock-out in respect of the same employees.
RatioOfTheDecision	The question of construction is really a question of intention-to be gathered primarily from the words used in the documents; and if the word-.
RatioOfTheDecision	used are ambiguous, then surrounding circumstances can be looked into for the purpose of construing the notices.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is worthy of note that the first notice states inter alia that in consequence of the illegal strikes which took place on several previous dates, the Management has no option but to declare a look-out of the entire <ORG> except some special shifts with effect from Monday, August 24, 1953; then in the concluding portion the notice states-" The services of all other workers shall be deemed to be discharged with effect from Monday, August 24, 1953.
RatioOfTheDecision	The expression " shall be de emed to be discharged has to be read in the context of the declaration of a lock-out; such an expression is neither usually employed nor apt to effectuate an intention to terminate the services of the workmen altogether.
RatioOfTheDecision	A 'lock- out', according to the definition in the Industrial Disputes Act , 1947, means the " closing of a place of employment, or the suspension of work, or the refusal by an employer to continue to employ any number of persons employed by him ".
RatioOfTheDecision	In this context, the notice when it said that the services of all other workers shall be deemed to.
RatioOfTheDecision	be discharged with effect from the date of the lock-out really meant that the <ORG> refused to employ the respondent workmen during the period when the place of employment was closed.
RatioOfTheDecision	The second notice dated September 17, 1953, places the matter beyond any doubt.
RatioOfTheDecision	It starts by saying that the "management have reasons to believe that many workers are desirous of resuming work" etc.
RatioOfTheDecision	; then it states that "all employees on the Works rolls of the <ORG>  on August 23, 1953, and who wish to report for duty, must resume work between 6 a.m. on Friday, September 18 1953, and 10 p.m. on Saturday, September 19, 1953."
RatioOfTheDecision	The expressions used in the second- notice clearly show that the intention was not reemployment of discharged workmen, but resumption of work by employees who desired to resume work and whose employment had been stopped on account of the 86 look-out.
RatioOfTheDecision	The third notice dated September 23, 1953, which extended the date of joining to October 2, 1953, again said that " a large number of workers might have been prevented from resuming their work for reasons beyond their control " and gave that as the reason for extending the date.
RatioOfTheDecision	If the three notices referred to above are read together against the background of events which bad happened prior to August 23, 1953, the only reasonable construction is the one adopted by the <ORG>, viz., that the employees whose employment had been refused during the lockout were permitted to resume work without any conditi ons if they reported for duty by a particular date, and on fulfilment of a condition if they reported for duty after that date.
RatioOfTheDecision	The learned Attorney-General has referred us to some oral and documentary evidence to show that the workmen themselves understood the notice dated August 23, 1953, as a notice of discharge.
RatioOfTheDecision	He has referred particularly to the letter dated September 2, 1953, written by <ORG> to the General Manager of the <ORG> in which the notice dated August 23, 1953, was referred to as " an illegal and unconstitutional notice of discharge".
RatioOfTheDecision	On the other side, Mr. <NAME> appearing for the respondent workmen has referred us to the evidence given by some of the <ORG>'s servants, which showed that no formal order of discharge was recorded in the service book of the employees, as required by the rules; nor any notice of one month given for discharging the workmen; but on the contrary the workmen were given continuity of service for the entire period of their absence.
RatioOfTheDecision	We do not,  however, think that when the words used in the notices sufficiently and clearly bring out the intention of the <ORG>, it is necessary to refer to other evidence in the record.
RatioOfTheDecision	Moreover, this <ORG> does not sit as a regular <ORG> of appeal over Industrial Tribunals, and does not ordinarily subject the evidence given on behalf of the parties to a fresh review and scrutiny, unless it is shown that exceptional or special circumstances exist, or that substantial and grave injustice has been done or that the case in question presents features of sufficient gravity to warrant a review of the decision appealed from.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is necessary now to consider an alternative argument of the learned Attorney-General.
RatioOfTheDecision	He has contended that assuming that the notices bear the construction which we have put on them, the respondent workmen did not join on or before Saturday, September 19, 1953,--the latest day by which they could resume work without any condition; they reported for duty on October 1, 1953, or October 2, 1953, but fai led to produce evidence satisfactory to the <ORG> of their absence as required by the notice dated September 17, 1953, and, therefore, they were not entitled to be taken back as of right and without any condition.
