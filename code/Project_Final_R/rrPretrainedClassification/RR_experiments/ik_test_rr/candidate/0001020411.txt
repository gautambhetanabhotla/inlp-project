Fact	JUDGMENT <NAME>, J.
Fact	1.
Fact	The suit which has given rise to this appeal was brought by the plaintiff, a Mahomedan lady, against the defendant, her <NAME>-in-law, to recover arrears of certain allowance, called Kharch-i-pandan, under the terms of an agreement executed by him on the 25th October, 1877, prior to and in consideration of her marriage with his son <NAME>, both she and her future <NAME> being minors at the time.
Argument	2.
Argument	The agreement in question recites that the marriage was fixed for the 2nd November, 1877, and that "therefore" the defendant declared of his own free will and accord that he " shall continue to pay Rs.
Argument	500 per month in perpetuity " to the plaintiff for " her betel-leaf expenses, etc., from the date of the marriage, i.e., from the date of her reception, " out of the income of certain properties therein specifically described, which he then proceeded to charge for the payment of the allowance.
Argument	3.
Argument	Owing to the minority of the plaintiff, her " reception " into the conjugal domicil e to which reference is made in the agreement does not appear to have taken place until 1883.
Argument	The <NAME> and wife lived together until 1896, when, owing to differences, she left her <NAME>'s home, and has since resided more or less continuously at Moradabad.
Argument	4.
Argument	The defendant admitted the execution of the document on which the suit is brought, but disclaimed liability principally on two grounds, viz., (I) that the plaintiff was no party to the agreement, and was consequently not entitled to maintain the action, and (2) that she had forfeited her right to the allowance thereunder by her misconduct and refusal to live with her <NAME>.
Argument	5.
Argument	Evidence of a sort was produced to establish the allegations of misconduct, but the Subordinate Judge considered that it was not "legally proved."
Argument	In another place he expresses himself thus: "Although unchastity is not duly proved, yet 1 have no hesitation in holding that plaintiff's character is not free from suspicion."
Argument	Their Lordships cannot help considering an opinion o f this kind regarding a serious charge as unsatisfactory.
Argument	Either the allegation of unchastity was established or it was" not; if the evidence was not sufficient or not reliable, there was an end of the charge so far as the particular matter in issue was concerned, and it was hardly proper to give expression to what the Judge calls "suspicion."
Argument	6.
Argument	The Subordinate Judge, however, came to the conclusion that the plaintiff's refusal to live with her <NAME> was satisfactorily proved, and, holding that on that ground she was not entitled to the allowance, he dismissed the suit.
Argument	7.
Argument	The plaintiff thereupon appealed to <ORG>, where the argument seems to have been confined solely to the question of the plaintiff's right to maintain the action, as the learned Judges observe that neither side called their attention to the evidence on the record.
Argument	They hold that she had a clear right to sue under the agreement, and they accordingly reversed the order of the first <ORG> and decreed the plaintiff's claim.
Argument	8.
Argument	The  defendant has appealed to His Majesty in Council, and two main objections have been urged on his behalf to the judgment and decree of <ORG>.
Argument	9.
Argument	First, it is contended, on the authority of <NAME> v. <NAME> (1861) 1 B.
Argument	& S. 393, that as the plaintiff was no party to the agreement, she cannot take advantage of its provisions.
Argument	With reference to this it is enough to say that the case relied upon was an action of assumpsit, and that the rule of common law on the basis of which it was dismissed is not, in their Lordships' opinion, applicable to the facts and circumstances of the present case.
Argument	Here the agreement executed by the defendant specifically charges immoveable property for the allowance which he binds himself to pay to the plaintiff j she is the only person beneficially entitled under it.
Argument	In their Lordships' judgment, although no party to the document, she is clearly entitled to proceed in equity to enforce her claim.
Precedent	10.
Precedent	Their Lordships desire to observe that in India and among communities ci rcumstanced as the <NAME>, among whom marriages are contracted for minors by parents and guardians, it might occasion serious injustice if the common-law doctrine was applied to agreements or arrangements entered into in connection with such contracts.
Precedent	11.
Precedent	It has, however, been urged with some force that the allowance for which the defendant made himself liable signifies money paid to a wife when she lives with her <NAME>, that it is analogous in its nature to the English pin-money, over the " application of which the <NAME> has a control, and that, as the plaintiff has left her <NAME>'s home and refused to live with him, she has forfeited her right to it.
Precedent	12.
Precedent	Kharch-i-pandan, which literally means " betel-box expenses," is a personal allowance, as their Lordships understand, to the wife customary among Mahomedan families of rank, especially in upper India, fixed either before or after the marriage, and varying according to the means and position of the parties.
Precedent	When they are minors, as is frequently  the case, the arrangement is made between the respective parents and guardians.
RatioOfTheDecision	Although there is some analogy between this allowance and the pin-money in the English system, it appears to stand on a different legal footing, arising from difference in social institutions.
RatioOfTheDecision	Pin-money, though meant for the personal expenses of the wife, has been described as " a fund which she may be made to spend during the converture by the intercession and advice and at the instance of the <NAME>."
RatioOfTheDecision	Their Lordships are not aware that any obligation of that nature is attached to the allowance called Kharch-i-pandan.
RatioOfTheDecision	Ordinarily, of course, the money would be received and spent in the conjugal domicile, but the <NAME> has hardly any control over the wife's application of the allowance, either in her adornment or in the consumption of the article from which it derives its name.
RatioOfTheDecision	13.
RatioOfTheDecision	By the agreement on which the present suit is based the defendant binds himself unreservedly to pay to the plaintiff the fixed allowance; there is  no condition that it should be paid only whilst the wife is living in the <NAME>'s home, or that his liability should cease whatever the circumstances under which she happens to leave it.
RatioOfTheDecision	14.
RatioOfTheDecision	The only condition relates to the time when, and the circumstances under which, his liability would begin.
RatioOfTheDecision	That is fixed with her first entry into her <NAME>'s home when, under the Mahomedan Law, the respective matrimonial rights and obligations come into existence.
RatioOfTheDecision	The reason that no other reservation was made at the time is obvious.
RatioOfTheDecision	The plaintiff was closely related to the ruler of the native state of Rampur; and the defendant executed the agreement in order to make a suitable provision for a lady of her position.
RatioOfTheDecision	The contingency that has since arisen could not have been contemplated by the defendant.
Precedent	15.
Precedent	The plaintiff herself was examined as a witness for the ' defence.
Precedent	She states in her evidence that she has frequently been visited by her <NAME> since she left his home.
RatioOfTheDecision	Neither he nor the defendant has come forw ard to contradict her statements.
RatioOfTheDecision	Nor does any step appear to have been taken on the <NAME>'s part to sue for restitution of conjugal rights,, which the Civil Law of India permits.
RatioOfTheDecision	On the whole their Lordships are of opinion that the judgment and decree of <ORG> are correct and ought to be affirmed.
RatioOfTheDecision	16.
RulingByPresentCourt	Their Lordships will therefore humbly advise His Majesty that the appeal be dismissed.
RatioOfTheDecision	17.
RulingByPresentCourt	The appellant will pay the costs.
