Fact	and sentenced each to rigorous imprisonment for a period of six months and a fine of Rs.

Fact	According to the judgment of the lower <ORG> there is evidence that a bullock was stolen from the house of one <NAME> and that the applicants agreed to take a sum of money to return the bullock.
Fact	There is also evidence that they did in fact take the complainant to the jungle where they pointed out the bullock tied to a tree.
Argument	It is urged in these circumstances that the facts do not warrant a conviction under Section 215 , I.P.C .
Argument	It is said in the first place, that there is no proof that the bullock was stolen because the evidence is only to the effect that the bullock was tied up during the night and was missing next morning.
Argument	It is urged that the applicant should not have been convicted unless it was proved as a positive fact that the bullock had been stolen.
RulingByLowerCourt	The wor ds of the section are:  Whoever takes or agrees or consents to take any gratification under pretence or on account of helping any person to recover any moveable property of which he shall have been deprived by any offence punishable under this Code, shall, unless he uses all means in his power to cause the offender to be apprehended and convicted of the offence, be punished.
RulingByLowerCourt	2.
Fact	The question therefore before the Courts below was whether the owner of the bullock had been deprived of it by an offence punishable under the Indian Penal Code .
Fact	It is argued that these words mean that the bullock must have been stolen.
RatioOfTheDecision	Learned Counsel suggests that "deprive" means "taken out of the possession of."
RatioOfTheDecision	I do not think that any such narrow interpretation can be placed upon that word.
RatioOfTheDecision	To deprive a person of any article may be either to take it away from him or to prevent him from getting possession of it if he would have!
RatioOfTheDecision	done so in the normal course of events.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the circumstances of this case, even if the bullock did stra y at night, although there is no reason for thinking that it did, yet the person who tied it up in the jungle was, in my opinion, depriving the owner of possession of it because normally a bullock which went away would return to its owner in the ordinary course and by being tied up it would be prevented from so doing.
RatioOfTheDecision	Learned Counsel has suggested that a person who commits criminal misappropriation does not deprive the real owner of possession of property.
RatioOfTheDecision	I cannot see that there is any force in this contention and there is no ruling which supports it.

RatioOfTheDecision	I do not think however that in the present case there is any question of presumption.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is a question of inference from the facts and what inference may properly be made is not a question of law but a question for the conscience of the person who is supposed to make the inference.
RatioOfTheDecision	I think therefore that it cannot be said that the <ORG> below were so utterly wrong in coming to the conclusion that the owner was deprived of possession of the bullock by means of an offence under the Indian Penal Code that this <ORG> should interfere in revision.
RatioOfTheDecision	It appears from the judgment that the applicants themselves promised to return the stolen bullock.
RatioOfTheDecision	It was not a case where they merely said that they would make their best endeavour to discover where the bullock was, nor a case where they ultimately failed to discover the property.
RatioOfTheDecision	From the evidence it appears that as soon as they received their money they took the owner direct to the jungle and pointed out the bullock tied up  to a tree.

RatioOfTheDecision	The second argument is that there is nothing in the record to show that the applicants had not used their best endeavours to cause the offenders to be apprehended and convicted of the offence.
RatioOfTheDecision	The applicants, I am informed, did not make any defence on this particular charge in the sense that they gave no explanation of how they discovered the bullock and there was nothing to show one way or the other that they knew or did not know who the offender was.

Precedent	I should like to point out that much inconvenience and error is caused by attempts to regard the dicta of learned Judges as statements of law.
Precedent	<CITATION_1014112> (1928) 15 A.I.R.
Precedent	All.
Precedent	22 the learned Judges certainly made use of expressions from which it might be inferred that they were of opinion that nobody could be convicted of an offence under Section 215 , I.P.C .
RatioOfTheDecision	unless he knew who the offender was but they were discussing the particular facts of  that case and I do not suppose for a moment that they meant to lay down as a general rule of law that knowledge of the offender was a necessary ingredient of that offence.
RatioOfTheDecision	There is not one word in the section that suggests that such knowledge is necessary.
RatioOfTheDecision	It may well be that a person who receives money for discovering stolen property may in the course of his investigations obtain information which if followed up would lead to the apprehension of the offender.
RatioOfTheDecision	If he withholds that information from the proper authorities it is obvious that it cannot be said that he used his best endeavour to cause the offender to be apprehended.
RatioOfTheDecision	The remarks of learned Judges should be read in connexion with the circumstances which they are discussing and the principles of law which they intend to lay down can be inferred only from the effective decisions at which they arrive.

RatioOfTheDecision	This also seems to be the conclusion to be drawn from the provisions of the Evidence Act .
RatioOfTheDecision	The clear meaning of the section in my judgment is that it is an offence to receive money for helping any person to recover property stolen or misappropriated and that there is an exception only in favour of a man who can show that he used all means in his power to cause the apprehension of the offender.
RatioOfTheDecision	Under the provisions of Section 105 , I.P.C .
RatioOfTheDecision	where a person is accused of any offence the burden of proving the existence of circumstances bringing the case within any special exception or proviso contained in the Code or in any law defining the offence is upon him and the Courts shall presume the absence of such circumstances.
RatioOfTheDecision	I therefore hold that the burden of proving that they had used jail means in their power to bring about the apprehension  of the offenders was upon the applicants in the present case and it is quite clear that they never made any attempt to discharge that burden.
