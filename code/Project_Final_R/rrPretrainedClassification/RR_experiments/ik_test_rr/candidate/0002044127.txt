Fact	There are two things which impress <ORG>.
Argument	Firstly, the appellant is not manufacturing any one product such as the boosters, which has  been mainly taken into consideration by <ORG>.
Argument	He is producing various electrical and electronic apparatus in many of which micro-chip technology is used.
Argument	Even the boosters which he manufactures and sells are of two types, viz, transistorised boosters and Integrated Circuit boosters whereas the respondent- plaintiff manufacturers aerial boosters only of the first type.
Argument	Thus micro-chip technology being the base of many of the products, the word 'micro' has much relevance in describing the products.
Argument	Further, the word 'micro' being descriptive of the micro technology used for production of many electronic goods which dairy come to the market, no one Can claim monopoly over the use of the said word.
Argument	Anyone producing any product with the use of micro chip technology would be justified in using the said word as a prefix to his trade name.
RatioOfTheDecision	What is further, those who are familiar with the use of electronic goods know fully well and are not only likely to be misguided or confused merely by, the prefix 'micro'  in the trade name.
RatioOfTheDecision	Once, therefore, it is held that tie word 'micro' is a common or general name descriptive of the products which are sold or of the technology by which the products are manufactured, and the users of such products are, therefore, not likely to be misguided or confused by the said word, the only question which has to be prima facie decided at this stage is whether the words 'tel and 'nix' in the trade names of the appellant and the respondent are deceptive for the buyers and users and are likely to misguide or confuse them in purchasing one for the other.
RatioOfTheDecision	According to us, phonetically the words being totally dissimilar are not going to create any such confusion in the mind of the users.
RatioOfTheDecision	Secondly, even the visual impression of the said two trade names is indifferent.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the first instance, the respondent's trade name 'MICRONIX' is in black and white in slimmer letters and they are ensconces in designs of elongated triangles both above and below the said name.
RatioOfTheDecision	On the other hand, the appellant' s trade name '<ORG>' is in thick bold letters in red colour without any design around.
Precedent	As regards the logo, the respondent's logo consists of the word 'M' in a slim letter with T sporting a dot on it and drawn in the well of 'M', Below the letter 'M' in small letters is written .the word 'MICRONIX' and all these letters and words are written in white in a black square in north-south direction.
Precedent	As against this, the appellant's logo is one letter, viz,.
Precedent	'M' which is drawn in bold broad letter with its left leg slimmer than all other parts which are in thick broad brush.
Fact	The letter has also white lines drawn across it which is in blue colour.
Fact	There is no other letter nor is it set against any background.
RatioOfTheDecision	We are, therefore- unable to see how the visual effect of both the logos will be the same oh the mind of the buyers.
RatioOfTheDecision	This being the case, we are of the view that there is not even the remotest chance of the buyers and users being misguided Or Confused by the two trade names and logos.
RatioOfTheDecision	Same is the case with th e carton which merely reproduces both trade names and the logos.
Argument	The learned counsel for the respondent-plaintiff contended that on the carton the same address, viz, "New Delhi-110020", has been given by the appellant.
Argument	We are unable to understand how anybody can claim monopoly in the said address.
Argument	He also tried to show us that there was a similarity in the words and letters in the two cartons inasmuch as the respondent- plaintiffs carton mentioned the words "MODEL TB-212 (in-door type)" while the appellant has also mentioned the words "MODEL MT-212 (Indoor Unit)".
RulingByLowerCourt	Hence, he submitted that there was a resemblance to the extent of the word "MODEL" and the figure "212".On a perusal, we do not find any such resemblance between the two descriptions.
RulingByLowerCourt	He then submitted that whereas the respondent-plaintiff had mentioned the words "Best for Colour Black and White TVs & FM Radio" with a black rectangular background of the word 'White' before the words "TVs and FM Radios", the appellant has also mentioned words "For C olour/Black & White TVs.
