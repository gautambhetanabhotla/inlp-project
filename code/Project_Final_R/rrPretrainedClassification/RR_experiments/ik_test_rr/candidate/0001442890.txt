Fact	After the appellants had sent their reply an adjudication was held by the Collector of <ORG> (Now Bombay-Ill on reorganisation) in which he held that the vial seals were classifiable as pilfer proof caps under TI 42 CET and the charges against the appellants were therefore made out.
Fact	But he restricted the demand to the normal period under Section 11-A of the Central Excises and Salt Act .
Fact	It is against the said order of the Collector dated 24-12-1985 that the present appeal has been preferred.
Fact	3.
Fact	We have heared <NAME>, Advocate for the appellants and <NAME>, JCDR for the department.
RatioOfTheDecision	4.
RatioOfTheDecision	Samples drawn of the products in question as manufactured by the appellants as also by another manufacturer were also produced for our perusal during the course of our hearing.
RatioOfTheDecision	5.
Argument	The contentions of the appellants stated in short are t hat :-  (i) The aluminium vial seals in question are not caps at all but only seals and therefore there is no question of these products being pilfer proof caps;  (ii) These seals are not known as pilfer proof caps in the trade;  (ill) Reliance of the Collector on two earlier decisions of this <ORG> is misplaced since in one of them the party was not represented and the other did not deal with a product similar to the product in question;  (iv) the classification of the product having been approved by the Assistant Collector the same cannot in law be disturbed by the Collector except by way of proceeding in review;  (v) the Collector had no jurisdiction to confirm the demand under Section 11-A of the Act as it then stood;  (vi) the confiscation of the goods was in any event not called for; and  (vii) the demand as confirmed was in any event in excess of the period prescribed in Section 11-A of the Act.
Argument	6.
Argument	As mentioned earlier there is no dispute regarding the classifiability under TI 42 CET of the screw d own caps manufactured by the appellants which they themselves have classified under TI 42 as pilfer proof caps.
Argument	The dispute relates to tear-off and tear-down aluminium Seals manufactured by them.
Argument	The tear-off seals are to be used in injection glass vials, the tear-down seals being used in other glass or plastic bottles meant to contain tablets, powder, capsules etc.
Argument	<NAME> contended that the function to be performed in case of tear-down seals are (i) to hold the plastic cap in position; (ii) to provide decorative packaging and (iii) to prevent accidental spilling of the contents or cap during handling.
Argument	In respect of the tear-off seals he claimed that the functions were (i) to hold the rubber plug in position; (ii) to prevent spillage of the contents during handling and (iii) to prevent contamination of the plug or the contents.
Argument	Therefore in effect he stated that while these tear-off and tear-down seals may contribute towards safety from pilferage, their main function was not that and that these b eing in the nature of seals only and not caps they could never be classified as pilfer proof caps.
Argument	7.
RatioOfTheDecision	The relevant tariff entry reads "pilfer proof cap for packaging, all sorts, with or without washers or other fittings of cork, rubber, polythene, or any other material".
RatioOfTheDecision	8.
RatioOfTheDecision	Before the Collector as well as before us the appellants have relied on the definition of pilfer proof caps in the Glossary of terms relating to metal containers trade published by <ORG> (IS: 1394-1973).
RatioOfTheDecision	It will be convenient at this stage to extract the definition of not merely pilfer proof caps but other definitions also therein.
RatioOfTheDecision	They are :-  Bung - A fitting used to seal a sheet-metal container, usually a metal plug provided with threads to fit a screwed flange.
RatioOfTheDecision	Cap - A light gauge metal or plastic cover used to seal the neck or opening of a container.
RatioOfTheDecision	Inner Seal - Refers to paper, foil or cork used to line the inside of a general line cap closure.
RatioOfTheDecision	Lid - The detachable closure component defined precisely b y qualification, for example, slip lid, screw, cap, etc.
RatioOfTheDecision	Pilfer proof caps - Screwed on closure with integral pilfer proof arrangements.
RatioOfTheDecision	Plug - A metal stamping fitted into a neckor orifice.
RatioOfTheDecision	Screw Cap - A screw-on (rolled thread) cap incorporating a sealing wad or liner and used in conjunction with an appropriate screwed neck.
RatioOfTheDecision	Washer - A disk or cork or other soft material inserted in the cap to ensure leak-proof seal.
Argument	9.
Argument	Thus so far as pilfer proof caps are concerned the <ORG> definition makes it clear that these are screw on closures with integral pilfer proof arrangement.
Argument	It is explained for the appellants that these are caps that get separated into the bottom and top portions when the caps are opened so that telltale evidence is left behind of the fact of the cap having been opened to get at the contents.
Argument	Further a cap would be a metal or plastic object to seal the opening of the container and the same would be available for use time and time again for the same purpose, whereas a tear-off or tear-down s eal would get destroyed the moment the seal is broken and could not be used again for preserving the contents thereafter.
Argument	That is to say they would be of one time use only and cannot be reused periodically.
Argument	This very circumstance, it appears to us, would establish that these seals are not caps much less pilfer proof caps.
Argument	10.
Argument	<NAME> contends that these seals safe-guard and, evidently, provide protection against tampering and they would therefore be pilfer proof caps.
Argument	This argument overlooks the fact that in order that the product may be described as a pilfer proof cap it must first be a cap.
Argument	A seal would not be a cap since it cannot be reused after opening.
Argument	<NAME> contends that in view of the words used in the entry such an argument cannot be accepted.
Argument	He says that as T.I.
Argument	42 is worded the presence or absence of washer and other fittings would not be material and therefore the seal even without the rubber plug beneath the same would still be a pilfer proof cap.
RatioOfTheDecision	We are unable to accept this contention.
RatioOfTheDecision	The washer or other fittings referred to in the entry, in our opinion, refer to the inner seals of paper, foil, or cork used to line the inside of a cap closure.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the case before us the rubber plug beneath the seal would be the cap itself and not the washer or other fitting.
RatioOfTheDecision	11.
RatioOfTheDecision	<NAME> relies upon two earlier decisions of this <ORG>, both of which have been referred to and relied upon by the Collector also in his order.
RatioOfTheDecision	They are in the case of <ORG> (1983 ELT 2506) and <ORG> (1983 ELT 1163).
RatioOfTheDecision	It is pointed out for the appellants that the assessee went unrepresented in the case of <ORG> and therefore the arguments now advanced were not put before the <ORG> and had therefore not been considered.
RatioOfTheDecision	On the perusal of the judgment we find that this aspect of the case, that seals would not be caps at all, had not been raised by the assessee and had therefore not been considered by the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	So far as the case of <ORG> it is clear that the produc t concerned in that case was not the product in issue before us.
RatioOfTheDecision	That case dealt with cap-seals used for sealing of drums and not vial seals.
RatioOfTheDecision	The definition of the cap seal in 1S:1394-1973 is as follows :-  "Capseal (Drums) - A light gauge metal closure component fitted over the primary seal (for example, inner plug, lever cap or cork shives); may be paper lined on the underside, usually applied by rolling-on with special tool".
RatioOfTheDecision	No doubt reference has been made in that judgment to the definition of pilfer proof caps in 15:1394-1973 but it has been observed that in the absence of any definition of the terms pilfer proof cap in the tariff entry the entry has to be given widest meaning particularly in the context of the qualifying word "all sorts".
RatioOfTheDecision	But in the light of the discussion earlier, especially that relating to the fact that a seal would never be a cap, it appears to us that the ratio of the said decision, which dealt with cap-seals for drums, should not be taken to apply to the facts of the instant cas e.  12.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the light of the above discussion we are satisfied that the tear-off and tear-down vial seals manufactured by the appellants, and which are the subject matter of the present appeal, would not be pilfer proof caps for the purposes of levy of duty under Tl 42 CET.
RatioOfTheDecision	13.
RulingByLowerCourt	<NAME> further contended that in any event the order of the Collector must fail on two other grounds.
RulingByLowerCourt	The first was that the Collector had no jurisdiction to go into the matter since the Assistant Collector had already once gone into the matter and given a finding that these seals are to be classified under Tl 68 GET and, in the absence of any further or better material, the Collector was not entitled to come to a different conclusion except by way of review under the provisions of Section 35-A of the Central Excises and Salt Act as it then stood.
RulingByLowerCourt	From the order of the Collector we find that no other material than was available to the Assistant Collector earlier had been placed before the Collector.
RulingByLowerCourt	The process of manufac ture continued to be the same and the use to which the product was being put also continued to be the same.
RulingByLowerCourt	In view of the said circumstances we agree with the learned counsel that the Collector had no jurisdiction to differ from the earlier conclusion of the Assistant Collector except by way of taking proceeding for review of the earlier order under Section 35-A .
Argument	14.
Argument	<NAME> further contends that so far as the demand for payment of duty is concerned the Collector had no jurisdiction under Section 11-A to do so.
Argument	As pointed out by him, Section 11-A conferred jurisdiction on the Assistant Collector to determine the amount of duty in terms of notice issued earlier by the proper <ORG>.
Argument	The jurisdiction was thus conferred on the Assistant Collector only as the Section stood at the relevant time.
RatioOfTheDecision	It was only on and after 20-12-1975 (20-12-85 ?)
RatioOfTheDecision	that, under the provisions of Section 3 of Act 79 of 1985, the said power of the Assistant Collector could have been exercised by the Collecto r also.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is true that under Rule 6 of the Central Excise Rules the Collector may perform all or any of the duties or exercise all or any of the powers assigned to an Officer under the Central Excise Rules.
RatioOfTheDecision	This empowering of the Collector would relate only to powers exercisable by any other Officer under the Rules and not under the Act.
Argument	<NAME> contends that it is a well recognised principle of administrative law that what a subordinate could do his superior could always do.
Argument	He therefore contends that what an Assistant Collector could do under the provisions of the Act the Collector as his superior could also do.
RatioOfTheDecision	We are unable to accept this proposition in the absence of any provision in the Act itself, as distinguished from the provision in the Rules, to this effect.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, we are convinced that so far as demand for duty is concerned the Collector had no power in the instant case.
RatioOfTheDecision	15.
RatioOfTheDecision	<NAME> in this connection refers to the fact that the demand had been raised under Rule 9(2) also.
RatioOfTheDecision	But we are sat isfied that no demand could have been raised under Rule 9(2) since the manufacture and clearances were in pursuance of an earlier order of the Assistant Collector in terms of which the goods in question were classifiable under TI 68 CET and under that classification the goods were exempt since the exemption limit of clearances had not been crossed.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the absence of any suppression or mis-statement of facts the demand could not be raised under Rule 9(2).
RatioOfTheDecision	16.
RatioOfTheDecision	<NAME> contends that the order for confiscation of the seized goods was also not proper.
RatioOfTheDecision	As already seen, the manufacture and clearances were in terms of the earlier order of the Assistant Collector under which the goods were classifiable under TI 68 CET and were exempt in view of the clearances not having crossed the exemption limit.
RatioOfTheDecision	In view of the above circumstances there had been no case made out of wilful infraction of any statutory provision making the goods liable for confiscation.
RatioOfTheDecision	We therefore hold that the order for confiscation was  also not proper.
RatioOfTheDecision	17.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the light of the above discussion we hold that the order of the Collector is liable to be set aside on the several grounds discussed earlier.
RulingByPresentCourt	Accordingly this appeal is allowed and the order of the Collector is set aside with consequential relief, if any.
