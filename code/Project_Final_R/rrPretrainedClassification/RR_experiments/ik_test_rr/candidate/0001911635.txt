RatioOfTheDecision	The first of the cases which requires to be considered in this respect is that of <CITATION_1186368> (1981-I-LLJ-103), which was decided by its <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	In that case, the question -arose whether <ORG>, which was a society registered under the Jammu & Kashmir Registration of Societies Act , 1898 was an authority within the meaning of Article 12 of t he Constitution of India.
Precedent	In answering the question,' the Court observed (pp.
Precedent	112-113):  "The tests for determining as to when a corporation can be said to be an instrumentality or agency of <ORG> may now be culled out from the judgment in <ORG> case (1979-II-LLJ-217).
Argument	These tests are not conclusive or clinching, but they are merely indicative indicia which have to be used with care and caution, because while stressing the necessity of a wide meaning to be placed on the expression 'other authorities', it must be realised that it should not be stretched so far as to bring in every autonomous body which has some nexus with the <ORG> within the sweep of the expression.
Argument	A wide enlargement of the meaning must be tempered by a wise limitation.
Argument	We may summarise the relevant tests gathered from the decision in <ORG> case as follows:  (1) "One thing is clear that if the entire share capital of the corporation is held by <ORG>, it would go a  long way towards indicating that the corporation is an instrumentality or agency of <ORG>."
Argument	(2) "Where the financial assistance of the <ORG> is so much as to meet almost entire expenditure of the corporation, it would afford some indication of the corporation being impregnated with governmental character."
Argument	(3) "It may also be a relevant factor .......whether the corporation enjoys monopoly status which is <ORG> conferred or <ORG> protected."
Argument	(4) "Existence of deep and pervasive <ORG> Control may afford an indication that the corporation is a <ORG> agency or instrumentality".

Argument	(6) "Specifically, if a department of <ORG> is transferred to a corporation, it would be a strong factor supportive of the inference of the corporation being an instrumentality or agency of <ORG>."
Precedent	If on a consideration of these relevant factors it is found that the corporation is an instrumentality or agency of Government, it would, as pointed out in <ORG> case, be an 'authority' and, therefore, 'State' within the meaning of the expression in Article 12."
Precedent	Applying the above tests in the facts of that case, the <ORG> found that the <ORG> which managed the Regional Engineering College at Srinagar was a 'State' under Article 12.
Precedent	The above quoted tests have been quoted with approval and followed by the Supreme <ORG> in the latest cases of <CITATION_1497108> (1988-I-LLJ-3-41) and <ORG> v. Defence Minister-cum-Chairman BOG, S.S. <ORG> (1989-I-LLJ-263).
Precedent	8.
Precedent	In the earlier case of <NAME> v. <ORG> (1981-I-LLJ-79) the above tests were quoted with approval.
Precedent	It was pointed out by the Supreme <ORG> that the finale was reached when their cumulative effect was assessed and once the body was found to be an instrumentality or agency of the Govern ment, the further conclusion emerged that it was 'State' and subject to the same constituional limitations as Government.
Precedent	In <NAME> case (supra), it was similarly observed that it was not necessary that all the tests should be satisfied for reaching the conclusion either for or against holding an institution to be 'State' and in a given case, some of the features might emerge so boldly and prominently that a second view might not be possible, but there might be other cases where the matter would be on the border line and it might be difficult to take one view or the other outright.
RatioOfTheDecision	When judged in the light of the above tests, keeping in view the observation made by <ORG> in the case of <NAME> (supra) in the passage quoted earlier that "the tests were neither clinching nor conclusive but they were merely indicative indicia which have to be used with care and caution, because while stressing the necessity of a wide meaning to be placed on the expression 'other authorities', it must be realise d that it should not be stretched so far as to bring in every autonomous body which had some nexus with the Government within the sweep of the expression" (exphasis supplied), the conclusion is inevitable that the appellant-Company cannot be brought within the meaning of 'State' under Article 12 of the Constitution.

Precedent	On December 4, 1985, a major leakage of oleum gas took place from one of the units of <ORG> and <ORG> in Delhi.
Precedent	This leakage affected a large number of persons, both amongst the workmen and the public, and an Advocate practising in the Tis Hazari Courts of Delhi died on account of inhalation of oleum gas.
Precedent	Hardly had the people got out of the shock of this disaster when, within two days, another leakage, though this time a minor one, took place as a result of escape of oleum gas from the joints of a pipe.
Precedent	Applications were, thereafter filed by <ORG> and <ORG> in <ORG> for award of compensation to the persons who had suffered  harm on account of escape of oleum gas.
Precedent	As those applications for compensation raised a number of issues' of great Constitutional importance, the Bench of the three Judges which was hearing the applications forwarded them to a larger Bench of five Judges.
Precedent	One of the questions which arose for consideration at the time of hearing was whether Article 21 of the Constitution was available against <ORG> declared industrial policies, was ultimately intended to be carried out by shares and which was engaged in an industry vital to public interest and with potential to affect the life and health of the people.
Argument	On behalf of the applicants, it was contended that Article 21 was available as <ORG> was carrying on an industry which, according to the <ORG>'s own declared industrial policies, was ultimately intended to be carried out by itself, but instead of the <ORG> immediately embarking on that industry, <ORG> was permitted to carry it on under the active control and regulation of the <ORG>.
Argument	Special  emphasis was laid on behalf of the applicants on the regulatory mechanism provided under the Industries (Development and Regulation) Act , 1951, where industries are included in the schedule if they vitally affect public interest.
Argument	It was pointed out that regulatory measures were also to be found under the Environment Act , 1986 and other allied legislations.
Argument	Reliance was also placed upon the fact that sizeable aid in loans, lend and other facilities were granted by the <ORG> to <ORG> in carrying on the industry.
Argument	Taking aid of the American <ORG> Action doctrine, it was also argued on behalf of the applicants that private activity, if supported, controlled or regulated by the <ORG> might get so entwined with <ORG>al activity as to be termed <ORG> action and it would then be subject to the same constitutional restraints on the exercise of power as the <ORG>.
Argument	12.
Argument	In controverting the above contentions, it was submitted on behalf of <ORG> that control or regulation of a private corporation's functio ns by the <ORG> under general statutory Law, such as the Industries (Development and Regulation) Act , 1951 was only in exercise of police power of regulation by the <ORG> and such regulation did not convert the activity of the private corporation into that of the <ORG>.
Argument	It was contended that control which deemed a <ORG> an agency of the <ORG>, must be of the type where the <ORG> controlled the management policies of the <ORG>, whether by sizeable representation on the board of management or by necessity of prior approval of the <ORG> before any new policy of management was adopted, or by any other mechanism.
Argument	13.
Argument	In order to deal with the rival contentions, <ORG> traced that part of the development of Article 12 where it embarked on the path of evolving criteria by which a <ORG> could be termed 'other authority' under Article 12.
Argument	In doing so, the <ORG> considered its earlier decisions and quoted with approval the tests laid down in <ORG> case (su pra) as applied in <NAME> case (supra) and the observations made in the latter.
Argument	The <ORG> then pointed out that the controversy as to whether the manner in which a <ORG> was brought into existance had any relevance to the question whether it was <ORG> instrumentality or agency was set at rest in the case of <NAME> (supra).
Argument	The <ORG> further pointed out that a <ORG> might be a Statutory <ORG> created by a statute or it might be a society registered under the Societies Registration Act or a Company formed under the Companies Act : any such <ORG> would come within the ambit of Article 12 , if it was found to be an instrumentality or agency of the <ORG> on proper assessment of the relevant factors.
Argument	14.
Argument	In the light of the above principles of law, the <ORG> proceeded to examine whether a Private <ORG> such as <ORG>, which was engaged in the manufacture of chemicals and fertilisers, came within the ambit of Article 12 so as to be amenable to the discipline of Article 21.
Argument	F or that purpose, the <ORG> first examined the Central <ORG>'s Industrial Policy Resolutions of 1948 and 1956 and then the Industries (Development and Regulation) Act , 1951 enacted to carry out the objectives of the policy, resolutions.
Argument	On such examination, the <ORG> found that the industry of 'Chemicals and Fertilisers' was specified in the First Schedule of the above Act as it was deemed by the <ORG> to be an industry of vital public interest, whose public import necessitated that the activity should be ultimately carried out by the <ORG> itself, though in the interim period with <ORG> support and under the <ORG> control, Private Corporations might also be permitted to supplement the <ORG> effort.
Argument	The <ORG> next took notice of fact that the Industries (Development and Regulation) Act , 1951 made the activities of an undertaking engaged in an industry scheduled therein subject to extensive and detailed control and supervision by the <ORG> and even empowered the <ORG> to take over and manage su ch undertaking if after investigation held under the Act it was found that its affairs were being managed in a manner detrimental to public interest.
Argument	The <ORG> also took notice of the fact that Shriram was required to obtain licences, under the Factories Act and Delhi Municipal Act and was subject to extensive environment regulation under the Water (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act , 1974 and Air (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act , 1981, that is, all such activities which could jeopardise public interest.
Argument	The <ORG> next observed that such functional control was of special significance as it was the potentiality of the fertiliser Industry to adversely affect the health and safety of the community and its being impregnated with public interest which perhaps dictated the policy decision of the <ORG> to ultimately operate this industry exclusively and invited functional control.
Argument	The <ORG> next took note of the fact that, along with the extensive functional control, Shriram received sizeable a ssistance in the shape of loans and overdrafts running into several crores of rupees through various agencies.
Argument	It was men said by the <ORG>, "Moreover, Shriram is engaged in the manufacture of Caustic soda, Chlorine etc.

Argument	Chlorine gas is admittedly dangerous to human life and health.

Argument	Thus, Shriram is engaed in an activity which has the potential to invade the right to life of large sections of people."

Precedent	The <ORG> then proceeded to deal with the other question raised in the case and after answering the same, observed in paragraph 33 that since it was not deciding the question as to whether <ORG> was an authority within the meaning of Article 12 so as to be subjected to the I discipline of the fundamental right under Article 21 , it did not think it would be justified in setting up a special machinery for investigation of the claims for compensation made by those who alleged that they had been victims of oleum gas escape.
Precedent	17.
RatioOfTheDecision	A detailed and careful reading of the above case clearly indicates that the Supreme <ORG>, in no uncertain terms, stated that it was not deciding the question as to whether a private corporation could be made amenable to the discipline of fundamental rights.
RatioOfTheDecision	In other words, even though the <ORG> mooted the question whether a <ORG>, like <ORG>, could be included within the ambit of Article 12 , it did not answer the same.
RatioOfTheDecision	It cannot, therefore, be said that the Supreme <ORG>  has expressed any opinion on the subject as to be binding upon us either as obiter or ratio decidendi.
Argument	18.
Argument	Mr. <NAME>, however, submitted that even if it was held that <ORG> did not make any decision on the point, still then the observations made by it would have a persuasive effect.
Argument	Accordingly, Mr. <NAME> submitted that from the observations made therein, he could successfully argue that the appellant-company was an intrumentality or agency of the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	We are unable to persuade ourselves to rely upon the observations of <ORG> referred to earlier for, in making the same, it was largely influenced by the fact that Shriram was engaged in an activity which was dangerous to life and health and had the potential to invade the right of large sections of people.
RatioOfTheDecision	19.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is of course true that the appellant-Compahy, like Shriram, is engaged in an industry which comes under the purview of the first schedule to the Industries (Development and Regulation) Act , 1951 and as such, the Government  has powers to assume management and control of the <ORG> if it is found that its affairs are being managed in a manner detrimental to public interest.
RatioOfTheDecision	In other words, the Government has the regulatory control over the affairs of the <ORG> through various legislations.
RatioOfTheDecision	But in our view, such control cannot be said to be "deep and pervasive <ORG> control" so as to satisfy the fourth test.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the case of <CITATION_426032> (1975-I-LLJ-399), Justice <NAME> pointed out that a mere finding of the <ORG> control was also not determinative of the question whether a <ORG> was an instrumentality or agency of the <ORG>, since a <ORG> had considerable measures of control under its police power over all types of business operations and it was not possible to assume that panoply of law and authority of a <ORG> under which people carry on ordinary business or their private affairs or own property, each enjoying equality in terms of legal capacity, would be extraordinary assistance.
RatioOfTheDecision	The learned judge next observed th at a finding of <ORG> financial support plus an unusual degree of control over the management and policies might lead one to characterize an operation as <ORG> action.
RatioOfTheDecision	While on this point, we may, even at the risk of repetition, remind ourselves of the observation made in the case of <NAME> (supra) that the six tests were to be used with care and caution, because while stressing the necessity of a wide meaning to be placed on the expression 'other authorities' it must be realised that it could not be stressed so far as to bring other autonomous body which has some nexus with the <ORG> within the sweep of the expression; a wide enlargement of the meaning must be tempered by a wise limitation.
RatioOfTheDecision	20.
RatioOfTheDecision	If merely on the basis of the facts that a Company incorporated under the Companies Act is running a business in any of the industries appearing in the first schedule of the Industries (Development and Regulation) Act , 1951 and that for running its business it has obtained loans from financial institution f or which such institution has placed a Director in <ORG>, an inference that it is an instrumentality or agency of the <ORG> has to be drawn, then in that case a private limited company carrying on business in cigarettes, which also finds place in the first Schedule of the above Act, with <ORG> loan would have to be called an instrumentality or agency of the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	Widening of the scope of Article 12 to such an extent will not only be unwise but unjustified also.
RatioOfTheDecision	21.
RatioOfTheDecision	In view of our above discussion, we must hold that by no stretch of imagination can the appellant-Company be said to be an instrumentality or agency of the <ORG> so as to bring it within the ambit of Article 12 of the Constitution of India.
RatioOfTheDecision	The first point raised on behalf of the appellant must, therefore, succeed.
RatioOfTheDecision	In view of that success, the other point raised on behalf of the appellant in support of the appeal need not be gone into.
