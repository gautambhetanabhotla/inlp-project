RatioOfTheDecision	Mr. <NAME>, the learned Counsel for the respondents drew my attention to the decision of <ORG> in <NAME> v. <NAME>, 1941 AC 157.
Precedent	Mr. <NAME> drew my attention to the following passage in the judgment of Viscount <NAME>, in the above case:--  "The question thus resolves itself into that of fixing a reasonable figure to be paid by way of damages for the loss of a measure of prospective happiness.
Precedent	Such a problem might seem more suitable for discussion in an easy or Artistotelian ethics than in the jud gment of a Court of law, but in view of the earlier authorities we must do our best to contribute to its solution.
Precedent	The Judge observed that the earlier decisions quoted to him assumed 'that human life is, on the whole, good'.
Precedent	I would rather say that, before damages are awarded in respect of the shortened life of a given individual under this head, it is necessary for the Court to be satisfied that the circumstances of the individual life were calculated to lead, on balance, to a positive measure of happiness, of which the victim has been deprived by the defendant's negligence.
Precedent	II the character or habits of the individual were calculated to lead him to a future of unhappiness or despondency, that would be a circumstance justifying a smaller award, it is significant, that at any rate in one case of which we were informed, the Jury refused to award any damages under this head at all.
Precedent	As Lord <NAME> said in <NAME> v. <NAME> 1937 AC 826, special cases suggest themselves where the termination of a life of constant pain  and suffering cannot be regarded as inflicting injury, or at any rate, as inflicting the same injury as in more normal cases.
Precedent	I would further lay it down that, in assessing damages for this purpose, the question is not whether the deceased had the capacity or ability to appreciate that his further life on earth would bring him happiness.
Precedent	The test is not subjective, and the right sum to award depends on the objective estimate of what kind of future on earth the victim might have enjoyed, whether he had justly estimated that future or not.
Precedent	Of course, no regard must be had to financial losses or gains during the period of which the victim has been deprived.
Precedent	The damages are in respect of loss of life, not of loss of future pecuniary prospects".
