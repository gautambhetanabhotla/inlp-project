Argument	JUDGMENT Fletcher, J.
Argument	1.
Argument	This is a hopeless and frivolous action.
Argument	2.
Argument	The plaintiff, Mr. <NAME>, was employed by <ORG> in a tea garden in Port Blair, in the Andaman Islands.
Argument	The plaintiff in his plaint alleges that in the month of February, 1887, Mr. <NAME>, the then Officiating Secretary to <ORG>, wrote to the then Superintendent of Port Blair setting out the terms on which the employment of the plaintiff was sanctioned by the Secretary of <ORG>, viz., that after an engagement of three years the plaintiff's services should be retained for an undefined period, on a salary of Rs.
Argument	350 a month increasing by annual increments together with a commission of five per cent, on the net profits of the tea garden.
Argument	There were certain other allowances which were to be made to the plaintiff and he was to be entitled to six calendar months notice if his engagement was to be terminated.
Argument	The plaintiff further alleges that on the 4th July, 1887, <ORG> dec ided that the grant of leave to the plaintiff should be regulated by the Rules of the Civil Leave Code applicable to uncovenanted officers of the Government, and such decision was communicated to the plaintiff by and on behalf of <ORG> and was accepted and agreed to by the plaintiff, and thereupon by mutual agreement the Regulations of the Civil Leave Code became a part of the contract between the parties.
Argument	3.
Argument	The plaintiff also alleges that by mutual consent and agreement the terms of his employment were further varied so that he should be entitled to receive one year's notice instead of six months to terminate his employment, and that this and other changes in the terms of his employment were duly sanctioned by the then Secretary of <ORG> and accepted by the plaintiff.
Argument	4.
Argument	On the 17th February, 1905, the then Superintendent of Port Blair wrote to the plaintiff and asked if he was prepared to dispense with the one year's notice to which he was entitled under the terms of his covenant and if  he was prepared to leave, the Government would pay him one year's salary and a bonus of £ 200 and he was informed that in any case he must accept that letter as a notice of the termination of his employment.
Argument	5.
Fact	On the 18th July, 1905, <ORG> closed this tea garden and paid to the plaintiff his salary up to the 17th February, 1906, and he also received from the Government the bonus of £ 200 or Rs.
Fact	3,000.
Argument	6.
Argument	The plaintiff's case is that he is entitled to damages, one year's salary from the 18th July 1905, and to the commission to which he was entitled under the terms of his contract.
Argument	7.
Fact	Now, the first question is, what is the nature of the plaintiff's employment?
RatioOfTheDecision	8.
RatioOfTheDecision	That he was employed under the <ORG> there is now doubt.
RatioOfTheDecision	9.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is a general rule and a well-established principle that the <ORG> in the absence of statutory provision can dismiss any servant in its Civil or Military employ.
RatioOfTheDecision	10.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is said that it does not apply to the persons employed under <ORG> who are eng aged in certain commercial undertakings of the Government.
RatioOfTheDecision	Now, the only right which the plaintiff can have against the <ORG>, which is not the ordinary right of a servant of the <ORG>, is in the event if he had a different right against <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	11.
Precedent	Section 65 of the statute, (21 and 22 Vict, Chap.
Fact	106), gives certain persons a right of action against the Secretary of <ORG>.
Fact	Now, what was the position of a servant of <ORG> prior to the time when British India was taken over by the British <ORG>?
RatioOfTheDecision	12.
RatioOfTheDecision	It appears to me that the position of a servant of the <ORG> was exactly the same as the position of a servant of the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is enacted by Section 75 of 3 and 4 Will.
RatioOfTheDecision	IV, Chapter 85 that "Nothing in this Act shall take away the power of the said <ORG> to remove or dismiss any of the officers or servants of the said <ORG> but that the said <ORG> shall and may at all times have full liberty to remove or dismiss any of such officers or servants at their will and  pleasure."
RatioOfTheDecision	13.
RatioOfTheDecision	The <ORG>, therefore, had exactly the same right of dismissing servants as the <ORG> has, but even in this case, if the plaintiff had a right of action against the <ORG>, I should hold that his suit could not succeed.
RatioOfTheDecision	It seems to me wholly wrong to say that the servant, who had received his notice of dismissal and got his wages for the remainder of the term covered by the notice, could maintain an action for wrongful dismissal.
RatioOfTheDecision	It seems to involve the anomaly that the person dismissed in the eleventh month of the notice would receive a larger amount than if he was dismissed in the first month.
RatioOfTheDecision	I think from the plaintiff's own statements in the plaint that the plaintiff has been treated by the <ORG> with great consideration.
RatioOfTheDecision	14.
RatioOfTheDecision	It only remains for me to say that the plaint discloses no cause of action against the Secretary of <ORG> and the suit must be dismissed with costs on scale No.
