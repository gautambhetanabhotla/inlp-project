Fact	JUDGMENT V. Bhargava, J.
Fact	1.
Fact	During the hearing of this appeal it was pointed out by learned counsel for the appellants that the appellants had been seriously prejudiced as the learned Sessions Judge during the trial had disallowed a certain question being put to one of the Important witnesses named <NAME>.
Fact	2.
Fact	According to the prosecution, the murder of <NAME> took place in a ghar while he was sleeping on a cot, and only other two persons who were in the ghar at the time of the murder were <NAME> and <NAME> who were also sleeping on two different cots a few paces away from the cot of <NAME>.
Fact	<NAME> and <NAME> are two of the witnesses on whose statements the prosecution relies to prove the case against the appellants.
Fact	The murder is said to have been committed by <NAME> appellant by firing two shots at <NAME>.
Fact	<NAME> in his evidence stated that he woke up on hearing the sound of a shot being fired and thereafter saw the appellants running away which implies that he did not actual ly see any of the two shots being fired by <NAME> appellant.
Fact	The other witness, <NAME>, who is a young boy aged 13 years also stated that he woke up on the sound of firing of a pistol and saw the three accused who were near <NAME>'s cot.
Fact	He went on to add that he saw the accused <NAME> firing a second shot with a pistol.
Fact	In this connection learned counsel for the accused, who was appearing in the trial Court, put the following question to this witness:  Q.
Fact	"Why did you not tell the Investigation Officer that you had seen the second shot being fired."
Fact	There is note by the learned <NAME> Judge that this question was disallowed as it referred merely to an omission.
Fact	It appears that the learned <NAME> Judge took the view that this omission In the statement of witness <NAME> made to the Investigating Officer under Section 161, Cr.P.C ., did not amount to a contradiction and consequently the witness could not be cross-examined as to the statement made by him to the investigating officer, in view of  the provisions of Section 162 , Cr.P.C .
Fact	In our opinion, the decision of the learned Sessions Judge on this question was quite wrong.
Fact	The question whether a statement recorded by the Investigation Officer under Section 161 , Cr.P.C .
Fact	can be used for the purpose of challenging the deposition made by a witness in <ORG> on the basis that he omitted to make that statement when examined by the Investigating Officer, came up for consideration in <NAME> v. State, Criminal Appeal No.
Fact	77 of 1952 (All) (A).
Precedent	In that case there was a difference of opinion between two learned Judges of this <ORG> and the case was thereupon referred for the opinion of a third Judge.
Precedent	At that stage it came up before one of us and it was held as follows:  "When examined in <ORG>, <NAME> stated that he went to the spot on hearing the uproar and heard <NAME> inciting <NAME> to give a blow with the spade to <NAME>, whereupon <NAME> gave a blow on <NAME>'s head and <NAME> fell down.
Precedent	Thereafter <NAME> rushed to the rescue of <NAME> <NAME> and wielded his lathi.
Fact	He then proceeded to give other details of the incident.
Fact	When examined, by the police during the investigation, he did not include these details about the beginning of the incident.
Fact	He began by stating that when he reached the scene of occurrence on hearing the alarm, he saw Bh<NAME> already lying on the ground while <NAME> was present there.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the statement before the police there was, therefore, omission to mention that he had seen the beginning of the fight-It is true that all omissions do not amount to contradictions and the statements given before the police cannot be used in evidence at all for any purpose except for the purpose of bringing out a contradiction.
RatioOfTheDecision	There are, however, certain omissions which amount to contradictions and have been treated as such by this <ORG> as well as other <ORG>s in this country.
RatioOfTheDecision	Those are omissions relating to facts which are expected to be included in the statement before the police by a person who is giving a narrative of what he sa w, on the ground that they relate to important features of the incident about which the deposition is made.
RatioOfTheDecision	In a case of this nature, where <NAME> was stating what he saw, he would certainly have stated to the police also the incitement by <NAME> to <NAME> to give a blow to <NAME> and the actual infliction of that blow by <NAME> if he had really witnessed that part of the incident.
RatioOfTheDecision	He could not have begun his statement to the police by saying that he saw <NAME> lying on the ground unless it was true that, at the time when he reached the place of the incident, <NAME> had already been injured and had fallen down.
RatioOfTheDecision	The omission in this respect Is, therefore, an omission of a very material part of the Incident which amounts to a contradiction so that the statement before the police under Section 162 of the Code of Criminal Procedure was admissible in evidence and could be relied upon to bring out that contradiction during the trial so as to show that the statement made by <NAME> in Court was  not reliable, in this respect."
RatioOfTheDecision	3.
RatioOfTheDecision	In our opinion the view expressed in the ease which has been reproduced above lays down the correct principle of law and that view should be applied to the present case also.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the present case it is clear that the question which was disallowed should have been permitted by the learned <ORG> Judge.
Precedent	<NAME> in his deposition in <ORG> purported to be an eye-witness of the act by which the murder of <NAME> was committed, by stating that he saw the accused <NAME> firing a second shot from the pistol.
Precedent	It appears that the defence case was that at the first stage when <NAME> was examined by the Investigating Officer, he did not make such a statement.
Precedent	All he stated was that he was awakened by the firing of the shot and then he saw these three appellants standing near the cot of <NAME>.
Precedent	The omission was on a very material point If this deposition in <ORG> is believed It Would mean that he is an eye-witness of the commission of the act of murder, whereas if it is disb elieved, it would mean that he did not actually witness the act by which the murder was committed.
RatioOfTheDecision	Such an omission would certainly amount to a contradiction.
RatioOfTheDecision	Had <NAME> actually seen the second shot being fired it must be expected that he would have mentioned this fact in his statement given to the Investigating Officer.
RatioOfTheDecision	The learned Sessions Judge was, therefore, wrong in disallowing the question which was put to the witness.
RatioOfTheDecision	4.
Precedent	In this connection reliance has been placed by the learned Deputy Government Advocate in support of the learned Sessions Judge on this point on the remarks by <NAME>, J., in the case of <CITATION_1073688> , AIR 1952 All 289 (B).
Precedent	The learned Deputy Government Advocate urged that the interpretation given of Section 162 , Cr.P.C .
Precedent	in that case was a decision by a Division <ORG>, and if we differ from that decision, it would be appropriate that the point be referred to a larger <ORG> in order that the principle of law, which is of great importance, may be properly settled so far a s this State is concerned.
RatioOfTheDecision	Having looked at the judgment delivered by the two learned Judges in that case We are unable to accept the contention of the learned Deputy Government Advocate that the interpretation placed on Section 162 in that case represents the view of the <ORG> as a whole.
Precedent	The other member of the <ORG>, <NAME>, J., nowhere discussed this question in his judgment.
Precedent	He began his judgment by saying that he agreed with <NAME>, J., that the appeal of one of the appellants be disallowed and the appeal of two appellants be allowed.
RatioOfTheDecision	He then proceeded to note down briefly his reasons and added that he would not enter into a discussion of the evidence of the witnesses who were cross-examined at a very great inordinate length which led to an undue prolongation of the trial and much unnecessary expenditure of time.
RatioOfTheDecision	Proceeding on this basis, <NAME>, J., did not at all discuss the question whether the omissions in the statement of certain witnesses did or did not amount to contradictions and whether it was po ssible to prove those omissions keeping in view the provisions of Section 162 , Cr.P.C .
RatioOfTheDecision	His silence on the point indicates that in his opinion it was not necessary for the decision of the appeal to enter into this question at all.
RatioOfTheDecision	He nowhere expressed his concurrence with the view expressed by <NAME>, J.
RatioOfTheDecision	He, of course, did not express any dissent either.
RatioOfTheDecision	In these circumstances, the views expressed by <NAME>, J., should be held to be the views of a single Judge of this <ORG> and it is, therefore, in our opinion, not necessary to refer this question to a larger <ORG>, as we can reconsider the views expressed by a single Judge.
RatioOfTheDecision	Even <NAME>, J., in that decision conceded that in some cases the omission in the statement under Section 162 may amount to contradiction of the deposition in <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	He proceeded further to hold that these are cases where what is actually stated is irreconcilable with what is omitted and impliedly negatives its existence.
RatioOfTheDecision	With respect, we are unable to agree with this limitation placed on  the nature of the omissions by him.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is of course correct that if the omission is such that what is actually stated is irreconcilable with what is omitted and impliedly negatives its existence, such an omission does amount to contradiction.
RatioOfTheDecision	But this does not define the entire scope of the principle.
Precedent	There can be other omissions also on material points which should have found place in the statement made under Section 161 , Cr.P.C .
Precedent	if what is stated in the deposition in <ORG> is really correct.
Precedent	5.
Precedent	In Criminal Appeal No.
Precedent	77 of 1952 (All) (A), to which we have already referred above, there was an emission by witness <NAME> about seeing a part of the incident.
Precedent	When he was examined by the Investigating Officer he stated that he came on the scene of occurrence and then, omitting part of the incident; put forward by the prosecution, began his deposition by relating a later part of the incident.
Precedent	It was held that the omission in his statement to the investigating Officer about the earlier part of the incident  amounted to contradiction and could be used for the purpose of disbelieving his deposition in <ORG> that he had seen that earlier part of the incident.
Precedent	His statement before the Investigating Officer did not expressly or impliedly negative the fact that that part of the incident had taken place.
Precedent	It only resulted in negativing his assertion that he had seen that part of the incident.
Precedent	It was held, and, in our opinion, rightly, that the omission in the statement to the Investigating officer in these circumstances amounted to contradiction and could be brought out in the cross-examination of the witness under Section 162 , Cr.P.C .
Precedent	In the present case the position is very similar.
Precedent	6.
Precedent	<NAME>, J., also went on to hold that if the statement under Section 162 , Cr.P.C .
RatioOfTheDecision	can be reconciled with the deposition in <ORG> and can stand with it then there is absolutely no contradiction.
RatioOfTheDecision	The question whether the deposition in <ORG> can or cannot be reconciled with the statement recorded under Section 161 , Cr.P.C .
RatioOfTheDecision	can only b e settled after the omission has been brought to the notice of the witness and the witness has had an opportunity to give his explanation.
RatioOfTheDecision	If after the explanation it appears that the two are reconcilable, it would cease to be a contradiction.
RatioOfTheDecision	But that can happen not only in the case of an omission, but even in the case of an apparent contradiction of positive facts included in the deposition and the statement under Section 161 .
RatioOfTheDecision	There may appear to be a contradiction between the deposition in court and the statement under Section 161 .
RatioOfTheDecision	but when it is put to the witness, he may give an explanation which may reconcile them, whereupon the contradiction may cease to be a contradiction.
RatioOfTheDecision	The mere fact that he may possibly I reconcile the two statements cannot affect the applicability of the proviso to Section 162 Cr.
RatioOfTheDecision	P. C. in the case of an omission which is of such a nature, that it can be held to be a contradiction.
