RatioOfTheDecision	A person who is given an officiating post to test his suitability to be made permanent later, holds it on the implied term that he would have to be reverted if he was found unsuitable.
RatioOfTheDecision	A reversion in such a case on the ground of unsuitability is an action in accordance with the terms on which the officiating post was being held and is not a reduction in rank by way of punishment to which s. 240 of the Government of India Act, 1935, would be attracted.

RulingByLowerCourt	The Judgment of the Court was delivered by <NAME>, J.-This is an appeal by the State of Maharashtra against the judgment of <ORG> at Nagpur confirming the decree of the Additional District Judge, Nagpur, declaring that the order reverting the respondent from the rank of officiating Deputy Superintendent of Police to the rank of Inspector of Police, was illegal and void, and granting certain consequential reliefs.
RulingByLowerCourt	The judgment of <ORG> and the learned Additional District Judge Seem to us to be cle arly unsustainable.
RulingByLowerCourt	The Courts below held that the respondent had been reduced in rank in violation of the terms of s. 240(3) of the Government of India Act, 1935, which corresponds to Art.
RulingByLowerCourt	311 of the Constitution, inasmuch as he was not given an opportunity to show cause against the order proposed to be made.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is not in dispute that the opportunity has not been given.
RatioOfTheDecision	In our view, however, for reasons to be presently stated, the respondent was not entitled to that opportunity.
Fact	On June 8, 1948, the respondent was holding the post of Inspector in <ORG>.
Fact	He was appointed to officiate as Deputy Superintendent of Police with effect from June 9,1948.
Fact	On January 27, 1949, his services were lent to <ORG> in connection with the police action then being taken there.
Fact	On February 5, 1949, he was sent back to the central Provinces and Berar.
Fact	On February 19, 1949, the Inspector General of Police, <ORG>, passed an order which reads as fol lows:  "<NAME> (respondent) Deputy Superintendent Police, Parbhani, is reverted to rank of Inspector."
Fact	It is this order which was sought to be impugned by the respondent in the suit out of which this appeal arises.
Fact	95  After the order of reversion had been made the respondent, on February 23, 1949, asked for the reason for which he was reverted.
Fact	On March 3, 1949, the Government refused to communicate the reasons to him.
Fact	On May 25, 1949, a confidential memorandum was sent by the District Superintendent of Police, Parbhani, to the Deputy Inspector General of Police, Aurangabad, in which he stated that he had conducted an inquiry into certain allegations of corruption made against the respondent while he was acting in the service of <ORG> at Parbhani and he thought that those allegations were of substance.
Fact	Thereupon, the Deputy Inspector General of Police, Aurangabad, held a departmental inquiry regarding these allegations and found that they had not been proved.
Fact	This inquiry had  been held behind the back of the respondent.
Fact	Notwithstanding this, the order reverting the respondent was maintained.
Fact	There is a letter addressed by the Inspector General of Police to the Chief Secretary to <ORG>, dated August 19, 1950, written after the departmental inquiry wherein it is stated that the respondents previous record was not satisfactory and that he had been promoted to officiate as Deputy Superintendent of Police as the Government was in need of officers and that he had been given a chance in the expectation that he would turn a new leaf but the complaint made in the confidential memorandum was a clear proof that the officer was habitually dishonest and did not deserve promotion.
Argument	The respondent made representations to the Government to revise the order reverting him to the lower rank but the Government expressed its inability to do so.
Precedent	It may be stated here that on the promulgation of the Constitution the central Provinces and Berar became the State of Madhya Prades h in <ORG>.
Precedent	In the judgment under appeal <ORG> followed its earlier decision in <NAME> v. State of Madhya Pradesh (1) in which it had been held that if a person officiating in a higher post is reverted to his original post in the normal course, that is, on account of the cessation of the vacancy or his failure to acquire the required qualification, the reversion does not amount to a reduction in rank but if he is reverted for unsatisfactory work, then the reversion amounts to reduction in rank.
RatioOfTheDecision	<ORG> held that the Government's plea that the respondent had been promoted as there was dearth of officers was an afterthought and that the fact that the respondent had been given a chance to officiate in the higher post prima facie showed that he was fit to hold that post.
RatioOfTheDecision	<ORG> also held that the Government's refusal to communicate to the respondent the reasons for his reversion or to give him the report of the inquiry, indicated that the Government was reverting him on t he ground that his work was not satisfactory.
RatioOfTheDecision	It, therefore, came to the conclusion on the authority of <NAME> case (1) that the respondent must be held to have reduced in rank and this reduction in rank was illegal as the respondent had not been given an opportunity to show cause against it.
RatioOfTheDecision	We are unable to agree with the observation in <NAME> case(1) that when a person officiating in a post, is reverted for unsatisfactory work, that reversion amounts to a reduction in rank.
RatioOfTheDecision	A person officiating in a post has no right to hold it for all times.
RatioOfTheDecision	He may have been given the officiating post because the permanent incumbent was not available, having gone on leave or being away for some other reasons.
Precedent	When the permanent incumbent comes back, the person officiating is naturally reverted to his original post.
Precedent	This is no reduction in rank for it was the very term on which he had been given the officiating post.
Precedent	Again, sometimes a person is given an officiating post to test his suitability to be made  permanent in it later.
Precedent	Here again.
Precedent	it is an implied term of the officiating appointment that if he is found unsuitable, he would have to go back.
Precedent	If, therefore, the appropriate authorities find him unsuitable for the higher rank and then revert him back to his original lower rank, the action taken is in accordance with the terms on which the officiating post had been given.
Precedent	It is in no way a punishment and is not, therefore, a reduction in rank.
Precedent	It has been held by this <ORG> in <CITATION_1270113> (1) that, "It is, therefore, quite clear that appointment to a permanent post in a <ORG> service, either on probation, or on an officiating basis, from the very nature of such employment, itself of a very transitory character and, in the absence of any special contract or specific rule regulating the conditions of the service, the implied term of such appointment, under the ordinary law of master and servant, is that it is terminable at any time.
Precedent	In short, in the case of an appointment to a permanent post in a  Government service on probation or on an officiating basis, the servant so appointed does not acquire any substantive right to the post and consequently cannot complain, any more than a private servant employed on probation or on an officiating basis can do, if his service is terminated at any time."
RatioOfTheDecision	The respondent had of course no right to the post of Deputy Superintendent of Police to which he had been given an officiating appointment and he does not contend to the contrary.
RatioOfTheDecision	He cannot therefore, without more, complain if he is sent back to his original post.
RatioOfTheDecision	This is what happened in this case even if it be taken that the respondent had been reverted to his original rank because he was found unsuitable for the higher rank to which he had been given an officiating appointment.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is however true that even an officiating person may be reverted to his original rank by way of punishment.
Precedent	It was therefore, observed in <NAME>'s case (1) at p. 863, "Thus if the order entails or provides for the forfeiture of his  pay or allowances or the loss of his seniority in his substantive rank or the stoppage or postponement of his future chances of promotion, then that circumstances may indicate that although in form the Government had purported to exercise its right to terminate the employment or to reduce the servant to a lower rank under the terms of the contract of employment or under the rules, in truth and reality the Government has terminated the employment as and by way of penalty."
RatioOfTheDecision	It is quite clear that the circumstances mentioned in this observation have not occurred in the present case.
RatioOfTheDecision	The reversion has not in any way affected the respondent so far as his condition and prospect of service are concerned.
RatioOfTheDecision	He of course, lost the benefit of the appointment to the higher rank but that by itself cannot indicate that the reversion was by way of punishment because he had no right to continue in the higher post or to the benefits arising from it.
RatioOfTheDecision	He had been reverted in exercise of a right which the Government had uncle t he terms of the officiating employment.
RatioOfTheDecision	<ORG> seems to us to have been in error in thinking that the Government's refusal to supply the respondent with the reasons why action has taken against him proved that the reversion was a reduction in rank by way of.
RatioOfTheDecision	punishment; the refusal cannot prove that.
Precedent	It may give rise to a suspicion about the motive which led the Government to take the action, but it is now firmly established that if the action is justifiable under the terms of the employment, then the motive inducing the action is irrelevant in deciding the question whether the action had been taken by way of punishment: see <NAME> case (1) at p. 862.
RatioOfTheDecision	It does not require to be repeated now that unless the reversion is by way of punishment, s. 240 (3) is not attracted.
RatioOfTheDecision	<ORG> seems to have been in error also in drawing an inference from the holding of the departmental inquiry that the respondent must have been reduced in rank by way of punishment.
RatioOfTheDecision	The departmental inquiry was  held long after the order reverting the respondent had been passed and could not have been the occasion for the reversion of the respondent.
RatioOfTheDecision	The Government had the right to consider the suitability of the respondent to hold the position to which he had been appointed to officiate.
RatioOfTheDecision	It was entitled for that purpose to make inquiries about his suitability.
RatioOfTheDecision	This is all that the Government did in this case.
RatioOfTheDecision	This inquiry cannot show, whatever the findings may have been, that the reversion earlier made was by way of punishment.
Precedent	Mr. <NAME> for the respondent referred us to <CITATION_32468> (2) in which it was observed, "But, if instead of terminating such a person's service without any enquiry, the employer chooses to hold an enquiry into his alleged misconduct, or inefficiency, or for or some similar reason; the termination of service is by way of punishment, because it puts a stigma on his competence and thus affect his future career."
Precedent	That case dealt with the discharge of a probationer officer on the ground tha t he was unsuitable.
Precedent	The observation there made was considered by this <ORG> in the later case of <CITATION_890488> (1) where it was said, "The third proposition in the latter case refers to an enquiry into allegations of misconduct or inefficiency with a view, if they were found established, to imposing punishment and not to an enquiry whether a probationer should be confirmed."
RulingByPresentCourt	We would repeat that in the present case the enquiry was concerned with ascertaining the suitability of the respondent for the higher rank and was not a punishment.
RulingByPresentCourt	At one stage Mr. <NAME> was inclined to argue that the enquiry was really a part of the original order of reversion and that it had been deliberately postponed to as to avoid the applicability of s. 240(3) of the Government of India Act, 1935 No such case is made in the plaint.
RulingByPresentCourt	Neither was it made in the courts below nor can it be based on their findings.
RulingByPresentCourt	Such a case cannot now be made.
RulingByPresentCourt	We think, therefore, that the appeal must be allowed with costs throughout and we o rder accordingly.
RulingByPresentCourt	Appeal allowed.
