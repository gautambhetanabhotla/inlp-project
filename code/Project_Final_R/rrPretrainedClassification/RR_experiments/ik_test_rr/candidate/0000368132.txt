Fact	SINHA , J :  Appellant insurer is before us questioning the correctness or otherwise of a judgment and order dated 10.09.1999 passed by a Division Bench of <ORG> in CIMA 21 of 1998 allowing the appeal preferred by the respondent herein from a judgment and order dated 16.12.1997 passed by <ORG>.
Fact	Respondent herein carries on business in jewellery It obtained a policy known as 'Jeweller Block Policy'.
Fact	A theft of 140 gms of jewellery worth of Rs.63,000/- occurred in his business premises.
Fact	A First Information Report was lodged therefor.
Fact	Respondent also lodged a claim with the appellant herein.
Fact	The same having not been settled for a long time, an application was filed before <ORG> constituted under  the J & K Consumers Protection Act, 1987.
Fact	The question raised before the <ORG> was as to whether the loss in question was covered by the insurance policy.
Fact	Appellant contended that the claim of the respondent is covered by an exclusion clause contained in the policy, which reads as under :  "8.
Fact	Loss or damage occasioned by theft or dishonesty or any attempt there at committed by or where such loss or damage has been expedited or in any way sustained or brought about by :  (a) any of the insured's family members;  (b) any servant or traveler or messenger in the exclusive employment of the insured;  (c) any customer or broker or their customer or angadias or cutters or goldsmiths in respect of the property hereby insured entrusted to them by the insured his or their servants or agents."
Precedent	The <ORG> by reason of its order dated 16.12.1997, inter alia, found the said claim to be not sustainable on the ground that the loss was not covered by the said policy, stating :  "It appears to us that while prepar ing the insurance agreement the insurer was aware of the fact that this could be the easiest way for any Jeweller to raise claims against the insurance companies and that is why this clause has been deliberately introduced and theft by customer if any has been put in exclusive clause of the policy"  <ORG>, as indicated hereinbefore, by reason of its judgment and order dated 10.09.1999, did not agree therewith.
Precedent	It was held :  "What is meant by the term 'entrustment' does handing over of jewellery to a customer amounts to entrustment.
Precedent	The dictionary meaning of the word 'entrust' would be to give to another for care, protection or to commit something trustfully or plays trust upon a person'.
Precedent	If a customer enters the premises of a shopkeeper and examines some movable property and takes away the same, then there hardly arises any occasion for entrustment to such a customer.

RatioOfTheDecision	There was no entrustment on the part  of the owner to the customer.
RatioOfTheDecision	The act of removal of the goods by the customer is nothing but a plain theft.
RatioOfTheDecision	This is a dishonest taking and removing the property by the customer with the intent of permanently depriving the owner."
RatioOfTheDecision	A short question which, thus, arises for our consideration is as to whether clause 8 of the policy is applicable to the facts and circumstances of the present case.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is not in dispute that an insurance cover against theft was granted by the appellant.
RatioOfTheDecision	The insurance policy, thus, covered the risk of theft also.
RatioOfTheDecision	An insurer determines the extent of its risk.
RatioOfTheDecision	It floats the policy knowing fully well the risk it seeks to cover.
RatioOfTheDecision	Having regard to the determination of the risk only he fixes the quantum of premium.
RatioOfTheDecision	The insured while entering into a contract of insurance must precisely know the extent of his cover so that he may take out additional insurance if it is so required.
RatioOfTheDecision	However, there may be an express clause excluding the applicability of insurance cover.
RatioOfTheDecision	Wherever such exclusio nary clause is contained in a policy, it would be for the insurer to show that the case falls within the purview thereof.
RatioOfTheDecision	In a case of ambiguity, it is trite, the contract of insurance shall be construed in favour of the insured.
Precedent	[ <CITATION_1739214> ], <CITATION_95274> .
Precedent	[2006 (14) SCALE 300] and <CITATION_705032> [].
RatioOfTheDecision	Clause 8 of the contract of insurance would be attracted only where the offences specified therein are committed by any of the persons mentioned therein.
RatioOfTheDecision	For defeating the claim of the respondent, it was, thus, obligatory on the part of the appellant to establish that the conditions prescribed therein were satisfied.
RatioOfTheDecision	Keeping in view the aforementioned legal aspect of the matter, we may advert to the meaning of the word 'entrust'.
RatioOfTheDecision	Its ordinary meaning, would mean "to charge or invest with a trust; to commit to another with a certain confidence regarding his care" [See Advanced Law Lexicon by <NAME>  3rd Edn.
RatioOfTheDecision	Book 2  page1613].
RatioOfTheDecision	It requires no elaboration that offences of 'breach  of trust' and 'theft' contain different ingredients.
Precedent	Whereas theft has been defined in Section 378 of the Indian Penal Code; breach of trust has been defined in Section 405 thereof, which read as under :  "378.
Precedent	Theft.- Whoever, intending to take dishonestly any moveable property out of the possession of any person without that person's consent, moves that property in order to such taking, is said to commit theft."
Precedent	Illustration (d) appended thereto reads as under :  "(d) A, being <NAME>'s servant, and entrusted by <NAME> with the care of <NAME>'s plate, dishonestly runs away with the plate, without <NAME>'s consent.
Precedent	A has committed theft."
Precedent	"405.
Precedent	Criminal breach of trust.-Whoever, being in any manner entrusted with property, or with any dominion over property, dishonestly misappropriates or converts to his own use that property, or dishonestly uses or disposes of that property in violation of any direction of law prescribing the mode in which such trust is to be discharged, or of any legal contract, express or implied, which he  has made touching the discharge of such trust, or willfully suffers any other person so to do, commits "criminal breach of trust""  The word 'entrust' would imply giving responsibility to a person upon whom the owner has confidence.
RatioOfTheDecision	It envisages establishment of a relationship.
RatioOfTheDecision	When a customer enters into a jewellery shop, as of necessity, the owner or his agent must allow him to inspect the merchandise, the customer intends to purchase.
RatioOfTheDecision	For the said purpose possession in the legal sense is not handed over.
RatioOfTheDecision	The owner or his agent does not loose complete control thereover.
RatioOfTheDecision	For the purpose of arriving at a conclusion as to whether the exclusion clause is attracted or not, loss or damage must be occasioned, inter alia, by a customer in respect of the property entrusted to him.
RatioOfTheDecision	The word 'customer' contained in clause 8 (c) of the Insurance Policy must be read ejusdem generis.
RatioOfTheDecision	A customer contemplated thereunder must have to be one who would be a man of trust.
RatioOfTheDecision	If a customer is not a man of trust or the property h ad not been entrusted to him, the exclusion clause would not apply.
RatioOfTheDecision	The customer who committed theft of jewellery was an unknown person.
RatioOfTheDecision	It was so categorically stated in the First Information Report.
RatioOfTheDecision	There was, thus, no occasion for the respondent to entrust the jewellery to him.

RatioOfTheDecision	The person must be handed over the possession of the property.
RatioOfTheDecision	Illustration (d) appended to Section 378  envisages a situation of this nature.
RatioOfTheDecision	It by no stretch of imagination would have contemplated a situation where an unknown customer would have committed theft.
