Statute	The legislative powers of the <ORG> and <ORG>s are governed by Article 246 to 255 of Part II of the Constitution.
Statute	Article 246 reads as follows:-  "Subject-matter of laws made by <ORG> and by the Legislature of <ORG>s.
Statute	 (1) Notwithstanding anything in clauses (2) and (3), <ORG> has exclusive power to make laws with respect to any of the matters enumerated in List I in the Seventh Schedule (in this Constitution referred to as the "Union List") .
Statute	(2) Notwithstanding anything in clause (3), <ORG>, and, subject to clause (1), the <ORG> of any state also, have power to make laws with respect to any of the matters enumerated in List III in the Seventh Schedule (in this Constitution referred to as the "Concurrent List").
Statute	(3) Subject to clauses (1) and (2), the <ORG> of any State has exclusive power to make laws for such State or any part thereof with respect to any of the matters enumerated in List II in the Seventh Schedule (in this Constitution referred to as the "State List").
Statute	(4) <ORG> has power to make laws with respect to any matter for any part of the territory of India not included [in a State] notwithstanding that such matter is a matter enumerated in the State List. "
Statute	The <ORG> has exclusive power to legislate with respect to any of the matters enumerated in List I, notwithstanding anything contained in clauses (2) and (3) of Article 246.
RatioOfTheDecision	The non-obstante clause under Article 246(1) indicates the predominance or su premacy of the law made by the Union legislature in the event of an overlap of the law made by <ORG> with respect to a matter enumerated in List I and a law made by the State legislature with respect to a matter enumerated in List II of the Seventh Schedule.
RatioOfTheDecision	There is no doubt that both <ORG> and the State legislature are supreme in their respective assigned fields.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is the duty of the <ORG> to interpret the legislations made by the <ORG> and the State legislature in such a manner as to avoid any conflict.
RatioOfTheDecision	However, if the conflict is unavoidable, and the two enactments are irreconcilable, then by the force of the non-onbstante clause in Clause (1) of Article 246 , the <ORG>ary legislation would prevail notwithstanding the exclusive power of the State legislature to make a law with respect to a matter enumerated in the State List.
RatioOfTheDecision	With respect to matters enumerated in the List III (Concurrent List), both the <ORG> and the State legislature have equal competence to legislate.
RatioOfTheDecision	Here ag ain, the courts are charged with the duty of interpreting the enactments of <ORG> and the <ORG> legislature in such manner as to avoid a conflict.
RatioOfTheDecision	If the conflict becomes unavoidable, then Article 245 indicates the manner of resolution of such a conflict.
RatioOfTheDecision	Thus, the question of repugnancy between the <ORG>ary legislation and the <ORG> legislation can arise in two ways.
RatioOfTheDecision	First, where the legislations, though enacted with respect to matters in their allotted sphere, overlap and conflict.
RatioOfTheDecision	Second, where the two legislations are with respect to matters in Concurrent List and there is a conflict.
RatioOfTheDecision	In both the situations, <ORG>ary legislation will predominate, in the first, by virtue of the non-obstante clause in Article 246(1 ), in the second, by reason of Article 245(1).
RatioOfTheDecision	Clause (2) of Article 245 deals with a situation where the <ORG> legislation having been reserved and having obtained President's ascent prevails in that <ORG>; this again is subject to the proviso that the <ORG> can again bring  a legislation to override even such State legislation.
