Fact	1.
Fact	These sixteen appeals arise out of as many applications presented by the different appellants under section 491 of the Criminal Procedure Code, complaining of illegal detention under section 2 (1) (a) of the Bihar Maintenance of Public Order Ordinance, 1949.
Fact	2.
Fact	The appellants were originally arrested under the Bihar Maintenance of Public Order Act, 1947.
Fact	That Act, which received the assent of the Governor-General on 15th of March, 1947, was to remain operative under section 1 (3) of the Act for a period of one year only from the date of its commencement, subject to a proviso engrafted upon the sub- section itself, which empowered <ORG> to extend it, with or without modifications, for a further period of one year, by means of a notification, on a resolution being passed to that effect by <ORG> and agreed to by <ORG>.
Fact	On the 11th of March, 1948, <ORG>, in exercise of their powers under  the proviso mentioned above, extended the application of the Act for a further period of one year from 15th March, 1948, and it was during this extended period that the orders for arrest and detention against the appellants were initially made.
Fact	3.
Fact	The competency of the provincial Government to extend the application of the Act by a notification, in the manner laid down in the proviso to section 1 (3) of the Act, was challenged in a number of analogous cases which came up to this court in May, 1949, <NAME> v. <ORG> and others (1).
Fact	By a judgment delivered on 28th May, 1949, this court held that the proviso to section 1 (3) of the Bihar Maintenance of Public Order Act was ultra vires <ORG> as it amounted to a delegation of legislative function to an outside authority and consequently the extension of the operation of the act beyond the period of one year originally fixed was void and inoperative.
Fact	It was further held that the Bihar Act V of 1949, which the Bihar L egislature had passed in the meantime, and which purported to amend the provision of section 1 (3) of the earlier Act was also invalid in law inasmuch as the enactment which it purported to amend was not legally in existence when this amending Act was passed.
Fact	In view of the pronouncements of this court, the Governor of Bihar promulgated an Ordinance on the model of the Maintenance of the Public Order Act, on 3rd June, 1949, by which provisions were made inter alia for preventive detention in connection with public safety and maintenance of order in the Province of Bihar.
Fact	On 21st June, 1949, this Ordinance was declared void and in operative by <ORG> on the ground that as <ORG>, though not actually sitting, was neither prorogued nor dissolved, the Governor could not promulgate an Ordinance under section 88(1) of the Government of India Act.
Fact	On 22nd June following, a fresh Ordinance was passed, which is Ordinance No.
Fact	IV of 1949 and which re-enacted in substance th e provisions of the earlier Ordinance..
Fact	Under this Ordinance fresh orders of detention were passed and served on all the appellants.
Fact	The appellants, along with other detenus filed applications before <ORG> under section 491 Criminal Procedure Code challenging the validity of the Ordinance itself and the propriety of the detention orders, made under it, on various grounds.
Fact	The applications were heard in different batches by different Benches of <ORG>.
Fact	There are altogether ten judgments which have been challenged in the appeals before us, the principals one being that of <NAME> and <NAME>, JJ.
Fact	dated the 12th July, 1949.
Fact	<ORG> rejected the applications of all the appellants but granted certificates in each of the cases under section 205(1) of the Government of India Act.
Fact	On the strength of these certificates, these sixteen appeals have come up to this court.
Fact	4.
Fact	When the appeals were called on for hearing, the appellants appeared in person and were not represented by an y lawyer.

Argument	5.
Argument	The main point convassed before us in support of these appeals is that the Ordinance, under which the appellants are detained, has been promulgated by the Governor in contravention of the provisions of section 88 of the Government of India Act, and consequently it is void and inoperative and any detention order passed under it must be held to be illegal.
Argument	6.
Statute	Section 88 of the Government of India Act runs as follows :  "(1) If at any time when the Legislature of a Province is not in session the Governor is satisfied that circumstances exist which render it necessary for him to take immediate action, he may promulgate such Ordinance as the circumstances appear to him to require.
Statute	Provided that the Governor shall not, without instructions from the Governor General, promulgate any such  Ordinance if any Act of the provincial <ORG> containing the same provisions would, under this Act, have been invalid unless, having been reserved for the consideration of the Governor General, it had received assent of the Governor General."
RatioOfTheDecision	7.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is admitted that the Bihar <ORG> was not in session when this Ordinance was passed.
RatioOfTheDecision	It was urged, however, in the court below, and the argument was repeated before us, that no circumstance existed as is contemplated by section 88(1) which could justify the governor in promulgating this Ordinance.
RatioOfTheDecision	This obviously is a matter which is not within the competence of courts to investigate.
RatioOfTheDecision	The language of the section shows clearly that it is the Governor and the Governor alone who has got to satisfy himself as to the existence of circumstances necessitating the promulgation of an Ordinance.
RatioOfTheDecision	The existence of such necessity is not a justiciable matter which the courts could be called upon to determine by applying an objective test.
RatioOfTheDecision	It may be noted here that unde r the Government of India Act the Governor-General has powers to make Ordinances in case of emergency (vide section 42 of the Government of India and section 72 of Schedule IX which is now omitted); and it was held by privy <ORG> in King Emperor v. <NAME> and <NAME> v. The King Emperor that the emergency which calls for immediate action has to be judged by the Governor- General alone.
RatioOfTheDecision	On promulgating an Ordinance, the Governor-General is not bound as a matter of law to expound reasons therefore, nor is he bound to prove affirmatively in a court of law that a state of emergency did actually exist.
RatioOfTheDecision	The language of section 88 postulates only one condition, namely, the satisfaction of the Governor as to the existence of justifying circumstances, and the preamble to the Ordinance expresses in clear terms that this condition has been fulfilled.
RatioOfTheDecision	The first contention of the appellants must therefore be rejected.
RatioOfTheDecision	8.
RatioOfTheDecision	The next and the more serious contention raised by the appellants is that the Ordinance in  question contains provisions which, if they were contained in an Act of <ORG>, would have been invalid without the assent of the Governor-General.
RatioOfTheDecision	In these circumstances, it is argued, that under section 88(1) of the Government of India Act, previous instructions of the governor General were necessary to validate this Ordinance and as admittedly there were no such instructions in the present case, the ordinance must be deemed to be one which is beyond the competence of the Governor to promulgate.
RatioOfTheDecision	To appreciate this contention of the appellants, it would be necessary to advert to a few material provisions of the Constitution Act .
RatioOfTheDecision	Under sections 100(1) of the Government of India Act, <ORG> is incapable of legislating on any of the matters enumerated in List 1 of the Seventh Schedule to the Act which has been described as the Federal Legislative List and which is reserved exclusively for the dominion <ORG>.
Statute	Sub-section (3) of the section empowers <ORG> egislature to make laws for a province or any part thereof with respect to any of the matters enumerated in List II (the Provincial Legislative List), and <ORG> is forbidden to legislate on such matters for any province or part of a province.
RatioOfTheDecision	As regards other parts of India which is outside the provinces as defined in the Act, <ORG> has been given the authority under sub-section (4) of the section to legislate on any matter specified in the Provincial List.
RatioOfTheDecision	Sub-section (2) of section 100 deals with what has been called the Concurrent Legislative List, and both <ORG> and the provincial <ORG> can legislate upon the items enumerated therein.
RatioOfTheDecision	When both the <ORG>s can operate on the same field, conflict is likely to arise and section 107 of the Government of India Act lays down rules as to how repugnancy is to be avoided when it arises between Provincial and Dominion Legislation.
Statute	Section 107 of the constitution Act is in these terms :  "(1) If an y provision of a Provincial Law is repugnant to any provision of Dominion law which <ORG> is competent to enact or to any provision of an existing law with respect to one of the matters enumerated in the Concurrent Legislative List then, subject to the provisions of the sections, the Dominion law, whether pass before or after the Provincial law, or, as the case may be, the existing law, shall prevail and the provincial law shall, to the extent of the repugnancy, be void.
Statute	(2) Where a Provincial law with respect to one of the matters enumerated in the Concurrent Legislative List contains any provision repugnant to the provisions of an earlier Dominion law or an existing law with respect to that matter, then, if the Provincial law having been reserved for the consideration of the Governor-General have received the assent of the Governor-General, the Provincial law shall in that Province prevail, but nevertheless <ORG> may at any time enact further legislation with respect t o the same matter :  Provided that no Bill or amendment for making any provision repugnant to any Provincial law, which, having been so reserved, has received the assent of the Governor-General, shall be introduced or moved in <ORG> without the previous sanction of the Governor-General."
RatioOfTheDecision	9.
RatioOfTheDecision	The contention of the appellants is that the impugned Ordinance is in conflict with certain provisions of the Criminal Procedure Code and has created new offences for the first time, and these matters are clearly included under Items (1) and (2) of the Concurrent Legislative List.
RatioOfTheDecision	Had this been an Act of <ORG>, there would have been a conflict between these provisions and those of the existing law contained in Criminal Procedure Code and other Acts and under sub-section (2) of section 107 , the assent of the Governor- General would have been necessary to validate such provisions.
RatioOfTheDecision	Under the proviso to section 88(1) of the Government of India Act, therefore, the Governor could not pro mulgate such Ordinance without instructions from the Governor-General.
RatioOfTheDecision	The contention does not appear to us to be sound.
RatioOfTheDecision	In order to succeed in their contention and attract the operation of section 107 , read with section 88(1) of the Government of India Act, two things have got to be established by the appellants.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the first place, the provisions of the impugned Ordinance and those of an existing law must be in respect of the same subject-matter and that subject-matter must be covered by one of the items in the Concurrent List.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the second place, there must be repugnancy between the two provisions.
RatioOfTheDecision	10.
RatioOfTheDecision	This leads us to enquire first of all whether the impugned Ordinance is in respect to matters enumerated in the <ORG> or in the Concurrent List?
RatioOfTheDecision	It is settled by the decision of <ORG> in <NAME> v. <NAME> (4) that when the province acts solely within its power under the <ORG> List without relying on any power conferred by the Concurrent List, no question of repugnancy under  section 107 of the Government of India Act would arise.
RatioOfTheDecision	The point raised in that case was whether the Punjab Restitution of Mortgaged Lands Act (Act IV of 1938) was void under section 107(1) of the Government of India Act to the extent that it conflicted with certain provisions of the Civil Procedure Code and other existing Indian law.
RatioOfTheDecision	What that statute enacted, in substance, was to set aside the normal procedure for redemption in the case of mortgages of land with possession and empower the Collector, on an application by the mortgagor, to extinguish the mortgage in certain circumstances or declare it extinguished and restore possession.
RatioOfTheDecision	The matter came up on appeal to this court and this court held that the Act was not void inasmuch as there was no repugnancy between its provisions and those of the Civil Procedure Code or the Indian Contract Act by reason of the exceptions expressly provided for in the latter enactments.
RatioOfTheDecision	Against this judgment there was an appeal taken to <ORG> and their Lordship s of <ORG> affirmed the decision of <ORG> but on different grounds.
Precedent	Their Lordships held that the impugned Act was by its very language confined exclusively to agricultural lands, and mortgage of agricultural land as well as the procedure for its enforcement were wholly within the competence of <ORG> being covered by Items Nos.
Precedent	(21) and (2) of List II.
RatioOfTheDecision	As agricultural land was expressly excluded from Items (7), (8) and (10) of List III, the whole of the Act fell within the powers given to the province by the Provincial List without any necessity to invoke powers from the Concurrent List.
RatioOfTheDecision	In these circumstances, no question of repugnancy under section 107 of the Constitution Act did at all arise or fall for consideration.
RatioOfTheDecision	It will be seen that while this Court based its decision in <NAME>'s case on the ground that there was no real conflict between the provisions of the impugned Act and those of any existing Indian law and kept open the question as to whether t he impugned Act extended to property other than agricultural lands, <ORG> held definitely that the Act related exclusively to agricultural lands and that the question of repugnancy was not material at all.
RatioOfTheDecision	Keeping in view this pronouncement of <ORG>, it seems to us that the Province of Bihar is right in their contention that the matters dealt with by the impugned Ordinance fall entirely within Items (1) and (2) of the Provincial List and that there has been no legislation on any item in the concurrent List.
RatioOfTheDecision	11.
Statute	Items (1) and (2) of the Provincial List are worded as follows :  "1.
Statute	Public Order but not including the use of his Majesty's naval, military or air forces in aid of the civil power; the administration of justice, constitution and organisation of all courts, except <ORG>, and fees taken therein; preventive detention for reasons connected with the maintenance or public order; persons subjected to such detention.
RatioOfTheDecision	2.
RatioOfTheDecision	Jurisdiction and powers of all courts exc ept <ORG>, with respect to any of the matters in this list; procedure in <ORG>."
RatioOfTheDecision	12.
RatioOfTheDecision	The expression "Public Order" with which the first item begins is, in our opinion, a most comprehensive term and it clearly indicates the scope or ambit of the subject in respect to which powers of legislation are given to the province.
Statute	Maintenance of public order within a province is primarily the concern of that province and subject to certain exceptions which involve the use of His Majesty's forces in aid of civil power, <ORG> is given plenary authority to legislate on all matters which relate to or are necessary for maintenance of public order.
Statute	Preventive detention for reasons connected with the maintenance of public order and persons subjected to such detention are expressly mentioned as being included in this item, whereas preventive detention for reasons of <ORG> and connected with <ORG>, <ORG> and relations with acceding <ORG>s have been placed separatel y under Item No.
Statute	(1) of the Federal List.
Statute	13.
Statute	Looking now to the specific provisions of the Ordinance we see that the preamble states in clear words that the Governor is satisfied that the circumstances exist which render it necessary for him to take immediate action and provide for preventive detention, imposition of collective fines and control of meetings and processions etc.
Statute	in connection with public safety and maintenance of public order in the Province of Bihar.
Statute	Section 2 gives power to <ORG> to make orders restricting the movements or action of or detaining a certain person with a view to preventing him from acting in any manner prejudicial to public safety and he maintenance of public order.
Statute	Section 3 specifies the duration of the order made under section 2 .
Statute	Section 4 lays down that the grounds of the order of detention are to be disclosed to the person affected by the order and the latter would have a right of representation by way of reply to the allegations made against him.
Statute	T he grounds and the representation are then to be placed before any Advisory council constituted in a particular manner and on the report of <ORG>, the final order is to be passed by the provincial Government.
Statute	Section 5 provides for imposition of collective fines on the inhabitants of any area who are concerned in commission of offences affecting public safety or public order.
Statute	Sub- section (5) of this section lays down that the portion of fine payable by person may be recovered from him in the manner provided for in section 386 , Criminal Procedure Code or <ORG> may make special rules for the purpose.
Statute	Section 6 relates to control of processions and meetings and sections 7 and 8 to imposition of Press censorship and control of documents printed outside the province.
Statute	Section 9 deals with requisitioning of property and sections 10 and 11 with unlawful drilling and use of unofficial uniforms.
Statute	Under Section 12 , an officer authorised in that behalf may require the assistance of an y male person in any area to assist him in the maintenance of law and order.
Statute	Under section 13 any place can be declared to be a protected place and under section 15 orders may be passed for controlling or regulating the admission of persons to and the conduct of persons in and in the vicinity of such place.
Statute	It is to be noted that violation of the orders passed under all the above sections are made criminal offences for which certain punishments have been provided.
Statute	The other material sections of the Ordinance are sections 21 to 24 .
Statute	Under section 21 any police officer may arrest without without warrant any person who is reasonably suspected of having committed an offence punishable under the Ordinance.
Statute	Section 22 lays down how cognizance has to be taken of any alleged contravention of the provisions of the Ordinance and sub-section (2) provides that any Magistrate or bench of Magistrates who are empowered to try cases summarily under section 160 (1) of the Code of Criminal Procedure could try any contravention  of the provisions of the Ordinance or any, order passed under it as <ORG> may be a notification direct.
Statute	Section 23 repeals the Bihar Maintenance of Public Order Act 1947, the Amending Act V of 1949 and also the previous Ordinance passed on 3rd June 1949.
Statute	Section 24 saves all proceedings commenced or acts done under the earlier Ordinance.
RatioOfTheDecision	14.
RatioOfTheDecision	Thus all the provisions of the Ordinance relate to or are concerned primarily with the maintenance of public order in the Province of Bihar and provide for preventive detention and similar other measures in connection with the same.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is true that violation of the provisions of the Ordinance or of orders passed under it have been made criminal offences but offences against laws with respect to matters specified in List II would come within Item (37) of List II itself, and have been expressly excluded from Item (1) of the Concurrent List.
RatioOfTheDecision	The ancillary matters laying down the procedure for trial of such offences and the conferring of jurisdiction on  certain courts for that purpose would be covered completely by Item (2) of List II and it is not necessary for <ORG> to invoke the powers under Item (2) of the Concurrent List.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is argued on behalf of the appellants that the provision of section 21 of the Ordinance which empowers any police officer to arrest without warrant any person suspected of having committed any offence punishable under the Ordinance really amounts to legislation on Criminal Procedure and conflicts with the provision of section 54 of the Criminal Procedure Code.
RatioOfTheDecision	In our opinion, if <ORG> can create offences in respect to matters which are exclusively within List II, it can also provide for arrest and trial of the offenders who violate such laws.
RatioOfTheDecision	This seems to be the clear implication of Item (37) of List II and Item (1) of the Concurrent List.
RatioOfTheDecision	15.
RatioOfTheDecision	It was laid down by this court in <ORG> <NAME> (5) that "none of the items in the lists is to be read in a narrow or re stricted sense and each general word should be held to extend to all ancillary or subsidiary matters which can fairly and reasonably be said to be comprehended in it."
RatioOfTheDecision	The three legislative lists in the Constitution Act are not always mutually exclusive.
RatioOfTheDecision	As <ORG> observed in a recent case <NAME> v. <ORG>, (6) "the overlapping of subject-matter is not avoided by substituting three lists for two, or even by arranging for a hierarchy of jurisdiction.
RatioOfTheDecision	Subjects must still overlap and when they do, the question must be asked what is pith and substance is the effect of the enactment of which complaint is made and in what list is its true nature and character to be found."
RatioOfTheDecision	To ascertain the class to which a particular enactment really belongs, we are to look to the primary matter dealt with by it, its subject-matter and essential legislative feature.
RatioOfTheDecision	Once the true nature and character of a legislation determine its place in a particular list, the fact that it deals incident ally with matters appertaining to other lists is immaterial.
RatioOfTheDecision	<ORG> made it perfectly clear in the case mentioned above that the extent of invasion by a Provincial Act into subjects enumerated in other lists is an important matter not because the validity of an Act can be determined by discriminating between degrees of invasions but for determining what is the "pith and substance" of the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	Judged by the test, it can scarcely be argued that the impugned Ordinance is a legislation not on public order or preventive detention for reasons connected with it but on criminal procedure.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is true that detention of a person without a judicial order in a sense goes against the provision of the criminal law but that is the very essence of preventive detention.
RatioOfTheDecision	The Ordinance lays down what in the opinion of the legislative authority is essential for the maintenance of public order in the province.
RatioOfTheDecision	That is the true nature and character of the legislation which unquestionably brings it within Item (1) of  List II.
RatioOfTheDecision	The offence that have been created and the procedure that have been laid down for arrest and trial of the offenders are only ancillary things without which no effective legislation would have been possible.
RatioOfTheDecision	We have, therefore, no hesitation in holding that the Ordinance is covered entirely by Items (1) and (2) of the Provincial List and as for no part of this provisions it is necessary to have recourse to the concurrent powers provided for in List III, the question of repugnancy under section 107(1) of the Government of India Act does not arise at all.
RatioOfTheDecision	16.
RatioOfTheDecision	Even assuming for argument's sake that the provisions relating to arrest without any warrant, by a police officer, of persons suspected of committing offences under the Ordinance as contained in section 21 , or that relating to trial of such persons by Magistrates empowered to try cases summarily, are matters of Criminal Procedure and come within the purview of Item (2) of the Concurrent List, we do not think that it will help the appellants in a ny way.
RatioOfTheDecision	The Concurrent List is not a forbidden field to <ORG> has legislated on any matter in the Concurrent List is not enough to attract the mischief of section 107 of the Government of India Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	There must be repugnancy between such legislation and an existing law prevail unless the procedure laid down in sub-section (2) of section 107 was followed.
RatioOfTheDecision	In our opinion, there is no repugnancy between the provisions of the impugned Ordinance and those of the Criminal Procedure Code .
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 54 of the Criminal Procedure Code does not purport to be exhausted or unqualified and various provisions for arrest without warrant are to be found in other Acts e.g., Police Act , Arms Act , Explosives Act , Indian Railways Act , etc.
RatioOfTheDecision	; and section 1 (2) of the Criminal Procedure Code expressly lays down that the provisions of the Code would not affect any special form of procedure prescribed by any law for the time being in force.
RatioOfTheDecision	The provision of section 21 of the Ordinance cannot be said therefore to  be repugnant to section 54 of the Criminal Procedure Code.
RatioOfTheDecision	Similarly, the investing of certain Magistrates with powers to try offences under the Ordinance does not affect or touch in the least any provision of the Criminal Procedure Code .
RatioOfTheDecision	As there is no repugnancy, section 107 of the Government of India Act cannot have any application to the present case.
RatioOfTheDecision	17.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is then argued by Mr. <NAME>, though somewhat faintheartedly, that the promulgation of the impugned Ordinance is not a bona fide act on the part of the Governor.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is difficult to see on what materials this contention can be seriously pressed.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is to be noted that Bihar Maintenance of Public Order Act, 1947 itself contained a provision under which <ORG>, with the assent of both Houses of legislature, could, by a notification, prolong its life for one year more after the 15th of March, 1948.
RatioOfTheDecision	Then again in March 1949 an Amending Act was passed, by which the original Act was further extended till 31st March 1940.
RatioOfTheDecision	On 28th May 19 49 this court held that the extension of the Act by means of notification of <ORG> as well as the subsequent amending Acts were ultra vires and void.
RatioOfTheDecision	In this circumstances, the Governor of Bihar promulgated an Ordinance on the 3rd of June 1949 which incorporated, in substance, the provisions of the Maintenance of Public Order Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is admitted that at that time the <ORG> was not actually sitting though no formal order of prorogation was made.
RatioOfTheDecision	On this ground the Ordinance was held to be inoperative by <ORG>, and on the very next day after the decision of <ORG> was given, the present Ordinance (which is Ordinance No.
RatioOfTheDecision	IV of 1949) was promulgated.
RatioOfTheDecision	It might have been a mistake on the part of those who advised the Governor, not to take into account the fact that the <ORG> has not been prorogued when the Ordinance of 3rd June 1949 was passed.
RatioOfTheDecision	But it would be idle to suggest that there was anything <NAME> fide or dishonest in the steps that were taken.
RatioOfTheDecision	18.
RatioOfTheDecision	The four th and the last contention raised by Mr. <NAME> is that sections 23 , 24 and the proviso to section 4 (1) of the <ORG> are illegal and ultra vires and as they are not severable from the rest of the <ORG>, the entire <ORG> is bad on account of these offending provisions.
RatioOfTheDecision	19.
RatioOfTheDecision	For the purpose of testing the soundness of his argument, it will be convenient, we think, to examine the three impugned provisions separately.
RatioOfTheDecision	So far as section 23 of the <ORG> is concerned, it purports, to repeal the Bihar Maintenance of public Order Act as extended by the notification of <ORG> and amended by subsequent <ORG> and also the earlier <ORG> passed on 3rd June 1949.
RatioOfTheDecision	All the enactments referred to in the section had been already pronounced to be invalid by competent courts and it is difficult to see why it was necessary to repeal them at all.
RatioOfTheDecision	As a superfluity, the section can certainly be ignored but it will not affect the validity of the <ORG> itself in any way.
RatioOfTheDecision	20.
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 24 is i n the nature of a saving provision and seeks to keep alive all proceedings commenced or orders made or sentences passed under Ordinance II of 1949 which was declared invalid by <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	It was argued that if the Ordinance itself had been pronounced to be illegal and ultra vires, how could anything done under it be regarded as valid.
RatioOfTheDecision	For our present purpose, this question is acedemic, for the detention of any of the appellants before us is not sought to be justified by the provisions of Ordinance II of 1949; and whether or not any order made or proceedings started under the old Ordinance could be kept at all, need not be considered in the present case.
RatioOfTheDecision	The only question to be decided by us is whether, assuming the provision to be bad, it is so inextricably woven in to the scheme of the whole Ordinance and so inseparably connected with its other provisions that this invalid section would make the whole Ordinance invalid.
RatioOfTheDecision	To this question, the answer must certainly be in the negative.
RatioOfTheDecision	There is no ne cessary and inseparable connection between this section and the rest of the Ordinance and none of the material provisions of the latter depend upon it any way.
RatioOfTheDecision	If the section is struck out, the rest of the Ordinance will certainly survive as an effective piece of legislation fulfilling the identical object for which the Ordinance was passed.
RatioOfTheDecision	21.
Argument	The main attack of Mr. <NAME> is, however, directed against the proviso to section 4 (1) of the Ordinance and it was strenuously argued by him that this clause was beyond the capacity of <ORG> to enact and it was not a matter included in any of the items in the Provincial List.
Argument	Now section 4 (1) of the Ordinance runs as follows :  "When an order is made in respect of any person under clause (a) of sub-section (1) of section 2 , as soon as may be after the order is made, the authority making the order shall communicate to the person affected thereby, so far as such communication can be made without disclosing facts which the said authority consid ers it would be against the public interest to disclose, the grounds on which the order has been made against him and such other particulars as are in the opinion of such authority sufficient to enable him to make, if he wishes, a representation against the order and such person may, within fifteen days of the receipt of such communication, make a representation in writing to such authority against the order and it shall be the duty of such authority to inform such person of his right of making such representation and to afford him the earliest practicable opportunity of doing so."

Argument	To this sub-section a proviso is added which lays down that :  "Where <ORG> is of opinion that it would be against the public interest to disclose all the grounds of the order, neither the said order nor the detention of the said person thereunder shall be invalid or unlawful or improper on the ground of any defect, vagueness on insufficiency of the communication made to such person under this section."
Argument	23 .
Argument	Mr. <NAME> argues that word "all" occurring in the proviso is obviously used in the sense of "any" and under the proviso, therefore <ORG> is not bound to disclose to the detenu any ground whatsoever in support of the order of detention.
RatioOfTheDecision	The result is that the only safeguard which was provided for in section 4 (1) and which entitled a detenu to make an effective representation by way of reply to the allegations made against him is wholly taken away.
RatioOfTheDecision	We may state here that we are not called upon to determine in the present case whether a proviso of this description which practically nullifies the main section upon which it is engrafted, would prevail over the section itself.
RatioOfTheDecision	This is a question of construction which can be raised on a proper occasion and we do not express any opinion upon it.
RatioOfTheDecision	The point which we have to decide is the legislative competency of the Governor which in this respect is the same as that of <ORG> to enact this proviso at all.
RatioOfTheDecision	Mr. <NAME>'s cont ention is that once the safeguard available to a detenu under section 4 (1) of the Ordinance is whittled down and rendered illusory by the proviso, the detention is no longer a "preventive detention" which Item (1) of the Provincial List contemplates; it degenerates into "arbitrary detention" and in respect of such a matter no power of legislation has been given to the province by the Government of India Act.
Precedent	As a part of his argument Mr. <NAME> laid considerable stress upon the following observations made by a Full Bench of <ORG> in Murat Patwa v. The Province of Bihar (7) "In our opinion, the phrase "preventive detention" means detention, not, as in the case of ordinary imprisonment, in respect of the actual commission of an illegal act, but detention in reasonable anticipation that some illegal act or acts may otherwise be committed and in the context of Item 1 of List II in the Seventh Schedule to the Government of India Act, the illegal act must be one connected with the maintenance of pu blic order.
