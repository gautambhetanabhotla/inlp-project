Argument	JUDGMENT Ford North, J.
Argument	1.
Fact	The only question to be decided on this appeal is, whether the appellant's <NAME> <NAME>, who died on the 23rd of April 1867, died intestate, as the appellant alleges, or left a will, as the respondents contend.
Fact	If he did make that will, the appellant is out of Court.
Fact	The Subordinate Judge of Rajshahye decided in the appellant's favour, holding that the will was a forgery ; but <ORG> reversed that decision, and dismissed the appellant's suit, with costs.
Fact	2.
Fact	<NAME>, who married <NAME>, and died many years before 1867, left three sons, who succeeded to their <NAME>'s property, viz., <NAME>, the appellant's <NAME>; <NAME>, who died about 1870, and whose sons are the respondents on this appeal; and <NAME>, who died before 1867, intestate and unmarried, whereupon his mother <NAME> succeeded to his share in the <NAME>'s estate.
Fact	3.
Fact	<NAME> resided at Sar ippur or Kasimpur, in the district of Rajshahye; but some months before his death he removed to Nattore, on account of his health, and there he remained until he went to Rampura.
Fact	There is no doubt that while at Nattore he was very ill, and ultimately his recovery was considered hopeless.
Fact	While he was there a cousin and great friend of his named <NAME>-who seems to have been in a superior station in life and had received the title of <NAME>-came over to Nattore, and took <NAME> back with him to his own house at Rampura, where he stayed till he died.
Fact	The appellant and one of her witnesses say the death was only two days after the removal; while two witnesses for the respondents put it at five or six days and 10 or 12 days respectively.
Fact	The doctor also who was called in at Rampura says he attended him for five or six days, and he saw from the first that the case was hopeless.
Fact	On the day before his death, according to the respondents' evidence, he went to <ORG>, and  there at 4 p. m. presented for registration the will in dispute.
Fact	It bore his signature and seal, and was attested by five witnesses.
Fact	Four of those witnesses died before the trial; but the fifth, the doctor of <NAME>, was called as a witness.
Fact	The execution of the will was admitted by <NAME>, who was identified, by two witnesses, and then the will was registered.
Fact	Each of these four stages was verified by the signature of <NAME>, the Registrar.
Fact	This was on the 22nd of April 1867.The doctor says in his evidence that he signed this will in the presence of <NAME>, and at his request, after some discussion with him as to whether he should make a will or not.
Fact	He thought he attended <NAME> for five or six days.
Fact	He said that many other persons were present when he signed the will, but he did not know whether any other witness or the testator had signed when he did.
Fact	He also said that many respectable people, came in to see the testator during the time of his atten dance.
Fact	The Subordinate Judge declined to believe this witness because there was some doubt as to an apparent alteration in the date of his attestation of the will, and the explanation he gave was not clear.
Fact	But this is not wonderful, as the event had happened 31 years before; and <ORG> thought, and their Lordships think, that the reasons for not giving weight to $he evidence of this witness are quite insufficient.
RatioOfTheDecision	They: see no reason to doubt his veracity.
RatioOfTheDecision	4.
RatioOfTheDecision	Another reason why the Subordinate Judge doubted the validity of the will was on the ground of the absence of any other respectable witnesses to it besides the doctor, and the probability that other persons were present who would have been more likely to be asked to attest it than the persons whose signatures were affixed to it.
RatioOfTheDecision	But there is not one word in the evidence affecting the respectability and competency of the four attesting witnesses, all of whom were dead before the trial.
RatioOfTheDecision	5.
RatioOfTheDecision	Then the Judge also stated that "the general reputation  of character which the two identifiers in the registration office enjoyed" tended to throw a cloud of doubt upon the bona fides of the transaction.
RatioOfTheDecision	Such evidence of the general reputation of the character of those persons (who are both dead) ought not to have been admitted at all.
RatioOfTheDecision	But it was admitted, and is insufficient to prove what it was said to prove.
RatioOfTheDecision	It would be waste of time to discuss it.
RatioOfTheDecision	But that the signatures of the identifiers were their true signatures was clearly proved.
RatioOfTheDecision	6.
Precedent	Then another circumstance relied upon by the learned Judge was " the untimely hour of registration at the registration office, .... though the testator was so weak that he did not survive even 24 hours."
Argument	The hour was 4 p. m. which prima facie is not unreasonable ; and if the evidence of the appellant is to be believed her <NAME> had taken a much longer journey (from Nattore to Rampura) on the previous day without apparently being any worse from it.
Argument	The appellant also says that on the day in question many gentlemen of the t own came in and saw her <NAME>.
Precedent	Then <NAME>, who was at the Registry, says that he sow <NAME> arrive there in a palanquin to get the will registered, and saw him sign and seal the registration.
Precedent	A little further on he says that at the request of <NAME> he came to see him at Rampura four or five days before his death; that sometimes he was helped to sit up, and sometimes used to get and sit up himself unaided; that on the morning of the next day he told him a will had been executed, and showed him the will, but did not request him to be a Witness'; that for two or three days before his death he could at times get up and sit up unaided, but from one day before his death he lost the power of getting and sitting up unaided.
Precedent	He was taken in a palanquin.
Precedent	The mukhtar Baboos helped him to sit up, and he sat up and admitted the will.
Precedent	Then <NAME> says that on the day just preceding his death he saw him going out of his lodgings in a palki, with a view to go to the Co urt.
Precedent	This evidence would-be quite sufficient, in their Lordships' opinion, to answer the observations of the Subordinate Judge.
Precedent	But they desire to put the case on a higher ground.
Precedent	The registration is a solemn act, to be performed in the presence of a competent official appointed to act as registrar, whose duty it is to attend the parties during the registration and see that the proper persons are present and are competent to act, and are identified to his satisfaction; and all things done before him in his official capacity and verified by his signature, will be presumed to be done duly and in order.
RatioOfTheDecision	Of course it may be shown that a deliberate fraud upon him has been successfully committed; but this can only be by very much stronger evidence than is forthcoming here.
RatioOfTheDecision	And this must be specially borne in mind, that no witness has been found who will say that the signature of <NAME> in the will and in the Registrar's book is not in his handwriting.
RatioOfTheDecision	The contrary is expressly stated by <NAME> <NAME>, who adds that his signatures when, in good health were better than these.
RatioOfTheDecision	The learned Judge did not believe this witness; nor that he was present at the time, upon the ground that if he had been he would probably have been invited to attest the will; but this difficulty does not weigh much with their Lordships.
RatioOfTheDecision	7.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is also suggested that <NAME> was presumably under the influence of his brother <NAME> and was very possibly induced by him to make the will.
RatioOfTheDecision	This assumes, of course, that it was executed by him.
RatioOfTheDecision	But this suggestion was not raised by the pleadings,; and is entirely unsupported by any evidence, and need not be further considered.
Fact	The will does not seem unreasonable for a gentleman in the position of <NAME>, and has a genuine look about it.
Fact	The testator says that, considering the realness of his property, he does not wish to adopt a son, and vests his property in his brother <NAME>, that he may perform all ceremonies and maintain his wife during her  life, and his four unmarried daughters, and also give those daughters in marriage, and, if there was any difficulty about living together, the appellant was to have from <ORG> an allowance of Rs.
Fact	120 a year at the rate of Rs.
RulingByLowerCourt	10 a month for her maintenance, which allowance <ORG> should get after the wife's death.
RulingByLowerCourt	He then added that he had married his eldest daughter, and she was to have Rs.
Fact	48 a year for her maintenance at the rate of Rs.
Fact	4 a month, and he provided that if <ORG> did not pay the allowance and daughters' money then he charged them on his estate.
Fact	Subject to the above the estate was to go to <ORG>.
Argument	8.
Argument	But it is said that the will was suppressed and never acted on.
Argument	This also is not in accordance with the evidence.
Argument	It has been proved that the allowance to the eldest daughter has always been paid; that the younger daughters have been provided for out of the estate on their marriages; and that the appellant also has been paid first Rs.
Fact	10, afterwards Rs.
Fact	15, and after that Rs.
Argument	20 per month,  the reason for the increase being that she complained that her allowance was not sufficient, and some increase should be made.
Argument	The Subordinate Judge said that the will was wisely and prudently kept dark, and that the fact that the appellant was paid twice as much as she was entitled to under the will showed that there was something rotten in the core of the will, and therefore precaution was taken that the appellant should have no occasion to dispute it.
RatioOfTheDecision	Their Lordships deprecate this suggestion as not supported by any of the proofs in the case ; and have no doubt that the increase of allowance was made by <NAME> at the suggestion of <NAME>, for the reason above mentioned.
RatioOfTheDecision	Excluding the evidence of the appellant, which <ORG> declined to accept as reliable, there is no evidence that the appellant ever claimed any share in the income of the estate.
RatioOfTheDecision	9.
RatioOfTheDecision	Their Lordships are clearly of opinion that the decision of <ORG> now appealed from was correct, and should be affi rmed; and they will humbly advise His Majesty that this appeal should be dismissed.
RatioOfTheDecision	10.
RulingByPresentCourt	The appellant must pay the costs of the appeal.
