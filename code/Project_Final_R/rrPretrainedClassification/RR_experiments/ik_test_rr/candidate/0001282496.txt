RatioOfTheDecision	HELD : In construing an Act it is necessary to adhere closely to the language employed in it.
RatioOfTheDecision	Only if the terms of a provision are ambiguous can recourse be had to the well-established principles of construction.

RulingByLowerCourt	[836 B] There is nothing in the language of s. 10 of the Indian Income-tax Act, 1922 from which it can be fairly implied that an e xpenditure or allowance falling within the section must fulfill some other condition before it can be allowed.
RulingByLowerCourt	Sub-section (1) of s. 10 directs that an assessee must be taxed in respect of the profits and gains of business carried on by him.
RulingByLowerCourt	Then under sub-s. (2) all the allowances permissible to him must be deducted.

Argument	The learned counsel for the <ORG>, Mr. <NAME>, submits that there is a general principle that no expenditure can be allowed as a deduction from the profits of a business unless the part of the business to which the expenditure is attributable is capable of producing income or profits liable to be taxed under the Act.

RatioOfTheDecision	There is force in the contention of Mr. <NAME>, and the appeal must fail on this ground alone.

RatioOfTheDecision	2Sup./65-10 Then is there such a principle as has been formulated above ?
RatioOfTheDecision	If there is one, can it be invoked to cut down the express language of S.  10(2) (iii), which expressly allows as a deduction interest on capital borrowed for the purpose of the business ?
RatioOfTheDecision	In our opinion.
RatioOfTheDecision	in construing the Act, we must adhere closely to the language of the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	If there is ambiguity in the terms of a provision, recourse must naturally be had to well-established principles of construc- tion but it is not permissible first to create an artificial ambiguity and then try to resolve the ambiguity by resort to some general principle.
RatioOfTheDecision	We are concerned with the interpretation of S. 10 .
RatioOfTheDecision	Let us then look at the language employed.
RatioOfTheDecision	Sub-section (1) directs that an assessee be taxed in respect of the profits and gains of business carried on by him.
RatioOfTheDecision	What is the business of the assessee must first be looked at.
RatioOfTheDecision	Does he carry on one business or two businesses along with the business carried on by him some activity which is not a business?
RatioOfTheDecision	If he is carrying on an activity which is not business, we must leave out of account the receipts of that activity.
RatioOfTheDecision	That is the first step.
RatioOfTheDecision	Seco ndly, we must look at S. 10(2) and deduct all the allowances permissible to him.
RatioOfTheDecision	In allowing a deduction which is permissible the question arises: Do we look behind the expenditure and see whether it has the quality of directly or indirectly producing taxable income ?
RatioOfTheDecision	The answer must be in the negative for two reasons: First, <ORG> has not directed us to undertake this enquiry.
RatioOfTheDecision	There are no words in s. 10(2) to that effect.
RatioOfTheDecision	On the other hand, indications are to the contrary.
RatioOfTheDecision	In S. 10(2) (xv), what <ORG> requires to be ascertained is whether the expenditure has been laid out or expended wholly and exclusively for the purpose of the business.
RatioOfTheDecision	The legislature stops short at directing that it be ascertained what was the purpose of the expenditure.
RatioOfTheDecision	If the answer is that it is for the purpose of the business, <ORG> is not concerned to find out whether the expenditure has produced or will produce taxable income.
RatioOfTheDecision	Secondly, the reason may well be that <ORG> assumes that most types of expenditure wh ich are laid out wholly and exclusively for the purpose of business would directly or indirectly produce taxable income, and it is not worth the administrative effort involved to go further and trace the expenditure to some taxable income.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, it seems to us that there is nothing in the language of S. 10 from which it can be fairly implied that an expenditure or allowance falling within the section must fulfill some other condition before it can be allowed.
