Fact	JUDGMENT  1.
Fact	This is an Appeal from the judgment of <ORG> in a mortgage suit and a question of some importance has been argued before us by Mr. <NAME> as to whether the starling point for an application for a decree absolute under Order XXXIV, Rule 5, Civil Procedure Code, which is governed by Article 181 of the Limitation Act , is the date of the original decree or the date of the appellate decree which affirmed the original decree.
Fact	2.
Fact	It had long been regarded as well-settled in this Court that the starting point under Article 181 was the date of the appellate decree, and there is a decision of <ORG> in Bhup Indar Bahadur Singh v. <NAME> (1901) I.L.R., 23 All., 152 (P.C.)
Fact	which strongly supports that view.
Fact	In that case, the District Judge passed a decree giving the plaintiff possession with future mesne profits.
Fact	That decree was set aside by <ORG> and was restored after a great many years by <ORG>.
Fact	The question then aros e in execution as to the date from which mesne profits should be calculated under the provisions of Section 211 of the Old Code (Act XIV of 1882), and their Lordships observed, at page 158, The <ORG> is now executing not the District Judge's decree of 1887, but the Queen's Order of 1895 which, by affirming the District Judge's decree, has adopted its terms and has carried on their effect down to a later date.
Fact	3.
RatioOfTheDecision	Those observations of <ORG> are entirely in accordance with what has till recently been the established view in this <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	If the date of the final decree was the proper date to have regard to for the purposes of Section 211 , Civil Procedure Code, it follows that it is equally the proper date to have regard to with reference to an application for a decree absolute under Order XXXIV, Rule 5.
RatioOfTheDecision	4.
Fact	We have, however, been asked to hold that this authority should be disregarded on the authority of a more recent decision of <ORG> in <NAME> v.. <NAME> (1919)  I.L.R., 46 Calc.
Fact	670 (P.C.)
Argument	and certain observations of <NAME>, one of the learned Judges who decided <CITATION_226662> (1921) 18 L.W., 37, which go to support this contention, The observations were obiter because both the learned Judges concurred in holding that the case was governed by Article 182 , the general article relating to execution.
Argument	We have carefully examined <CITATION_682162> (1919) I.L.R., 46 Calc., 670 (P.C.
Argument	), and have come to the conclusion that it is not inconsistent with the previous decision to which we have referred.
Argument	In the recent case, an auction-purchaser sued to recover from certain decree-holders his purchase money in consequence of the sale being set aside.
Argument	Assuming that Article 97 was the article applicable, <ORG> held that consideration failed when the sale itself was set aside in the first Court, that time then began to run under Article 97 and that consideration did not fail afresh when the order setting aside the sale was affirmed by <ORG>.
Argument	Here, as in the well-known case of <NAME> v. <NAME> (1889) I.L.R., 11 All., 47 (P.C.)
Argument	the question their Lordships had to decide was when the consideration in fact failed.
RatioOfTheDecision	Once that was established the application of Article 97 was clear.
RatioOfTheDecision	With reference to this question they observed, in accordance with the express provisions of the Code, that an original decree is not suspended by presentation of an appeal nor is its operation interrupted where the decree on appeal is one of dismissal, but those observations must in my opinion be considered as inapplicable to questions arising under Articles 181 and 182.
RatioOfTheDecision	By Article 182 the appellate decree is expressly made a fresh starting point for purposes of execution, and an appellant is not required to proceed with the execution of a decree, against which he has appealed, while the appeal is pending on pain of becoming barred if he does not.
RatioOfTheDecision	It would be strange if a different rule were applicable under Article 181 as regards applications for a decree absolute w here the preliminary decree is under appeal, an application so much resembling an application for execution that, under the repealed chapter of the Transfer of Property Act , it was held by this Court to be one.
RatioOfTheDecision	That was a question which their Lordships were not considering.
RatioOfTheDecision	5.
Precedent	The view that for the purpose of such an application the appellate decree supersedes the original decree appears to be in accordance with the earlier decision in <NAME> v. <NAME> (1901) I.L.R., 23 All., 152 (P.C.)
Argument	where their Lordships held that the decree that they were executing was the decree of <ORG>;.
Argument	Applying that to the present case the preliminary decree which is being executed must be held to be the preliminary decree passed by <ORG>.
Argument	It would obviously be so if the decree of <ORG> had varied the preliminary decree passed by the lower Court and there are no good reasons for applying different rules as regards questions arising under Article 181 , when th e <ORG> affirms the decree of the lower <ORG> and when it varies it.
Argument	Consequently, an application for a decree absolute has to be made under Article 181 within three years from the passing of the preliminary decree by the <ORG>, and therefore the present application was in time and the objection fails.
Argument	6.
Argument	The other objection which was taken is an objection to the preliminary decree which has already been confirmed by this <ORG> and cannot be now questioned.
Argument	7.
Argument	Objection has then been taken that the legal representative of the fourth defendant, who was one of the representatives of the original mortgagee, was not brought on record in time.
RatioOfTheDecision	We think that in the circumstances, if that is so, the delay should be excused.
RatioOfTheDecision	8.
RulingByPresentCourt	The Appeal accordingly fails and is dismissed with costs.
