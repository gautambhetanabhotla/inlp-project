RatioOfTheDecision	The Judgment of the Court was delivered by--   GAJENDRAGADKAR, C. J.-This group of 12 writ petitions raises a common question about the validity of the( Orissa Sales Tax Validation Act, 1961 (Act No.
RatioOfTheDecision	7 of 1961) (hereinafter referred to as the Act).
RatioOfTheDecision	The facts on which the petitioners rely are similar, and so, we shall mention the facts in the first group consisting of writ petitions Nos.
RatioOfTheDecision	125-135 of 1963.
Argument	The petitioner in this group is Shri <NAME>, Proprietor, <NAME> & Sons, Berhampur, Orissa.
Argument	He is a merchant who carries on business in "bullion and specie" and gold and silver ornaments at Berhampur and as such merchant, he has been registered as 'dealer' under the Orissa Sales Tax Act, 1947 (Act No.
Argument	14 of 1947).
Argument	After the said Act came into force, <ORG> purporting to exercise its authority under s. 6 of the said Sales Tax Act issued a notification exempting certain articles from the operation of the charging section of that Act.
Argument	Under this notification, gold ornaments were ordered to be exempt ed from sales-tax 'when the manufacturer selling them charges separately for the value of gold and the cost of manufacture.'
Argument	This notification was issued on July 1, 1949 <NAME> the course of his business, the petitioner manufactures gold ornaments by supplying the gold to the artisans and getting ornaments prepared by them under his supervision and when the ornaments are so prepared, he sells them in his shop and has been showing the value of gold and the cost of manufacture separately.
Argument	That is why the petitioner alleges that he is entitled to claim the benefit of the exemption notification.
Argument	Consistently with this plea, the petitioner filed his returns before the Sales-tax Officer at Berhampur and had been claiming exemption of Sales-tax on the sales as being entitled to exemption under the said notification.
Fact	Upto June, 1952, the claim for exemption made by him was upheld and the amount represented by sales of the said gold ornaments was deducted from the taxable turnover shown by the petitioner in his retur ns.
Fact	Subsequently, however, these assessments were re-opened under s. 12(7) of the Act and it was claimed that the deductions made on certain sales transactions of gold ornaments were not justified and to that extent, the petitioner had escaped assessment.
Fact	The petitioner resisted this attempt to re-open the assessment and he pleaded that he was entitled to claim exemption under the notification, because he belonged to the class of manufacturers to which the notification referred.
Fact	The Sales-tax Officer, however, disallowed the petitioner's contention and proceeded to levy tax on the sales transac- tions in question.
Fact	The petitioner then challenged the said decision by preferring appeals, but the said appeals were also dismissed.
Fact	While the appeals were pending similar assessments made in respect of other dealers including the petitioner were challenged by them by writ petitions before <ORG>.
Fact	(Nos.
Argument	151, 161, 162, 204--209 and 110 of 1957 respectively).
Argument	<ORG> of the Orissa <ORG> t which heard the writ petitions upheld the petitioner's case and issued appropriate writs directing the Sales-tax Officer to allow the petitioner's claim for exemption 'under the notification in question.
Argument	The main controversy before <ORG> was about the precise denotation of the word 'manufacturer' used in the notification.
Argument	<ORG> hold that the expression manufacturer' meant the first owner of the finished products for whom it was mad(.
Argument	either by his paid employee or even by independent artisans on receipt of raw materials and labour charges from him.
Argument	According to this view, the petitioners before <ORG> were found to be manufacturers and as such entitled to claim exemption in respect of sale of -old ornaments made by them.
RulingByLowerCourt	This judgement was pronounced on March 13, 1959.
Fact	Against this judgement the State of Orissa has filed appeals to this Court and they are numbered as Civil Appeals Nos.
Fact	92 to 94 of 1963.
Fact	These appeals are till pending disposal.
Fact	After <ORG> pronounced  its judgement in the writ petitions to which reference has been made, the impugned Act was passed by <ORG> on August 1, 1961.
Fact	This Act received the assent of the Governor on September 10, 1961, and was published on September 18, 1961.
Fact	It contains one operative provision in s. 2 .
Fact	Section 2 provides that notwithstanding anything contained in any judgment, decree or order of any court, the word 'manufactu- rer' occurring against item 33 in the schedule to the notification of <ORG> dated July 28, 1947 as amended by another notification of the 1st July, 1949 shall mean and shall always be deemed to have meant a, person who by his own labour works up materials into suitable forms and a person who owns or runs a manufactory for the purpose of business with respect to the articles manufactured therein.
Fact	It is the validity of this section which is challenged before us by the petitioners in the present writ petitions.
Fact	It is clear that the object of s. 2 of the impugned Act is to ma ke it clear that the legislature's intention was not, as <ORG> had held, to, include within the notification all persons who are first owners of the finished product of gold.
Statute	Section 2 shows that the legislative intention was to give benefit of the said exemption only to persons who themselves work and produce gold ornaments or who run or own a manufactory for the purpose of business with respect to the articles manufactured therein.
Statute	In other words, the intention of the Government in issuing the notification was not to give the benefit of the exemption to traders or shop-keepers who were no more than commission agents and who did not personally, work for making gold ornaments or who did not own a manu- factory employing artisans for that purpose.
RatioOfTheDecision	If this section is valid, it is common ground that the petitioners are not entitled ,to claim the exemption.
RatioOfTheDecision	On the other hand, if this section is invalid, the petitioners would be the first owners of gold ornaments and may be entitled to claim exemption.
RatioOfTheDecision	T he first argument which has been urged before us by Mr. <NAME> is that since the exemption was granted by <ORG> by virtue of the powers conferred on it by s. 6 , it was not open to the legislature to take away that exemp- tion retrospectively.
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 4 of the parent Sales-tax Act is the charging section and s. 6 is the section which confers on <ORG> power to issue a notification exempting from the tax the sale of any goods or class of goods and likewise withdraw any such exemption subject to such conditions and exceptions as it may deem fit.
RatioOfTheDecision	The argument is, the power to grant exemption having been conferred on <ORG>, it was validly exercised by <ORG> and though the legislature may withdraw such exemption, it cannot do so retrospectively.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is obvious that if the <ORG> ,Government which is the delegate of the legislature can withdraw the exemption granted by it, the legislature cannot be denied such right.
RatioOfTheDecision	But it is urged that once exemption was va lidly granted, the legislature cannot withdraw it retrospectively, because that would be invalidating the notification itself.
RatioOfTheDecision	We are not impressed by this argument.
RatioOfTheDecision	What the legislature has purported to do by s. 2 of the impugned Act is to make the intention of the notification clear.
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 2 in substance declares that the intention of the delegate in issuing the notification granting exemption was to confine the benefit of the said exemption only to persons who, actually produce -old ornaments or employ artisans for that purpose.
RatioOfTheDecision	We do not see how any question of legislative incompetence can come in the present discussion.
RatioOfTheDecision	And, if <ORG> was given the power either to grant or withdraw the exemption that cannot possibly affect the legislature's competency to make any provision in that behalf either prospectively or retrospectively.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, there is no substance in the argument that the retrospective operation of s. 2 of the impugned Act 'is invalid.
RatioOfTheDecision	Then Mr. <NAME> contends that this prov ision is discrimina tory and as such, contravenes the equality before the law, guaranteed by Art.
RatioOfTheDecision	14.
RatioOfTheDecision	This argument is also misconceived, is not seriously disputed that the petitioners belong to the class of traders or shopkeepers who are like commission agents.
RatioOfTheDecision	They give gold to the artisans, paying the artisans their labour charges and when the ornaments are thus produced, they charge commission before they are sold to the customers.
RatioOfTheDecision	In such a case, it is not easy to understand how this class of traders can be said belong to same class of persons who produce gold ornaments themselves or run manufactories where artisans are engaged for producing them.
RatioOfTheDecision	The counter-affidavit filed on behalf of the respondent- State has also averred that the petitioners sometimes sell goods manufactured by firms outside the State of Orissa and in no case had they manufactured ornaments themselves.
RatioOfTheDecision	Whether the gold which they give to the artisans is their own or is supplied to them by customers is not a matter of any significanc e, because what is important in this connection is that they are not directly concerned with the production of ornaments, and admittedly, they do not produce the said ornaments themselves.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, the persons who get the benefit of the exemption notification as a result of the provisions of s. 2 of the impugned Act cannot be said to belong to the same class as that of the petitioners, and if that is so, the main argument on the basis of Art.
RatioOfTheDecision	14 does not subsist.
RatioOfTheDecision	Besides, one of the objects of the impugned Act appears to be to make it clear that the legislature intends to benefit the goldsmiths who actually make gold ornaments and that object can be carried out only if exemption is granted to persons who keep in their continuous employment artisans who produce gold ornaments.
RatioOfTheDecision	If a person produces gold ornaments himself and if a person employs artisans to produce gold ornaments for him, they fall within the protection of the exemption.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the case of the petitioners, however, they do not keep any artisans i n their continuous employment, and so, if the legislature thought it was not necessary to give them the benefit of the exemption, it cannot be said that the classification made by the legislature has no rational connection with the object intended to be achieved by it.
RatioOfTheDecision	This argument assume,,, that the petitioners belong to the same class as the persons to whom the benefit of the exemption is available.
RatioOfTheDecision	But as we have already stated, these two categories are distinct and there is no sameness or similarity between them.
RatioOfTheDecision	It was also suggested by Mr. <NAME> that the result of the impugned provision is to deny the benefit of the exemption to the poorer classes of persons who are engaged in the business of manufacturing gold ornaments, and in that con- nection, he has commented on the fact that the notification gives the benefit of the exemption to persons who run manu- factories but it denies that benefit to persons who carry on the work of producing gold ornaments on a smaller scale, and so, are unable to run a  manufactory.
RatioOfTheDecision	This argument is fal- lacious.
RatioOfTheDecision	The notification as interpreted by s. 2 of the impugned Act benefits the artisans who produce ornaments themselves and that obviously covers a very large section of independent artisans engaged in the trade.
RatioOfTheDecision	The notification also benefits persons who run manufactories and that ensures the continuous employment of artisans.
RatioOfTheDecision	That is why it seems to us that the main object of -ranting exemption can be said to be achieved by holding that 'manufacturer, means either a, manufacturer properly so called or one who engages artisans lo manufacture gold ornaments.
RulingByLowerCourt	Mr. <NAME> also argued that the retrospective operation ,of the impugned section should be struck down as unconsti- tutional,, because it imposes an unreasonable restriction on the petitioners' fundamental right under Art.
RatioOfTheDecision	19(1)(g).
RatioOfTheDecision	It is true that in considering the question as to whether legislative power to pass an Act retrospectively has been reasonably ,exercised or not, it is relevant to enquire how the retros pective operation operates.
RatioOfTheDecision	But it would be difficult to accept the argument that because the retrospective operation may operate harshly in some cases, therefore, the legislation itself is invalid.
RatioOfTheDecision	Besides, in the present case, the retrospective operation does not spread over a very long period either.
RatioOfTheDecision	Incidentally, it is not clear from the record that the petitioners did not recover sales, tax from their customers when they sold the gold ornaments to them.
RatioOfTheDecision	The counter-affidavit filed by the, respondent-State alleges that even where sales-tax has not been charged separately, the price charged included sales- tax because it was the usual practice of every registered dealer ,doing similar business to collect sales-tax either by showing it as such separately and thereby claiming deduction of the, ,sales-tax from the gross turnover to arrive at the taxable turn-, ,over shown separately or by including it in the price and thereby collecting it as a part of the price charged.
RulingByPresentCourt	In any event, -we do not think that in  the circumstances of this case it would be possible to hold that by making the provision of s. 2 of' the impugned Act retrospective the legislature has imposed a restriction on the petitioners' fundamental right under Art.
RulingByPresentCourt	19(1)(g) which is not reasonable and is not in the interest of the general public.
RulingByPresentCourt	The result is, the petitions fail and are dismissed with costs.
