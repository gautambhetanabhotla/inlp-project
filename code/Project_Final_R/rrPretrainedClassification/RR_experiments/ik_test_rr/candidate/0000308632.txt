Fact	: Appeal (civil) 1913 of 2002 PETITIONER: FIRST LAND ACQUISITION COLLECTOR & ORS.

Fact	The acquisition proceeding in respect of Premises No.
Fact	27/1 and 27/B on Dehi Serampore Road, Calcutta having been set aside by <ORG>, the Land Acquisition Collector is in appeal against the same.
Fact	The premises in question had been requisitioned under the provisions of West Bengal Requisition and Control (Temporary Provision) Act, 1947, for accommodating students of <ORG>, Calcutta by order dated 10th April, 1948.
Fact	An acquisition proceeding in respect of the same premises was initiated by issuance of a notice under Section 4 of the Land Acquisition Act (hereinafter referred to as " The Act "), by Notification dated 17.12.1982.
Fact	Declaration under Section 6 of the Act was issued on 13.12.1989.
Fact	The acquisition proceedings related t o both the premises, as aforesaid as well as a common passage.
Fact	One <NAME> and one <NAME> claiming right of way on the common passage assailed the aforesaid acquisition proceeding by filing a Writ Petition in <ORG>.
RulingByLowerCourt	The learned Single Judge of <ORG> quashed the Notification by order dated 3rd April, 1992.
RulingByLowerCourt	The said order was assailed by filing an appeal to <ORG>, but the appeal itself being barred by time and the application for condonation not having been allowed the appeal stood dismissed.
Fact	Subsequently a fresh Notification was issued under Section 4 only in respect of the premises No.
Fact	27/1a and 27/1b excluding the common passage and notices were issued under Sections 9 , 10 and 11 of the Act requiring interested persons to file application for compensation.
Fact	Be it be stated, that the premises in question stood de-requisitioned by an order dated 2.7.1993.
Fact	Writ Petition having been filed Challenging the notices issued under sections 9 , 10 and 11 was regi stered as Writ Petition No.
Fact	805 of 1994.
Fact	<ORG> quashed the notices issued under Sections 9 , 10 & 11 of the Act by Order dated 25.8.1994 and it was further directed that the vacant possession of the disputed premises should be handed over within a period of six months.
Fact	The aforesaid Writ Petition had been filed by the owner of the premises in question.
Fact	Though time was extended by the <ORG> for delivery of possession but it is alleged that no possession had been delivered to the owner.
Fact	On 29.11.1994 a fresh Notification was issued under Sections 4(1) and 17(4) of the Act for the purpose of use of <ORG>.
Fact	Declaration under Section 6 of the Act was issued on 30.8.1995.
Fact	Public Notices of substance of the declaration was also given at the convenient places in the locality in two daily newspapers published on 10.9.95.
Fact	Notices under Sections 9 and 10 of the Act were served upon the interested persons on 18.10.95 inviting claims by 2.11.95.
Fact	<ORG> sanctioned Rs.50,41,515/- for  paying the compensation amount.
Fact	The Land Acquisition Collector issued notices under Section 11 on 27.10.95 and the same was served upon the persons interested on 2.11.95.
Fact	The aforesaid notice was assailed by filing a Writ Petition which was registered as Writ Petition No.
Fact	1900 of 1995 challenging the entire acquisition.
RulingByLowerCourt	The learned Single Judge of <ORG> disposed of the Writ Petition by judgment dated 14.10.199 by holding that the <ORG> issued under Sections 4 and 6 as well as invocation of Section 17(1) and 17(4) dispensing with hearing under Section 5A of the Act is not assailable.
RulingByLowerCourt	It , however, directed that the appropriate authority should proceed afresh from the stage of issuance of <ORG> under Section 9 and the acquisition proceeding should be completed with utmost expedition.
RulingByLowerCourt	Pursuant to the aforesaid judgment of the learned Single Judge, <ORG> under Section 9 of the Act was issued again on 15.10.1999.
Fact	But the owners assailed the legality of the order of the learn ed Single Judge by filing an appeal to <ORG>.
RulingByLowerCourt	<ORG> of <ORG> by judgment dated 17.5.2000 having allowed the appeal and having quashed the acquisition proceeding, the Special Land Acquisition Officer has approached this Court.
RulingByLowerCourt	<ORG> while disposing of the Writ Appeal by the impugned judgment have been persuaded to interfere, substantially on the ground that since the earlier judgment and direction of the learned Single Judge of <ORG> dated 25.8.1994 directing delivery of the possession to the owner has not been complied with, the acquisition is malafide and is not in accordance with law.
RulingByLowerCourt	<ORG> in the impugned judgment came to hold that grave urgency and emergency being the pre-condition for invoking powers under sub-sections (1) and (4) of Section 17 , and in the case in hand, no such urgency having been present, invocation of power under Section 17(4) gets vitiated and, therefore, the commencement of the acquisition proceeding  must be held to be not in accordance with law.
RulingByLowerCourt	Having quashed the acquisition proceeding the <ORG> further observed that it would be open to start a proceeding afresh in accordance with law.
Argument	Mr. <NAME>, learned Additional Solicitor General appearing for the appellant contended, that the purpose of acquisition being undoubtedly a public purpose, namely, for the use of <ORG> and <ORG> being of the opinion that the facts situation require invocation of urgency clause under Section 17 of the Act, the <ORG> in exercise of its extraordinary jurisdiction could not have interfered with the aforesaid subjective satisfaction of the authority in the matter of urgency and, therefore, the order is vitiated.
Argument	The learned Additional Solicitor General further contended, that non-delivery of possession, pursuant to the earlier direction of the High <ORG>, could not be a ground for quashing a valid Notification issued under Sections 4(1) & 17(4) of the Act.
Argument	The High <ORG>, therefore,  committed serious error in interfering with the acquisition in question.
Argument	According to the learned Additional Solicitor General the conclusion of <ORG> that the acquisition is malafide is based upon non-existence of any materials, and therefore, the same must be interfered with.
Argument	In reply to the submission of the counsel appearing for the respondents to the effect that the object of invoking the urgency clause under Section 17 being to take immediate possession of the acquired land and in the case in hand that possession being with <ORG> ever since the premises stood requisitioned under the provisions of requisition under West Bengal Act of 1947, the very pre-condition of attracting Section 17 was non existent, it is stated that the earlier possession was not the possession of a lawful owner and the Notification for acquisition having been quashed by the <ORG> on two earlier occasions, the acquiring authority felt the necessity of urgent acquisition, and therefore the contention of the re spondents' counsel is unsustainable.
Argument	Mr. <NAME>, learned senior counsel appearing for one of the respondent and Mr. <NAME>, learned senior counsel, appearing for other respondents vehemently urged that in respect of a land which is under possession of <ORG> or any other authority, the emergency power under Section 17 of the Act cannot be invoked inasmuch as the legislature have conferred power to take possession of the acquired land even without complying with the provision of Section 5A of the Act only in case of grave emergency where the acquisition cannot brook the delay of 30 days period which is contemplated for filing of Petition under Section 5A .
Argument	This being the position and <ORG> being in possession of the premises right from 1948 the appropriate authorities could not have invoked the emergency provision under Section 17 of the Act.
Argument	The counsel also further urged that the right conferred under Section 5A is a valuable right to the owner and the owner should  not be deprived of the same unless there exists real emergency.
Argument	It was further urged that <ORG> was justified in taking into account the defiant attitude of the <ORG> in not delivering possession notwithstanding the earlier orders of the <ORG> directing possession to be delivered within a period of six months.
RatioOfTheDecision	The question of urgency of an acquisition under Section 17(1) and (4) of the Act is a matter of subjective satisfaction of the <ORG> and ordinarily it is not open to the <ORG> to make a scrutiny of the propriety of that satisfaction on an objective appraisal of facts.
RatioOfTheDecision	In this view of the matter when the <ORG> takes a decision, taking all relevant considerations into account and is satisfied that there exists emergency for invoking powers under Section 17 (1) and (4) of the Act, and issues Notification accordingly, the same should not be interfered with by the <ORG> unless the <ORG> comes to the conclusion that the appropriate authority had not applied its mind to the relevant f actors or that the decision has been taken by the appropriate authority <NAME> fide.
RatioOfTheDecision	Whether in a given situation there existed urgency or not is left to the discretion and decision of the concerned authorities.
RatioOfTheDecision	If an order invoking power under Section 17(4) is assailed, the <ORG> may enquire whether the appropriate authority had all the relevant materials before it or whether the order has been passed by non-application of mind.
RatioOfTheDecision	Any post Notification delay subsequent to the decision of <ORG> dispensing with an enquiry under Section 5(A) by invoking powers under Section 17(1) of the Act would not invalidate the decision itself specially when no <NAME> fides on the part of the government or its officers are alleged.
RatioOfTheDecision	Opinion of <ORG> can be challenged in a Court of law if it could be shown that <ORG> never applied its mind to the matter or that action of <ORG> is <NAME> fide.
RatioOfTheDecision	Though the satisfaction under Section 17(4) is a subjective one and is not open to  challenge before a <ORG> of law, except for the grounds already indicated, but the said satisfaction must be of <ORG> and that the satisfaction must be, as to the existence of an urgency.
RatioOfTheDecision	The conclusion of the Government that there was urgency even though cannot be conclusive but is entitled to great weight, as has been held by this <ORG> in <CITATION_20367> AIR 1971 Supreme <ORG> 1033.
RatioOfTheDecision	Even a mere allegation that power was exercised <NAME> fide would not be enough and in support of such allegation specific materials should be placed before the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	The burden of establishing <NAME> fides is very heavy on the person who alleges it.
RatioOfTheDecision	Bearing in mind the aforesaid principles, if the circumstances of the case in hand are examined it would appear that the premises in question was required for the students of <ORG> Calcutta and the Notification issued in December 1982 had been quashed by the <ORG> and the subsequent Notification issued on 25.2.1994 also had been quashed by th e Court.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is only thereafter the Notification was issued under Section 4(1) and 17(4) of the Act on 29.11.1994 which came up for consideration before <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	Apart from the fact that there had already been considerable delay in acquiring the premises in question on account of the intervention by <ORG>, the premises was badly needed for the occupation of the students of <ORG>, Calcutta.
RatioOfTheDecision	Thus, existence of urgency was writ large on the facts of the case and therefore, said exercise of power in the case in hand, cannot be interfered with by <ORG> on a conclusion that there did not exist any emergency.
RatioOfTheDecision	The conclusion of the <ORG> of <ORG>, therefore, is unsustainable.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is indeed difficult for us to uphold the conclusion of the <ORG> that acquisition is <NAME> fide on the mere fact that physical possession had not been delivered pursuant to the earlier directions of a learned Single Judge of <ORG> dated 25.8.94.
RatioOfTheDecision	When the Court is  called upon to examine the question as to whether the acquisition is <NAME> fide or not, what is necessary to be inquired into and found out is, whether the purpose for which the acquisition is going to be made, is a real purpose or a camouflage.
RatioOfTheDecision	By no stretch of imagination, exercise of power for acquisition can be held to be <NAME> fide, so long as, the purpose of acquisition continues and as has already been stated, there existed emergency to acquire the premises in question.
Argument	The premises which was under occupation of the students of <ORG>, Calcutta, was obviously badly needed for the college and the appropriate authority having failed in their attempt earlier twice, the orders having been quashed by <ORG>, had taken the third attempt of issuing notification under Sections 4(1) and 17(4) of the Act, such acquisition cannot be held to be <NAME> fide and, therefore, the conclusion of <ORG> in the impugned Judgment that the acquisition is <NAME> fide, must be set aside an d we accordingly set aside the same.
Argument	The arguments advanced on behalf of the respondents that as the premises in question continued to be under possession of <ORG>, invocation of special powers under Section 17 was vitiated and a valuable right of the land owners to file objections under Section 5A could not have been taken away.
RatioOfTheDecision	According to the counsel for the respondents, Section 5A of the Act, merely gives an opportunity to the land owner to object to the acquisition within 30 days from the date of publication of the notification under Section 4 , the power under Section 17 dispensing with inquiry under Section 5A can, therefore, be invoked where there exists urgency to take immediate possession of the land, but where possession is with the acquiring authority, there cannot exist any urgency, and, therefore the exercise of that power is patently erroneous.
Precedent	In support of this contention, reliance was placed on the decision of this <ORG> in <CITATION_1537954> ., 1976(1) S.C.C.
Precedent	700 .
RatioOfTheDecision	We are unable to accept this contention since the same proceeds on a basic misconception about the possession of the premises.
RatioOfTheDecision	The premises in question had been requisitioned under the provisions of the Requisition Act and stood released from requisition by operation of Section 10B of the said Act, since 1993.
RatioOfTheDecision	Even though the premises stood occupied by the students of the medical college, but such occupation was neither as owner nor was lawful in the eye of law.
RatioOfTheDecision	To effectuate lawful possession and the purpose being undoubtedly a public purpose, <ORG> had been attempting ever-since December, 1982 and each of its attempt had failed on account of <ORG>'s intervention.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is in this context, the legality of exercise of power under Section 17 of the notification dated 29.11.94 is required to be adjudicated upon.
RatioOfTheDecision	In our considered opinion, having regard to the facts and circumstances narrated above, the exercise of power under Section 17 by <ORG>, cannot be held to be illegal or <NAME> f ide and consequently, the impugned Judgment of the Division Bench of <ORG> cannot be sustained.
RatioOfTheDecision	The learned Judges of <ORG> have been totally swayed away by the fact of non-implementation of the directions of <NAME>, in his order dated 25.8.1994, but that by itself would not be a ground for annulling lawful exercise of power under the provisions of the Land Acquisition Act .
RulingByPresentCourt	We, therefore, set aside the impugned Judgment of the Division Bench of <ORG> and hold that the acquisition in question is not vitiated on any ground.
RulingByPresentCourt	The acquisition proceeding, therefore, is held to be in accordance with law.
RulingByPresentCourt	The appeal is allowed.
RulingByPresentCourt	There will be no order as to costs.
