RatioOfTheDecision	The question which arises for our consideration is whether looking to the terms of the Payment of Wages Act (iv of 1936), it is competent to the <ORG> under the Payment of Wages Act to direct payment of wages when it is disputed that there is a subsisting contract of service between the employer and his employee.
RatioOfTheDecision	Is it open to the <ORG> to decide whether a contract of service has been properly and validly terminated?
RatioOfTheDecision	Is it open to him to decide that the dismissal of a servant by his master is wrongful?
RatioOfTheDecision	In order to decide this question, it is necessary to look at the purpose of the Payment of Wages Act and also its general provisions.
RatioOfTheDecision	As the preamble itself says, the purpose of the Act was to regulate the payment of wages to certain class of persons employed in the industry, and the application o f the Act was restricted to wages payable in respect of a wage period which over such wage period averaged Rs.
RatioOfTheDecision	200 a month or less.
RatioOfTheDecision	"Wages" are defined as meaning all remuneration capable of being expressed in terms of money which would, if the terms of the contract of employment expressed or implied were fulfilled, be payable whether conditional upon the regular attendance, good work or conduct or other behaviour of the person employed or otherwise to a person employed in respect of his employment or of work done in such employment.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore it is clear that as far as this part of the definition is concerned wages are to be construed as remuneration due to a person who is employed and it is remuneration which is payable under the terms of the contract whether express or implied.
RatioOfTheDecision	The definition goes on to mention certain other attributes which according to the <ORG> would also constitute remuneration to a person as wages, and that further inclusive definition is :  "Wages includes any bonus or other ad ditional remuneration of the nature aforesaid which would be so payable and any sum payable to such person by reason of the termination of his employment."
RatioOfTheDecision	Emphasis is laid on behalf of the respondents on the expression 'any sum payable to such person by reason of the termination of his employment.'
RatioOfTheDecision	Now "such person" is the person employed, and any sum which is payable by reason of the employment of such person coming to an end is also wages for the purpose of this Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	Looking' to the whole scheme of the Act and looking to the earlier part of the definition, in my opinion, the sum payable by reason of the termination of his employment is not any damages or compensation to which a servant would be entitled on a wrongful termination of his service.
RatioOfTheDecision	The sum payable here referred to is only a sum payable under the terms of the contract.
RatioOfTheDecision	A term of the contract may well provide that a servant is entitled to a salary for a month on the termination of his contract without notice.
RatioOfTheDecision	If the contract so provides, then t he sum payable to the employee on termination of his service without notice would be wages in lieu of that one month's notice and such a sum payable to him would undoubtedly come under the definition of wages as amplified by the subsequent part of Section 2(vi) .
RatioOfTheDecision	Such remuneration in lieu of notice may even be an implied term of the contract of service.
RatioOfTheDecision	If it is an implied term it would be as much wages within the meaning of this definition.
Statute	Then Section 3 provides for responsibility for payment of wages and it deals with cases where the employer is directly responsible and cases where a person other than an employer may be made responsible for payment of wages.
Statute	Then Section 5 deals with time for payment of wages.
Statute	Section 7 deals with deductions which may be validly made from the payment of wages.
Statute	Sections 8 to 33 deal with different kinds of deductions.
Statute	Section 14 deals with Inspectors with which we are not concerned.
Statute	Then we come to Section 15 which is the section directly in question, and that provides :   "<ORG> may, by notification in the Official Gazette, appoint any Commissioner for Workmen's Compensation or other officer with experience as a Judge of <ORG> or as a stipendiary Magistrate to be the authority to hear and decide for any specified area all claims arising out of deductions from the wages, or delay in payment of the wages, of persons employed or paid in that area."
Statute	Therefore the jurisdiction of the <ORG> appointed under this section is clearly limited to (1) all claims arising out of deductions from wages and (2) delay in payment of the wages.
Statute	It must be borne in mind that Section 22 ousts the jurisdiction of civil <ORG> in respect of all claims which can be entertained by the <ORG> under Section 15 .
Statute	Therefore, the scheme of the Act is to set up a special tribunal, confer a special jurisdiction upon that tribunal, and to the extent that special jurisdiction is conferred upon that tribunals to oust the jurisdiction of ordinary civil <ORG>.
Statute	Therefore the juri sdiction conferred upon the special tribunal must be strictly construed, and Section 15 itself clearly limits the jurisdiction only to the two points to which reference has just been made, and this jurisdiction conferred upon the authority under Section 15 must be read in the light of the previous sections of the Act.
Statute	Jurisdiction with regard to delay in payment of the wages must be read in connection with Section 5 which lays down the time for payment of wages.
Statute	Therefore if there is delay in reference to the paint of time indicated in Section 5 , then an employee is entitled to make a grievance of that fact and the <ORG> is entitled to order wages which have not been paid and which should be paid in accordance with Section 5 .
RatioOfTheDecision	Farther, there is jurisdiction to adjudicate with regard to deductions from the wages and this must again be read with reference to Section 7 which permits certain deductions and which makes it illegal for the employer to make deductions other than those provided in the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	Theref ore if an employer makes certain deductions from the wages of an employee, he is entitled to go to the <ORG> and complain of unauthorised deductions and get an order from the authority compelling the employer to make good the deductions wrongly made by the employer.
Statute	There is also a provision for appeal under Section 17 , but that right is restricted to cases where the total sum directed to be paid by way of wages and compensation exceeds "Rs.
RatioOfTheDecision	300.
RatioOfTheDecision	In cases where the payment is less than Es, 300, there is no right of appeal at all and the decision of the <ORG> is final.
RatioOfTheDecision	4.
RatioOfTheDecision	Now, what is contended by Mr. <NAME> on behalf of the <ORG> and supported by Mr. <NAME> on behalf of respondent No.
RatioOfTheDecision	2 is that it is competent to the <ORG> to decide whether a contract of service was terminated or not, because if he comes to the conclusion that the contract of service was not validly terminated, then the employee continues in service, and what the <ORG> directs the employer to pay his wages within the  meaning of the definition in the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is further pointed out that in this particular ease the employee is a <ORG> servant and in the light of decisions of <ORG> if the proper procedure is not followed for termini-ting the services of a <ORG> employee, the <ORG> employee continues in service and he is entitled to the payment of his salary.
RatioOfTheDecision	There, fore, what is urged is that all that the <ORG> has decided in this case is that the employee being a <ORG> servant and proper procedure not having been followed in terminating his services, he continues to be a <ORG> servant still employed by <ORG> and as such servant he is entitled to the payment of wages, and as there has been delay in payment of wages, the employer is bound to pay those wages.
RatioOfTheDecision	5.
RatioOfTheDecision	Now, if that were the true construction of Section 15 , I must say that the <ORG> has used most inappropriate language to convoy that intention.
RatioOfTheDecision	Delay in payment of wages can only mean delay in payment of  wages which are admitted.
RatioOfTheDecision	Wages are due but for some reason or other those wages have not been paid at the time when they should have been paid under the law.
RatioOfTheDecision	Mr. <NAME> wants us to read "delay in payment of wages" as if it meant the same thing as refusal to pay wages.
RatioOfTheDecision	In this case there is no delay on the part of the petitioner to pay wages.
RatioOfTheDecision	He has refused to pay wages, rightly or wrongly, contending that respondent 2 is not his employee; he has dismissed him and therefore nothing is due to him.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, the issue which really arises and which the <ORG> ha3 assumed jurisdiction to decide is whether the refusal of the petitioner to pay wages is justified or is valid in law.
RatioOfTheDecision	If the <ORG> wanted to confer such a jurisdiction upon the <ORG>, it would certainly have done so by appropriate language.
RatioOfTheDecision	Really Mr. <NAME> goes to the utmost length by suggesting that all questions arising out of a contract of personal service and all questions arising out of relationship of master and servant, have all  been transferred from the civil <ORG> to this special tribunal.
RatioOfTheDecision	Mr. <NAME> says that the expression "any sum payable to such person by reason of the termination of his employment" is wide enough even to cover a claim for damages for wrongful dismissal.
RatioOfTheDecision	If the intention of the <ORG> was what Mr. <NAME> says it is, nothing could have been easier than for the <ORG> to have indicated in clear explicit language that the jurisdiction of civil <ORG> to adjudicate upon the quantum of wages, to adjudicate upon the question whether a contrast of service has terminated or not, the jurisdiction to adjudicate upon the question whether a dismissal by a master of his servant was wrongful or not and the jurisdiction to adjudicate upon the damages which the servant is entitled to on a wrongful dismissal, all these questions and all these jurisdictions have been transferred from the civil <ORG> to the special tribunal set up under the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	As I said before, one would have expected very different language, muc h more appropriate language, to oust the jurisdiction of the civil <ORG> and to confer that jurisdiction upon the special tribunal.
RatioOfTheDecision	The jurisdiction of a special tribunal cannot be inferred by implication.
RatioOfTheDecision	Jurisdiction must be expressly given and we must find from the language of the statute itself that there is express ouster of the jurisdiction of the civil <ORG> and an express conferment of such jurisdiction upon the special tribunal set up under the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	6.
RatioOfTheDecision	We would like to make it clear, as the matter is of considerable importance, as to what we think is the jurisdiction of the <ORG> under the statute.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is certainly competent to the <ORG> to construe the terms of the contract of employment in order to determine what wages are to be paid, and even if the contract of employment has been terminated, it is open to him to construe its terms in order to determine whether any sums are payable by reason of the termination.
RatioOfTheDecision	It would also be open to him to determine whether a person has been employed  or not, because the question of contract of employment and the terms of the contract can only arise provided the person seeking relief was employed.
RatioOfTheDecision	The mere denial of the factum of employment cannot oust the jurisdiction of the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	If the employer denies or disputes the fact that the servant was employed by him, it will be for the <ORG> to decide that question, and it is only after the question of employment has been decided that the question would arise as to what are the terms of the contract and what is the liability of the master under the terms with regard to wages.
RatioOfTheDecision	It has been suggested by Mr. <NAME> that this construction of the statute really confines the jurisdiction of the <ORG> only to cases where wages are admitted, and Mr. <NAME> says that if the <ORG> intended that the <ORG> should only try cases of admitted wages, there was nothing easier than for the <ORG> to have so stated.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is not correct that our decision leads to the conclusion that the jurisdiction of t he <ORG> is so limited or restricted because there may be various cases within its jurisdiction where the liability to wages is denied or disputed and which the <ORG> would still be competent to decide.
RatioOfTheDecision	The question as to whether X amount or Y amount is due under the terms of the contract is a case where wages may not be admitted.
RatioOfTheDecision	The employer may say that X amount is due and the servant might say that Y amount is due, and the <ORG> would have to decide on a true construction of the terms of the contract as to what is the amount due.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, the jurisdiction of the <ORG> really is to determine the terms of the contract in so far as they relate to the payment of wages and in so far as he has to decide the liability of the employer to pay wages under the terms of the contract.
RatioOfTheDecision	But that jurisdiction does not extend to determining the question as to whether the contract has terminated as alleged by the employer or the contract is still subsisting as alleged by the servant.
Argument	7.
Argument	Mr. <NAME> has  contended that this petition is not maintainable inasmuch as a specific legal remedy was open to the petitioner and he should have availed himself of that remedy rather than come to this <ORG> on a petition for a writ of certiorari, and reliance is placed on Article 227 of the Constitution which confers upon <ORG> powers of superintendence not only over all <ORG> but also over all tribunals throughout the territories in relation to which <ORG> exercises jurisdiction.
RatioOfTheDecision	We have already taken the view that the superintendence of <ORG> is not confined to administrative superintendence, it also includes judicial superintendence, and undoubtedly <ORG> has power to correct any decision of a tribunal within its jurisdiction through its powers of superintendence.
RatioOfTheDecision	But in order that a petition for a writ of certiorari would not lie, the petitioner must have a specific legal remedy, and specific legal remedy in this context can only mean that he must have a right to approach a <ORG> an d he must have a right to a remedy if his case was just.
RatioOfTheDecision	Article 227 only deals with the power of <ORG> and not with tbe rights of litigant.
RatioOfTheDecision	A litigant may approach <ORG>, but he has no right to do so, nor has he a right to a remedy because <ORG> may refuse a remedy under Article 227.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore the mere power of superintendence conferred upon <ORG> does not disentitle a petitioner seeking a writ of certiorari from coming to this Court and asking for that writ.
RatioOfTheDecision	It cannot be said under the circumstances of the case that he had an alternative specific legal remedy open to him which he should have availed of before he asked for a prerogative writ.
RatioOfTheDecision	8.
RatioOfTheDecision	The result is that in our opinion the order passed by the <ORG> was an order without jurisdiction.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, that order must be corrected by a writ of certiorari.
RatioOfTheDecision	We, therefore, direct the issue of a writ of certiorari.
