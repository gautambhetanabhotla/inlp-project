RatioOfTheDecision	We have also found no evidence of any 'retracting letter' having been received by the departmental investigators.
RatioOfTheDecision	None of the officers in the investigating team (<ORG>) was examined by the appellants to prove their point.
RatioOfTheDecision	We are also at a loss to understand as to why the appellants did not resort to an effective mode of dispatch which was acknowledgeable by the addressee.
Fact	Annexure 'J' to the memorandum of appeal is a 'certificate of posting' bearing the postal date-seal on which the date is not legible.
Fact	It has been claimed by the appellants that this document is evidence of their retrac ting letter dated 26.4.94 having been sent to the <ORG>.
Fact	Annexure 'L' to the memorandum of appeal is another 'certificate of posting' which also carries a postal seal on which the date is not legible.
RatioOfTheDecision	It has been claimed that this document proves dispatch of the appellants' retracting letter dated 9.11.94 to the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	By no stretch of imagination can a certificate of posting issued by postal authorities to the sender be considered as evidence of receipt of the postal article by the addressee.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, in this case, there is no sustainable evidence of any retraction of the statements dated 25.4.94 and 8.11.94.
RatioOfTheDecision	The statements have been rightly relied upon by the adjudicating authority.
RatioOfTheDecision	<NAME> clearly admitted that the export goods covered by the shipping bill had not been manufactured by <ORG> <ORG> or even by their supporting manufacturers <ORG> <ORG> and that the goods had been purchased from the local market.
RatioOfTheDecision	He never claimed that the goods were new and unused.
RatioOfTheDecision	On the other  hand, he conceded that he did not know as to whether the goods were in serviceable condition.
RatioOfTheDecision	He even went to the extent of admitting that the suppliers of the goods had told him that the goods were old and used ones.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, the fact that the Video Magnetic Tapes in hubs seized by the departmental officers had not been manufactured out of the raw material imported duty-free under the DEEC Scheme and were old/used second hand goods procured from the local market stands proved.
RatioOfTheDecision	The appellants were admittedly aware of the legal requirement that the export goods under the DEEC Scheme should be new and not old and used ones.
RatioOfTheDecision	The Shipping Bill described the goods as Video Magnetic Tapes in hubs only.
RatioOfTheDecision	The FOB value (Rs.
RatioOfTheDecision	10,14,949/-) of the old goods declared in the Shipping Bill was undisputedly that of new goods.
RatioOfTheDecision	The appellants have never had a case that old/used video magnetic tapes in hubs had any appreciable commercial value.
RatioOfTheDecision	For all these reasons, the case of the <ORG> that the appellants had misdecla red and overvalued the goods has to be sustained.
RatioOfTheDecision	8.
RatioOfTheDecision	It has been held by <ORG> in the case of <NAME> (supra) that misdeclaration of value of goods by exporter amounts to violation of prohibition deemed to have been imposed under Section 11 of the Customs Act attracting the provisions of Section 113 of the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	This view of <ORG> is contained in para 20 of its judgment, which reads as under:-  "Similarly, under the Foreign Trade (Regulation) Rules, 1993 which have been framed under the Foreign Trade (Development and Regulation) Act , 1992 Rule 2(i) provides that 'value' would have the same meaning as was assigned to it in Section 2(41) of the act.
RatioOfTheDecision	Thus the procedure for determination of value under Section 14 of the act is brought in for the purposes of the 1993 Rules.
RatioOfTheDecision	Rule 11 of the 1993 Rules clearly states that the value of the goods must be stated whether the goods are liable to duty or not and such statement must be certified as being correct.
RatioOfTheDecision	If therefore an incorrect  declaration is made this would be a violation of Rule 11 of the 1993 Rules.
RatioOfTheDecision	If an incorrect declaration of the value is made this would violate Section 11 of the 1992 Act which forbids the export or import of goods in violation of the Rues.
RatioOfTheDecision	This in turn would attract the provisions of Sections 111 and 13 of the Act as goods imported or exported contrary to law and give <ORG> the power to investigate the correct value of the gods in question and for that purpose utilize the method prescribed under Section 14(1) ."
RatioOfTheDecision	The above ruling of <ORG> has been followed by this <ORG> in the case of <NAME> (supra).
RatioOfTheDecision	It was pointed out by the Counsel that <ORG>'s ruling was not followed by the <ORG> in the case of <CITATION_894621> [2001 (137) ELT 916].
RatioOfTheDecision	It was not followed in the case of Modern World Trade (supra) either.
RatioOfTheDecision	We find that, in both the cases, the <ORG> relied on the <ORG>'s Larger <ORG> decision in <CITATION_1480227> [2000 (121) ELT 754].
RatioOfTheDecision	But the view taken  by the 3-Member larger bench in <ORG> has been overruled by a larger bench of Five Members of the Tribunal in the case of <CITATION_1630921> [2001 (127) ELT 81].
Precedent	The latter larger Bench relied on <ORG> judgment in <ORG> case and also distinguished <ORG> decision in <CITATION_1451490> [2000 (115) ELT A-219].
RatioOfTheDecision	In the result, it is the decision of <ORG> that should be followed in the instant case.
RatioOfTheDecision	On a reading of the apex court's judgment in Rai Bahadur Shreeram Durga Prasad (supra) relied on by the learned counsel, we are unable to accept his argument that <ORG> decision is hit by the said judgment of the apex court.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the result, we hold that the appellants had contravened Section 11 of the Customs Act (thereby attracting the penal provisions of Section 113 of the Act) by overvaluing the export goods in the Shipping Bill with an oblique purpose.
RatioOfTheDecision	The Commissioner has rightly invoked the provisions of Section 113 to confiscat e the goods.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the facts and circumstances of the case, we do not find any reason to interfere with the quantum of redemption fine imposed by him under Section 125 in lieu of confiscation.
RatioOfTheDecision	Further, a personal penalty under Section 114 (i) of the Customs Act would be liable to be imposed on <ORG> as they had rendered the goods liable to confiscation under Section 113 of the Act.
