Fact	<NAME>, J.
Fact	1.
Fact	This appeal arises out of an application made under the Trade Marks Act , 1940, hereinafter called the Act, for registration of a trade mark.
Fact	The application was made by the respondent and it was opposed by the appellant.
Fact	2.
Fact	The respondent is a manufacturer of biscuits.
Fact	On November 5, 1949, it made the application for registration of the mark 'Gluvita' in respect of the goods specified in class 30, which mark, it appeared later, it had not used prior to that date.
Fact	The Registrar ordered the application to be advertised before acceptance on the respondent's agreeing to limit the registration to biscuits only which were one of the classes of goods specified in class 30.
Fact	3.
Fact	The appellant is a corporation organised under the laws of the State of New Jersey in the United States.
Fact	On August 31, 1942, it had registered the mark 'Glucovita' under the Act in class 30 in respect of "Dextrose (d-Glucose powder mixed with vitamins), a substance used as food or as an ingredient in food; glucose for  food."
Fact	On the same date the appellant had also registered the same trade mark in class 5 in respect of "Infants' and invalids' foods."
Fact	4.
Fact	The appellant opposed the respondent's application contending that it should be refused under ss.
Fact	8(a) and 10(1) of the Act to which opposition the respondent duly filed a counter statement.
Fact	Both sides filed affidavits in support of their respective cases.
Argument	5.
Argument	The Deputy Registrar held that Section 12 as for biscuits included in class 30 were not goods of the same description as glucose powder mixed with vitamins which was in the same class.
Argument	He further held that the words 'Glucovita' and 'Gluvita' were not visually or phonetically similar and that there was no reasonable likelihood of any deception being caused by or any confusion arising from, the use of the respondent's proposed mark.
Argument	In regard to s. 8(a) , he held applying the cases of In re : an application by <ORG> [(1945) 63 R.P.C.
Argument	97], that the syllable 'co' which was absent in the respondent's pr oposed mark sufficiently distinguished the two marks and made it impossible for any one to be deceived or confused.
Argument	One of the findings made by the Deputy Registrar was that the appellant had acquired a reputation and goodwill for its trade mark 'Glucovita' in respect of glucose powder mixed with vitamins.
Argument	6.
Argument	The appellant then preferred an appeal to <ORG> at Bombay from the order of the Deputy Registrar.
Fact	That appeal was heard by <ORG>, exercising the original jurisdiction of that Court.
RulingByLowerCourt	It was not seriously pressed before him on behalf of the appellant that the goods were of the same description and he himself also came to the conclusion that they were not so.
RulingByLowerCourt	He, therefore, held that the registration could not be refused under s. 10(1) of the Act.
RulingByLowerCourt	<ORG>, agreed with the Deputy Registrar's finding that the appellant had acquired a reputation among the public for the mark 'Glucovita' in respect of glucose powder mixed with vitamins.
RulingByLowerCourt	He however disagreed with the view of the Deputy Registrar  that the respondent's mark was not likely to cause deception or confusion.
RulingByLowerCourt	He held that the two marks were sufficiently similar so as to be reasonable likely to cause deception and confusion.
RulingByLowerCourt	He, therefore, set aside the order of the Deputy Registrar and held that the respondent's mark could not be registered in view of s. 8(a) .
RulingByLowerCourt	7.
RulingByLowerCourt	Now the respondent appealed from the judgment of <NAME>, J.
Fact	This appeal was heard by <NAME>, C.J., and <NAME>, J., of the same <ORG>.
Argument	The learned Judges of the appellate bench took the view that there was no evidence that the appellant's mark had acquired any reputation among the public but that the evidence produced showed that it had acquired a reputation among the trades people only who were discerning and were not likely to be deceived or confused.
Argument	According to them, the public not being aware of the reputation of the appellant's trademark were not likely to be deceived or confused by the use by the respondent of its proposed trademark.
Argument	They also held that the evidence esta blished that there were a series of marks in which the prefix or suffix 'Gluco' or 'Vita' occurred and that it could not therefore be said that the common features 'Gluco' and 'Vita' were only associated with the manufacture of the appellant's goods.
RulingByLowerCourt	On both these grounds they held that the proposed mark of the respondent was not likely to confuse or deceive any one.
RulingByLowerCourt	In the result, they set aside the order of <NAME>, J., and restored that of the Deputy Registrar.
Fact	The appellant has now appealed to this Court from the judgment of the appellate Judges of <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	8.
RatioOfTheDecision	As we have earlier stated, the appellant had opposed the registration of the respondent's mark under s. 8(a) and also under s. 10(1) .
RatioOfTheDecision	In order that s. 10(1) might apply to the case, the appellant had to establish that its mark had been registered in respect of the same goods or description of goods for which the respondent had made its application for registration.
RatioOfTheDecision	On this question the decisions of the Deputy Registrar and <NAME>, J., are agai nst the appellant.
RatioOfTheDecision	Before the appellate Judges of <ORG> the appellant proceeded on the basis that the goods were not of the same description.
RatioOfTheDecision	Before us also the appellant has adopted the same attitude.
RatioOfTheDecision	That puts s. 10(1) out of the way.
RatioOfTheDecision	We are thus left only with the question whether s. 8(a) prevents the registration of the respondent's mark.
RatioOfTheDecision	9.
RatioOfTheDecision	In our judgment the view of the appellate Judges of <ORG> that there was no evidence that the appellant's trade mark had acquired a reputation among the public cannot be sustained.
RatioOfTheDecision	In coming to this view, they relied on the affidavits filed by the appellant wherein it was stated that "Glucovita is a well-known mark in the trade" and denoted only the products of the appellant.
RatioOfTheDecision	We think that the learned appellate Judges put too strict a meaning on the words "in the trade" in thinking that they referred only to the tradespeople.
RatioOfTheDecision	In our view, these words may refer also to the public.
RatioOfTheDecision	If they do, then of course, that would be evidence that the appellant' s mark had acquired a reputation among the public.
RatioOfTheDecision	10.
RatioOfTheDecision	But apart from this, there is other evidence on the record to support the conclusion that the appellant's mark had acquired a reputation among the public.
RatioOfTheDecision	The appellant had in its opposition categorically stated that its mark had acquired a reputation among the Indian buying public.
RatioOfTheDecision	There is no clear denial of this in the counter-statement filed by the respondent.
RatioOfTheDecision	There is further ample evidence to show that the appellant's goods under its mark 'Glucovita' were sold in very large quantities in small containers of one pound and four ounces capacities.
RatioOfTheDecision	The small sizes of the containers would indicate that the goods were sold to the public for if those sales had been to tradespeople for industrial purposes, they would have been made in bulk on in much larger containers.
RatioOfTheDecision	It appears that the appellant spent considerable sums for the advertisement of its mark 'Glucovita' in ordinary popular journals and this would indicate that it had a large sale for its good s among the general public.
RatioOfTheDecision	But the most conclusive evidence of the sale of the appellant's goods under the mark 'Glucovita' to the general public comes from the affidavits filed by the respondents itself.
RatioOfTheDecision	From the affidavit of <NAME>, a partner in a firm of <ORG>, filed by the respondent, it appears that "a number of customers come to buy the products 'Gluvita' and the products 'Glucovita'."
RatioOfTheDecision	Similar statements appear in eight other such affidavits.
RatioOfTheDecision	The evidence provided by these affidavits make it perfectly clear that the appellant's mark had acquired a reputation among the general buying public.
RatioOfTheDecision	We think it right in this connection also to refer to the respondent's grounds of appeal against the judgment of <NAME>, J.
RatioOfTheDecision	In these the respondent does not dispute, and in fact it assumes, that the appellant's mark had acquired a reputation among the public.
RatioOfTheDecision	We are, therefore, fully satisfied that the appellant has established that its mark has acquired a reputation among the buying public.
RatioOfTheDecision	11.
RatioOfTheDecision	In conn ection with the question of reputation, the learned appellate Judges seem to have fallen into another curious error.
RatioOfTheDecision	They said "A commodity may acquire reputation by its being made by a particular manufacturer and the consumers may require not a particular commodity but the commodity made by a particular manufacturer.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is only in the latter case that it could be said that the commodity manufactured by a particular manufacturer has acquired a reputation among the public.
RatioOfTheDecision	On those affidavits there is not even a suggestion that the public wanted the 'Glucovita' powder mixed with vitamins manufactured by the respondent and not by anybody else."
RatioOfTheDecision	The respondent referred to by the learned Judge is the appellant before us.
RatioOfTheDecision	We are unable to agree with the view expressed by them.
RatioOfTheDecision	It seems clear to us that what is necessary is that the reputation should attach to the trade mark; it should appear that the public associated that trade mark with certain goods.
RatioOfTheDecision	The reputation with which we are concerned in the present cas e is the reputation of the trade mark and not that of the maker of the goods bearing that trade mark.
RatioOfTheDecision	A trade mark may acquire a reputation in connection with the goods in respect of which it is used though a buyer may not know who the manufacturer of the goods is.
RatioOfTheDecision	12.
RatioOfTheDecision	In our view, therefore, it would be wrong in this case to say that the appellant's trade mark had not acquired any reputation among the general public and that hence there is no reasonable apprehension of their being confused or deceived by the use of the respondent's proposed mark.
RatioOfTheDecision	13.
RatioOfTheDecision	The second point on which the learned appellate Judges based themselves in arriving at the conclusion that there was no reasonable apprehension of confusion or deception was, as we have earlier stated, that there were various trade marks with a prefix or suffix 'Gluco' or 'Vita' and that made it impossible to say that the common features 'Glu' and 'Vita' were only associated with the appellant's products.
Precedent	This view was founded on a passage which the learned ap pellate Judges quoted from <NAME> on Trademarks, 7th Edn.
Precedent	p. 624.
Precedent	That passage may be summarised thus : Where there are a "series" of marks, registered or unregistered, having a common feature or a common syllable, if the marks in the series are owned by different persons, this tends to assist the applicant for mark containing the common feature.
Precedent	This statement of the law in <NAME>'s book is based on In re : an application by <ORG> [64 R.P.C.
Precedent	76].
Precedent	It is clear however from that case, as we shall presently show, that before the applicant can seek to derive assistance for the success of his application from the presence of a number of marks having one or more common features which occur in his mark also, he has to prove that those marks had acquired a reputation by user in the market.
Precedent	We will read a portion of the judgment at pp.
Precedent	82 & 83 in <ORG> case [64 R.P.C.
Precedent	76] from which what we have stated earlier, appears :  "I am disposed to agree with Mr. <NAME>'s submi ssion that the "series" objections is primarily founded upon user, because the inference which the Registrar is asked to draw is that traders and the public have gained such a knowledge of the common element or characteristic of the "series" that when they meet another mark having the same characteristic they will immediately associate the later mark with the "series" of the marks with which they are already familiar.
RatioOfTheDecision	But I do not think that this statement necessarily implies that the whole issue must be determined solely under the provisions of section 11 , as I will now endeavour to explain.
RatioOfTheDecision	"When an application for registration is before the Registrar it frequently happens that the search for conflicting marks reveals several marks having some characteristic feature in common with the mark of the application, which marks may stand on the Register in the name of one proprietor only, or in the name of several different proprietors.
RatioOfTheDecision	At this stage, when the matter is one between the applicant and the Registra r, the latter generally has before him no evidence as to whether the registered marks are in actual use or not, but in forming an opinion under Section 12 as to whether or not confusion or deception is likely to arise, he is bound, I think, in the absence of evidence to presume that, prima facie some at least of the registrations have been effected bona fide by persons who at the date of their respective applications were using or proposed in the near future to use their marks.
RatioOfTheDecision	If, therefore, all the marks were owned by one proprietor, the Registrar would presume that the latter was using a "series" of marks and judge the conflict between the applicants' mark and each of the proprietor's marks with this consideration in mind.
RatioOfTheDecision	Of course, if the registrations merely consisted of one and the same word registered separately in respect of several articles to be found in a single class of the Trade Marks Schedules, the Registrar would in general regard all these registrations as but the equivalent of a single regis tration covering all the items, for they would not constitute a "series" as now under discussion.
RatioOfTheDecision	On the other hand, if the registered marks found were owned by several different proprietors, this would be a circumstance which might considerably assist the applicant, who would be in a position to submit that the common characteristic was one well recognised in marks in use in the particular trade.
RatioOfTheDecision	In short, when the Registrar comes to compare the applicant's mark with the registered marks, using the principles laid down in the "Pionotist" case the presence of marks on the Register other than the one with which the comparison is being made is regarded an one of the surrounding circumstances which he is required to take into account.
Precedent	"But when the same question comes before the Registrar in opposition proceedings, it appears to me that he is no longer in a position to make any presumption as regards the surrounding circumstances, but that before he can draw the suggested inference based upon the user of other  marks either in the applicant's or the opponent's favour, any such user must be established by evidence (see, e.g.
Precedent	<ORG>'Application, 52 R.P.C., p. 70, 1.
Precedent	39-p. 71, 1.
Precedent	15, where the Registrar refused to draw the necessary inference in favour of the applicants in the proceedings)."
Precedent	14.
Precedent	We have no reason to think that <NAME> in stating the law on the subject intended to depart from the principle stated in the passage that we have just now read, from <ORG> case.
Precedent	15.
Precedent	We may also refer to In re : <ORG>' application [52 R.P.C.
RatioOfTheDecision	65], mentioned in the quotation from <ORG> case [64 R.P.C.
RatioOfTheDecision	76], set out in the preceding paragraph.
RatioOfTheDecision	It was there said at p. 70,  16.
RatioOfTheDecision	Now it is a well recognised principle, that has to be taken into account in considering the possibility of confusion arising between any two trademarks, that, where those two marks contain a common element which is also contained in a number of other marks in use in the same market such a common occurrence in the market tends  to cause purchasers to pay more attention to the other features of the respective marks and to distinguish between them by those features.
RatioOfTheDecision	This principle clearly requires that the marks comprising the common element shall be in fairly extensive use and, as I have mentioned, in use in the market in which the marks under consideration are being or will be used.
RatioOfTheDecision	17.
RatioOfTheDecision	The series of marks containing the common element or elements therefore only assist the applicant when those marks are in extensive use in the market.
RatioOfTheDecision	The onus of proving such user is of course on the applicant, who wants to rely on those marks.
RatioOfTheDecision	Now in the present case the applicant, the respondent before us, led no evidence as to the user of marks with the common element.
RatioOfTheDecision	What had happened was that Deputy Registrar looked into his register and found there a large number of marks which had either 'Gluco' or 'Vita' as prefix or suffix in it.
RatioOfTheDecision	Now of course the presence of a mark in the register does not prove its user all.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is possible that the mar k may have been registered but not used.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is not permissible to draw any inference as to their user from the presence of the marks on the register.
RatioOfTheDecision	If any authority on this question is considered necessary, reference may be made to <NAME> p. 507 and <ORG> v. <ORG> [39 R.P.C.
RatioOfTheDecision	285 p. 289].
RatioOfTheDecision	It also appears that the appellant itself stated in one of the affidavits used on its behalf that there were biscuits in the market bearing the marks '<ORG>se Biscuits', '<ORG> biscuits' and '<ORG>a Lactine biscuits'.
RatioOfTheDecision	But these marks do not help the respondent in the present case.
RatioOfTheDecision	They are ordinary dictionary words in which no one has any right.
RatioOfTheDecision	They are really not marks with a common element or elements.
RatioOfTheDecision	We, therefore, think that the learned appellate Judges were in error in deciding in favour of the respondent basing themselves on the series marks, having '<ORG>' or 'Vita' as a prefix or a suffix.
RatioOfTheDecision	18.
RatioOfTheDecision	We have already said that in our view the mark '<ORG>vita' has acquired a reputation am ong the general buying public.
RatioOfTheDecision	The first question that then arises is whether the marks 'Glucovita' and' Gluvita' are so similar as to be likely to cause confusion to the buying public or deceive them.
RatioOfTheDecision	On this matter, we have not the advantage of the view of the learned appellate Judges of <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	They did not express any view on this aspect of the question at all.
RatioOfTheDecision	We are however inclined to think that their answer to the question would have been in the affirmative.
RatioOfTheDecision	However that may be, the Deputy Registrar felt that the words were not so similar as to be likely to give rise to confusion or to cause deception.
RatioOfTheDecision	He felt that the syllable 'co' in the appellant's mark was an emphatic characteristic and was not likely to be slurred over.
RatioOfTheDecision	He apparently felt that this syllable would prevent any confusion arising between the two marks or any person being deceived by the use of them both.
RatioOfTheDecision	He thought that the test down in what is called the "<NAME>" case (In re : an application by <ORG> [63 R. P.C.
Precedent	97], should be applied and that the emphatic characteristic of the second syllable in the mark 'Glucovita' should decide that there was no likelihood of confusion arising.
Precedent	<NAME>, J., thought that the Deputy Registrar was wrong.
Precedent	19.
Precedent	We think that the view taken by <NAME>, J., is right.
Precedent	It is well known that the question whether the two marks are likely to give rise to confusion or not is a question of first impression.
Precedent	It is for the court to decide that question.
Precedent	English cases proceeding on the English way of pronouncing an English word by Englishmen, which it may be stated is not always the same, may not be of much assistance in our country in deciding questions of phonetic similarity.
Precedent	It cannot be overlooked that the word is an English word which to the mass of the Indian people is a foreign word.
Precedent	It is well recognised that in deciding a question of similarity between two marks, the marks have to be considered as a whole.
RatioOfTheDecision	So considered, we are inclined to agree with <NAME>, J., that the marks with which  this case is concerned are similar.
RatioOfTheDecision	Apart from the syllable 'co' in the appellant's mark, the two marks are identical.
Precedent	That syllable is not in our opinion such as would enable the buyers in our country to distinguish the one mark from the other.
Precedent	20.
Precedent	We also agree with <NAME>, J., that the idea of the two marks is the same.
Precedent	The marks convey the ideas of glucose and life giving properties of vitamins.
Precedent	The Aquamatic case (<NAME> v. <ORG>) [1958 R.P.C.
Precedent	387], is a recent case where the test of the commonness of the idea between two marks was applied in deciding the question of similarity between them.
Precedent	Again, in deciding the question of similarity between the two marks we have to approach it from the point of view of a man of average intelligence and of imperfect recollection.
Precedent	To such a man the overall structural and phonetic similarity and the similarity of the idea in the two marks is reasonable likely to cause a confusion between them.
Precedent	21.
RatioOfTheDecision	It was then said that the goods were not of the same de scription and that therefore in spite of the similarity of the two marks there would be no risk of confusion or deception.
RatioOfTheDecision	We are unable to accept this contention.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is true that we have to proceed on the basis that the goods are not of the same description for the purposes of s. 10(1) of the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	But there is evidence that glucose is used in the manufacture of biscuits.
RatioOfTheDecision	That would establish a trade connection between the two commodities, namely, glucose manufactured by the appellant and the biscuits produced by the respondent.
RatioOfTheDecision	An average purchaser would therefore be likely to think that the respondent's 'Gluvita biscuits' were made with the appellant's 'Glucovita' glucose.
RatioOfTheDecision	This was the kind of trade connection between different goods which in the "Black Magic" case (In re : an application by <NAME> [58 R.P.C.
RatioOfTheDecision	91]), was taken into consideration in arriving at the conclusion that there was likelihood of confusion or deception.
RatioOfTheDecision	The goods in this case were chocolates and laxatives and it was proved that la xatives were often made with chocolate coatings.
RatioOfTheDecision	We may also refer to the "Panda" case (In re : an application by <NAME> [63 R.P.C.
Precedent	59]).
Precedent	The goods there concerned were shoes and shoe polishes.
Precedent	It was observed that shoe polishes being used for shoes, there was trade connection between them and that this might lead to confusion or deception though the goods were different.
Precedent	The application for registration was however refused under that section of the English Act which corresponds to s. 8 of our Act on the ground that the opponents, the manufacturers of shoes, had not established a reputation for their trademark among the public.
Precedent	22.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is true that in both the above-mentioned cases the two competing trade marks were absolutely identical which is not the case here.
RatioOfTheDecision	But that in our opinion makes no difference.
RatioOfTheDecision	The absolute indentity of the two competing marks or their close resemblance is only one of the tests for determing the question of likelihood of deception or confusion.
RatioOfTheDecision	Trade connection betwee n different goods is another such test.
RatioOfTheDecision	Ex hypothesi, this latter test applies only when the goods are different.
RatioOfTheDecision	These tests are independent tests.
Precedent	There is no reason why the test of trade connection between different goods should not apply where the competing marks closely resemble each other just as much as it applies, as held in the "Black Magic" and "Panda" cases, where the competing marks were identical.
Precedent	Whether by applying these tests in a particular case the conclusion that there is likelihood of deception or confusion should be arrived at would depend on all the facts of the case.
Precedent	23.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is then said that biscuits containing glucose are manufactured with liquid glucose whereas the appellant's mark only concerns powder glucose.
RatioOfTheDecision	We will assume that only liquid glucose is used in the manufacture of biscuits with glucose.
RatioOfTheDecision	But there is nothing to show that an average buyer knows with what kind of glucose, biscuits containing glucose are or can be made.
RatioOfTheDecision	That there is trade connection between glucose and b iscuits and a likelihood of confusion or deception arising therefrom would appear from the fact stated by the appellant that it received from a tradesman an enquiry for biscuits manufactured by it under its mark 'Glucovita'.
RatioOfTheDecision	The tradesman making the enquiry apparently thought that the manufacturer of 'Glucovita' glucose was likely to manufacture biscuits with glucose; he did not worry whether biscuits were made with powder or liquid glucose.
RatioOfTheDecision	Then again it is stated in one of the affidavits filed by the appellant that the respondent's director told the appellant's manager that the respondent had adopted the name 'Gluvita' to indicate that in the manufacture of its biscuits glucose was used.
RatioOfTheDecision	Those statements of behalf of the appellant are not denied by the respondent.
RatioOfTheDecision	So, a trade connection between glucose and biscuits would appear to be established.
RatioOfTheDecision	We are therefore of opinion that the commodities concerned in the present case are so connected as to make confusion or deception likely in view of the similarity  of the two trade marks.
RatioOfTheDecision	We think that the decision of <NAME>, J., was right.
RatioOfTheDecision	24.
RulingByPresentCourt	In the result, we set aside the order of the learned appellate Judges of <ORG> and restore that of <NAME>, J.
RulingByPresentCourt	The appeal is accordingly allowed.
RulingByPresentCourt	The appellant will get the costs before the appellate Judges in <ORG> and in this <ORG>.
Precedent	25.
