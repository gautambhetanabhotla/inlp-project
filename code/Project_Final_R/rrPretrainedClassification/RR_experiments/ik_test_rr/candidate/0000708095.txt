Fact	2) The appellants have come before this Court, being aggrieved by the judgment and order passed by <ORG> at Chandigarh dated 11.09.2006 in CWP No.
Fact	5142/2005 and CWP No.
RulingByLowerCourt	5144/2005.

Fact	Consequently, both of them are clubbed and disposed of by this common Judgment.
Fact	3) The appellants in the instant case are `volunteers' of <ORG>.
Fact	They were recruited and appointed sometime in the year 1989 under <ORG> Act, 1947 and the Rules framed thereunder.
Fact	They were paid consolidated wages of Rs.2700/- per month, from the date of their appointment till their services were dispensed with.
Fact	In the order of termination, it is alleged that the appellants were involved in an act of indiscipline at the Amritsar railway station on 02.10.2004.
Fact	The order of termination was challenged before <ORG> in the above mentioned civil writ petitions.
RatioOfTheDecision	The view of <ORG> is that `volunteers' are persons engaged in Honorary capacity.
RatioOfTheDecision	They have no civil rights and, therefore, the termination of their services on account of the allegations leveled against them cannot be considered to be in violation of law.
Precedent	<ORG> has also placed reliance on the observations made by this Court in the case of State of Gujarat Vs. <NAME> Thakkar (2006) 2 SCC  309.
Argument	4) The learned counsel <NAME> appearing for the appellants, apart from others, submitted, that, the appellants, being temporary employees working from last 15 to 17 years, were entitled to Protec tion Guaranteed under Article 311(2) of the Constitution of India before being terminated.
Argument	Reference is made to the decision of this Court in the case of <NAME> Vs. <ORG> (1958) SCR 828; and <ORG>, <ORG> Mysore Vs. <NAME> (1966) 3 SCR 106; since the appellants are governed by the provisions of Punjab Home Guards Act, 1947 and Punjab Home Guards and Civil Defence (Field) Class III Rules, 1983, their services could not have been terminated without issuing Show Cause Notice and without holding departmental enquiry.
Argument	Alternatively, it is contended that the order of termination passed by the respondents is not only stigmatic but the same has been passed as a consequence of an alleged misconduct committed by the appellants at <ORG>, Amritsar on 02.12.2004.
Argument	Therefore, it is submitted that before any action could have been taken against the appellants, they ought to have been afforded a reasonable opportunity of hearing in consonance  with the principles of natural justice.
Argument	It is, therefore, contended that the action of the respondents is arbitrary and in violation of Principles of Natural Justice.
Argument	5) It is the contention of the learned senior counsel Dr. <NAME> that the appellants are `volunteers', though their appointment is under the Act and the Rules and, therefore, in view of the specific provisions under the Rules, their services could be discharged at any time without issuing a Show Cause Notice and without holding any enquiry, much less a departmental enquiry.
Argument	Alternatively, it is contended that the appellants have no civil rights as they are engaged only as volunteers.
RatioOfTheDecision	Since, the appellants have no civil rights, their services could be terminated for the reasons stated in the order of termination.
RatioOfTheDecision	The learned senior counsel invites our attention to the extract of Para 14.4 of Compendium of Instructions on Home Guards issued by <ORG>, which authorizes the Commandant General or the Commandant to dischar ge any <ORG> at any time, if in his opinion, the services of such <ORG> are no longer required.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is also submitted that the appellants are not temporary employees, but only volunteers in the <ORG> and they are governed by <ORG>, 1963 and not the Punjab <ORG>s and Civil Defence (Field) Class III Rules, 1983.
RatioOfTheDecision	6) In view of the rival contentions canvassed by the learned counsel, the first issue which requires our consideration is, which is the rule which may be made applicable to the parties to this lis.
Argument	The appellants contend, that, they are governed by Punjab <ORG>s and <ORG>, 1983, whereas the Respondents contend that the appellants are governed by the Punjab <ORG> Rules, 1963.
Argument	They also contend that the Punjab <ORG> Rules, 1963 have been repealed by the Punjab <ORG>s and <ORG>, 1983.
Argument	In support of their submission, they cite Rule 20 of the latter which reads as unde r:  "20.Repeal and Saving :- The Punjab Home Guard Rules 1963 as these are the applicable to the members of the service are hereby repealed.
Fact	Provided that anything done or any action taken under the rules so repealed shall be deemed to have been done or taken under the corresponding provisions of these rules."
Fact	7) The respondents submit that there is a distinction between the `volunteers' and `members of the service" of <ORG>.
Fact	It was argued before us, that the 1963 Rules were still applicable to the `volunteer' members of <ORG> and that they are not to be considered as `members of the service' as mentioned under Rule 20 of the 1983 Rules.
Fact	The question before us is, whether the 1983 Rules provide for the repeal of the 1963 rules only in matters relating to `members of the service' and whether the 1963 rules are still applicable for the purpose of recruitment, discharge and dismissal of `volunteers' of <ORG>.
Fact	8) The Punjab Home Guard Rules, 1963 were earlie r repealed by the Punjab Home Guard Rules, 1960.
Fact	Rule 31 of the Punjab Home Guard Rules, 1963 reads:  "Repeal :- The Punjab Home Guard Rules 1960, republished with <ORG> Notification No.
Fact	G.S.R 9P-A-8/47/S.9/6/ dated 19th December 1961 are hereby repealed."
RatioOfTheDecision	9) If we were to juxtapose the two corresponding provisions relating to repeal, there is a marked difference in the wording of Rule 31 of The Punjab Home Guard Rules,1963 and Rule 20 of Punjab Home Guards and Civil Defense (Field) Class III Service Rules, 1983.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is to be noted that the Rule 31 of the 1963 rules unlike Rule 20 of the 1983 rules are categorical in repealing the 1960 Rules.
RatioOfTheDecision	There is no reference to the `members of the service'.
RatioOfTheDecision	10) There is further evidence for such a distinction in Rule 22(2) of the Home Guard Act, 1963.
RatioOfTheDecision	Rule 22 is as under:-  "22 (1) Training: - Every member shall be required to undergo a preliminary course of training in drill, discipline, weapon training and special training of service he be longs to for such period as may be fixed by the Commandant-   General in the case of <ORG> and the Gram Raksha Dal Chief in the case of <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	(2) Such members of the public as may offer themselves voluntarily may also be given training in drill, discipline and the use of weapons."
RatioOfTheDecision	11) It is relevant to note that there is no such provision empowering the state to recruit volunteers from the public under the scheme of the Punjab Home Guards and Civil Defense (Field) Class III Service Rules, 1983.
RatioOfTheDecision	In fact, Rule 22 of the Punjab Home Guard Rules, 1963, is the only Provision which seems to empower the recruitment of volunteers.
RatioOfTheDecision	The 1983 Rules prescribe an elaborate scheme for appointment of members to specific posts enumerated in Appendix `A'.
RatioOfTheDecision	This can be seen from Rule 3 of Punjab Home Guards and Civil Defense (Field) Class III Service Rules, 1983 which states :-  "3.
RatioOfTheDecision	Number and Character of Posts.
RatioOfTheDecision	- The service shall comprise of the posts shown in Appendix `A' to these rules."
RatioOfTheDecision	1 2) The 1983 Rules envisage a scheme where a person who fulfills the requirements under Rule 5 which deals with nationality, domicile and character of candidates; secondly, he must not be specifically disqualified under Rule 6, thirdly, he has to fall under the age group prescribed under Rule 7.
RatioOfTheDecision	On fulfilling these three criteria, he would be eligible for appointment to any of the posts mentioned under Appendix `A' as long as he has the necessary educational qualification.
RatioOfTheDecision	The educational requirement differs depending on the post to which the person is being appointed.
RatioOfTheDecision	On appointment there is a period of probation as prescribed under Rule 9 during which the work and conduct of the appointee is evaluated.
RatioOfTheDecision	The proviso to Rule 9 states that:-  "Provided that the total period of probation, including extension, if any, shall not exceed three years."
RatioOfTheDecision	13) The 1983 Rules deal with the appointment of individuals to specific posts mentioned in Annexure `A', when a temporary or permanent vacancy arises.
RatioOfTheDecision	It does not de al with volunteers who are recruited from the general public.
RatioOfTheDecision	14) The legislative intent for such a distinction with respect to application of the 1983 Rules to `non-volunteer' members is also discernible from a combined reading of Rule 2(n) and Rule 3.
RatioOfTheDecision	15) Rule 2(n) defines `service' as follows:- `Service' means <ORG> and <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	16) If we read the above definition alongwith Rule 3 which states as under :-  "3.
RatioOfTheDecision	The service shall comprise of the posts shown in Appendix `A' to these rules."
RatioOfTheDecision	17) It is, therefore, apparent from the scheme of the Rules that the appointment of volunteers is not envisaged under the scope of the 1983 Rules.
Precedent	Volunteers could be appointed only under Rule 22(2) of the Punjab Home Guard Rules, 1963.
Precedent	(18)Rule 20 is as under:-  "20.
Precedent	Repeal and Saving :- The Punjab Home Guard Rules 1963 as these are the applicable to the members of the service are hereby repealed."
RatioOfTheDecision	1  (19)The repeal applies only to the members of the service ment ioned under Annexure `A' and not to volunteers.
RatioOfTheDecision	Furthermore, the 1983 rules do not confer upon the appointing authority any power to discharge the volunteer when his services are no longer required as provided under Rule 18 of the 1963 Rules.
RatioOfTheDecision	This is because, the 1983 Rules are not meant to apply to volunteers.
RatioOfTheDecision	Under the 1983 Rules, a member of the service can be dismissed only after following the procedure prescribed for that purpose under the Punjab Civil Services (Punishment and Appeal) Rules, 1970.
RatioOfTheDecision	But such a detailed procedure is not envisaged while discharging a volunteer under 1963 rules.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is therefore evident that the legislature intended to preserve this distinction between the `volunteers' and `members of the service' within the scheme of the 1983 Rules.
RatioOfTheDecision	That being the case, we think that it is neither possible nor desirable to dilute the distinction which the legislature intended to preserve, something which falls squarely in the realm of policy.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, in our opinion, the 1963 Rules are appl icable to the appellants in these appeals.
RatioOfTheDecision	(20)Now we take up the second issue.
RatioOfTheDecision	The appellants contend that <ORG> erred in dismissing the writ petition filed by the appellants relying on the decision of this court in <CITATION_904727> .
RatioOfTheDecision	In that case, <NAME> was appointed to the honorary post of District Commandant in <ORG> and then subsequently the order of disengagement was passed by <ORG> vide its order dated 02.12.1995.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is this order which was impugned in the writ petition.
RatioOfTheDecision	This court sustained the order passed by <ORG> primarily on the ground, that the persons involved therein did not act in the terms of undertaking given by them.
RatioOfTheDecision	It has also observed, that the services rendered by those persons was honorary, therefore, no civil consequences were involved.
RatioOfTheDecision	In our view, the facts of that case are different from that of the instant case.
RatioOfTheDecision	In that case, <NAME> was being discharged from a honorary post, his employment was not being termin ated as is being done in the present case.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, in our opinion, <ORG> was not justified in placing reliance on this decision to unsuit the appellants.
RatioOfTheDecision	(21)A judgment, as is well known is the authority for the proposition which it decides and not what can logically be deduced from.
Precedent	This <ORG> in the case of <CITATION_1129696> , has observed:    "The courts should not place reliance on decisions without discussing as to how the factual situation fits in with the fact situation of the decision on which reliance is placed.
Precedent	Observations of the courts are neither to be read as <NAME>'s theorems nor as provisions of the statute and that too taken out of their context.
Precedent	These observations must be read in the context in which they appear to have been stated.
Precedent	<NAME>ments of the courts are not to be construed as statutes.
Precedent	To interpret words, phrases and provisions of a statute, it may become necessary for judges to embark into lengthy discussions but the discussion is meant to explain and not to define.
Precedent	<NAME> es interpret statutes, they do not interpret judgments.
Precedent	They interpret words of statutes; their words are not to be interpreted as statutes."
Precedent	The court has proceeded to add:  "Circumstantial flexibility, one additional or different fact may make a world of difference between conclusions in two cases.
Precedent	Disposal of cases by blindly placing reliance on a decision is not proper."
RatioOfTheDecision	1  (22)There is a substantial difference in the circumstances surrounding the lis in <NAME>'s case and the present case.
RatioOfTheDecision	Firstly, as stated earlier, the appellants in that case were not being terminated from service.
RatioOfTheDecision	Secondly, the revocation of appointment was merely with regard to an honorary post.
Precedent	(23)It is, therefore, necessary to consider whether Order No.
Precedent	E//285 dated 02.12.2004 passed by the respondents is one without authority of law and whether the said order suffers from any other legal infirmities.
Precedent	(24)The order of termination served on the appellants reads :  "In response to above said letters, the following guards who had  created indiscipline at the Railway station Amritsar while boarding the train for going to Maharashtra in connection with election duty maybe terminated today the 2.12.2004 and they cannot be given any allowance from 3.12.2004.
Precedent	Immediate [action] in this regard should be taken on this letter."
Precedent	(25)It is argued on behalf of the Respondents that the appellants were discharged under Rule 18 of the 1963 rules read with para 14.4 of compendium of instructions on Home Guards.
Precedent	Rule 18 of 1963 reads:  "Discharge of Members :- any member may be discharged at any time by the authority which had appointed him when his services are no longer required."
Precedent	(26)The expression `Discharge' was interpreted by this <ORG> in the case of <CITATION_1430996> , wherein, it is stated, "Discharge would connote for any other reason ejusdem generis due to abolition of the post or course of study or such similar circumstances except for discharge due to misconduct.".
RatioOfTheDecision	(27)The abovesaid Rule does not contemplate the requirement of conduc ting an enquiry or giving notice to the concerned person and, therefore, the respondents maintain that the termination order was therefore within the scope and scheme of the Home Gaurds Act, 1947 and the 1963 Rules made thereunder.
RatioOfTheDecision	(28)The order terminating the services of the appellants specifically cites indiscipline at the Amritsar Railway Station as the cause for the termination.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, it is not a case where the appointing authority is discharging the services of the appellants on the ground that their services are no longer required but it is a case where their services are sought to be dispensed with on the ground of indiscipline, which would come within the meaning of the expression `Misconduct'.
RatioOfTheDecision	In such a situation, the respondents cannot terminate the services of the appellants without following the procedure prescribed under Rule 27 of the Rules, the said rules, specifically deals with Discipline.
Statute	It reads as under :-  "Dismissed :- (1) Any officer may for misconduct or for absence without su fficient cause, be dismissed from service.
Precedent	Provided that an order of dismissal shall not be passed unless reason of dismissal are recorded in writing and the member concerned has been given a reasonable opportunity of showing cause against the action proposed to be taken against him."
RatioOfTheDecision	(29)The language employed in the Rule is clear and unambiguous.
RatioOfTheDecision	The Rule envisages that any officer may be dismissed from service either for misconduct or for unauthorized absence.
RatioOfTheDecision	Proviso appended to the Rules speaks of giving an opportunity of hearing to the delinquent officer or the member appointed under the Act and the Rules.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is an admitted position that no such opportunity of hearing or notice was given to the appellants in the present case as is required under Rule  27.
RatioOfTheDecision	In this view of the matter, the respondents cannot be permitted to contend that the appellants being `volunteers', their services could be terminated without complying with the procedure prescribed in the Statutory Rules, which speaks of providing an  opportunity of hearing to the person who would be affected by the proposed action.
RatioOfTheDecision	(30)To us, it appears, after going through the Act and the Rules framed thereunder, that the expression `volunteers' appears to be misnomer.
RatioOfTheDecision	We do not intend to dwell on this issue, since we are told that the writ petitions for the regularization of similarly placed persons are pending before <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	The facts and circumstances pleaded by the appellants and the number of years they have spent as `volunteers' and since they have no other avenue for their alternate employment because of their age factor, we are impelled to look into the reason for the termination of the services of the appellants.
RatioOfTheDecision	The letter discharging their services explicitly states that the reason for discharge is the indiscipline at Amritsar railway station before the appellants were to board the train for Maharashtra on election duty.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, in our view, it is not a case of discharge simplicitor.
RatioOfTheDecision	Under Rule 18 of the 1963 Rules, any member appoi nted under the rules may be discharged at any time by the authority which had appointed him when his services are no longer required.
RatioOfTheDecision	If it is instance of discharge simplicitor, it would necessarily relate to instances where the post has been abolished or where there is a surplus of employees or other similar circumstances.
RatioOfTheDecision	The respondents have not raised the existence of any circumstances which required the discharge of any volunteers, neither has it been urged that there exists any condition which would require the appellants specifically to be discharged apart from the allegation of indiscipline.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, in our view, services of the appellants are discharged for acts of alleged misconduct.
RatioOfTheDecision	It casts a stigma on their competence and affects their future career.
RatioOfTheDecision	(31)In our considered view, even in matters of discharge, the authority concerned cannot act arbitrarily while discharging an employee.
RatioOfTheDecision	However, in the instant case, the appellants are being discharged from service for indiscipline.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, as  provided in proviso to rule 27 of the rules, the appellants should have been given a reasonable opportunity of showing cause against the action proposed to be taken against them.
RatioOfTheDecision	Admittedly, no such opportunity was given to them.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, we are of the view that the action of the respondents is contrary to their own statutory rules and in violation of principles of natural justice.
RatioOfTheDecision	(32)Even without going into the question whether the appellants are eligible for the protection under Article 311 of the Constitution, in our view, the respondents seem to have acted in an arbitrary manner by terminating the services of the appellants, who have been working as Home Guards for the last 15-17 years.
RatioOfTheDecision	They are all over-aged.
RatioOfTheDecision	They may find it difficult to find alternate employment.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, in the facts and circumstances of this case and in the interest of justice, we deem it proper to set aside the order of termination passed by the respondents dated 02.12.2004 and direct the respondents to reinstate the appellan ts as Home Guards without back wages.
RatioOfTheDecision	(33) Before parting with the case, we should also notice the minor issue raised by learned senior counsel for respondents.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is submitted that the appellants without exhausting the appeal remedy provided under rule 27(3) of 1963 rules could not have approached <ORG> under Article 226 of the Constitution, inter-alia, requesting <ORG> to quash the order passed by respondents dated 02.12.2004.
RatioOfTheDecision	We do not find any merit in their submission, for the reason that this issue was not raised nor argued before <ORG> and, therefore, we will not permit this issue to be raised for the first time before us.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is also argued that para 14.4 of compendium of instructions on Home Guards authorizes the Commandant General or the Commandant to discharge <ORG> at any time, if in his opinion, the services of <ORG> are no longer required.
RatioOfTheDecision	These instructions are reiteration of Rule 18 of the Rules.
RatioOfTheDecision	We have already dealt with these rules.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, repet ition of our reasoning once over again may not be necessary.
RulingByPresentCourt	(34) For the reasons stated, we allow these appeals and set aside the impugned judgment.
RulingByPresentCourt	We direct the respondents to reinstate the appellants within four weeks' time from today without back wages.
