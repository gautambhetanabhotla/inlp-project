Fact	1.
Fact	The basic issue involved in this appeal is whether the customs authorities have the right to refuse to endorse the exports made by an Exporter in the <ORG> Book of export Part II after the export is effected by the Exporter.
Fact	2.
Fact	The writ petitioner had exported plastic Flat Jet Nozzles made from <ORG> Granules on 7th January, 1994 to a purchaser at Singapore.
Fact	The FOB value of the Nozzles was declared as Rs.
Fact	48,46,850/-.

Fact	Samples were drawn by them to verify the value of the Nozzles.
Fact	The goods were suspected of being over invoiced.
Fact	Pending enquiry the export was allowed provisionally against a bond.
Fact	Assessment was made provisionally and an endorsement was made on the Shipping bill to this effect by the <ORG> Authorities.
Fact	The <ORG> Authorities however refused to endorse the fact of export in Part II of the <ORG> book of the writ petitioner.
Argument	Unless the <ORG> Authorities make such endorsement the writ petitioner canno t apply for transfer of the import licence for import of duty free goods to which the writ petitioner became entitled by reason of such export.
Argument	It is the petitioner's case that the customs authorities were duty bound to make the endorsement of the export and they had no jurisdiction to question the valuation of the goods.

Argument	9.

Statute	Section 14 provides inter alia that for the purposes of Customs Tariff Act , 1975, or any other law for the time being in force whereunder a duty of customs is chargeable on any goods by reference to their value, the value of such goods shall be deemed to be a price at which such or like goods are ordinarily sold, or offered for sale, for delivery at the time and place of importation or exportation, as the case may be, in the course of international trade, where the seller and the buyer have no interest in the business of each other and the price is the sole consideration for the sale  or offer for sale.

Argument	According to the writ petitioner the jurisdiction of the customs authorities to determine the value was limited to cases where duty is leviable and not in the instant case.

RatioOfTheDecision	We are unable to accept the submission of the writ petitioner.
RatioOfTheDecision	10.
RatioOfTheDecision	To appreciate the scope of the powers of <ORG> it is necessary to consider the origin, nature and scope of the <ORG> scheme.
RatioOfTheDecision	The <ORG>  or the Duty Exemption Entitlement Certificate scheme is a part of the package of incentives formulated by <ORG> for boosting exports.
RatioOfTheDecision	Briefly stated if envisages the grant of import licences to exporters for import of those materials duty free which were required for manufacture of the goods exported.
RatioOfTheDecision	The sale of such import licence or the materials imported duty free in the Indian market are other benefits to attract exporters to increase their exports.
RatioOfTheDecision	Another such beneficial measure for exporters is the total exemption from income tax on the export profits granted under Section 80HHC of the Income Tax Act, 1961.
RatioOfTheDecision	11.
RatioOfTheDecision	The <ORG> scheme in so far as it relates to exemption of import duty has its origin in the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	Under Section 12 of the Act duty of customs are leviable on all goods imported into or exported from India except as otherwise provided in the Act itself or in any other law for the time being in force.
RatioOfTheDecision	<ORG> has the power to exempt certain goods from the levy of cus toms duty by notification in the Official Gazette under Section 25(1) of the Act.
Fact	On 19th May, 1992 in exercise of powers conferred by Sub-section (1) of Section 25 of the Act <ORG> published a notification being Notification No.
Fact	203/92-Cus., dated 19-5-1992 exempting materials imported into India against a value based advance licence issued in terms of Para 49 of the Export and Import Policy, 1992-97 from the whole of the customs duty leviable thereon and from the whole of the additional duty leviable thereon subject to diverse conditions including "(i) that the materials imported are covered by a Value Based Duty Exemption Entitlement Certificate (hereinafter referred to as the said certificate), issued by <ORG> in the form specified in the Schedule annexed to this notification;  * * * * * * (v) that the export obligation is discharged, within the period specified in the said certificate or within such extended period as may be granted by <ORG>, by export ing goods manufactured in India; * * * * * * (vi) where benefit of the notification is sought by a person other than licencee, such benefit shall be allowed against the said licence and the said certificate only if it bears endorsement of transferability by <ORG>."
Fact	On the same date by Notification No.
Fact	204/92-Cus., dated 19-5-1992 also issued under Section 25(1) of the Act similar provisions were made for quantity based duty exemption entitlement certificates.
Fact	12.
Fact	The Licensing Authorities in this case are the Respondents Nos.
Fact	2 and 3.
Fact	The form specified in the schedule for Value Based Exemption Certificate (with which we are concerned in this case) consists of two parts.
Fact	Part I relates to the particulars of import and Part II relates to the particulars of the export.
Fact	Part II in turn consists of nine parts namely, Parts A to I.
Fact	We are concerned particularly with Part F which requires the diverse particulars of exports to be entered into the certificate including the FOB value of the product  in Rupees and US Dollars.
Statute	Column 10 of the Part F requires the "signature of <ORG> Officer with name, designation and seal and remarks if any."
Statute	At the end of the certificate the exporter has to sign a declaration that the information given in the statement are correct.
Statute	The actual imports and exports are also required to be certified by a Chartered Accountant.
Statute	Upon discharge of the prescribed export obligation and realisation of the sale proceeds the exporter is at liberty under Part I to apply for transfer of the licence and the <ORG> to the licensing authority.
Statute	13.
Statute	The requirement in Column 10 of Part II-F of the notification for the <ORG> Authorities to sign the declaration of value would appear to indicate that the <ORG> Authority would have to ascertain the correct value of the exports.
RatioOfTheDecision	If the <ORG> Authorities were merely to act as a rubber stamp on the value declared by the exporter there would have been no necessity for the customs authorities to separately make an endorsement in the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	It m ust also be remembered that it is on the basis of the value that the exporter becomes entitled to import duty free materials.
RatioOfTheDecision	14.
RatioOfTheDecision	This inference is supported by the provisions of the Duty Exemption Scheme itself.
RatioOfTheDecision	The scheme forms part of the Export and Import Policy for the period 1992-97 formulated in exercise of the powers under Section 3 of the Imports and Exports (Control) Act, 1947.
RatioOfTheDecision	The Imports and Exports (Control) Act has been replaced by the Foreign Trade (Development and Regulation) Act , 1992.
RatioOfTheDecision	The policy is now deemed to have been notified under Section 5 of the 1992 Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	The procedures laid down for implementation of the policy are contained in the Handbook of Procedures issued by <ORG> Government India for the period from 1-4-1992 to 31-3-1997.
RatioOfTheDecision	Chapter VII of the Handbook deals with the Duty Exemption Scheme.
RatioOfTheDecision	Para 119 incorporates the substance of the Notifications 203/92 and 204/92.
RatioOfTheDecision	The nature of the obligation of the customs authorities vis-a-vis Column 10 of Part II-F is cl ear from this paragraph.
RatioOfTheDecision	It requires "the details of the actual imports and exports made" to "be entered and authenticated by the customs authorities".
RatioOfTheDecision	The word 'authentication' has been defined as to "to establish the truth of" (See : Shorter Oxford English Dictionary, 3rd Edn., The World Book Dictionary and Black's Law Dictionary, 6th Edn.).
RatioOfTheDecision	Given this meaning of authentication, the submission of the writ petitioner that the customs authorities have to accept the declaration of the exporter as to the value of the goods as being correct is unacceptable.
RatioOfTheDecision	15.
Statute	The <ORG> is in the form of a Book according to Appendix XX to the Handbook.
Statute	The prescribed format is a substantial reproduction of Notification No.
Statute	203/92-Cus., dated 19-5-1992 and 204/92-Cus., dated 19-5-1992 with the same requirement of the signature of the Customs Officer.
Statute	16.
Statute	The only method in which the Customs Authorities can discharge this obligation to authenticate the value is under Section 14(1) of the Act.
Statute	17.
Statute	This follows from Section 20 of  the General Clauses Act, 1897 which provides for the construction of notifications etc.
Statute	issued under enactments and which reads :  "20.
Statute	Whereas, by any Central Act or Regulation, a power to issue any notification, order, scheme, rule, form, or bye-law is conferred then expressions used in the notification, order, scheme, rule, form or bye-law, if it is made after the commencement of this Act, shall unless there is anything repugnant in the subject or context, have the same respective meanings as in the Act or regulation conferring the power."
RatioOfTheDecision	[emphasis supplied] As the <ORG> scheme in fact incorporates the exemptions granted under Section 25(1) of the Act, the value must be determined with reference to the definition of the word in the Act itself.
RatioOfTheDecision	18.
RatioOfTheDecision	The word value has been defined in the Act in Section 2(41) as follows:  "(41) "value", in relation to any goods, means the value thereof determined in accordance with the provisions of Sub-section (1) of Section 14 "  Section 14(1) of the Act therefore merely  lays down a method for the determination of the value of goods and does not limit the jurisdiction of the customs authorities to determine the value of [goods] only to cases of assessment of duty.
RatioOfTheDecision	19.
RatioOfTheDecision	In fact Section 14 is utilised for determination of value under several other enactments where duty is not assessable at all.
RatioOfTheDecision	For example, under the Export Control Order, 1988 Clause 2-h also defines value as having the same meaning as in Section 14(1) of the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	Clause 3(3) of the order also provides that if the value of the goods has been incorrectly declared by the exporter then the export of such goods is prohibited.
RatioOfTheDecision	The export of prohibited goods is impermissible under Section 11 of the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is the customs authorities which will have to determine the question of value under Section 14(1) for the purpose of prohibiting the export of goods in question.
RatioOfTheDecision	This is independent of any question of assessability of the goods exported to duty.
RatioOfTheDecision	20.
RatioOfTheDecision	Similarly, under the Foreign Trade (Regulation) Rules, 1993 whic h have been framed under the Foreign Trade (Development and Regulation) Act , 1992, Rule 2(i) provides that "value" would have the same meaning as was assigned to it in Section 2(41) of the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	Thus the procedure for determination of value under Section 14 of the Act is brought in for the purposes of the 1993 Rules.
RatioOfTheDecision	Rule 11 of the 1993 Rules clearly states that the value of the goods must be stated whether the goods are liable to duty or not and such statement must be certified as being correct.
RatioOfTheDecision	If therefore an incorrect declaration is made this would be a violation of Rule 11 of the 1993 Rules.
RatioOfTheDecision	If an incorrect declaration of the value is made this would violate Section 11 of the 1992 Act which forbids the export or import of goods in violation of the Rules.
RatioOfTheDecision	This in turn would attract the provisions of Sections 111 and 113 of the Act as goods imported or exported contrary to law and give the <ORG> Authorities the power to investigate the correct value of the goods in question and for that purpose utilise t he method prescribed under Section 14(1) .
RatioOfTheDecision	21.
RatioOfTheDecision	Finally, the Foreign Exchange (Regulation) Act , 1973 also provides in Section 18(1) that no goods can be exported unless the exporter furnishes to the prescribed authority a declaration in the prescribed form of inter alia the full export value of the goods supported by evidence.
RatioOfTheDecision	The prescribed authority under Section 18(1) is the customs authorities.
Statute	Under Section 67 of the 1973 Act, the restriction imposed by inter alia Section 18 "shall be determined to have been imposed under Section 11 of the Customs Act and all the provisions of that Act shall have effect accordingly."
RatioOfTheDecision	In other words, the correctness of the declaration may be investigated into by the customs authorities and for such purpose resort may be had to Section 14(1) .
RatioOfTheDecision	22.
RatioOfTheDecision	The argument of the writ petitioner overlooks the provisions of the notifications under Section 25(1) as also the Export Policy as contained in the Handbook of Procedures.
RatioOfTheDecision	Besides if goods are in fact over-invoiced, this means  that the exporter is realising proceeds not really relatable to the export made.
RatioOfTheDecision	Yet by virtue of the exporter's declaration the excess amount would enjoy the benefit of the concessions to which they would otherwise not be entitled.
RatioOfTheDecision	This possibility cannot be ruled out at this stage.
RatioOfTheDecision	23.
RatioOfTheDecision	In this case, according to the customs authorities, the writ petitioner had inflated the value of the Jet Nozzles 84 times so that the profit earned was 8,429 per cent.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is not unreasonable in the circumstances for the customs authorities to hold an enquiry into the proper value after an appropriate enquiry.
RatioOfTheDecision	This determination would be essential particularly in the case of value based license as exemption of duty would be granted on the goods imported corresponding to the export value.
RatioOfTheDecision	To compel <ORG> to make the endorsement on the <ORG> certificate would be to deny the customs authorities' right and indeed their obligation to question the value of the exports made - an obligation which is not discharged onl y because the foreign exchange had been received on the invoiced amount.
RatioOfTheDecision	In fact the receipt of foreign exchange might in reality be a mirage.
RatioOfTheDecision	Apart from the public revenue being defrauded of large amounts of money both on account of income tax and on account of import duty by a false declaration, of value of exports by the exporter, no benefit would accrue to the national exchequer at all as the foreign exchange earned would be used to purchase the duty free imported material.
RatioOfTheDecision	24.
RatioOfTheDecision	To sum up : the <ORG> Authorities power to assess the value of goods is not limited to cases where the goods are assessable to duty.
RatioOfTheDecision	<ORG> are to certify the correctness of the value of the goods under the DEEC Scheme and they cannot be compelled to accept the declared value as correct.
RatioOfTheDecision	For the purposes of ascertaining such correct value the <ORG> are required to follow the mode prescribed in Section 14(1) of the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	25.
