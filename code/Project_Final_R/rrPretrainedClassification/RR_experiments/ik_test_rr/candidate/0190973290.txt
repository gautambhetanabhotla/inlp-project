RatioOfTheDecision	That is what is held in <NAME> case and in <NAME>'s case.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the latter case, the departmental inquiry was stopped because the employer was not sure of establishing the quilt of the employee.
RatioOfTheDecision	In all these cases the allegations against the employee merely raised a cloud on his conduct and as pointed by <NAME>, J. in <ORG> case, the employer was entitled to say that he would not continue an employee against whom allegations were made the truth of which the employer was not interested to ascertain.
RatioOfTheDecision	In fact, the employer, by opting to pass a simple order of termination as permitted by the terms of appointment or as permitted by the rules was conferring a benefit on the employee by passing a simple order of termination so that the employee would not suffer from any stigma which would attach to the rest of his career if a dismissal or other punitive order was passed.
RatioOfTheDecision	The above are all examples where the  allegations whose truth has not been found, and were merely the motive.
RatioOfTheDecision	But in cases where the termination is preceded by an inquiry and evidence is received and findings as to misconduct of a definitive nature are arrived at behind the back of the Officer and where on the basis of such a report, the termination order is issued, such an order will be violative of principles of natural justice inasmuch as the purpose of the inquiry is to find out the truth of the allegations with a view to punish him and not merely to gather evidence for a future regular departmental inquiry.
RatioOfTheDecision	In such cases, the termination is to be treated as based or founded upon misconduct and will be punitive.
RatioOfTheDecision	These are obviously not cases where the employer feels that there is a mere cloud against the employees conduct but are cases where the employer has virtually accepted the definitive and clear findings of the Inquiry Officer, which are all arrived at behind the back of the employee - even though such acceptance of findings is not re corded in the order of termination.
RatioOfTheDecision	That is why the misconduct is the foundation and not merely the motive, in such cases.
RulingByLowerCourt	Coming now to the facts of the case before us, the inquiry officer, Sri <NAME> examined witnesses and in his report dated 22.1.76 has said: "I conclude that Sri <NAME> took a sum of Rs.2000/- from Sri <NAME>, thereafter referring to certain facts said they 'go to prove the correctness of the complaint".
RulingByLowerCourt	Not only that, he concluded "I therefore suggest that service of <NAME> may be terminated and one month salary may be given to him in lied of the notice".
RatioOfTheDecision	The very next day, the impugned simple order of termination followed.
RatioOfTheDecision	In our view, it is an absolutely clear case where the inquiry officer examined witnesses, recorded their statements and gave a clear finding of the appellant accepting a bribe and even recommended his termination.
RatioOfTheDecision	All these were done behind the back of the appellant.
RatioOfTheDecision	The Managing Director passed the termination order the very next day.
RatioOfTheDecision	It ca nnot in the above circumstances be stated, by any stretch of inspection that the report is a preliminary inquiry report.
RatioOfTheDecision	It's findings are definitive.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is not a preliminary report where some facts are gathered and a recommendation is made for a regular departmental inquiry.
RatioOfTheDecision	In view of the principles laid down in the cases referred to above, this case is an obvious case where the report and its findings are the foundation of the termination order and not merely the motive.
RatioOfTheDecision	The <ORG> was right in its conclusion.
RatioOfTheDecision	<ORG> was in grave error in treating such a report as a preliminary report.
RulingByPresentCourt	For all the above reasons, we set aside <ORG> Judgment and restore the <ORG>'s order.
