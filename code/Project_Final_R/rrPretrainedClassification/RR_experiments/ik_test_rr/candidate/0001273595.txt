Precedent	13.
Statute	Before we advert to the aforesaid question, it is appropriate that we briefly notice the relevant provisions contained in the Act.
Statute	The Act was enacted for the acquisition of land needed for public purposes and for companies and for determining the amount of compensation to be made on such acquisition.
Statute	Section 4 makes a provision for publication of preliminary notification notifying that land mentioned therein is needed for a public purpose.
Statute	It provides for the mode of publication of such notification and empowers the authorized officers to make survey and set out the boundaries of the land proposed to be taken amongst other acts as provided in  the said Section.
Statute	Section 5A confers a right on the person interested in any land which has been notified under Section 4(1) as being needed for a public purpose or likely to be needed for public purpose to object to the acquisition of the land.
Statute	It provides that the objector shall be provided an opportunity of hearing and after hearing all such objections and after making such further enquiry, the Collector may submit his report to the appropriate government along with his recommendations on the objections and the record of proceedings.
Statute	Section 6 provides for declaration of intended acquisition in the mode prescribed thereunder.
Statute	The declaration made under Section 6 is conclusive evidence that the land is needed for a public purpose and after making such declaration, the appropriate government may acquire the land in the manner provided in subsequent provisions.
Statute	Section 6 also prescribes time limit in making such declaration.
Statute	Section 9 provides a public notice to be given by the Collector stating that the gov ernment intends to take possession of the land and that claims to compensation for all interests in such land may be made to him.
Statute	As per Section 11 , the Collector is required to enquire into the objections that may be received from the persons interested pursuant to the notice issued under Section 9 and determine the award of compensation, inter alia by enquiring into the value of the land and the respective interests of the persons claiming compensation.
Statute	Section 11-A prescribes the limitation for making an award under Section 11 .
Statute	Section 16 provides for taking possession of the land after the Collector has made an award under Section 11 .
Statute	The special powers in cases of urgency and unforeseen emergency are conferred upon the government under Section 17 .
Statute	Sub-section (1) thereof provides that in case of urgency the appropriate government may direct the Collector to take possession of any land needed for public purpose on expiration of fifteen days from the publication of the notice mentioned in Section 9 , a lthough no award has been made.
Statute	Sub-section (2) confers power on the appropriate government to acquire the immediate possession of the land for the purposes specified thereunder in the cases of unforeseen emergency.
Statute	Sub-section (3A), however mandates that before taking possession of any land under sub-section (1) or sub-section (2), the Collector shall tender payment of 80% of the compensation as estimated by him to the persons entitled thereto and pay the said compensation to them unless prevented by the contingencies under Section 31(2) .
Statute	Sub- section (4) empowers the government to direct that the provisions of Section 5A shall not apply, on its satisfaction that the provisions contained in sub-section (1) or sub-section (2) are applicable and a declaration may be made under Section 6 after the publication of the notification under Section 4(1) .
Precedent	Insofar as Uttar Pradesh is concerned, sub-section (1A) has been inserted after sub-section (1) of Section 17 which provides that the power to take possession unde r sub-section (1) may also be exercised, inter alia, if the land is required for `planned development'.
Precedent	Section 48 gives liberty to the government to withdraw from acquisition of any land.

RatioOfTheDecision	This Court stated the legal position as follows :  "37.
RatioOfTheDecision	We think that Section 17(4) cannot be read in isolation from Section 4(1) and 5-A of the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	The immediate purpose of a notification under Section 4(1) of the Act is to enable those who may have any objections to make to lodge them for purposes of an enquiry under Section 5-A of the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is true that, (1971) 1 SCC 671 (1977) 1 SCC 133 although only 30 days from the notification under Section 4(1) are given for the filing of these objections under Section 5-A of the Act, yet, sometimes the proceedings under Section 5-A are unduly prolonged.
RatioOfTheDecision	But, considering the nature of the objections which are capable  of being successfully taken under Section 5-A , it is difficult to see why the summary enquiry should not be concluded quite expeditiously.
RatioOfTheDecision	In view of the authorities of this <ORG>, the existence of what are prima facie public purposes, such as the one present in the cases before us, cannot be successfully challenged at all by objectors.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is rare to find a case in which objections to the validity of a public purpose of an acquisition can even be stated in a form in which the challenge could succeed.
RatioOfTheDecision	Indeed, questions relating to validity of the notification on the ground of <NAME> fides do not seem to us to be ordinarily open in a summary enquiry under Section 5-A of the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	Hence, there seems to us to be little difficulty in completing enquiries contemplated by Section 5-A of the Act very expeditiously.
RatioOfTheDecision	38.
RatioOfTheDecision	Now, the purpose of Section 17(4) of the Act is, obviously, not merely to confine action under it to waste and arable land but also to situations in which an inquiry under Section 5-A will serve no us eful purpose, or, for some overriding reason, it should be dispensed with.
RatioOfTheDecision	The mind of the officer or authority concerned has to be applied to the question whether there is an urgency of such a nature that even the summary proceedings under Section 5-A of the Act should be eliminated.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is not just the existence of an urgency but the need to dispense with an inquiry under Section 5-A which has to be considered.
RatioOfTheDecision	39.
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 17(2) deals with a case in which an enquiry under Section 5-A of the Act could not possibly serve any useful purpose.
RatioOfTheDecision	Sudden change of the course of a river would leave no option if essential communications have to be maintained.
RatioOfTheDecision	It results in more or less indicating, by an operation of natural physical forces beyond human control, what land should be urgently taken possession of.
RatioOfTheDecision	Hence, it offers no difficulty in applying Section 17(4) in public interest.
RatioOfTheDecision	And, the particulars of what is obviously to be done in public interest need not be concealed when its validity is questioned in a Cou rt of justice.
RatioOfTheDecision	Other cases may raise questions involving consideration of facts which are especially within the knowledge of the authorities concerned.
RatioOfTheDecision	And, if they do not discharge their special burden, imposed by Section 106 , Evidence Act , without even disclosing a sufficient reason for their abstention from disclosure, they have to take the consequences which flow from the non-production of the best evidence which could be produced on behalf of the <ORG> if its stand was correct.
RatioOfTheDecision	40.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the case before us, the public purpose indicated is the development of an area for industrial and residential purposes.
RatioOfTheDecision	This, in itself, on the face of it, does not call for any such action, barring exceptional circumstances, as to make immediate possession, without holding even a summary enquiry under Section 5-A of the Act, imperative.
RatioOfTheDecision	On the other hand, such schemes generally take sufficient period of time to enable at least summary inquiries under Section 5-A of the Act to be completed without any impediment whatsoev er to the execution of the scheme.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, the very statement of the public purpose for which the land was to be acquired indicated the absence of such urgency, on the apparent facts of the case, as to require the elimination of an enquiry under Section 5-A of the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	41.
RatioOfTheDecision	Again, the uniform and set recital of a formula, like a ritual or mantra, apparently applied mechanically to every case, itself indicated that the mind of the Commissioner concerned was only applied to the question whether the land was waste or arable and whether its acquisition is urgently needed.
RatioOfTheDecision	Nothing beyond that seems to have been considered.
RatioOfTheDecision	The recital itself shows that the mind of the Commissioner was not applied at all to the question whether the urgency is of such a nature as to require elimination of the enquiry under Section 5- A of the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	If it was, at least the notifications gave no inkling of it at all.
RatioOfTheDecision	On the other hand, its literal meaning was that nothing beyond matters stated there were considered.
RatioOfTheDecision	42.
RatioOfTheDecision	All schemes  relating to development of industrial and residential areas must be urgent in the context of the country's need for increased production and more residential accommodation.
RatioOfTheDecision	Yet, the very nature of such schemes of development does not appear to demand such emergent action as to eliminate summary enquiries under Section 5-A of the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	There is no indication whatsoever in the affidavit filed on behalf of the <ORG> that the mind of the Commissioner was applied at all to the question whether it was a case necessitating the elimination of the enquiry under Section 5-A of the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	The recitals in the notifications, on the other hand, indicate that elimination of the enquiry under Section 5-A of the Act was treated as an automatic consequence of the opinion formed on other matters.
RatioOfTheDecision	The recital does not say at all that any opinion was formed on the need to dispense with the enquiry under Section 5-A of the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is certainly a case in which the recital was at least defective.
RatioOfTheDecision	The burden, therefore, rested upon the  <ORG> to remove the defect, if possible, by evidence to show that some exceptional circumstances which necessitated the elimination of an enquiry under Section 5-A of the Act and that the mind of the Commissioner was applied to this essential question.
RatioOfTheDecision	It seems to us that <ORG> correctly applied the provisions of Section 106 of the Evidence Act to place the burden upon the <ORG> to prove those special circumstances, although it also appears to us that <ORG> was not quite correct in stating its view in such a manner as to make it appear that some part of the initial burden of the petitioners under Sections 101 and 102 of the Evidence Act had been displaced by the failure of the <ORG> to discharge its duty under Section 106 of the Act.

Precedent	In our view, since the existence of houses/structures and buildings as on November 22, 2003/February 20, 2004 over the appellants' land has been seriously disputed, it may not be appropriate to issue any direction to <ORG>, as prayed for by the appellants, for release of their land from acquisitio n. However, as the possession has not been taken, the interest of justice would be subserved if the appellants are given liberty to make representation to the <ORG> authorities under Section 48(1) of the Act for release of their land.
Precedent	We, accordingly, grant liberty to the appellants to make appropriate representation to <ORG> and observe that if such representation is made by the appellants within two months from today, <ORG> shall consider such representation in accordance with law and in conformity with the <ORG> policy for release of land under Section 48(1) without any discrimination within three months from receipt of such representation.
Precedent	34.
Precedent	In the result, these appeals fail and are dismissed, subject to the liberty reserved to the appellants for making representations under Section 48 (1) of the Act.
