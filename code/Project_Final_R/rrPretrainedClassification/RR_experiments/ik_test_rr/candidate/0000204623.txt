RatioOfTheDecision	15.
RatioOfTheDecision	The learned counsel appearing for the assessee firm has submitted before us - and we think rightly - that the approach of the High <ORG> was erroneous in that they took up the discussion of question No.
RatioOfTheDecision	1 first.
RatioOfTheDecision	That ques tion, as framed, proceeded on the assumption that section 10A applied to the case and only raised the question as to whether there was any evidence to support the finding of <ORG> arrived at as a result of the enquiry under that section, namely, that the main purpose of the transaction was the avoidance of excess profits tax.
RatioOfTheDecision	The long title and the preamble of the Excess Profits Tax Act refer to the imposition of tax on excess profits arising out of certain businesses.
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 4 , which is the charging section and section 5 which lays down the application of the Act to certain business, clearly postulate the existence of a business carried on by the assessee on the profits of which the excess profits tax can be imposed.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, if there is such a business during the relevant period, then and then alone can arise the question of the applicability of section 10A .
RatioOfTheDecision	If there is no such business as is contemplated by the Act, then the Act does not apply and section 10A cannot come into operatio n at all.
RatioOfTheDecision	Before the Excess Profits Tax Officer can embark upon an enquiry as to whether a transaction was effected for the avoidance or reduction of liability to excess profits tax and to make such adjustments as he considers appropriate there must be proof that the assessee was, during the chargeable accounting period, carrying on any business of the kind referred to in section 5 of the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	Logically, therefore, <ORG> as well as <ORG> should have taken up question No.
RatioOfTheDecision	3 first, for on a decision of that question would depend the applicability of section 10A and if that question were answered in favour of the assessee firm the further question of law as raised in question No.
RatioOfTheDecision	1 would not, in such event, arise.
RatioOfTheDecision	The approach of <ORG> was, therefore, logically misconceived on the facts of this case.
RatioOfTheDecision	16.
RatioOfTheDecision	What then are the facts found by <ORG> apart from its findings under section 10A ?
RatioOfTheDecision	The findings are that after the formation of the company the assessee firm  was left with no business at all.
RatioOfTheDecision	The company purchased the leasehold rights in the lands and buildings where the plant, machinery, etc., were installed.
RatioOfTheDecision	The firm as such ceased to manufacture any ribbons and laces.
RatioOfTheDecision	It was left with the plant, machinery, etc., which it did not require and which ceased to be a commercial asset in its hands, for it had no longer any manufacturing business at all.
RatioOfTheDecision	Further, the assessee firm had put it out of its power to use the plant, machinery, etc., for it had no right in the lands and buildings where the plant, machinery, etc., had been installed.
RatioOfTheDecision	In these circumstances, the assessee firm let out the plant, machinery, etc., to the company.
RatioOfTheDecision	It was thenceforth the company which was carrying on the business of manufacturing ribbons and laces and for that purpose hired the plant, machinery, etc., from the assessee firm.
Fact	Prima facie it was the company which appointed the managing agents and the selling agents.
Fact	Ex facie and apart from the alleged result of any enquiry under sectio n 10 or section 10A of the Excess Profits Tax Act those were not transactions of the assessee firm.
Fact	The assessee firm was, therefore, left only with some property which at one time was a commercial asset but had ceased to be so.
Fact	The assessee firm thereupon let out that property on rent.
Fact	The question is whether such letting out in such circumstances amounted to carrying on of a business.
RatioOfTheDecision	17.
RatioOfTheDecision	"Business" as defined in section 2(5) of the Excess Profits Tax Act includes amongst others, any trade, commerce or manufacture or any adventure in the nature of trade, commerce or manufacture.
RatioOfTheDecision	The first part of this definition of "a business" in the Excess Profits Tax Act is the same as the definition of a business in section 2(4) of the Indian Income-tax Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	Whether a particular activity amounts to any trade, commerce or manufacture or any adventure in the nature of trade, commerce or manufacture is always a difficult question to answer.
RatioOfTheDecision	On the one hand it has been pointed out by <ORG> in <CITATION_607 861> .
RatioOfTheDecision	((1932) I.L.R.
RatioOfTheDecision	59 Cal.
RatioOfTheDecision	1343), that the words used in that definition are no doubt wide but underlying each of them is the fundamental idea of the continuous exercise of an activity.
RatioOfTheDecision	The word "business" connotes some real, substantial and systematic or organised course of activity or conduct with a set purpose.
RatioOfTheDecision	On the others hand, a single and isolated transaction has been held to be conceivably capable of falling within the definition of business as being an adventure in the nature of trade provided the transaction bears clear indicia of trade.
RatioOfTheDecision	The question, therefore, whether a particular source of income is business or not must be decided according to our ordinary notions as to what a business is.
Precedent	The case of <CITATION_990746> .
Precedent	( S.C.R.
Precedent	1), decided by this <ORG> is clearly distinguishable.
Precedent	There, the respondent company which was formed for the purpose of manufacturing silk cloth installed a plant for dying silk yarn as a part of its business.
Precedent	During the relevant chargeable accounting period, owing t o difficulty in obtaining silk yarn on account of the war, it could not make any use of this plant and it remained idle for some time.
Precedent	In August, 1943, the plant was let out to another company on a monthly rent.
Precedent	The question arose whether the income received by the respondent company in the chargeable accounting period by way of rent was income from business and assessable to excess profits tax.
Precedent	It should be noted that in that case the respondent company was continuing its business of manufacturing silk cloth.
Precedent	Only a part of its business, namely, that of dying silk yarn had to be temporarily stopped owing to the difficulty in obtaining silk yarn on account of the war.
Precedent	In such a situation, this Court held that that part of the assets did not cease to be commercial assets of that business since it was temporarily put to different use or let out to another and accordingly the income from the assets would be profits of the business irrespective of the manner in which that asset was exploited by the company.
Precedent	This  Court clearly indicated that no general principle could be laid down which would be applicable to all cases and that each case must be decided on its own circumstances according to ordinary common sense principles.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the case before us the assessee firm's business had entirely closed.
RatioOfTheDecision	It no longer manufactured any ribbons and laces.
RatioOfTheDecision	It had accordingly no further trading or commercial activity.
RatioOfTheDecision	It could not in fact use the plant, machinery, etc., after the land and the buildings where they were installed had been sold to the company.
RatioOfTheDecision	In these circumstances the assessee firm let out the plant, machinery, etc., on an annual rent of Rs.
RatioOfTheDecision	40,000.
RatioOfTheDecision	These facts are very similar to those found in <ORG> ( 2 A.E.R.
RatioOfTheDecision	609).
RatioOfTheDecision	There the war conditions had reduced the company's business to very small proportions.
RatioOfTheDecision	In that situation it was observed that in that case the company dealt with part of its property which had become redundant and was sublet purely to produce income - a tr ansaction quite apart from the ordinary business activities of the company.
RatioOfTheDecision	The ratio decidendi in that case which was noticed in the judgment of this Court appears to us to apply to the facts found in the present case apart from the findings under section 10A .
RatioOfTheDecision	Applying also the common sense principle to the fact so found it is impossible to hold that the letting out of the plant, machinery, etc., was at all a business operation when its normal business activity had come to a close.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is interesting to note that sub-sections (3) and (4) of section 12 of the Indian Income-tax Act recognise that letting out of plant, machinery, etc., may be a source of income falling under the head "other sources" within that section and not necessarily under the head "business" dealt with in section 10 of that Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the facts and circumstances of this case, therefore, the letting out of the plant, machinery, etc., cannot be held to fall within the body of the definition of "business" under section 2(5) of the Excess Profit s Tax Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	In this view of the matter it is not necessary for us to express an opinion as to the meaning or implication of the proviso to that definition or rule 4(4) of Schedule I to the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	In our opinion, in the facts and circumstances of this case, question No.
RatioOfTheDecision	3 should have been answered in the negative.
