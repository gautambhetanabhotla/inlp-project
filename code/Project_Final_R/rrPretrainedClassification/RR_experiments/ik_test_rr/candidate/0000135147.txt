RatioOfTheDecision	That clearly is a question of interpretation of the sub-section.
RatioOfTheDecision	Now the language of sub-s. (2) seems to us to be perfectly plain and to admit of no doubt or confusion.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is that an insurer to whom the requisite notice of the action has been given " shall be entitled to be made a party thereto and to defend the action on any of the following grounds, namely," after which comes an enumeration of the grounds.
RatioOfTheDecision	It would follow that an insurer is entitled to defend on any of the grounds enumerated and no others.
RatioOfTheDecision	If it were not so, then of course no grounds need have been enumerated.
RatioOfTheDecision	When the grounds of defence have been specified, they cannot be added to.
RatioOfTheDecision	To do that would be adding words to the statute.
RatioOfTheDecision	Sub-section(6) also indicates clearly how sub-s. (2)should be read.
Precedent	It says that no insurer to whom the notice of the action has  been given shall be entitled to avoid his liability under sub-s. (1) " otherwise than in the manner provided for in sub-section.

Precedent	(2) is by successfully raising any of the defences therein mentioned.

Precedent	Therefore sub-s. (6) clearly contemplates that he cannot take any defence not mentioned in subS.
Precedent	(2).
Precedent	If he could, then he would have been in a position to avoid his liability in a manner other than that provided for in sub-s. (2).
Precedent	That is prohibited by sub-s. (6).
Precedent	We therefore think that sub-s. (2) clearly provides that an insurer made a defendant to the action is not entitled to take any defence which is not specified in it.
Precedent	Three reported decisions were cited at the bar and all of them proceeded on the basis that an insurer had no right to defend the action except on the grounds mentioned in sub-s. (2).
Precedent	These are <ORG> v. <ORG> askar (1), <CITATION_1908874> (2) and <CITATION_1141965> (3).

RatioOfTheDecision	If that was what the legislature intended, then there was nothing to prevent it from expressing its intention.
RatioOfTheDecision	What the legislature has done is to enumerate in sub-s. (2) the defences available to an insurer and to provide by sub-s. (6) that he cannot avoid his liability excepting by  means of such defences.

RatioOfTheDecision	The learned Solicitor-General concedes this and says that the only word that has to be added is the word " also" after the word "grounds".
RatioOfTheDecision	But even this the rules of interpretation do not permit us to do unless the section as it stands is meaningless or of doubtful meaning, neither of which we think it is.
RatioOfTheDecision	The addition suggested will, in our view, make the language used unhappy and further effect a complete change in the meaning of the words used in the sub-section.
Precedent	As to sub-s. (6) the learned Solicitor-General contended that the proper reading of it was that an insurer could not avoid his liability except by way of a defence upon being made a party to the action under sub-s. (2).

Precedent	(2) but only meant, by way of defending the suit the right to do which is given by  sub-s. (2).
Precedent	We think that this is a very forced construction of sub-s. (6) and we are unable to adopt it.
Precedent	The only manner of avoiding liability provided for in sub-s. (2) is through the defences therein mentioned.
Precedent	Therefore when sub-s. (6) talks of avoiding liability in the manner provided in sub-s. (2), it necessarily refers to these defences.
Precedent	If the contention of the learned Solicitor- General was right, sub-s. (6) would have provided that the insurer would not be entitled to avoid his liability except by defending the action on being made a party thereto.
Precedent	There is another ground on which the learned Solicitor- General supported the contention that all defences are open to an insurer excepting those taken away by sub-s. (3).
Precedent	He said that before the Act came into force, an injured person had no right of recourse to the insurer and that it was s. 96(1) that made the judgment obtained by the injured person against the assured binding on the insurer and gave him a right against the insurer.
Precedent	He then said that b eing so, it is only fair that a person sought to be made bound by a judgment should be entitled to resist his liability under it by all defences which he can in law advance against the passing of it.
Precedent	Again, we find the contention wholly unacceptable.
Precedent	The Statute has no doubt created a liability in the insurer to the injured person but the statute has also expressly confined the right to avoid that liability to certain grounds specified in it.
Precedent	It is not for us to add to those grounds and therefore to the statute for reasons of hardship.
Precedent	We are furthermore not convinced that the statute causes any hardship.
RatioOfTheDecision	First, the insurer has the right, provided he has reserved it by the policy, to defend the action in the name of the assured and if he does so, all defences open to the assured can then be urged by him and there is no other defence that he claims to be entitled to urge.
RatioOfTheDecision	He can thus avoid all hardship if any, by providing for a right to defend the action in the name of the assured and this he has full libert y to do.
RatioOfTheDecision	Secondly, if he has been made to pay something which on the contract of the policy he was not, bound to pay, he can under the proviso to sub-s. (3) and under sub-s. (4) recover it from the assured.
RatioOfTheDecision	It was said that the assured might be a man of straw and the insurer might not be able to recover anything from him.
RatioOfTheDecision	But the answer to that is that it is the insurer's bad luck.
RatioOfTheDecision	In such circumstances the injured person also would not have been able to recover the damages suffered by him from the assured, the person causing the injuries.
RatioOfTheDecision	The loss had to fall on some one and the statute has thought fit that it shall be borne by the insurer.
RatioOfTheDecision	That also seems to us to be equitable for the loss falls on the insurer in the course of his carrying on his business, a business out of which he makes profit, and he could so arrange his business that in the net result he would never suffer a loss.
RulingByLowerCourt	On the other hand, if the loss fell on the injured person, it would be due to no fault of his; it would have been a loss suf fered by him arising out of an incident in the happening of which he had no hand at all.
RulingByLowerCourt	We therefore feel that the plain words of sub-s.(2) should prevail and that no ground exists to lead us to adopt the extraordinary course of adding anything to it.
RulingByLowerCourt	We think that <ORG> was right in the view that it took, In the result these appeals are dismissed with costs.
