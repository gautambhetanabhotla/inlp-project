Fact	JUDGMENT <NAME>, J.
Fact	1.
Fact	This is an appeal under Section 2(1)(a) of the Supreme Court (Enlargement of Criminal Appellate Jurisdiction) Act, 1970.
Fact	The appellants were tried by the <ORG> Judge, Panaji for the offence of intentionally causing the death of one <NAME> (hereinafter referred to as the deceased) on 19th October, 1971 at a place between two villages in Goa, namely, Cananguinim and Nuvem.
Fact	The learned <ORG> Judge acquitted both the appellants since in his opinion the evidence led on behalf of the prosecution suffered from serious infirmities and did not bring home the offence against the appellants beyond reasonable doubt.
Fact	The <ORG> preferred an appeal against the acquittal of the appellants to <ORG>, Goa.
Fact	The learned Additional Judicial Commissioner, who heard the appeal, disagreed with the view taken by the learned <ORG> Judge reversing the acquittal of the appellants, convicted them of the offence under Section 302 read with Section 34 of the  Indian Penal Code for intentionally causing the death of the deceased and sentenced each of them to suffer imprisonment for life.
Fact	The appellants challenge their conviction and sentence in the present appeal.
Fact	2.
Fact	The prosecution case against the appellants was that the incident which took place on 19th October, 1971 resulting in the death of the deceased was the culmination of a long standing feud between the deceased and the members of his family on the one side and almost the entire village of Canenguinim on the other.
Fact	The conflict centered round a plot of land adjoining the property of <ORG> and though the plot was in the actual possession of the deceased and the members of his family, a claim was made to it on behalf of the <ORG> and the rest of the villagers were supporting the claim of the <ORG>.
Fact	There were various incidents of violence between the two groups since 1969 and there were complaints and cross-complaints resulting in prosecutions of persons belonging to both groups.
Fact	This h ad led to great tension in the village and as a security measure, two con stables had been posted in the village as duty guards.
Fact	3.
Fact	It appears that on 19th October, 1971 the deceased, who was an old man aged more than 60 years, accompanied by his grandson <NAME>, went to the neighbouring village Nuvem to engage labourers for work in his paddy field.
Fact	He left village Cananguinim at about 2.30 p.m. and after arranging for labourers in village Nuvam, he started for the return journey along with <NAME>.
Fact	The road from Nuvem to Kananguinim was a mere footpath and it led across a hill covered with dense thorny shrubs and trees.
Fact	Whilst the deceased and <NAME> were passing through the forest on the hill, the appellants came from behind and attacked the deceased.
Fact	The first appellant had a knife and the second appellant a 'coita' and with these weapons they caused injuries on the neck and other parts of the body of the deceased resulting in his death.
Fact	The appellants then dragged the dead-body from the footpath inside th e forest.
Fact	On seeing this <NAME> ran towards the village shouting that his grand-<NAME> has been cut.
Fact	On the way, he met his <NAME> <NAME> who was returning from his field and on being questioned by <NAME>, <NAME> told him that '<NAME>', meaning thereby the first appellant, and <NAME>'s son, meaning thereby appellant No.
Fact	2, had killed his grand-<NAME>.
Fact	<NAME> was accompanied by his servant <NAME> and he sent <NAME> home along with <NAME> and himself rushed to the scene of the offence.
Fact	After he had gone a little distance, he noticed a black cloth bag lying on the footpath and he also found blood stains and chappals of his <NAME> and following drag marks on the grass, which also bore blood stains, he walked down the slope of hill inside the forest and saw the dead body of the deceased lying on the ground with both the appellants gazing at it.
Fact	On seeing this he shouted "<NAME>, why are you killing my <NAME>" and also pretended to call for help, on which the appellants ran away.
Fact	<NAME> thereafter pr oceeded towards the village and on the way he met his sister, mother, daughter of his sister and <NAME> who were coming towards the scene of the offence.
Fact	<NAME> took all of them to the place where the dead body was lying and after leaving them to guard the dead body, he came home with <NAME> and then after leaving <NAME> at home, he proceeded to Quepem for the purpose of lodging the first information report.
Fact	The first information report was taken down by <NAME> and he immediately thereafter, started the investigation.
Fact	On the completion of the investigation the appellants were ultimately charge-sheeted and tried before the learned Sessions Judge.
Fact	The trial ended in the acquittal of the appellants, but on appeal the acquittal was reversed and the appellants were convicted under Section 302 read with Section 34 and sentenced to suffer life imprisonment.
Fact	Hence the present appeal.
Argument	4.
Argument	It is evident from the judgment of the learned Additional Judicial Commissioner that the conviction of the appellants r ests primarily on the oral evidence of <NAME> and <NAME> and certain circumstantial evidence relating to the arrest of appellant No.
Argument	1 and the recovery of 'coita' as a result of statement made by appellant No.
Argument	2.
RatioOfTheDecision	We will presently examine first the evidence, but before we do so, we must point out that this is a case where the acquittal of the appellants by the learned Sessions Judge has been reversed by the learned Additional Judicial Commissioner.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is now well settled that though the appellate court has the same powers as <ORG> of appreciating evidence and coming to its own conclusion on questions of fact, it should not interfere with an acquittal, unless it finds that the view taken by <ORG> is unreasonable or perverse.
RatioOfTheDecision	If the view taken by <ORG> is a reasonably possible view, the appellate court should not disturb an acquittal merely because it thinks that another view is better or more preferable.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is from this point of view that we must proceed to examine whether the le arned Additional Judicial Commissioner was right in reversing the acquittal of the appellants.
RatioOfTheDecision	5.
RatioOfTheDecision	Turning first to the evidence of <NAME>, it may be pointed out straightway that he was a child witness aged only 6 years at the time when he gave evidence.
RatioOfTheDecision	His evidence is, therefore, to be approached with great caution.
RatioOfTheDecision	He was, according to the prosecution, the only eye-witness to the crime.
RatioOfTheDecision	We have carefully gone through his evidence, but we are constrained to observe that even after making the utmost allowance in his favour in view of the fact that he is a child witness, we find it difficult to accept his testimony.
RatioOfTheDecision	There are several contradictions from which his evidence suffers, such as who had which weapon, but it is not merely on account of these contradictions of a minor character that we are inclined to reject his evidence.
RatioOfTheDecision	There are serious infirmities affecting his evidence and of them, the most important is that he is supposed to have given the name of appellant No.
RatioOfTheDecision	2 as the assailant of the deceased  even though he had never seen him before the date of the incident.
RatioOfTheDecision	He stated in his evidence that when on seeing the appellants dragging the body of the deceased after attacking him, he ran towards the village, he met his <NAME> <NAME> on the way and on being questioned by <NAME>, he said that the deceased had been cut by <NAME> and <NAME>, that is appellants Nos.

Argument	2 from before, to which he answered by saying that he had not seen appellant No.
RatioOfTheDecision	2 at any earlier point of time.
RatioOfTheDecision	Now, if this witness had never seen appellant No.
RatioOfTheDecision	2 before, it is impossible to understand how he  could give his name as the assailant of the deceased when he met <NAME> on the way to the village.

RatioOfTheDecision	This answer given by <NAME> clearly impairs the value of his evidence and casts a serious doubt on his veracity.
Argument	It shows that he had been prevailed upon by the prosecution to falsely implicate appellant No.
Argument	2 and if his evidence in regard to the presence of appellant No.

RatioOfTheDecision	Then again, according to <NAME>, his <NAME> <NAME> sent him back home with <NAME> and atone proceeded to the place where the body of the deceased was lying.

RatioOfTheDecision	However, turning back to the evidence of <NAME>, we find that in the committal court <NAME> narrated an entirely different story.
RatioOfTheDecision	There he stated that he accompanied his <NAME> <NAME> back to the scene of offence and showed him the place where the deceased had been assaulted and then <NAME> walked to the place where the dead body of the deceased was lying.
RatioOfTheDecision	When <NAME> was confronted with this statement made by him in the committal court, he first refused to admit that he had made such a statement, but then he accepted it.
RatioOfTheDecision	This contradiction is again of a very serious nature and we do not think it would be safe at all to rely on the testimony of <NAME>.
RatioOfTheDecision	6.
RatioOfTheDecision	The evidence of <NAME> also suffers from inherent improbabilities.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is difficult to accept that <NAME> asked <NAME> to take <NAME> er home and proceeded alone to the scene of offence not knowing where the offence had taken place.

RatioOfTheDecision	Then again, when <NAME> traces the body of the deceased by following the clues left on the foot path, he finds the appellants standing near the body gazing at it.
RatioOfTheDecision	That is again something wholly impossible to believe.

RatioOfTheDecision	8.
RatioOfTheDecision	There was also evidence of test identification parade of the appellants held by <NAME>, <NAME> of Salceta.

RatioOfTheDecision	If he does not himself give such evidence and leaves it to the officer holding the identification parade to do so, the defence would be deprived of an opportunity of cross-examination for the purpose of showing that the witness had an o pportunity of seeing the accused before they were brought for identification.
RatioOfTheDecision	In any event, the evidence in regard to identification at the test identification parade is at the highest corroborative piece of evidence and if the evidence of <NAME> suffers from serious infirmities and cannot be accepted, the evidence in regard to identification by him at the test identification parade cannot improve the situation.
