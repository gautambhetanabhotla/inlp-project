Precedent	27.
RatioOfTheDecision	We find considerable force in the above argument of the learned counsel for the appellants.
RatioOfTheDecision	The scheme of the Act is clear which provides for issuance of preliminary notification under sub-section (1) of Section 4 of the Act empowering the appropriate <ORG> to issue such notification for acquisition of land needed or likely to be needed for any public purpose.
RatioOfTheDecision	Since the property belongs to a private individual, unless there is a `public purpose' as defined in clause (f) of Section 3 of the Act, no acquisition of land can be made.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is in exercise  of power of eminent domain that a sovereign may acquire property which does not belong to him.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the circumstances, as a general rule, before exercise of power of eminent domain, law must provide an opportunity of hearing against the proposed acquisition.
RatioOfTheDecision	Even without a specific provision to that effect, general law requires raising of objections by and affording opportunity of hearing to the owner of the property.
RatioOfTheDecision	The Land Acquisition (Amendment) Act , 1923 (Act 38 of 1923), however, expressly made such provision by inserting Section 5A in the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is, therefore, clear that after issuance of preliminary notification under Section 4 before and final notification under Section 6 of the Act, the appropriate Government is enjoined to hear persons interested in the property before he is deprived of his ownership rights.
RatioOfTheDecision	But then there may be cases of `urgency' or `unforeseen emergency' which may brooke no delay for acquisition of such property in larger public interest.
RatioOfTheDecision	The <ORG>, therefore, thought it  appropriate to deal with such cases of exceptional nature and in its wisdom enacted Section 17 .
RatioOfTheDecision	28.
RatioOfTheDecision	Whereas sub-section (1) of Section 17 deals with cases of `urgency', sub-section (2) of the said section covers cases of `sudden change in the channel of any navigable river or other unforeseen emergency'.
RatioOfTheDecision	But even in such cases i.e.
RatioOfTheDecision	cases of `urgency' or `unforeseen emergency', enquiry contemplated by Section 5A cannot ipso facto be dispensed with which is clear from sub-section (4) of Section 17 of the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	29.
RatioOfTheDecision	Sub-section (4) of Section 17 is an enabling provision and it declares that if in the opinion of the appropriate <ORG>, the provisions of sub-section (1) or (2) are applicable, it may direct that the provisions of Section 5A would not apply.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is, therefore, clear that the <ORG> has contemplated that there may be `urgencies' or `unforeseen emergencies' and in such cases, private properties may be acquired.
RatioOfTheDecision	But, it was also of the view that normally even in such cases, i.e.
Precedent	cases of  urgencies or unforeseen emergencies, the owner of property should not be deprived of his right to property and possession thereof without following proper procedure of law as contemplated by Section 5A of the Act unless the urgency or emergency is of such a nature that the Government is convinced that holding of enquiry or hearing of objections may be detrimental to public interest.

Precedent	The Court stated;  "It will be seen that s. 17(1) gives power to the Government to direct the Collector, though no award has been made under s. 11 , to take possession of any waste or arable land needed for public purpose and such land thereupon vests absolutely in the Government free from all encumbrances.

RatioOfTheDecision	Then comes s. 17(4) which provides that in case of any land to which the provisions of sub-s. (1) are  applicable, the <ORG> may direct that the provisions of s. 5-A shall not apply and if it does so direct, a declaration may be made under s. 6 in respect of the land at any time after the publication of the notification under s. 4(1) .
RatioOfTheDecision	It will be seen that it is not necessary even where the <ORG> makes a direction under s. 17(1) that it should also make a direction under s. 17(4) .
RatioOfTheDecision	If the <ORG> makes a direction only under s. 17(1) the procedure under s. 5-A would still have to be followed before a notification under s. 6 is issued, though after that procedure has been followed and a notification under s. 6 is issued the Collector gets the power to take possession of the land after the notice under s. 9 without waiting for the award and on such taking possession the land shall vest absolutely in <ORG> free from all encumbrances.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is only when the <ORG> also makes a declaration under s. 17(4) that it becomes unnecessary to take action under s. 5-A and make a report thereunder.
RatioOfTheDecision	It may  be that generally where an order is made under s.17(1), an order under s. 17(4) is also passed; but in law it is not necessary that this should be so.
RatioOfTheDecision	It will also be seen that under the Land  Acquisition Act an order under s. 17 (1) or s. 17(4) can only be passed with respect to waste or arable land and it cannot be passed with respect to land which is not waste or arable and on which buildings stand".
RatioOfTheDecision	(emphasis supplied)  33.
RatioOfTheDecision	From the above observations, it is clear that even in cases falling under or covered by sub-sections (1) and (2) of Section 17 of the Act and the Government intends to acquire land in cases of `urgency' or `unforeseen emergency', it is still required to follow procedure under Section 5A of the Act before issuance of final notification under Section 6 of the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is only when the Government also makes a declaration under sub- section (4) of Section 17 that it becomes unnecessary to take recourse to procedure under Section 5A of the Act.

RatioOfTheDecision	In our judgment, from the above case law, it is clear that normal rule for acquisition of land under the Act is issuance of notification under sub-section (1) of Section 4 , hearing of objections under Section 5A and issuance of final notification under Section 6 of the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	Award will be made by  the Collector, notice has to be issued to the land- owners or the person interested and thereafter possession can be taken.
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 17 , no doubt, deals with special situations and exceptional circumstances covering cases of `urgency' and `unforeseen emergency'.
RatioOfTheDecision	In case of `urgency' falling under sub-section (1) of Section 17 or of `unforeseen emergency' covered by sub-section (2) of Section 17 , special powers may be exercised by appropriate <ORG> but as held by a three Judge Bench decisions before more than four decades in Nandeshwar Prasad and reiterated by a three Judge Bench decision in Mukesh Hans, even in such cases, inquiry and hearing of objections under Section 5A cannot ipso facto be dispensed with unless a notification under sub-section (4) of Section 17 of the Act is issued.
RatioOfTheDecision	The legislative scheme is amply clear which merely enables the appropriate <ORG> to issue such notification under sub- section (4) of Section 17 of the Act dispensing with inquiry under Section 5A if the <ORG>  intends to exercise the said power.
RatioOfTheDecision	The use of the expression `may' in sub-section (4) of Section 17 leaves no room of doubt that it is discretionary power of the <ORG> to direct that the provisions of Section 5A would not apply to such cases covered by sub-section (1) or (2) of Section 17 of the Act.

RatioOfTheDecision	Acceptance of such contention or upholding of this argument will make sub-section (4) of Section 17 totally otiose, redundant and nugatory.
