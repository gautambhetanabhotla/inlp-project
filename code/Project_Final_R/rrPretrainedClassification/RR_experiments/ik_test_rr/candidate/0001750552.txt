Precedent	The Bench negatived the contention and observed:  It is not correct to say that if a number of vacancies are notified for appointment and adequate number of candidates are found fit, the successful candidates acquire an indefeasible right to be appointed which cannot be legitimately denied.
RatioOfTheDecision	Ordinarily the notification me rely amounts to an invitation to qualified candidates to apply for recruitment and on their selection they do not acquire any right to the post.
RatioOfTheDecision	Unless the relevant recruitment rules so indicate, the <ORG> is under no legal duty to fill up all or any of the vacancies.
RatioOfTheDecision	However, it does not mean that the <ORG> has the licence of acting in an arbitrary manner.
RatioOfTheDecision	The decision not to fill up the vacancies has to be taken bona fide for appropriate reasons.
RatioOfTheDecision	And if the vacancies or any of them are filled, the <ORG> is bound to respect the comparative merit of the candidates, as reflected at the recruitment test, and no discrimination can be permitted.
RatioOfTheDecision	This correct position has been consistently followed by this <ORG>, and we do not find any discordant note in the decisions in <CITATION_470118> , <NAME> v. <ORG> ofHaryana, or <NAME> v. <ORG> of Punjab.
RatioOfTheDecision	Thus, <ORG> while referring with approval the judgment in <NAME> case (supra) in unequivocal terms reiterated the se ttled law that the existence of vacancies does not confer a legal right on a selected candidate to be appointed unless the relevant Rules provide specifically to the contrary.
RatioOfTheDecision	The <ORG>, of course, must all through act bona fide and not arbitrarily both in making appointments and in not filling the existing vacancies.
RatioOfTheDecision	Mr. <NAME> and Mr. <NAME> appearing tor the petitioners in W.r. No.
RatioOfTheDecision	911/1991, however, placed reliance on <CITATION_872416> .
RatioOfTheDecision	[1983J 3 SCC 33, in support of their plea.
Argument	<ORG> in <NAME>'s case was considering the question of retrospectivity of statute law on the existing rights of the employees and it was in that context that this Court opined tha't existing rights cannot be taken away by giving retrospective effect to a statutory provision unless the statute expressly or by necessary implication provides so.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the present case, the <ORG> does not claim the retrospectivity of the 1991 Rules to deny appointments to those who had been brought on the panels.
RatioOfTheDecision	The panel came to be canc elled, as already noticed, in view of the judgment in <NAME> case and, therefore, appointments from that panel after 2.7.1989 were not made.
RatioOfTheDecision	Even otherwise, the nature of the panel prepared in the present case was only that of a list of the eligible candidates possessing requisite qualifications arranged in a chronological order based on their merit in a particular year.
RatioOfTheDecision	No process of selection what so ever had been undergone to prepare the panel as none who was otherwise eligible and qualified was excluded from being brought on the panel.
RatioOfTheDecision	The panel prepared, therefore, was not even the product of any selection process, unlike in <NAME>'s case (supra) where a process of selection had been undergone as is apparent from the following observation of the <ORG>:  It is no doubt true that the Act was amended by U.P.
RatioOfTheDecision	Act 26 of 1975 which came into force on August 18,1975 taking away the power of the Director to make an appointment under Section 16-F(4) of the Act in the case of minority institutions.
RatioOfTheDecision	The amen ding Act did not, however, provide expressly that the amendment in question would apply to pending proceedings under Section 16-F of the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	Nor do we find any words in it which by necessary intendment would affect such pending proceedings.
RatioOfTheDecision	The process of selection under Section 16-F of the Act commencing from the stage of calling for applications for a post up to the date on which the Director becomes entitled to make a selection under Section 16-F(4) (as it stood then) is an integrated one.
RatioOfTheDecision	At every stage in that process certain rights are created in favour of one or the other of the candidates.
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 16-F of the Act cannot, therefore be construed as merely a procedural provision.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is true that the legislature may pass laws with retrospective effect subject to the recognised constitutional limitations.
RatioOfTheDecision	But it is equally well settled that no retrospective effect should be given to any statutory provision so as to impair or take away an existing right, unless the statute either expressly or by necessary  implication directs that it should have such retrospective effect.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the instant case admittedly the proceedings for the selection had commenced in the year 1973 and after the Deputy Director had disapproved the recommendations made by <ORG> twice the Director acquired the jurisdiction to make an appointment from amongst the qualified candidates who had applied for the vacancy in question.
RatioOfTheDecision	At the instance of the appellant himself in the earlier writ petition filed by him <ORG> had directed the Director to exercise that power.
RatioOfTheDecision	Although the Director in the present case exercised that power subsequent to August 18, 1975 on which date the amendment came into force, it cannot be said that the selection made by him was illegal since the amending law had no retrospective effect.
RatioOfTheDecision	It did not have any effect on the proceedings which had commenced prior to August 18, 1975.
RatioOfTheDecision	Such proceedings had to be continued in accordance with the law as it stood at the commencement of the said proceedings.
RatioOfTheDecision	We do not, therefore, find any substance in the contention of the learned Counsel for the appellant that the law as amended by the U.P.
RatioOfTheDecision	Act 26 of 1975 should have been followed in the present case.
RatioOfTheDecision	It was, therefore, in the context of the peculiar facts of that case that this court declined to interfere with the judgment of <ORG> and it has no application to the situation existing in the present case.
