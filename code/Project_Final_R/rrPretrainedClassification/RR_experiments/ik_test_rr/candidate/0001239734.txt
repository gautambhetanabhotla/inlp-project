Fact	In the above appeals, common questions of law are involved, hence these appeals are disposed of by this common order.
Fact	For the purpose of convenience, we shall refer to the facts of the case as found in C.A.
Fact	Nos.303-304 of 1999.
Fact	The respondent in the above appeals is engaged in the manufacture of Toilet Soaps and organic surface active agents.
Fact	It submitted various price lists under Rule 173-C of the Central Excise Rules, 1944 (for short the Rules) claiming deductions from the assessable value.
Fact	The Assistant Collector provisionally approved the prices after disallowing some of the deductions claimed.
Fact	Being aggrieved by the said order of disallowing some of the deductions claimed by it, the respondent preferred appeals before the Commissioner of Appeals who allowed most of the deductions sought by the responden t except three items out of which discount damages was one of the items.
Fact	In an appeal filed to <ORG> (<ORG>), the <ORG> allowed the said appeal following its earlier orders in <ORG> Collector of Central Excise (1989 (43) ELT 360) and <CITATION_1846815> (1992 (60) ELT 512) and directed the original <ORG> to consider afresh claim for deduction in accordance with law in the light of observations contained in the said order.
Fact	On remand, the original <ORG> again disallowed some of the deductions claimed by the respondent including the deductions in regard to damages.
RulingByLowerCourt	Having failed in the appeal before the Appellate <ORG>, the respondent approached the <ORG> once again and the <ORG> by the impugned order allowed the appeal of the respondent once again solely relying on its judgments in <ORG> and <ORG> cases (supra) and held that discount should be allowed in regard to the value of compensation paid  to the buyers in lieu of damages caused to goods during transit depending on the nature and extent of damage.
RulingByLowerCourt	It is against this order of the <ORG>, the Collector of Central Excise has preferred the above appeals.
Argument	Learned Attorney General for India appearing for the appellant contended that the <ORG> erred in placing reliance on Assam Valley and <ORG> cases (supra) as they had no relevance whatsoever to the facts of the case in hand.
Argument	He further contended that the deduction claimed being in the nature of a post- manufacturing deduction, under Section 4(4)(d)(ii) of the Central Excise Act, 1944 (hereinafter referred to as the Act), the same is not deductible from the assessable value.
Argument	He also contended that assuming that such post-manufacturing expenses are deductible, the assessee will have to establish that such deduction is being claimed because same is a part of the trade discounts allowed in accordance with normal practice of that wholesale trade which fact, according to learned Attor ney General, the respondent has failed to establish.
Argument	He also argued that the contention of the respondent that, as a matter of fact, such a practice existed stood belied by the agreement entered into by the respondent with its buyers.
Argument	<NAME>, learned senior counsel appearing for the respondent firstly contended that the appellants should not be permitted to question the finding of the <ORG> as to the deductibility of the damage discount because this question was finally adjudicated and decided by the <ORG> in the first round of litigation between the parties and the appellants did not, at that stage, question the finding of the <ORG> which remanded the matter back to the original <ORG> to decide certain other questions.
Argument	Hence, the issue in hand having attained finality, the same should not be permitted to be reopened by way of the present appeal.
Argument	He also defended the impugned order of the <ORG> on the ground that the deductions permissible under Section 4(4)(d)(ii) are not confined on ly to deductions available at the time of removal of the goods from the factory-gate but are also available to such of those deductions whose existence in practice has been established even though they get computed subsequent to the removal of the goods from the factory gate.
Argument	He also contends that the respondent has established the normal practice of the wholesale trade with reference to this deduction by filing affidavits of its buyers and certificates of <ORG>.
Argument	He contended that the respondent is not relying upon the agreement referred to by the learned Attorney General for the purpose of claiming the deductions in question.
RatioOfTheDecision	We will first deal with the objection of <NAME> which is in the nature of a preliminary objection.
RatioOfTheDecision	As noted, he contended that the issue in question is finally decided inter se between the parties in an earlier proceedings which was not challenged by the <ORG>; therefore, so far as the parties to these appeals are concerned, the matter stands concluded and t he parties cannot reopen the said issue.
Fact	It is true that this issue was decided by the <ORG> in the earlier round of litigation primarily relying upon two orders to which we have already made reference; the correctness of that finding was not challenged in this Court because the matter stood remanded to the original <ORG>.
Fact	Inspite of this finding of the <ORG>, the parties again joined issue before the original <ORG> on this issue by producing materials like affidavits and made their submissions based on which the original <ORG> gave a finding against the respondent, who took the matter in appeal before the Appellate Commissioner and having lost before the Appellate Commissioner, the respondent once again took the matter before the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	From the materials on record, we do not find any argument based on finality of the decision having been urged before the <ORG> nor the <ORG> having decided this issue on that basis.
RatioOfTheDecision	On the contrary, it seems that the <ORG> considered the issue af resh but, relying on the two decisions referred to above, once again decided to hold in favour of the respondent which decision is now under challenge before us.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, on facts, finality is not the ground on which the <ORG> allowed the appeal.
Argument	That apart, even in law, so far as this <ORG> is concerned, it is not bound by the finding of the <ORG> rendered in the first instance while remanding the case to the lower authorities because this <ORG> is now hearing an appeal against the order of the <ORG> in which the earlier order has merged.
Argument	This <ORG> in the case of <CITATION_322093> {} has held :- In an appeal against the High <ORG>s finding the Supreme <ORG> is not bound by what the High <ORG> might have held in its remand order.
Argument	It is true that a subordinate court is bound by the direction of the High <ORG>.
Argument	It is equally true that the same High <ORG>, hearing the matter on a second occasion or any other court of coordinate authority hearing the matter cannot discard the earlier holding, but a fi nding in a remand order cannot bind a higher <ORG> when it hears the matter in appeal.
Argument	(emphasis supplied).
Argument	Therefore, the above contention of the respondent has to be rejected.
Argument	In support of the contention as to the applicability of Section 4(4)(d)(ii) of the Act, the learned Attorney General relied on the observation of this <ORG> in the case of <CITATION_1893386> .
Argument	(1986 Supple.
Argument	SCC 751) wherein this <ORG> held at para 9 of the said report that trade discounts of any nature should be allowed to be deducted provided, however, the discount is known at or prior to the removal of the goods.
Argument	Taking support from these observations, learned Attorney General contended that the discounts in regard to which the deduction is sought by the respondent pertain to the damages to the goods which have occurred in transit, admittedly after the goods were removed from the factory-gate; therefore, such a deduction is not permissible in law.
Argument	He also relied upon a subsequent decision of this <ORG> in <ORG> case  reported in , at page 384) wherein this <ORG> after referring to the judgment in the earlier case of <NAME> (supra) held: - As rightly pointed out by <NAME>, C.J.
Argument	in the order dated 20.12.1986, what is really relevant is the nature of the transaction (<ORG> p.760, para 8).
Argument	The learned Chief Justice pointed out further that the warranty is not a discount on the tyre already sold, but relates to the goods which are being subsequently sold to the same customers.
Argument	It cannot be strictly called as discount on the tyre being sold.
Argument	It is in the nature of a benefit given to the customers by way of compensation for the loss suffered by them in the previous sale (<ORG> p.760, para 8).
Argument	He characterised it as a compensation in the nature of warranty allowance on a defective tyre (<ORG> p.760, para 11).
RatioOfTheDecision	We express our respectful concurrence with the said observations.
Argument	Therefore, it is contended that the claim of the respondent for deduction being one arising out of a post-manufacturing situation, the same is outside the purvi ew of Section 4(4)(d)(ii) of the Act.
Argument	He further contended that the claim of the respondents based on normal practice of the wholesale trade is also unacceptable in view of the agreement between the respondent and its buyers wherein at Clause 14 of the agreement it is unequivocally stated that the claim for damages in transit to the goods sold will be borne by the buyer.
Argument	Therefore, according to the appellant, the respondent cannot claim any trade practice in regard to reimbursement of any value of the goods which have suffered damage in view of Clause 14 of the said agreement.
Argument	He further contended that the affidavits of the buyers filed by the respondents which are contrary to the terms of the agreement are neither admissible nor can be relied upon.
Argument	<NAME>, per contra, contends that the deductions contemplated under Section 4(4)(d)(ii) of the Act are not confined only to deductions that could be attributable to a stage prior to the removal of the goods from the factory gate only.
Argument	According to him, ev en those expenses which have occurred subsequent to removal of the goods from the factory gate have been held to be deductible expenditure from the assessable value of the goods by this <ORG>.
Argument	As an example, he submitted that the expenses incurred on the insurance of the goods, year ending discount, interest on receivables etc.
Argument	even though are expenses incurred after the removal from the factory gate have been accepted as deductible expenditure by various pronouncements of this <ORG>; on this basis he contends that the respondents are entitled for damage deduction.
Argument	In support of this contention, Mr. <NAME> relied on the judgments of this <ORG> in <CITATION_1893386> .
Precedent	(1986 Supp.

Argument	(1995 (4) SCC 349), <ORG>Ltd.

Argument	He laid  special emphasis on deduction allowed on account of insurance.
Argument	He said that the deductions now sought by the respondent are similar to the insurance deductions and as a matter of fact in the instant case in effect the company is acting as an insurer of its own goods.
RatioOfTheDecision	We will now consider the correctness of the <ORG> finding based on its earlier two decisions in the cases of <ORG> and <ORG> (supra).
RatioOfTheDecision	The <ORG> was of the view that the said decisions fully covered the issue that arose for its consideration in the impugned order.
Argument	In <ORG>, the <ORG> at para 6 of its order held thus: In Appeal No.E-396/80-A, there is an additional point of dispute.
Argument	It relates to the quantum of discount or reduction in value for damaged goods cleared by the appellants.
Argument	The lower authorities have allowed the minimum discount of 25%.
Argument	Before the Appellate Collector, the appellants claimed that they had allowed 40% to 50% discount for the damaged goods.
Argument	In t he appeal before us, it is contended that there was reduction of upto 75% in some cases.
Argument	The appellants explained that the nature and extent of damage on different pieces of their products was not uniform and hence the price reduction for the damaged goods could not be uniform.
RatioOfTheDecision	They had allowed varying discount/reduction depending upon the nature and extent of the damage in each case and they prayed that the discount/reduction so actually allowed from case to case may be accepted after necessary verification by the authorities.
RatioOfTheDecision	The learned representative of the department had no comments to make.
RatioOfTheDecision	We find the appellants request quite fair and reasonable, and allow it.
RatioOfTheDecision	From a perusal of this part of the order, we are unable to deduce any principle either in law or in facts decided by the <ORG> so as to make it applicable to the issue that arose before it in the case with which we are concerned.
RatioOfTheDecision	None of the questions which are addressed before us or decisions cited have been considered by the <ORG> in the  said order in Assam Valley case.
RatioOfTheDecision	After carefully considering the said order, we are unable to agree with the <ORG> that those cases either wholly or in part cover the issue involved in the present case.
RulingByLowerCourt	While in the case of <ORG> case (supra), the <ORG> held in para 19 of its order thus: Admittedly, the appellants are giving discount for damages to the goods caused during transit.
RulingByLowerCourt	<NAME> relied on the order on this <ORG> in <ORG> reported in 1989 (43) ELT 360 (Tri.).
RulingByLowerCourt	According to the said order, varying discount reduction depending upon the nature and extent of damage in each case is admissible.
RulingByLowerCourt	It is also a case of damage caused to the goods during transit.
RatioOfTheDecision	Following the above order, we direct the <ORG> to verify the nature and quantum of discount for damage caused to the goods in the transit and allow the same.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is seen that this order of the <ORG> is based on certain admissions made by the parties and reliance was once again placed on the order in Assam  Valley which, according to us, does not lay down any principle in law.
RatioOfTheDecision	Tribunal, in our opinion, erred in applying the orders delivered by it in the cases of <ORG> and <ORG> to the facts of the present case.
RatioOfTheDecision	This, however, will not assist us to dispose of these appeals finally.
RatioOfTheDecision	When the original <ORG> took up the matter for consideration after remand, the respondent filed certain affidavits of its buyers who in their own words have stated that some trade practice was existing whereby the respondent company was reimbursing the loss suffered by the buyers due to the damages caused to the goods.
Argument	Learned Attorney General has argued that these affidavits cannot be relied upon.
Argument	He also urged that the appellant has produced a copy of the agreement between the respondent and its buyers which, according to him, belies the existence of the trade practice as claimed by the respondent.
RatioOfTheDecision	Even though this document was produced only at the first appellate stage, we find it has direct bearing on t he question in issue in view of the contents of clause 14 of the said agreement.
Argument	Based on this clause, it is contended for the appellant that no trade practice in regard to damage deduction is in existence.
Argument	On behalf of the respondent, it is contended that they are not relying on the agreement to establish the trade practice; still, to rebut the argument of the appellant based on clause 14 of the agreement.
Argument	the respondent places reliance on clause 15 of the said agreement which, according to the respondent, creates an obligation on the respondent to compensate the buyers for the loss suffered by them due to damage to the goods.
RatioOfTheDecision	We do not want to express any opinion in regard to these arguments addressed by the parties.
RatioOfTheDecision	The authorities below have not gone into the impact of the various clauses of the agreement on the claim of the respondent as to the existence of trade practice in the wholesale market.
RatioOfTheDecision	We feel this raises primarily a question of fact and is a matter which goes to the root of the claim, therefo re, it should be decided in the first instance by the original <ORG>.
RulingByPresentCourt	Therefore, we consider it appropriate to remand the matter back to the original <ORG> directing it to decide the matter afresh after giving the parties concerned an opportunity of producing such evidence as they desire to produce and after hearing the parties.
RulingByPresentCourt	We make it clear that we have not expressed any opinion with reference to the various arguments addressed before us and referred to in this order of ours except to the extent of the finding delivered by us in regard to the finality of the decision rendered by the <ORG> in the earlier round of proceedings and in regard to the correctness of the reliance placed by the <ORG> on the two orders of its own while allowing the appeal of the respondent, which according to us, is unsustainable.
RulingByPresentCourt	For the reasons stated above, the impugned order is quashed, the matter is remitted to the original <ORG> for fresh disposal in accordance with the observations made hereinabove.
