RatioOfTheDecision	Enactments relating to procedure in courts are usually construed as imperative.
RatioOfTheDecision	A kind of duty is imposed on court or a public officer when no general inconvenience or injustice is caused from different construction.
RatioOfTheDecision	A provision of a statute may impose an absolute or qualified duty upon a public officer which itself may be a relevant consideration while understanding  the provision itself.

RatioOfTheDecision	For example, in <CITATION_175463> [AIR 1961 SC 1480], the word `shall' has been held to be merely directory.
RatioOfTheDecision	<NAME> in the same edition of the above-mentioned book, at page 409, stated that the use of the word `shall' with respect to one matter and use of word `may' with respect to another matter in the same section of a statute will normally lead to the conclusion that the word `shall' imposes an obligation, whereas the word `may' confers a discretionary power.
RatioOfTheDecision	But that by itself is not decisive and the <ORG> may, having regard to the context and consequences, come to the conclusion that the  part of the statute using `shall' is also directory.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is primarily the context in which the words are used which will be of significance and relevance for deciding this issue.
RatioOfTheDecision	Statutes which encroach upon rights, whether as regards person or property, are subject to strict construction in the same way as penal Acts.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is a recognized rule that they should be interpreted, if possible, so as to respect such rights and if there is any ambiguity, the construction which is in favour of the freedom of the individual should be adopted.
