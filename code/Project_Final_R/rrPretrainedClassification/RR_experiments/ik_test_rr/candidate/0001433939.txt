RatioOfTheDecision	FINDINGS:  10.
RatioOfTheDecision	What is a provision?
RatioOfTheDecision	This is the question which needs to be answered.
RatioOfTheDecision	A provision is a liability which can be measured only by using a substantial degree of estimation.
RatioOfTheDecision	A provision is recognized when: (a) an enterprise has a present obligation as a result of a past event; (b) it is probable that an outflow of resources will be required to settle the obligation; and (c) a reliable estimate can be  made of the amount of the obligation.
RatioOfTheDecision	If these conditions are not met, no provision can be recognized.
RatioOfTheDecision	11.
RatioOfTheDecision	Liability is defined as a present obligation arising from past events, the settlement of which is expected to result in an outflow from the enterprise of resources embodying economic benefits.
RatioOfTheDecision	12.
RatioOfTheDecision	A past event that leads to a prese nt obligation is called as an obligating event.
RatioOfTheDecision	The obligating event is an event that creates an obligation which results in an outflow of resources.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is only those obligations arising from past events existing independently of the future conduct of the business of the enterprise that is recognized as provision.
RatioOfTheDecision	For a liability to qualify for recognition there must be not only present obligation but also the probability of an outflow of resources to settle that obligation.
RatioOfTheDecision	Where there are a number of obligations (e.g.
RatioOfTheDecision	product warranties or similar contracts) the probability that an outflow will be required in settlement, is determined by considering the said obligations as a whole.
RatioOfTheDecision	In this connection, it may be noted that in the case of a manufacture and sale of one single item the provision for warranty could constitute a contingent liability not entitled to deduction under Section 37 of the said Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	However, when there is manufacture and sale of an  army of items running into thousands of units of sophis ticated goods, the past event of defects being detected in some of such items leads to a present obligation which results in an enterprise having no alternative to settling that obligation.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the present case, the appellant has been manufacturing and selling <ORG> Actuators.
RatioOfTheDecision	They are in the business from assessment years 1983- 84 onwards.
RatioOfTheDecision	<ORG> Actuators are sophisticated goods.
RatioOfTheDecision	Over the years appellant has been manufacturing <ORG> Actuators in large numbers.
RatioOfTheDecision	The statistical data indicates that every year some of these manufactured Actuators are found to be defective.
RatioOfTheDecision	The statistical data over the years also indicates that being sophisticated item no customer is prepared to buy <ORG> Actuator without a warranty.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, warranty became integral part of the sale price of the <ORG> Actuator(s).
RatioOfTheDecision	In other words, warranty stood attached to the sale price of the product.
RatioOfTheDecision	These aspects are important.
RatioOfTheDecision	As stated above, obligations arising from past events have to be recognized as provisions.
RatioOfTheDecision	These past events are  known as obligating events.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the present case, therefore, warranty provision needs to be recognized because the appellant is an enterprise having a present obligation as a result of past events resulting in an outflow of resources.
RatioOfTheDecision	Lastly, a reliable estimate can  be made of the amount of the obligation.
RatioOfTheDecision	In short, all three conditions for recognition of a provision are satisfied in this case.
RatioOfTheDecision	13.
RatioOfTheDecision	In this case we are concerned with Product Warranties.
RatioOfTheDecision	To give an example of Product Warranties, a company dealing in computers gives warranty for a period of 36 months from the date of supply.
RatioOfTheDecision	The said company considers following options : (a) account for warranty expense in the year in which it is incurred; (b) it makes a provision for warranty only when the customer makes a claim; and (c) it provides for warranty at 2% of turnover of the company based on past experience (historical trend).
RatioOfTheDecision	The first option is unsustainable since it would tantamount to accounting for warranty expenses on cash basis, which is pro hibited both under the Companies Act as well as by the Accounting Standards which require accrual concept to be followed.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the present case, the <ORG> is insisting on the first option which, as stated above, is erroneous as it rules out the accrual concept.
RatioOfTheDecision	The second option is also inappropriate since it does not reflect the expected warranty costs in respect of revenue already recognized (accrued).
RatioOfTheDecision	In other words, it is not based on matching concept.
RatioOfTheDecision	Under the matching concept, if revenue is recognized the cost incurred to earn  that revenue including warranty costs has to be fully provided for.
RatioOfTheDecision	When <ORG> Actuators are sold and the warranty costs are an integral part of that sale price then the appellant has to provide for such warranty costs in its account for the relevant year, otherwise the matching concept fails.
RatioOfTheDecision	In such a case the second option is also inappropriate.
RatioOfTheDecision	Under the circumstances, the third option is most appropriate because it fulfills accrual concept as well as the matching concept .
RatioOfTheDecision	For determining an appropriate historical trend, it is important that the company has a proper accounting system for capturing relationship between the nature of the sales, the warranty provisions made and the actual expenses incurred against it subsequently.
RatioOfTheDecision	Thus, the decision on the warranty provision should be based on past experience of the company.
RatioOfTheDecision	A detailed assessment of the warranty provisioning policy is required particularly if the experience suggests that warranty provisions are generally reversed if they remained unutilized at the end of the period prescribed in the warranty.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, the company should scrutinize the historical trend of warranty provisions made and the actual expenses incurred against it.
RatioOfTheDecision	On this basis a sensible estimate should be made.
RatioOfTheDecision	The warranty provision for the products should be based on the estimate at year end of future warranty  expenses.
RatioOfTheDecision	Such estimates need reassessment every year.
RatioOfTheDecision	As one reaches close to the end of the warranty period, the probability that the wa rranty expenses will be incurred is considerably reduced and that should be reflected in the estimation amount.
RatioOfTheDecision	Whether this should be done through a pro rata reversal or otherwise would require assessment of historical trend.
RatioOfTheDecision	If warranty provisions are based on experience and historical trend(s) and if the working is robust then the question of reversal in the subsequent two years, in the above example, may not arise in a significant way.
RatioOfTheDecision	In our view, on the facts and circumstances of this case, provision for warranty is rightly made by the appellant-enterprise because it has incurred a present obligation as a result of past events.
RatioOfTheDecision	There is also an outflow of resources.
RatioOfTheDecision	A reliable estimate of the obligation was also possible.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, the appellant has incurred a liability, on the facts and circumstances of this case, during the relevant assessment year which was entitled to deduction under Section 37 of the 1961 Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, all the three conditions for recognizing a liability for the purposes of prov isioning stands satisfied in this case.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is important to note that there are four important aspects of provisioning.
RatioOfTheDecision	They are - provisioning which relates to present obligation, it arises out of obligating events, it involves outflow of  resources and lastly it involves reliable estimation of obligation.
RatioOfTheDecision	Keeping in mind all the four aspects, we are of the view that <ORG> should not to have interfered with the decision of the <ORG> in this case.
RatioOfTheDecision	14.
RatioOfTheDecision	In this case <ORG> has principally gone by the judgment of <ORG> in the case of <NAME> (supra).
RatioOfTheDecision	That was the case of gratuity.
Precedent	For the assessment year 1974-75 the assessee-company sought to deduct a sum of Rs.18,37,727/- towards the amount of gratuity payable to its employees and worked out actuarially.
Precedent	No provision was made for Rs.18,37,727/-.
Precedent	The claim for deduction was made on the ground that the liability stood ascertained by actuarial valuation and, therefore, was deductible under Section 37 of the 1961 Act.
Precedent	Th e <ORG> allowed the deduction only in respect of the amounts actually paid by the assessee and the rest was disallowed on the ground of non-compliance with the provisions of Section 40A(7) of the 1961 Act.
Precedent	This view of the <ORG> was affirmed by CIT(A).
Precedent	The <ORG> held that for the earlier assessment year relating to 1973-74, actuarially ascertained liability for gratuity arising under Payment of Gratuity Act , 1972 was an allowable deduction.
Precedent	However, for the  assessment year in question, the <ORG> held that the increased liability claimed by the assessee for deduction was allowable on general principles of accounting.
Precedent	This view was taken by the <ORG> on the basis that the actuarially determined liability was not provided for in the assessee's books of account.
Precedent	In appeal by the <ORG>, <ORG> held that the assessee was not entitled to deduction without complying with the provisions of Section 40A(7) of the 1961 Act.
Precedent	This view of <ORG> was affirmed by this <ORG>.
Precedent	It was held that Secti on 40A(7) which stood inserted by Finance Act , 1975 w.e.f.
Precedent	1.4.73 has been given an overriding effect over Section 28 as well as Section 37 of the 1961 Act.
Precedent	Consequently, the deduction allowable on general principles was ruled out as Section 40A(1) made it clear that Section 40A had effect notwithstanding anything contained in Sections 30 to 39 of the 1961 Act.
Precedent	In other words, as regards deduction in respect of gratuity, the assessee was required to comply with the provisions of Section 40A(7) after Finance Act , 1975.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is interesting to note that prior to 1.4.73 actual payment or provision for payment was eligible for deduction either under Section 28 or under Section 37 of the 1961 Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	This has been reiterated in <ORG> (supra).
RatioOfTheDecision	The position got altered only after 1.4.73.
RatioOfTheDecision	Before that date,  provision made in the P & L Account for the estimated present value of the contingent liability properly ascertained and discounted on an accrued basis could be deducted either under Section 28 or Section  37 of the 1961 Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	This has been explained in <ORG> (supra) at page 599.
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 40A(7) deals only with the case of gratuity.
RatioOfTheDecision	Even in the case of gratuity but for insertion of Section 40A(7) , provision made in the P & L Account on the basis of present value of the contingent liability properly ascertained and discounted on an accrued basis was entitled to deduction either under Section 28 or under Section 37 of the said Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	This aspect, therefore, indicates that the present value of the contingent liability like the warranty expense, if properly ascertained and discounted on accrued basis, could be an item of deduction under Section 37 of the said Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	This aspect is not noticed in the impugned judgment.
RatioOfTheDecision	We may add a caveat.
RatioOfTheDecision	As stated above, the principle of estimation of the contingent liability is not the normal rule.
RatioOfTheDecision	As stated above, it would depend on the nature of business, the nature of sales, the nature of the product manufactured and sold and the scientific method of accounting being  adopted by the assessee.
RatioOfTheDecision	It will also depend upon the historical trend.
RatioOfTheDecision	It would also depend upon the number of articles  produced.
RatioOfTheDecision	As stated above, if it is a case of single item being produced then the principle of estimation of contingent liability on pro rata basis may not apply.
RatioOfTheDecision	However, in the present case, it is not so.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the present case, we have the situation of large number of items being produced.
RatioOfTheDecision	They are sophisticated goods.
RatioOfTheDecision	They are supported by the historical trend, namely, defects being detected in some of the items.
RatioOfTheDecision	The data also indicates that the warranty cost(s) is embedded in the sale price.
RatioOfTheDecision	The data also indicates that the warranty is attached to the sale price.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the circumstances, we hold that the principle laid down by this <ORG> in the case of <ORG> (supra) will apply.
