Fact	These appeals by special leave arise from the judgment and decree of <ORG> made on April 7, 1994 in S.A. No.1526 of 1988.
Fact	The facts are not in dispute.
Fact	Notification under Section 4(1) of the Land Acquisition Act, 1894 (Act 1 of 1894) (for short, the "Act") was published on September 17, 1958 acquiring a large extent of 339 acres of land comprising Kodambakkam and Pudoor villages known as "Part Neighborhood Scheme" and renamed as "Ashok Nagar Scheme of Madras City for planned development.
Fact	Declaration under Section 6 was published on November 26, 1958.
Fact	The Land Acquisition Officer made his award under Section 11 on February 28, 1966.
Fact	It is the case of the appellant that the Land Acquisition Officer had taken possession of the land o n February 28, 1966 and delivered possession to the appellant on March 21, 1966.
Fact	It is not in dispute that under the Scheme as many as 3639 residential houses have been constructed and delivered possession of.
Fact	The disputed land in an extent of one acre and thirty two cents is set apart for public park in the Scheme which stood vested in the <ORG>.
Fact	It is the case of the respondent that he is the owner of the land having title to and possession of the sane for over 30 years preceding the date of filing of the suit, Viz., April 19, 1984 and the appellant was sought to interdict his possession and enjoyment.
Fact	Consequentially, he filed the suit for perpetual injunction against the appellant.
Fact	Admittedly, he was a servant of <NAME>.
Fact	<ORG> dismissed the suit.
Fact	On appeal, the City Civil Judge decreed the suit and <ORG> confirmed the same.
Fact	Thus these appeals by special leave.
Fact	It is contended by Mr. <NAME>, learned senior counsel appearing for the respondents, that in a mere  suit for injunction though incidentally founded on title, the courts are require to record a finding whether the respondents were in possession of the land as on the date of the suit and if finding of being in possession is recorded then they are entitled to perpetual injunction against everyone except the true owner.
Fact	In this cases all the three courts concurrently found as a fact that the respondents were in possession of the land as on the date of the suit.
Fact	The appellant had not proved that possession was taken by the Land Acquisition Officer from the respondents.
RulingByLowerCourt	Thereby the right, title and interest held by the respondents was not divested by operation of Section 16 of the Act.
RulingByLowerCourt	Therefore, the respondents continue to remain to be the lawful owner.
RulingByLowerCourt	Accordingly, they are entitled to injunction against everyone including the appellant-Board.
RulingByLowerCourt	In support of his contention, he placed strong reliance on <CITATION_1537954> .
Precedent	[(1975) Supp.
Precedent	SCR 250].
Precedent	The question is: whether the premise on which the learned counse l has projected the case is based on legally acceptable legal premise?
RatioOfTheDecision	It is true that when <ORG> has, as a pure appreciation of evidence, considered and recorded as a fact a finding on possession, normally this Court would accept such finding and proceed on that premise to decide substantial question of law of public importance, exercising the power under Article 136.
RatioOfTheDecision	As stated earlier, whether <ORG> has proceeded on that premise is the question.
RatioOfTheDecision	With due respect, the learned Judge has proceeded in recording a fact without adverting to operation of relevant provisions of the Act, failed to draw legal inferences from admitted or proved facts and had wrongly drawn the inference that the acquisition stood lapsed which constitute patent error giving rise to substantial question of law.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is an admitted fact that the land was acquired under the Act after due publication of the declaration under Section 6 .
RatioOfTheDecision	As rightly contended by Mr. <NAME>, learned senior counsel appearing for some  of the respondents, conclusiveness of the public purpose stands established.
RatioOfTheDecision	Thereafter, procedure prescribed in Chapter III of the Act requires to be followed and as a fact, admittedly, the <ORG> made his award on February 28, 1966 and issued notice under Section 12 .
RatioOfTheDecision	All the parties received compensation except in respect of the land in question.
RatioOfTheDecision	As a matter of law under Section 30 , when claimant/owner receives compensation with without protest, <ORG> should pay the same.
RatioOfTheDecision	In case, no one received compensation, he is enjoined under Section 30 to deposit the compensation in the court to which reference under Section 18 would lie and to make the reference under Section 30 accordingly.
Fact	It is seen from the evidence that the <ORG> found one <NAME> and <NAME> had interest in the land bearing Survey No.140/4 of an extent of one acre and thirty two cents.
RulingByLowerCourt	Accordingly, in his award he mentioned that since all of them have laid the claim, he referred the dispute under Section 30 and deposited the comp ensation in the court.
RulingByLowerCourt	As a corollary, possession would be taken and thereafter the land stands vested in the <ORG> under Section 16 free from all encumbrances.
Fact	The question is: whether the land in question was taken possession?
RatioOfTheDecision	The issue squarely arises vis a vis the respondents.
RatioOfTheDecision	Unfortunately, the respondents had not impleaded the <ORG> who had taken possession and delivered possession of the land to the appellant.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is not in dispute that under Ex.P-5, the <ORG> delivered possession to the appellant.
Argument	Therefore, as rightly contended by <NAME>, learned senior counsel for the appellant, that the presumption under Section 114(e) of the Evidence Act would consequently get attracted to the facts in this case.
Argument	The <ORG> in discharge of his official duty after taking possession of the disputed land along with other lands, had, in turn, delivered the same to the appellant.
Fact	It is seen that 339 acres of land acquired by a common notification was taken and the award came to be made and possession was taken of a ll the lands.
Fact	Question arises: whether it would be possible for the <ORG> to take physical possession of the entire 339 acres of land and deliver the same to <ORG>?
RatioOfTheDecision	The approach to the question must be pragmatic and realistic but not purely legalistic.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is true that in <NAME> case, <ORG>, J. had held at page 263 thus:  "The question is what is the mode of taking possession?
RatioOfTheDecision	The Act is silent on the point.
RatioOfTheDecision	Unless possession is taken Concerned the mode of taking the possession obviously would be for the authority to go upon the land and to do some act which would indicate that the authority has taken possession of the land.
Precedent	It may be in the form of a declaration by beat of drum or otherwise or by hanging a written declaration on the spot that the authority has taken possession of the land"  <NAME>, J.
Precedent	(as he then was) speaking for two members had held that :  "There can be no question of taking 'symbolical' possession in the sense understood by judicial decisions under the Cod e of Civil Procedure.
RatioOfTheDecision	Not would possession merely on paper by enough.
RatioOfTheDecision	What the Act contemplates as a necessary condition of vesting of the land in the Government is the taking of actual possession of the land.
RatioOfTheDecision	How such possession would have to be taken as the nature of the land admits of.
RatioOfTheDecision	There can be no hard and fast rule laying down what act would be sufficient to constitute taking of possession of land.
RatioOfTheDecision	We should not, therefore, be taken as laying down as absolute and inviolable rule that merely going on the spot and making a declaration by beat of drum or otherwise would be sufficient to constitute taking of possession of land in every case.
RatioOfTheDecision	But here, in our opinion, since the land was laying fallow and there was no crop on it at the material time, the act of the Tehsildar in going on the spot and inspecting the land for the purpose of determining what part was waster and arable and should, therefore, be taken possession of and determining its extent, was sufficient to constitute taking of possession.
RatioOfTheDecision	It  appears that the appellant was not present when this was done by the Tehsildar, but the presence of the owner or the occupant of the and is not necessary to effectuate the taking of possession.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is also not strictly necessary as a matter of legal requirement that notice should be given to the owner or the occupant of the land that possession would be taken at a particular time, though it may be desirable where possible, to give such notice before possession is taken by the authorities as that would eliminate the possibility of any fraudulent or collusive transaction of taking mere paper possession, without the occupant or the owner every coming to know of it."
RatioOfTheDecision	It is settled law by series of judgments of this Court that one of the accepted modes of taking possession of the acquired land is recording of a memorandum or Panchnama by the <ORG> in the presence of witnesses winged by him/them and that would constitute taking possession of the land as it would be impossible to take physical possession of the acquir ed land.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is common knowledge that in some cases the owner/interested person may not cooperate in taking possession of the land.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is seen that in a letter written by the respondent himself, admitting the title of the <ORG> to the land in the said survey number, he sought for allotment of alternative site.
RatioOfTheDecision	In other words, unless possession is taken and he is divested of the title and the same is vested in the appellant, he cannot make request to the appellant for providing him alternative site.
Precedent	It is not his case that at that stage he was still continuing to have title to the land in dispute.'
RatioOfTheDecision	The admission is inconsistent with and incongruous to his interest.
RatioOfTheDecision	He was also aware that award was made and the possession obviously should have been taken thereunder.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is true that normally possession is nine times the title.
RatioOfTheDecision	If that principle is extended to public acquisition by illegal squatting, erstwhile owner has compensation as well as possession of the land by encroachment upon his erstwhile land and cla im that he remained in possession.
RatioOfTheDecision	Such construction would defeat the public purpose.
RatioOfTheDecision	As pointed out earlier, the <ORG> is the best person to speak to the factum of taking and giving delivery, to the appellant, of the possession of the land in survey No.140/4 along with other lands but he was not impleaded as party defendant to the suit.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is seen that when the respondent is asserting his legal title to the acquired land, he should have necessarily the <ORG> impleaded as party and claimed his possession as against the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	That was not done.
RatioOfTheDecision	The <ORG> having had possession from the <ORG>, cannot be expected to prove how the <ORG> had taken possession of the land.
RatioOfTheDecision	From the facts in this case, it would be clear that possession must have been taken of the land consisting of 339 acres including 1.33 acres in survey No.
RatioOfTheDecision	140/4.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is seen that when the land was acquired for planned development of the city and a large chunk of buildings has already been built up and the land admeasuring about 1 acre 32 cents  has been set apart for park purpose, obviously along with other lands, the disputed land was taken possession and construction was made as per plans.
RatioOfTheDecision	Would it be possible for the appellant, without delivery of possession to <ORG>, to construct such massive constructions and leave out only this part of the land bearing survey No.140/4 which was set a part for public purpose, namely, public amenity of part?
RatioOfTheDecision	The making of the plan would emerge only after the land is taken possession and demarcation thereof is made and constructions are carried out.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is erroneous to believe that possession still remained with the respondents and the <ORG> had not taken possession only of this piece of land.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is not the case of the respondent that he resisted taking possession of the land by <ORG> and thereafter the <ORG> took no action to have him dispossessed.
RatioOfTheDecision	The single Judge has not adverted to these material facts and the circumstantial evidence available from the established facts.
RatioOfTheDecision	He proceeded to consider on the  premise that since the acquired land was not used for building purpose and possession was not taken, acquisition stood lapsed.
RatioOfTheDecision	Equally erroneous is the reasoning given by the District Judge.

RatioOfTheDecision	Section 12  of the Act itself exempts registration of the land acquired under the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	The District Judge had obviously ignored the statutory provisions.
RatioOfTheDecision	It was unnecessary for <ORG> to have the lands mutated in the revenue records and have its name entered therein.
RatioOfTheDecision	It was not for its purpose.
RatioOfTheDecision	It was for public purpose, i.e., for construction of the houses and allotment thereof to the needy persons.
Fact	After the construction of the houses, the public park stood vested in the acquisition.
Fact	Obviously, at this stage the Municipality wou ld have come to take possession exercising its jurisdiction when illegal encroachment was found on the land.

Argument	Thus considered, the title of the land in Survey No.
Argument	140/4 having been vested in the appellant, to whomsoever it belonged earlier, it stood divested from him/them and no one can lay any claim to the said acquired land once over and claim injunction on that basis.
RatioOfTheDecision	The injunction, therefore, cannot be issued against the true owner, namely, <ORG> in whom the land ultimately stood vested and then stood transferred to <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	A trespasser can not claim injunction against the owner nor can the court to issue the same.
