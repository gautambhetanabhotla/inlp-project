RatioOfTheDecision	6.We will first deal with the matters in groups 1, 2 and 3 and, therefore, with the question whether the appellants/petitioners have a fundamental right to carry on business in liquor.
RatioOfTheDecision	7.Before we proceed to examine the question, it is necessary at the outset to focus our attention on the precise controversy raised before us and which,it is claimed, arises out of the conflicting decisions of this Court.
RatioOfTheDecision	For the purposes of contentions advanced before us, liquor covers not only those alcoholic liquids which are generally used as beverages and produce intoxication but also all liquids containing alcohol.
RatioOfTheDecision	Liquor is classified broadly into three classes, viz., (i) potable liquor which is used as beverage,  (ii) liquor used in medicinal and toilet preparations and  (iii) industrial liquor used for industrial purpose.
RatioOfTheDecision	Two rival contentions of law are canvassed before us.
RatioOfTheDecision	One, that there is no fundamental right to trade or business in liquor and that the <ORG> has power to regulate the trade or business by placing restrictions on such trade or business in the interests of the general public even to the extent of prohibiting completely such business or trade.
RatioOfTheDecision	The incidental consequence which flows from this contention is that the <ORG> has the exclusive privilege to sell liquor and this privilege can be sold under the relevant law.
RatioOfTheDecision	The <ORG> can, therefore, have also a monopoly in manufacturing, possessing and distributing liquor.
RatioOfTheDecision	The other contention is that a citizen has a fundamental right to trade or business in liquor and the <ORG> can only place reasonable restrictions on the said right in the interests of general public by law made for the purpose under Article 19(6) of the Constitution.
RatioOfTheDecision	The <ORG> cannot, therefore, prohibit completely the said trade or business in liquor in the garb of regulating it, and the limitations or restrictions placed have to pass the test of re asonableness as in the case of trade or business in any other article.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is in the light of these rival contentions that we have to examine the question raised before us.
RatioOfTheDecision	8.
RatioOfTheDecision	Two incidental questions which, therefore, arise are  (i) whether a monopoly for the manufacture, trade or business in liquor can be created in favour of the <ORG> and  (ii) whether reasonable restrictions under Article 19(6) of the Constitution can be placed only by Act of Legislature or by a subordinate legislation as well.
RatioOfTheDecision	9.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is contended that the <ORG> cannot carry on trade in liquor under Article 47 of the Constitution.
RatioOfTheDecision	If the law on the subject is considered to be law under Article 19(6 ), it has to be on the basis that a citizen had got a fundamental right to trade in liquor.
RatioOfTheDecision	If the law is that a citizen has no fundamental right, then Article 19(6) cannot be applied because the said article applies only to those rights which a citizen possesses.
RatioOfTheDecision	What a citizen cannot do under Article 19(1 ), the <ORG> cannot do under Article  19(6).
RatioOfTheDecision	Secondly, it is submitted that assuming that the <ORG> has got the power to carry on trade in liquor dehors Article 19(6) and under Article 298 of the Constitution, the power under Article 298 cannot extend to trade in liquor.
RatioOfTheDecision	This is so because <ORG> has no executive power to trade in a commodity which under Article 47 it is enjoined to prohibit.
RatioOfTheDecision	10.
RatioOfTheDecision	In support of the contention that the appellants/petitioners have a fundamental right to trade in liquor, it is argued firstly, that Entry 51 of List 11 specifically accepts the fact that the manufacture of alcohol can be for human consumption.

Fact	Secondly, it is submitted that there are other substances like tobacco which are more harmful to health than alcohol and they are being sold freely.
Fact	A majority of the <ORG> did not introduce prohibition and some <ORG> which purported to do it, failed and reverted to the earlier pre-prohibition condition.
Fact	On the other hand, the revenue from the auction of excise, vend fees, liquor and other levies forms a major source of the revenue of the <ORG>.

RatioOfTheDecision	11.We will first refer to the relevant provisions of the Constitution which have a bearing on the subject.
Statute	12.
Statute	Article 19(1)(g) provides that all citizens shall have the right to practise any profession or to carry on any occupation, trade or business.
Statute	This right conferred by the aforesaid provision is circumscribed by the provisions of clause (6) of the very article which reads as follows: "(6).
Statute	Nothing in sub-clause (g) of the said clause shall affect the operation of any existing law insofar as it imposes, or prevent the <ORG> from making any law imposing, in the interests of the general public, reasonable restrictions on the exercise of the right conferred by the said sub-clause, and, in particular, nothing in the said sub-clause shall affect the operation of any existing law insofar as it relates to, or prevent the <ORG> from making any law relating to,-  (i) t he professional or technical qualifications necessary for practising any profession or carrying on any occupation, trade or business, or  (ii)the carrying on by the <ORG>, or by a corporation owned or controlled by the <ORG>, of any trade, business, industry or service, whether to the exclusion, complete or partial, of citizens or otherwise."
RatioOfTheDecision	Thus Article 19(1)(g) read with Article 19(6) spells out a fundamental right of the citizens to practise any profession or to carry on any occupation, trade or business so long as it is not prohibited or is within the framework of the regulation, if any, if such prohibition or regulation has been imposed by the <ORG> by enacting a law in the interests of the general public.
RatioOfTheDecision	It cannot be disputed that certain professions, occupations, trades or businesses which are not in the interests of the general public may be completely prohibited while others may be permitted with reasonable restrictions on them.
RatioOfTheDecision	For the same purpose, viz., to subserve the interests of general publ ic, the reasonable restrictions on the carrying on of any profession, occupation, trade, etc., may provide that such trade, business etc., may be carried on exclusively by the <ORG> or by a <ORG> owned or controlled by it.
RatioOfTheDecision	The right conferred upon the citizens under Article 19(1)(g) is thus subject to the complete or partial prohibition or to regulation, by the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	However, under the provisions of Article 19(6) the prohibition, partial or complete, or the regulation, has to be in the interests of the general public.

Precedent	Narula case17 that a citizen has a fundamental right to trade or business in liquor are to be understood, as explained above, to mean only that when the <ORG> does not prohibit the trade or business in liquor, a citizen has the right to do business in it subject to the restrictions and limitations placed upon it.
RatioOfTheDecision	Those observations cannot be read to mean that a citizen  has an unqualified and an absolute right to trade or business in potable liquor.
Precedent	This position in law is explained by this <ORG> also in Har Shankar case22.
Precedent	The decision in the second <ORG> case30 also cannot be read to mean that the <ORG> in that case has taken the view that a citizen has a right to trade or business in potable liquor.
RatioOfTheDecision	That decision is confined to trade or business in industrial alcohol which is legitimately used for industrial purpose and not for consumption as an intoxicating drink.
RatioOfTheDecision	The <ORG> has also there not taken any exception to the right of the <ORG> to place reasonable restrictions on the trade or business even of industrial alcohol to prevent its diversion for the use In or as intoxicating beverage.
RatioOfTheDecision	62.
RatioOfTheDecision	We, therefore, hold that a citizen has no fundamental right to trade or business in liquor as beverage.
RatioOfTheDecision	The <ORG> can prohibit completely the trade or business in potable liquor since liquor as beverage is res extra commercium.
RatioOfTheDecision	The <ORG> may also create a m onopoly in itself for trade or business in such liquor.
RatioOfTheDecision	The <ORG> can further place restrictions and limitations on such trade or business which may be in nature different from those on trade or business in articles res commercium.
RatioOfTheDecision	The view taken by this <ORG> in K.K.
RatioOfTheDecision	Narula case17 as well as in the second <ORG> case30 is not contrary to the aforesaid view which has been consistently taken by this <ORG> so far.
RatioOfTheDecision	63.
RatioOfTheDecision	One of the incidental contentions, viz., whether the <ORG> can create monopoly in trade or business in potable liquor is already answered above.
RatioOfTheDecision	This is apart from the fact that Article 19(6) provides for such monopoly in favour of the <ORG> even in trades and businesses which are legitimate.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is not, therefore, necessary to dilate upon this aspect any further.
Precedent	64.
Precedent	The last contention in these groups of matters is whether the <ORG>, can place restrictions and limitations under Article 19(6) by subordinate legislation.
Precedent	Article 13(3)(a) of the Constitution states that law  includes "any ordinance, order, bye-law, rule, regulation, notification, custom or usage having in the territory of India the force of law".
Precedent	Clauses (2) to (6) of Article, 19 make no distinction between the law made by the legislature and the subordinate legislation for the purpose of placing the restrictions on the exercise of the respective fundamental rights mentioned in Article 19(1)(a) to (g).
Precedent	We are concerned in the present case with clause (6) of Article 19.
Precedent	It will be apparent from the said clause that it only speaks of "operation of any existing law insofar as it imposes ..." "from making any law imposing" reasonable restrictions on the exercise of the rights conferred by Article 19(1)(g).
Precedent	There is nothing in this provision which makes it imperative to impose the restrictions in question only by a law enacted by the legislature.
Precedent	Hence the restrictions in question can also be imposed by any subordinate legislation so long as such legislation Is not violative of any provisions of the Constitution.
Precedent	This  is apart from the fact that the trade or business in potable liquor is a trade or business in res extra commercium and hence can be regulated and restricted even by executive order provided it is issued by the Governor of the State.
Precedent	We, therefore, answer the question accordingly.
Precedent	65.
Precedent	In the view that we have taken, the appeals, special leave petitions and writ petitions will now be placed before an appropriate Bench for decision in accordance with the law laid down above.
