RulingByLowerCourt	Section 5-A(1)  (a) of the Act provides:  "5-A.
RulingByLowerCourt	Levy of purchase tax-  (1) Every dealer who, in the course of his business, purchases from a registered dealer or from any other person any goods the sale or purchase of which is liable to tax under this Act, in circumstances in which no tax is payable under section 5, and either-  (a) consumes such goods in the man ufacture of other goods for sale or otherwise; or ........................................ shall, whatever be the quantum of the turnover relating to such purchase for a year, pay tax on the taxable turnover relating to such purchase for the year at the rates mentioned in section 5."
RulingByLowerCourt	The assessee maintained that by the conversion of pineapple fruit into its products no new commodity was created and it was erroneous to say that there was a consumption of pineapple fruit "in the manufacture of" those goods.
RulingByLowerCourt	The Sales Tax Officer did not accept the contention and completed the assessment on the finding that a manufacturing process was involved and that, therefore, the case fell within s. 5-A(1)(a).
RulingByLowerCourt	In revision before <ORG>, the assessee conceded that pineapple jam and pine-apple squash would be covered by s. 5-A(1)(a), and in regard to pineapple juice the <ORG> found that s. 5-1(a) was attracted.

RatioOfTheDecision	It appears that the pineapple purchased by the assessee is washed and then the inedible portion, the end crown, skin and inner core are removed, thereafter the fruit is sliced and the slices are filled in cans, sugar is added as a preservative, the cans are sealed under temperature and then put in boiling water for sterilisation.
RatioOfTheDecision	Is the pineapple fruit consumed in the manufacture of pineapple slices ?
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 5-A(1)(a) of the Kerala General Sales Tax Act envisages the consumption of a commodity in the manufacture of another commodity.
RatioOfTheDecision	The goods purchased should be consumed, the consumption should be in the process of manufacture, and the result must be the manufacture of other goods.
RatioOfTheDecision	There are several  criteria for determining whether a commodity is consumed in the manufacture of another.
RatioOfTheDecision	The generally prevalent test is whether the article produced is regarded in the trade, by those who deal in it, as distinct in identity from the commodity involved in its manufacture.
RatioOfTheDecision	Commonly, manufacture is the end result of one or more processes through which the original commodity is made to pass.
RatioOfTheDecision	The nature and extent of processing may vary from one case to another, and indeed there may be several stages of processing and perhaps a different kind of processing at each stage.
RatioOfTheDecision	With each process suffered, the original commodity experiences a change.
RatioOfTheDecision	But it is only when the change, or a series of changes, take the commodity to the point where commercially it can no longer be regarded as the original commodity but instead is recognised as a new and distinct article that a manufacture can be said to take place.
RatioOfTheDecision	Where there is no essential difference in identity between the original commodity and the processed article it is  not possible to say that one commodity has been consumed in the manufacture of another.
RatioOfTheDecision	Although it has undergone a degree of processing, it must be regarded as still retaining its original identity.
RatioOfTheDecision	A large number of cases has been placed before us by the parties, and in each of them the same principle has been applied: Does the processing of the original commodity bring into existence a commercially different and distinct article ?
RatioOfTheDecision	Some of the cases where it was held by this <ORG> that a different commercial article had come into existence include <CITATION_1416018> (where raw tobacco was manufactured into bidi patti), <CITATION_1105204> (raw hides and skins constituted a different commodity from dressed hides and skins with different physical properties), <CITATION_1609169> (raw tobacco manufactured into chewing tobacco) and <CITATION_453569> , (paddy dehusked into rice).
RatioOfTheDecision	On the other side, cases where this <ORG> has held that although the original commodity has undergone a degree of processing it has not  lost its original identity include <CITATION_903306> (where hydrogenated groundnut oil was regarded as groundnut oil) and <ORG>, U.P., Lucknow v. <NAME> and sons (where bristles plucked from pigs, boiled, washed with soap and other chemicals and sorted out in bundles according to their size and colour were regarded as remaining the same commercial commodity, pigs bristles).
RatioOfTheDecision	In the present case, there is no essential difference between pineapple fruit and the canned pineapple slices.
RatioOfTheDecision	The dealer and the consumer regard both as pineapple.
RatioOfTheDecision	The only difference is that the sliced pineapple is a presentation of fruit in a more convenient form and by reason of being canned it is capable of storage without spoiling.
RatioOfTheDecision	The additional sweetness in the canned pineapple arises from the sugar added as a preservative.
RatioOfTheDecision	On a total impression, it seems to us, the pineapple slices must be held to possess the same identity as the original pineapple fruit.
Precedent	While on the point, we may refer to East Texas  <ORG> v. <ORG>, where <ORG> held that dressed and frozen chicken was not a commercially distinct article from the original chicken.
Precedent	It was pointed out:  "Killing, dressed and freezing a chicken is certainly a change in the commodity.
Precedent	But it is no more drastic a change than the change which takes place in milk from pasturising, homogenizing, adding vitamin concentrates, standardising and bottling."
Precedent	It was also observed:  ".................. there is hardly less difference between cotton in the field and cotton at the gin or in the bale or between cottonseed in the field and cottonseed at the gin, than between a chicken in the pen and one that is dressed.
Precedent	The ginned and baled cotton and the cottonseed, as well as the dressed chicken, have gone through a processing stage But neither has been "manufactured" in the normal sense of the word."
Precedent	Referring to <ORG> v.  United States the Court said:  "Manufacture implies a change but every chang e is not manufacture and yet every change in an article is the result of treatment, labour and manipulation.
Precedent	But something more is necessary...................There must be transformation; a new and different article must emerge, having a distinctive name, character or use."
Precedent	And further:  "At some point processing and manufacturing will merge.
RatioOfTheDecision	But where the commodity retains a continuing substantial identity through the processing stage we cannot say that it has been "manufactured".
RatioOfTheDecision	The comment applies fully in the case before us.
RatioOfTheDecision	Although a degree of processing is involved in preparing pineapple slices from the original fruit, the commodity continues to possess its original identity, notwithstanding the removal of inedible portions, the slicing and thereafter canning it on adding sugar to preserve it.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is contended for the <ORG> that pineapple slices have a higher price in the market than the original fruit and that implies that the slices constitute a different commercial commodity.
RatioOfTheDecision	The higher price,  it seems to us, is occasioned only because of the labour put into making the fruit more readily consumable and because of the can employed to contain it.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is not as if the higher price is claimed because it is a different commercial commodity.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is said that pineapple slices appeal to a different sector of the trade and that when a customer asks for a can of pineapple slices he has in mind something very different from fresh pineapple fruit.
RatioOfTheDecision	Here again, the distinction in the mind of the consumer arises not from any difference in the essential identity of the two, but is derived from the mere form in which the fruit is desired.
RatioOfTheDecision	Learned counsel for the <ORG> contends that even if no manufacturing process is involved, the case still falls within s. 5-A(1) (a) of the Kerala General Sales Tax Act, because the statutory provision speaks not only of goods consumed in the manufacture of other goods for sale but also goods consumed otherwise.
RatioOfTheDecision	There is a fallacy in the submission.
RatioOfTheDecision	The clause, truly read, speaks  of goods consumed in the manufacture of other goods for sale or goods consumed in the manufacture of other goods for purposes other than sale.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the result, we hold that when pineapple fruit is processed into pineapple slices for the purpose of being sold in sealed cans there is no consumption of the original pineapple fruit for the purpose of manufacture.
RulingByPresentCourt	The case does not fall within s. 5-A(1)(a) of the Kerala General Sales Tax Act.
RulingByPresentCourt	<ORG> is right in the view taken by it.
RulingByPresentCourt	The appeal fails and is dismissed with costs.
