RulingByLowerCourt	PETITIONER: <ORG> BOMBAY AND ORS.
Fact	Vs. RESPONDENT: M/S SRIYANESH KNITTERS DATE OF JUDGMENT: 30/07/1999 BENCH: <NAME>, <NAME> JUDGMENT:  <NAME>, J.
Fact	The common question involved in these appeals is whether the appellant - <ORG> constituted under the Major Port Trusts Act , 1963 (for short the MPT Act) have a general lien for their dues over the present or future consignments imported by the importers at the Bombay Port when the said dues are in respect of the past imports made by the said importers.
Fact	The respondents in these appeals are importers who had imported various consignments of woollen rags from time to time.
Fact	After the arrival of these consignments at the Bombay Port a dispute arose between the respondents and the custom authorities as to whether the imported goods were woollen rags or woollen garments.
Fact	After considerable period of time the imported goods were confiscated by the custom authorities but the importers secured orders to  get the goods released on payment of fine.
Fact	During this period the imported goods remained at the docks till the order of confiscation was passed.
Fact	In respect of the period during which the goods remained at the docks the appellants issued notices to the respondents demanding demurrage charges.
Fact	With the exporters denying the liability the <ORG> authorities instituted various suits to recover wharfage and demurrage charges.
Fact	We are in these appeals not concerned with the suits.
Fact	After the aforesaid suits had been instituted acrylic fibre was imported by the respondents.
Fact	When the consignment arrived in Bombay Port the respondents filed bills of entry for clearance of the goods for home consumption.
Fact	When necessary permission was granted by the custom authorities the appellant Board was called upon to release the goods.
Fact	These goods were not released by the appellant as it demanded payment in respect of wharfage and demurrage which was due from these respondents in respect of earlier consignments of woollen rag s which had been imported by them.
Fact	This refusal of the appellants to allow the removal of the goods was based on a circular dated 2nd October 1979 which had been issued by the appellants.
Fact	The said circular, inter alia, stated as follows:  <ORG> have been advised that under Section 171 of the Indian Contract Act, 1872, they have a general lien which they can exercise on the goods which came into their custody of importers, exporters, owners, consignee who have for any reason whatsoever not paid the <ORG> charges such as wharfage, crainage, storage demurrage or any other dues in respect of any earlier consignment/s imported/exported or sought to be exported by them.
Fact	In the circumstances this department will exercise a lien for General Balance of account in respect of wharfage, crainage, storage, demurrage and other dues of <ORG> against the importers/exporters, owners of consignees of the goods taken charge of by <ORG>  Trustees.
Fact	The respondents then filed writ petitions under Article 226 of the Constitution of India in <ORG> seeking a declaration that the aforesaid circular was ultra virus of MPT Act and was violative of Articles 14, 19, 265 and 300A of the Constitution of India.
Fact	The relief which was sought was that the appellants herein should withdraw or cancel the circular and deliver the consignments of goods imported by the respondents and detained under the Circular.
Argument	During the pendency of the writ petition a single judge of <ORG>, by an interim order, directed the release of the consignments of acrylic fibre on the undertaking of the respondents to give a bank guarantee for an amount due which may be claimed by the appellants in respect of the suits filed in the court.
Argument	The contention of the respondents before <ORG> was that the appellants were not entitled to claim general lien under Section 171 of the Contract Act, inter alia, for the reason that there was no existing contractual rela tionship between the appellants and the respondents.
Argument	They also contended that the MPT Act was a complete code in itself and it was not permissible for the appellants to rely on the provisions of the Contract Act so as to claim a general lien.
RulingByLowerCourt	The appellants herein contended that they were entitled to exercise general lien as provided by Section 171 of the Contract Act as they were wharfingers to whom acrylic fibre had been bailed.
RulingByLowerCourt	The single judge by judgment dated 24th November, 1982 allowed the writ petition and granted the relief sought for.
RulingByLowerCourt	The appellants were directed to withdraw or cancel the circular dated 2nd October, 1979 and it was, inter alia, held that the appellants herein could not in law claim general lien under Section 171 of the Contract Act.
Argument	The appellants then filed LPA before <ORG>, but without any success.
Argument	<ORG> held as under:  (a) There is no right of general lien in favour of <ORG> under the provisions of <ORG> Act.
Argument	(b) <ORG> does not h ave a right of general lien under Section 171 of the Indian Contract Act.
Argument	(c) The right of <ORG> flows only from the provisions of <ORG> Act and thus the claim for a general lien by reason of a possessory bailment has been negatived by the Learned Judges.
RatioOfTheDecision	(d) The general lien in favour of <ORG> is excluded by the provisions of <ORG> Act which is a complete code itself and is comprehensive in respect of collection and recovery of charges.
Argument	The learned Additional Solicitor General first contended that the appellants had a general lien under the provisions of the MPT Act on the acrylic fibre which had been imported in respect of the earlier dues.
Argument	For this submission reliance was sought to be placed primarily on Sections 59 and 61 of the MPT Act.
Statute	Section 59 and sub-section (1) of Section 61 , which are relevant, read as follows:  59.
Statute	<ORG>s lien for rates - [1] For the amount of all rates (leviable under this Act) in respect of any goods, and for the rent due to the <ORG> for  any buildings, plinths, stacking areas, or other premises on or in which any goods may have been placed, the Board shall have a lien on such goods and may seize and detain the same until such rates and rents are fully paid.
Statute	[2] Such lien shall have priority over all other liens and claims, except for general average and for the ship-owners lien upon the said goods for freight and other charges where such lien exists and has been preserved in the manner provided in sub-section (1) of Section 60 , and for money payable to <ORG> (under any law for the time being in force relating to customs, other than by way of penalty or fine.]
Statute	61.
Statute	Sale of goods after two months if rates or rent are not paid or lien for freight is not discharged - [1] A Board may, after the expiry of two months from the time when any goods have passed into its custody, or in the case of animals and perishable or hazardous goods after the expiry of such shorter period not being less than twenty-four hours after the landing o f the animals or goods as the <ORG> may think fit, sell by public auction (or in such case as the <ORG> considers it necessary so to do, for reasons to be recorded in writing, sell by tender, private agreement or in any other manner], such goods or so much thereof as, in the opinion of the <ORG>, may be necessary -  (a) if any rates payable to the <ORG> in respect of such goods have not been paid, or  (b) if any rent payable to the <ORG> in respect of any place on or in which such goods have been stored has not been paid, or ( c) if any lien of any ship-owner for freight or other charges of which notice has been given has not been discharged and if the person claiming such lien for freight or other charge has made to the <ORG> an application for such sale.
RatioOfTheDecision	Plain reading of Section 59 shows that in respect of any goods which are imported the <ORG> has a lien for the amount of all rates leviable under the Act and for the rent due to it and it also has a lien on such goods and the <ORG> may seize and detain the  same until such rates are paid.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is clear that it is only in respect of the amount due qua the goods imported and existing there that the <ORG> has alien under Section 59 .
RatioOfTheDecision	Under Section 61 (1), in exercise of its lien, the <ORG> is empowered to sell the said goods for realisation of the amount due to it.
RatioOfTheDecision	Reading the two sections together it is clear that the goods which can be sold in exercise of its lien are only those in respect of which amount is due and payable to the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	The words such goods in Section 61 (1) has reference to those goods in respect of which rates due to the <ORG> have not been fully paid.
RatioOfTheDecision	Coming to the facts of the instant case the amount which was claimed by the appellants was in respect of the consignment of woollen rags.
RatioOfTheDecision	There can be little doubt that in respect of the amount claimed by the <ORG> the provisions of Sections 59 and 61 (1) would have been applicable with regard to the said consignment of woollen rags.
RatioOfTheDecision	But the contention now is that it is in respect of the said du es, relatable to woollen rags, that the <ORG> has a general lien on the subsequent consignment of acrylic fibre.
RatioOfTheDecision	This contention is clearly untenable because, as we have already observed, Sections 59 and 61(1) give a lien on those goods in respect of which amount is claimed or due under Section  59.
RatioOfTheDecision	The <ORG> was not demanding or claiming lien on acrylic fibre on the ground that any amount in respect of acrylic fibre was due.
RatioOfTheDecision	Once it appears that the lien referred to in Sections 59 and 61(1) is only those goods in respect of which amount is due it is clear that the said provisions do not contemplate a general lien as contended by the appellants.
RatioOfTheDecision	<ORG>, in our opinion, was right in coming to the conclusion that the lien conferred on the <ORG> under Section 59 of the MPT Act was not a general lien but was a lien on specific goods.
Argument	It was then contended on behalf of the appellants that as wharfingers they are entitled to a general lien under Section 171 of the Contract Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	In this connection it was su bmitted that <ORG> erred in coming to the conclusion that the MPT Act was a complete code in itself and that Section 171 of the Contract Act was not applicable.
RatioOfTheDecision	The MPT Act is not, in our opinion, an exhaustive and comprehensive code and the said Act has to be read together with other acts wherever the MPT Act is silent in respect of any matter.
RatioOfTheDecision	The MPT Act itself refers to other enactments which would clearly indicate that the MPT Act is not a complete code in itself which ousts the applicability of other acts.
RatioOfTheDecision	The preamble of the Act does not show that it is a codifying Act so as to exclude the applicability of other laws of the land.
RatioOfTheDecision	Even if it is a codifying Act unless a contrary intention appears it is presumed not to be intended to change the law.
RatioOfTheDecision	[See Bennions Statutory Interpretation, Second Edition page 444] Furthermore where codifying statute is silent on a point then it is permissible to look at other laws.
RatioOfTheDecision	In this connection it will be useful to refer to the following observation of the  <ORG> in <ORG> Vs. <ORG> and others [(1984)] 2 All ER 358 at page 363]:  Planning law, though a comprehensive code imposed in the public interest, is, of course, based on land law.
RatioOfTheDecision	Where the code is silent or ambiguous, resort to the principles of private law (especially property and contract law) may be necessary so that the courts may resolve difficulties by application of common law or equitable principles.
RatioOfTheDecision	But such cases will be exceptional.
RatioOfTheDecision	And, if the statute law covers the situation, it will be an impermissible exercise of the judicial function to go beyond the statutory provision by applying such principles merely because they may appear to achieve a fairer solution to the problem being considered.
RatioOfTheDecision	As ever in the field of statute law it is the duty of the courts to give effect to the intention of <ORG> as evinced by the statute, or statutory code, considered as a whole.
Precedent	In <ORG> Vs. <ORG> ([1969] 2 SCR 481 ) it w as held that cognate and pari -materia legislation should be read together as forming one system and as interpreting and enforcing each other.
Precedent	<CITATION_195129> ( 6 SCR 129} it was held that Code of Civil Procedure has to be read along with the Limitation Act .
Precedent	<CITATION_151821> ( 1958 SCR 580) at page 590 it was held that Prevention of Corruption Act should be read along with the Evidence Act .
Precedent	In <NAME> Vs. Mst.
Precedent	<NAME> ( 1 SCR 253) it was held that the Code of Civil Procedure has to be read along with the Court Fees Act .
Precedent	In V.R.
Precedent	Shelat Vs. Pranlal ([1975) 1 SCR 534) this Court observed that the Companies Act should be read along with the Transfer of Property Act .
RatioOfTheDecision	From the aforesaid decisions it clearly follows that it is permissible to read the provisions of the two Acts together when the same are complementary to each other.
RatioOfTheDecision	In fact some provisions of the MPT Act themselves show that other laws are applicable.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is an Act which makes provision for the constitution of port authorities and ve sts the administrative control and management of such ports in such authorities and provides for matters connected therewith.
RatioOfTheDecision	To the extent provisions of the said Act are applicable, there can be little doubt that any provision which is in conflict therewith contained in any other Act would not apply.
RatioOfTheDecision	The enactment of MPT Act does not ipso facto exclude the operation of other laws which may be applicable.
RatioOfTheDecision	Wherever a departure from the general law has to be made the Act specifically provides for the same.
RatioOfTheDecision	This is evident from the following provisions:  {a} Section 29 (2) provides that the provisions of the Industrial Act 1947 or any other law for the time being in force will not apply to the claim for compensation made by an employee whose services are transferred to the <ORG>.
Statute	{b} Section 47 of the MPT Act provides for compensation payable in certain cases where use of any private wharf etc.
Statute	is rendered unlawful.
Statute	Sub-section (3) provides for the manner in which the compensation is to be determined and in the  absence of agreement arbitration is contemplated.
Statute	Clause (i) of Section 47(3) , however, specifically states that the Arbitration and Conciliation Act , 1996 shall not apply to the arbitrations under the said section.
RatioOfTheDecision	The said provision makes the general law of arbitration contained in the Arbitration and Conciliation Act inapplicable.
RatioOfTheDecision	{c } Section 68 of the MPT Act is important as it provides that notwithstanding the provisions contained in Section 45 of the Indian Contract Act, 1872 in case of <ORG> security payment would be made to joint promise in accordance with the provisions contained in Section 68 of the MPT Act and not in accordance with Section 45 of the Indian Contract Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	Thus Section 68 makes a specific departure from the provisions of Section 45 of the Indian Contract Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	{d} Sections 70 and 71 of the MPT Act make specific departure of the provisions contained in the Negotiable Instruments Act , 1881 regarding endorsements to be made on <ORG> security and the effect thereof.
RatioOfTheDecision	The  aforesaid sections of the MPT Act clearly show that the said Act is not exhaustive or comprehensive code and it envisages joint reading with other relevant statutes.
RatioOfTheDecision	Whenever any departure has to be made from other laws specific provision to that effect has been made in the MPT Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	<ORG> has rightly come to the conclusion that the MPT Act and Sections 59 and 61 in particular do not give to the appellants the general lien which it is claiming.
RatioOfTheDecision	In other words it is because the MPT Act does not provide for a general lien that the appellants are relying on the provisions of Section 171 of the Contract Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	This, in our opinion, is permissible.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is not possible to hold that the MPT Act ousts the applicability of the provisions of Section 171 of the Contract Act under which the <ORG> is claiming a right of general lien as a wharfinger.
RatioOfTheDecision	The general lien of the type contemplated by Section 171 in respect of the past dues is not provided for by the MPT Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	There is another aspect which is relevant.
RatioOfTheDecision	Sect ion 171 of the Contract Act only enables the retention of goods as security.
RatioOfTheDecision	On the other hand in respect of current dues in respect of existing goods in their possession the <ORG> not only has a lien under Section 59 of the MPT Act but it also has the power to sell the said goods and realise its dues by virtue of Section 61 of the MPT Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	The procedure for exercising this power of sale of the goods in respect of which the <ORG> has lien is contained in the said section.
RatioOfTheDecision	Before selling the goods no order of any court or other judicial authority is required.
RatioOfTheDecision	On the other hand the general lien contemplated by Section 171 of the Contract Act only enables the retention of the bailed goods as a security.
RatioOfTheDecision	Their retention does not give any power to sell the goods, unlike the power contained in Section 61 of the MPT Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	If payment is not made by the consignee to the wharfinger, in a case where Section 171 of the Contract Act applies, the wharfinger can only retain the goods bailed as security and will have to take  recourse to other proceedings in accordance with law for securing an order which would then enable the goods to be sold for realisation of the amounts due to it.
RatioOfTheDecision	It may in this connection, be necessary for the wharfinger to file a suit for the recovery of the amount due to it and Section 131 of the MPT Act clearly provides that such a remedy of filing a suit is available to the Board.
RatioOfTheDecision	The added advantage of sale given by Section 61 of MPT Act in respect of current dues cannot be regarded as whittling down the right of general lien contained in Section 171 of Contract Act in respect of old dues.
RatioOfTheDecision	Having come to the conclusion that the MPT Act does not oust the provisions of Section 171 of the Contract Act what we have now to see is whether the appellants can claim any relief or benefit under the said section.
Statute	Section 171 of the Indian Contract Act, 1872, reads as follows:  171 General lien of bankers, factors, wharfingers, attorneys, and policy-brokers - Bankers, factors, wharfingers, attorneys of <ORG>  and policy-brokers may, in the absence of a contract to the contrary, retain as a security for a general balance of account, any goods bailed to them; but no other persons have a right to retain, as a security for such balance, goods bailed to them, unless there is an express contract to that effect.
Statute	This section is in two parts.
Statute	The first part gives statutory right of lien to four categories only, namely, bankers, factors, wharfingers and attorneys of <ORG> and policy-brokers subject to their contracting out of Section 171 .
Statute	The second part of Section 171 applies to persons other than aforesaid five categories and to them Section 171 does not give a statutory right of lien.
Statute	It provides that they will have no right to retain as securities bailed to them unless there is an express contract to that effect.
RatioOfTheDecision	Whereas in respect of the first category of person mentioned in Section 171 section itself enables them to retain the goods as security in the absence of a contract to the contrary but in respect of an y other person to whom goods are bailed the right of retaining them as securities can be exercised only if there is an express contract to that effect.
RatioOfTheDecision	The appellants in the present case are contending that they are wharfingers and the goods which were imported and off loaded at the port were with them as <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	The submission of the learned counsel for the appellants was that in the absence of a contract to the contrary as <ORG> of the goods now imported, namely, acrylic fibre the said consignment could be retained by the appellants as security for the amount due to them towards wharfage and demurrage charges in respect of the earlier consignment of woollen rags.
RatioOfTheDecision	While considering this contention we have also to examine whether the claim for wharfage and demurrage could be covered by the expression general balance of account occurring in Section 171 of the Contract Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	Wharf is defined in <ORG>, Second Edition, as being a broad plain place, near some creek or haven, to lay goods  and wares on that are brought to on from the water.
RatioOfTheDecision	In <NAME> dictionary wharf is defined as a structure of masonry or timber erected on the shore of a harbor, river, or the like, alongside which vessels may lie to load or unload cargo, passengers etc.
RatioOfTheDecision	; also, any landing place for vessels, as a pier or quay.
RatioOfTheDecision	<NAME> The Law Lexicon, Second Edition, defines wharf as a landing stage built especially along the shore for loading or unloading vessels.
RatioOfTheDecision	The MPT Act contains an inclusive definition of wharf in Section 2 (za) and provides that wharf includes any wall or stage and any part of the land or foreshore that may be used for loading or unloading goods, or for the embarkation or disembarkation of passengers and any wall enclosing or adjoining the same.
RatioOfTheDecision	Wharfinger is not defined in the Act but in <NAME> dictionary of English Law wharfinger is defined as the occupier of wharf and it is further stated that as a rule, wharfingers have a general lien for the balance of their account.
RatioOfTheDecision	In <NAME>  The law Lexicon wharfinger is defined as meaning the occupier of a wharf or a person who owns a wharf.
RatioOfTheDecision	The appellants are the owners of the wharf at Bombay where the consignments of the respondents were discharged.
Statute	The services which are provided by the appellants in respect thereof as wharfingers are, inter alia, contained in Section 42 of the MPT Act which reads as follows:  42.
Statute	Performance of services by <ORG> or other person  - [1] A <ORG> shall have power to undertake the following services -  [a] landing, shipping or transshipping passengers and goods between vessels in the port and the wharves, piers, quays or docks belonging to or in the possession of the <ORG>;  [b] receiving, removing, shifting, transporting, storing or delivering goods brought within the <ORG>s premises;  [c] carrying passengers by rail or by other means within the limits of the port or port approaches, subject to such restrictions and conditions as <ORG> may think fit to impose;  [d] receiving and delivering, tr ansporting and booking and despatching goods originating in the vessels in the port and intended for carriage by the neighbouring railways, or vice versa, as a railway administration under the Indian Railways Act 1890 ; (and) [e] piloting, hauling, mooring, remorring, hooking, or measuring of vessels or any other service in respect of vessels.
Statute	[2] A <ORG> may, if so requested by the owner, take charge of the goods for the purpose of performing the service or services and shall give a receipt in such form as the <ORG> may specify.
Statute	[3] Notwithstanding anything contained in this section, the <ORG> may, with the previous sanction of <ORG>, authorise any person to perform any of the services mentioned in sub-section (1) on such terms and conditions as may be agreed upon.
Statute	[4] No person authorised under sub-section (3) shall charge or recover for such service any sum in excess of the amount {specified by the <ORG>, by notification in the Official Gazette}.
Statute	[5] Any such person shall, if so re quired by the owner, perform in respect of goods any of the said services and for that purpose take charge of the goods and give a receipt in such form as the <ORG> may specify.
Statute	[6] The responsibility of any such person for the loss, destruction or deterioration of goods of which he has taken charge shall, subject to the other provisions of this Act, be that of a bailee under sections 151 , 152 and 161 of the Indian Contract Act, 1872 .

Statute	Reading of the aforesaid section shows that the services required to be performed by the <ORG> is not only of loading and unloading of the cargo but would also include storing and delivering of goods.
Statute	Under sub-section (2) if the <ORG> is requested by the owner to take c harge of the goods then it is required to give a receipt in such form as the <ORG> may specify.
Statute	Sub-section (6), inter alia, states that responsibility of any such person who takes charge of such goods shall be that of a bailee under Sections 151 , 152 and 161 of the Contract Act.
Statute	Sub-section (7) absolves the person to whom receipt is given of any liability for any loss or damage which may occur to the goods.
Statute	The responsibility of the <ORG> for the loss of goods is provided for in Section 43 of the MPT Act which reads as follows:  43.
Statute	Responsibility of <ORG> for loss, etc.
Statute	of goods  - [1] Subject to the provisions of this Act, the responsibility of any <ORG> for the loss, destruction or deterioration of goods of which it has taken charge shall, -  (i) in the case of goods received for carriage by railway, be governed by the provisions of the Indian Railways Act , 1890 ; and (ii) in other cases, be that of a bailee under sections 151 , 152 and 161 of the Indian Contract Act, 1872 , omitting the words in the ab sence of any special contract in section 152 of that Act;  {Provided that no responsibility under this section shall attach to the <ORG> -  (a) until a receipt mentioned in sub-section (2) of section 42 is given by the <ORG>; and  (b) after the expiry of such period as may be prescribed by regulations from the date of taking charge of such goods by the <ORG>.}

RatioOfTheDecision	Reading the aforesaid and other provisions of the MPT Act it is abundantly clear that the appellants are wharfingers who not only provide space at the port for the loading and unloading of the goods but also provide for the storage of the goods till the same are removed.
RatioOfTheDecision	We may here notice that in exercise of the powers conferred by Section 126 read with Sections 42 and 43 of the MPT Act <ORG> issued a notification dated 1st February 1975 notifying the Port of Bombay (Responsibility for Goods) Regulations, 1975.
Statute	The said regulations, inter alia, provide that a receipt referred to in sub-section (2) of Section 42 shall be given in the f orm annexed to the said regulations.
Statute	The said form, which is a receipt contemplated by Section 42 (2), gives particulars of the goods which are unloaded and stored in a shed or open area of berth or a warehouse.
RatioOfTheDecision	Whether the issuance of such a receipt would amount to an agreement or concluded contract coming into being between the appellants and the respondents is wholly immaterial because the receipt evidences the goods coming into the possession of the appellants and under Section 42 (6) the appellants would be regarded as a bailee thereof to whom the provisions of Sections 151 , 152 and 161 of the Contract Act become applicable.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is because of this the relationship of bailor and bailee comes into existence when the Board is required to store the imported goods.
RatioOfTheDecision	At this juncture it is appropriate to deal with the conclusion of <ORG> to the effect that with the issuance of the receipt under Section 42 (2) the contract, if any, is between the ship owner and the port trust and not between the consi gnee who is true owner of the goods and <ORG>.
Precedent	In coming to this conclusion <ORG> has placed reliance on the decision of this Court in <ORG> by its Chairman Vs. <ORG> and Ors.
Precedent	( Supp.
Precedent	2 SCR  915).
Precedent	In that case the question which arose was as to who was responsible for idle labour charges.
RatioOfTheDecision	Labour was supplied by <ORG> authorities but their services were not fully utilised and the question arose as to whether it was the consignee or the ship owner who was liable to pay the said charges.
RatioOfTheDecision	This Court held that <ORG> took charge of the goods on behalf of the ship owner and not on behalf of the consignee and whatever services were performed at the time of landing of the goods or on their removal were rendered to the ship owner and, therefore, the charges were rightly leviable not on the consignee but on the streamer agents.
RatioOfTheDecision	The service of providing the labour was, in that case, therefore, to the owners of the streamer and n ot to the consignee and it is for that reason the liability was held not to be that of the latter.
RatioOfTheDecision	This decision has no relevance to the point in issue which has to be decided in the present case.
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 2 (o) contains the definition of owner.
RatioOfTheDecision	In relation to goods the said section states that the word owner includes any consignor, consignee, shipper or agent for the sale, custody, loading or unloading of such goods.
RatioOfTheDecision	By referring to this sub-section this Court in <ORG> and <ORG> ( 1 SCC 142) held that in the case of imports the liability to pay demurrage, on the endorsement being made on the bill of lading, would be that of the consignee.
RatioOfTheDecision	This is in consonance with the provisions of the Bills of Lading Act , 1856.
RatioOfTheDecision	The preamble of this Act provides that by custom of merchants a bill of lading of goods being transferable by endorsement, the property in the goods may thereby pass to the endorsee, but nevertheless all rights in respect of the contract  contained in the bill of lading continue in the original shipper or owner and, therefore, it is expedient that such rights should pass with a property.
Statute	Section 1 of the Bills of Lading Act provides that rights under bills of lading vest in the consignee or endorsee and reads as under:  1.
Statute	Rights under bills of lading to vest in consignee or endorsee - Every consignee of goods named in a bill of lading and every endorsee of a bill of lading to whom the property in the goods herein mentioned shall pass, upon or by reason of such consignment or endorsement, shall have transferred to and vested in him all rights of suit, and be subject to the same liabilities in respect of such goods as if the contract contained in the bill of lading had been made with himself.
RatioOfTheDecision	The provisions of Section 2 (o) of the MPT Act regards, in relation to goods, the consignee as the owner thereof.
RatioOfTheDecision	Reading the same along with the Bills of Lading Act the consignee of the goods named in the bill of lading or every endorsee of the bill of l ading, for the purpose of MPT Act is regarded as the owner of the goods and it is from that owner that the appellant is entitled to recover charges under the MPT Act in respect of the said goods.
RatioOfTheDecision	<ORG> was not right in holding that the contract was between the ship owner and <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	The correct position is that the contract is between <ORG> and the holder of the bill of lading which, in this case, would be the consignee.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is the consignee which is the bailor with <ORG> being the consignee.
RatioOfTheDecision	It was then argued by <NAME> that under Section 171 of the Contract Act the lien is available only in the absence of a contract to the contrary.
Argument	He contended that the MPT Act was a special statute which provides not only the services of wharfingers to be provided by the <ORG> but also for various other services to be provided by it.
Argument	In respect of these services the <ORG> is entitled to impose and recover rates/charges for the services rendered.
Argument	Chapter VI provides for the s cale of rates and the matters connected therewith including the exercise by the Board for its lien and for recovery of the charges due to it by sale of goods.
Argument	In this context it was submitted that Chapter VI of the MPT Act is a contract to the contrary between the parties.
RatioOfTheDecision	We are unable to accept this submission.
RatioOfTheDecision	As has already been held earlier the general lien contained in Section 171 of the Contract Act is not covered by the provisions of Chapter VI of the MPT Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	The MPT Act no doubt deals with lien in respect, inter alia, of the goods imported but it does not deal with the general lien of the type we are concerned with in this case, namely, amounts due in respect of earlier consignments for which payment has not been made.
RatioOfTheDecision	The contract to the contrary as envisaged in Section 171 of the Contract Act has to be specific.
RatioOfTheDecision	The MPT Act including Chapter VI no where provides that the general lien under Section 171 of the Contract Act would not be available to the wharfingers in case where the MPT Act is appli cable.
Argument	It was also contended on behalf of the respondents that even if Section 171 of the Contract Act applies the appellants can exercise their lien under Section 171 of the Contract Act for the recovery of their dues for the services rendered by them as wharfingers only and not for any other services provided by them as detailed in the MPT Act.
Argument	It was contended that wharfage is the money paid for landing goods at a wharf or for shipping and taking goods into a boat or barge.
RatioOfTheDecision	The general lien of wharfinger as understood under Section 171 of the Contract Act would limit to the charges due to a wharfinger for the services rendered as a wharfinger and not otherwise.
RatioOfTheDecision	On this premise it was submitted that once the appellant takes charges of the goods from the ship owner it does not act as a wharfinger but acts in another capacity which may be that of warehouse owner, <NAME> etc.
RatioOfTheDecision	and, therefore, lien cannot be claimed in respect of demurrage etc.
RatioOfTheDecision	but, at best, can be claimed only for wharfage charges.
RatioOfTheDecision	Attractive  as it may appear, we do not find any merit in the aforesaid submission.
RatioOfTheDecision	The first part of Section 171 of the Contract Act identifies five categories of persons who can have a general lien and retain the goods bailed to them.
RatioOfTheDecision	Wharfinger is one of them.
RatioOfTheDecision	The submission of the learned counsel for the respondents does not take into account the fact that Section 171 of the Contract Act enables these five categories to retain as security the goods bailed to them in respect of general balance of account.
RatioOfTheDecision	The general balance of account has to be of the amount legally due to bankers, factors, wharfingers, attorneys and policy brokers.
RatioOfTheDecision	The appellants come in the category of wharfingers, namely, the owners of the wharf.
RatioOfTheDecision	The duties which they are required to perform are provided in the statute itself, namely, Section 42 of the MPT Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	In other words the services which are undertaken under Section 42 have to be paid for and any amount due in respect thereof will be regarded as general balance of account There is no reason  to give a restricted meaning to the expression general balance of account to mean only wharfage charges which, according to the respondents, would imply the charges for loading or unloading of goods, and would not include demurrage.
RatioOfTheDecision	Once goods are taken charge of by the appellants as a wharfingers then in respect of the services rendered, as contemplated by Section 42 , if there is any amount which is due and payable to it the same would be regarded as general balance of account in respect of which it has a general lien over the goods bailed to it.
RatioOfTheDecision	In our opinion the circular dated 2nd October, 1979 issued by the appellants was valid and the appellants could retain the goods which were in their possession as bailees as security for realisation of the amount of wharfage, demurrage and other charges which were due to them.
RulingByPresentCourt	We accordingly allow these appeals and set aside the judgment of <ORG> with the result that the writ petitions filed by the respondents in <ORG> stand dismissed.
RulingByPresentCourt	The appea ls are allowed with costs throughout.
