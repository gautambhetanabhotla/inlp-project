(J) CITATION: 1996 AIR 1239 1996 SCC (4) 212 JT 1996 (3) 60 1996 SCALE (2)577 ACT: HEADNOTE: JUDGMENT:  O R D E R Notification under Section 4 [1] of the Land Acquisition Act , 1894 [for short, the "Act"] was published in <ORG> on February 26, 1976.
Fact	Dispensing with the enquiry under Section 5-A , declaration under Section 6 was published on the same day.
Fact	Notice under Section 9 was served on March 3, 1976.
Fact	The appellant filed Writ Petition No.1359 of 1976 on March 7, 1976.
Fact	On March 19,1976, <ORG> ordered stay of dispossession.
Fact	After the counter- affidavit was filed by the respondents, <ORG> dismissed the writ petition on April 4, 1976.
Fact	In the meanwhile, award enquiry was conducted and the award under Section 11 was made on March 18, 1976.
Fact	Possession of the land was taken on April 17,1976 .
Fact	It would appear that the Land Acquisition Officer [LAO] had deposited the compensation on May 18, 1976.
Fact	Record has been placed before us to show that the land was earmarked and distributed to 592 landless workers on April 22, 1976.
Fact	On December 11, 1979, the appellant again filed Writ Petition No.4460 of 1979 which came to be dismissed by <ORG> on February 7, 1980.
Fact	Thus this appeal by special leave.
Fact	Interim stay granted on May 8, 1980 was modified by this Court, maintaining status quo, by order dated September 13, 1981.
Fact	It is clear from these facts that after dispensing with the enquiry under Section 5-A , immediate action was taken by issuance of the notice under Section 9 and award enquiry was conducted since dispossession was ordered by <ORG>.
Fact	After the dismissal of the writ petition, possession was taken on April 17, 1976.
Fact	Thus the process of the requisition was completed and the acquisition became final.
Fact	What remained to be done was only the determination of the compensation in respec t of the acquired land.
Fact	In this case, the land acquired is of the extent of 121 canals 10 marlas.
Argument	<NAME>, learned counsel appearing for the appellant with his thorough preparation, has contended that dispensing with enquiry under Section 5 A is bad in law.
RatioOfTheDecision	However, we find no force in the contention.
Statute	<ORG> has amended the Central Act by Amendment Act s II/1954, XVII/6 and XLVIII/1956 whereunder an explanation has been added to sub-section  of Section 17 of the Act.
Statute	Sub-section  was also added in which clause [b] of the sub-section envisages thus:  "(b) whenever in the opinion of the Collector it becomes necessary to acquire the immediate possession of any land for the purpose of any library or educational institution or for the construction, extension or improvement of any building or other structure in any village for the common use of inhabitants of such village, or any godown for any society registered under Co- operative Societies Act , 1912, or any dwelling house for the poor, o r the construction of labour colonies or houses for any other class of people under a <ORG> sponsored housing scheme, or any irrigation tank, irrigation or drainage channel, or any well, or any public road;"  Thus the <ORG>, by virtue of State Amendment is empowered to exercise the urgency clause under sub-section [4] of Section 17 and to dispense with the enquiry under Section 5-A of the Act.
Argument	<NAME> has contended that mere existence of the power is not sufficient.
Argument	The urgency should be such as would not brook delay of 30 days in conducting the enquiry contemplated under Section 5-A .
Argument	In this case, allotment of the house sites to the poor is not such an urgency which cannot wait for conducting the enquiry.
Argument	Therefore, exercising the power under Section 17  is bad in law.
Argument	He seeks to place reliance on the decision of this Court in <ORG> v. State of Maharashtra [(1977) 1 SCR 763].
Argument	In a recent decision in <ORG> v. State of U.P.
Precedent	& Anr.
Precedent	[[(1996) 1 SCALE 101], th is <ORG> considered the entire case law and held that providing house sites to the poor is an urgent necessity and exercise of the power under Section 17  to dispense with the enquiry under Section 5-A would be justified.
Precedent	The reasoning of this <ORG> in <NAME>'s case also was considered and it was held that exercising the power under Section 17  cannot be struck down when the Government was of the opinion that it urgently required the possession of the land for providing house sites to the poor.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is seen that the entire gamut of the acquisition proceedings stood completed by April 17, 1976 by which date possession of the land had been taken.
RatioOfTheDecision	No doubt, <NAME> has contended that the appellant still retained their possession.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is now well-settled legal position that it is difficult to take physical possession of the land under compulsory acquisition.
RatioOfTheDecision	The normal mode of taking possession is drafting the <ORG> in the presence of <ORG> and taking possession and giving delivery to the beneficiaries is  the accepted mode of taking possession of the land.
RatioOfTheDecision	Subsequent thereto, the retention of possession would tantamount only to illegal or unlawful possession.
RatioOfTheDecision	Under these circumstances, merely because the appellant retained possession of the acquired land, the acquisition cannot be said to be bad in law.
Argument	It is then contended by <NAME> that the appellant-Institution is running an educational institution and intends to establish a public school and that since other land was available, the Government would have acquired some other land leaving the acquired land for the appellant.
Argument	In the counter-affidavit filed in <ORG>, it was stated that apart from the acquired land, the appellant also owned 482 canals 19 marlas of land.
Argument	Thereby, it is seen that the appellant is not disabled to proceed with the continuation of the educational institution which it seeks to establish.
Argument	It is then contended that an opportunity may be given to the appellant to make a representation to <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	We find that  it is not necessary for us to give any such liberty since acquisition process has already been completed.
RatioOfTheDecision	Pursuant to the directions issued by this <ORG>, the Chief Secretary himself has conducted an enquiry and identified the officer who was responsible for the lapses in omitting to instruct the counsel and for not producing the record as part of the record of the <ORG>.
RulingByPresentCourt	We accept the report submitted by the Chief Secretary.
RulingByPresentCourt	It would be open to him to pursue further action to reach a logical conclusion.
RulingByPresentCourt	The appeal is dismissed accordingly.
RulingByPresentCourt	No costs.
