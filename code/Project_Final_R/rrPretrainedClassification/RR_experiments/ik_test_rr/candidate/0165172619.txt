Fact	Aggrieved by their conviction and sentence, <NAME> and Pro santa have preferred this appeal.

Fact	19.
Fact	The crucial issue for consideration, therefore, relates to interference by <ORG> in an acquittal given by <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	Recently, in <NAME> v. <ORG> of Haryana[3] it was held, after referring to <CITATION_802260>  that “Before we proceed to consider the rivalised contentions raised at the bar and independently scrutinize the relevant evidence brought on record, it is fruitful to recapitulate the law enunciated by this <ORG> pertaining to an appeal against acquittal.
RatioOfTheDecision	In <NAME> (supra), it has been stated that <ORG> can exercise the power or jurisdiction to reverse  an order of acquittal in cases where it finds that the lower court has "obstinately blundered" or has "through incompetence, stupidity or perversity" reached such "distorted conclusions as to produce a positive miscarriage of justice" or has in some other way so conducted or misconducted himself as to produce a glaring miscarriage of justice or has been tricked by the defence so as to produce a similar result.” Unfortunately, the paraphrasing of the concerned passage from Sheo Swarup gave us an impression that <ORG> can reverse an acquittal by a lower court only in limited circumstances.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, we referred to the passage in Sheo Swarup and find that what was stated was as follows:  “There is in their opinion no foundation for the view, apparently supported by the judgments of some <ORG> in India, that <ORG> has no power or jurisdiction to reverse an order of acquittal on a matter of fact, except in cases in which the lower Court has "obstinately blundered," or has "through incompetence,  stupidity or perversity" reached such "distorted conclusions as to produce a positive miscarriage of justice," or has in some other way so conducted itself as to produce a glaring miscarriage of justice, or has been tricked by the defence so as to produce a similar result.” The legal position was reiterated in <CITATION_1202593>  after citing <NAME> and it was held:  “Their Lordships do not think it necessary to read it all again, but would like to observe that there really is only one principle, in the strict use of the word, laid down there; that is, that <ORG> has full power to review at large all the evidence upon which the order of acquittal was founded, and to reach the conclusion that upon that evidence the order of acquittal should be reversed.” We are mentioning this only to dispel the possibility of anyone else getting an impression similar to the one that we got, though nothing much turns on this as far as this case is concerned.
RatioOfTheDecision	20.
RatioOfTheDecision	The entire case law on the subject was discussed  in <NAME> v.  State of Karnataka[6] beginning with perhaps the first case decided by this <ORG> on the subject being <NAME> v.
Argument	State.
Precedent	[7] It was held in <NAME> as follows:  “(1) An appellate court has full power to review, reappreciate and reconsider the evidence upon which the order of acquittal is founded.
Precedent	(2) The Code of Criminal Procedure, 1973 puts no limitation, restriction or condition on exercise of such power and an appellate court on the evidence before it may reach its own conclusion, both on questions of fact and of law.
Precedent	(3) Various expressions, such as, ‘substantial and compelling reasons’, ‘good and sufficient grounds’, ‘very strong circumstances’, ‘distorted conclusions’, ‘glaring mistakes’, etc.
Precedent	are not intended to curtail extensive powers of an appellate court in an appeal against acquittal.
Precedent	Such phraseologies are more in the nature of ‘flourishes of language’ to emphasise the reluctance of an appellate court to interfere with acquittal than to curtail the power of the court to revie w the evidence and to come to its own conclusion.
Precedent	(4) An appellate court, however, must bear in mind that in case of acquittal, there is double presumption in favour of the accused.
Precedent	Firstly, the presumption of innocence is available to him under the fundamental principle of criminal jurisprudence that every person shall be presumed to be innocent unless he is proved guilty by a competent court of law.
Precedent	Secondly, the accused having secured his acquittal, the presumption of his innocence is further reinforced, reaffirmed and strengthened by the trial court.
Precedent	(5) If two reasonable conclusions are possible on the basis of the evidence on record, the appellate court should not disturb the finding of acquittal recorded by the trial court.”  21.
Precedent	The principles laid down in <NAME> were generally reiterated but mainly reformulated in <NAME> v. State of Haryana[8] though without reference to <NAME> and by referring to decisions not considered therein.
Precedent	The reformulation of the principles in <NAME> is as follows:   “(i) There is no limitation on the part of the appellate court to review the evidence upon which the order of acquittal is founded and to come to its own conclusion.
Precedent	(ii) The appellate court can also review the trial court’s conclusion with respect to both facts and law.
Precedent	(iii) While dealing with the appeal preferred by the <ORG>, it is the duty of the appellate court to marshal the entire evidence on record and by giving cogent and adequate reasons may set aside the judgment of acquittal.
Precedent	(iv) An order of acquittal is to be interfered with only when there are “compelling and substantial reasons” for doing so.
RatioOfTheDecision	If the order is “clearly unreasonable”, it is a compelling reason for interference.
RatioOfTheDecision	(v) When the trial court has ignored the evidence or misread the material evidence or has ignored material documents like dying declaration/report of ballistic experts, etc.
RatioOfTheDecision	the appellate court is competent to reverse the decision of the trial court depending on the materials placed.
RatioOfTheDecision	(<NAME> v. <ORG> of J&K[9 ], <NAME> v. State of U.P.
RatioOfTheDecision	[10], <NAME> v. State of M.P.
RatioOfTheDecision	[11] and <NAME> v. State of Haryana[12].
RatioOfTheDecision	)”  22.
RatioOfTheDecision	Undoubtedly, we are suffering from an overdose of precedents but be that as it may, from the principles laid down, it appears at first blush that <ORG> is entitled to virtually step into the shoes of <ORG> hearing submissions of learned counsel and then decide the case as a court of first instance.
RatioOfTheDecision	Perhaps this is not what is intended, notwithstanding the broad language used in <NAME> and <NAME>.
RatioOfTheDecision	Otherwise, the decision of <ORG> would be a meaningless exercise and this Court would become a first appellate court from a decision of <ORG> in a case of acquittal by <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	Realistically speaking, although the principles stated are broad, it is the obligation of <ORG> to consider and identify the error in the decision of <ORG> and then decide whether the error is gross enough to warrant interference.
RatioOfTheDecision	<ORG> is n ot expected to merely substitute its opinion for that of <ORG> only because the first two principles in Chandrappa and Ganpat permit it to do so and because it has the power to do so – it has to correct an error of law or fact significant enough to necessitate overturning the verdict of <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	This is where <ORG> has to exercise its discretion very cautiously, keeping in mind the acquittal of the accused and the rights of the victim (who may or may not be before it).
RatioOfTheDecision	This is also where the fifth principle laid down in Chandrappa and Ganpat comes into operation.

RatioOfTheDecision	The High <ORG> believed the testimony of <NAME> and <NAME> and came to the conc lusion that they had crossed the river along with <NAME>, <NAME> and <NAME>.
RatioOfTheDecision	However, <ORG> did not take into consideration the view of <ORG>, based on the evidence on record, that it was doubtful if the five persons mentioned above boarded the boat belonging to <NAME> to cross the river as alleged by the prosecution.
RatioOfTheDecision	<ORG> also did not consider the apparently incorrect testimony of <NAME> who had stated that he had gone to the police station and given his version but despite this, he was not cited as a witness.

RatioOfTheDecision	29.
RatioOfTheDecision	When the basic fact of <NAME> having boarded a boat and crossing the river with <NAME> and <NAME> is in doubt, the substratum of the prosecution’s case virtually falls flat and the truth of the subsequent events also becomes doubtful.
RatioOfTheDecision	Unfortunately, <ORG> does not seem to have looked at the evidence from the point of view of the accused who had already secured an acqui ttal.
RatioOfTheDecision	This is an important perspective as noted in the fourth principle of <NAME>.
RatioOfTheDecision	<ORG> was also obliged to consider (which it did not) whether the view of <ORG> is a reasonable and possible view (the fifth principle of <NAME>) or not.
RatioOfTheDecision	Merely because <ORG> disagreed (without giving reasons why it did so) with the reasonable and possible view of <ORG>, on a completely independent analysis of the evidence on record, is not a sound basis to set aside the order of acquittal given by <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	This is not to say that every fact arrived at or every reason given by <ORG> must be dealt with – all that it means is that the decision of <ORG> cannot be ignored or treated as non-existent.
RatioOfTheDecision	30.
RatioOfTheDecision	What is also important in this case is that it is one of circumstantial evidence.
RatioOfTheDecision	Following the principles laid down in several decisions of this <ORG> beginning with <NAME> v. State of Maharashtra[13] it is clear that the chain of events m ust be so complete as to leave no room for any other hypothesis except that the accused were responsible for the death of the victim.
RatioOfTheDecision	This principle has been followed and reiterated in a large number of decisions over the last 30 years and one of the more recent decisions in this regard is <NAME> v. <ORG> (NCT of Delhi) and Another.
RatioOfTheDecision	[14] <ORG> did not take this into consideration and merely proceeded on the basis of the last seen theory.
RatioOfTheDecision	31.
RatioOfTheDecision	The facts of this case demonstrate that the first link in the chain of circumstances is missing.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is only if this first link is established that the subsequent links may be formed on the basis of the last seen theory.
RatioOfTheDecision	But <ORG> overlooked the missing link, as it were, and directly applied the last seen theory.
RatioOfTheDecision	In our opinion, this was a rather unsatisfactory way of dealing with the appeal.
RatioOfTheDecision	32.
RatioOfTheDecision	Under the circumstances, we are unable to agree with learned counsel for the <ORG> and are of the opinion that there was really no occasion for  <ORG> to have overturned the view of <ORG> which was not only a reasonable view but a probable view of the events.

RatioOfTheDecision	In the view that we have taken, it is not necessary to deal with these submissions.
RatioOfTheDecision	34.
RatioOfTheDecision	Learned counsel for the <ORG> relied on the evidence of Dr. <NAME> to submit that <NAME> died between 65 and 70 hours before the post mortem examination was conducted.
RatioOfTheDecision	As observed by <ORG>, this placed <NAME>’s death soon after 5.30 p.m. on 19th May, 1995.
RatioOfTheDecision	The significance of this is only with respect to the time of death and has no reference to the persons who may have caused the death of <NAME>.
RulingByLowerCourt	The evidence of Dr. <NAME>, therefore, does not take the case of the <ORG> any further.

RulingByLowerCourt	Consequently, there was no occasion for <ORG> to set aside the acquittal of <NAME> and <NAME>.
