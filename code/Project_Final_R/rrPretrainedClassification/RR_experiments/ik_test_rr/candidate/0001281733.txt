RatioOfTheDecision	6.
RatioOfTheDecision	It seems to us quite clear that the problem referred is solved as soon as the fundamental concept and incidents of motor vehicles insurance, particularly insurance against third party risk s, are first borne in mind and then the provisions of the statute are examined to ascertain their object, purpose and effect.
RatioOfTheDecision	7.
RatioOfTheDecision	With the increase in the use of motor vehicles as a means of public and private transport on road, and its potentiality to endanger life and property, a complex form of insurance known as "motor vehicles insurance" became evolved.
RatioOfTheDecision	Initially, motor vehicles insurance was a kind of property insurance for the purpose of indemnifying the owner of the vehicle against loss or damage to it and its accessories through accident.
RatioOfTheDecision	Then arose the necessity of relief against per-  sonal injuries to the owner and also the person or property of third parties using the road.
RatioOfTheDecision	Thus came into being the comprehensive policy.
RatioOfTheDecision	In this way, the motor vehicles insurance provided for protection of the owner of the vehicle against loss of three kinds: (1) Against loss or damage to the vehicle and its accessories; (2) against personal injury to the owner; and (3) against loss arising from liability for death  or injury caused to third parties or for damage to their property.
RatioOfTheDecision	8.
RatioOfTheDecision	Regarding the third species of protection, the insurance policy is a contract of indemnity under which the insurer agrees to reimburse the assured to the extent of the amount of damages which the latter may become liable to pay for the death or injury to a third party, or damages to his property caused in an accident.
RatioOfTheDecision	This is and has always been undoubted law.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is stated in 32 Halsbury (Simonds) 354:-- "  "In its main features a contract of motor insurance is a contract of indemnity : <ORG> v. <ORG>, 1908-1 KB 1006 at page 1014; <ORG> v. <NAME>, 1919-1 KB 520 at p. 529 = 1920 AC 956.
RatioOfTheDecision	This means that, so far as concerns the insurance of the car as an example of property insurance or the insurance of third party liability (whether limited or general) as an example of public liability insurance, the ordinary principles of these classes of insurance are applicable."
Fact	It is s tated in <NAME> and <NAME> on Law of Insurance (2nd Edition) at page 4:--  "Indemnity, it has been said, is the controlling principle in insurance Law: <NAME> v. <NAME>, 1883-1 QBD 380 at page 386 and by reference to that principle a great many difficulties arising on insurance contracts can be settled.
Fact	Except in insurance on life and against accident, the insurer contracts to indemnify the assured for what he may actually lose by the happening of the events upon which the insurer's liability is to arise, and in no circumstances is the assured in theory entitled to make a profit of his loss.
Fact	1883-I QBD 380 at p. 386 (supra) and <NAME> v. <NAME>, 1922 AC 180.
Fact	It is not however on such consideration as public policy or the prevention of crime that the principle of indemnity is based; it derives from the intrinsic nature of the bargain."
Fact	9.
Fact	At one time, the legality of insurance against third party risks was in doubt.
RatioOfTheDecision	But in <NAME> v. <ORG>, 1921-3 KB 327 its validity was  established.
RatioOfTheDecision	Even so, the third party had no right under the law to obtain the benefit of the insurance.
RatioOfTheDecision	The third party, being not a privity to the contract of insurance, had no right to the insurance money either under the common law or in equity.
RatioOfTheDecision	The third party could not sue the insurer directly nor indirectly in the name of the assured, to compel the insurer to pay the insurance money to him.
RatioOfTheDecision	The reason was that under the common law, a stranger to a contract could not sue upon it.
Fact	In England, the Third Parties (Rights against Insurers) Act, 1930, was enacted to confer on third parties rights against insurer of third party risks in the event of an assured becoming insolvent and certain other events.
RatioOfTheDecision	But tills Act could not come to the aid of a person injured by an insolvent whose vehicle was not insured.
RatioOfTheDecision	To meet that situation, the Road Traffic Act, 1930, was enacted.
RatioOfTheDecision	It prohibits the use of a motor vehicle on a road, unless the owner or other person using it takes a policy of insurance or gives security  against liability to third parties.
RatioOfTheDecision	Yet another lacuna was detected.
RatioOfTheDecision	The insurer and the insured could, while satisfying the requirements of the compulsory insurance, introduce stipulations in the policy, breach of which would render it void and by this device the insurer could escape liability to third parties.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, the Road Traffic Act, 1934, was passed to prevent the insurer from escaping the liability under the insurance policy by compelling him to satisfy the judgment obtained against the assured, and also by rendering ineffective certain clauses in the policy which may be aimed at avoiding the liability arising under it.
RatioOfTheDecision	10.
RatioOfTheDecision	In our country, before July 1, 1946, (on which date Chapter VIII of the Motor Vehicles Act, 1939, came into force) the legal position was the same as obtained in England before 1930.
RatioOfTheDecision	The case of <ORG> v. <ORG>, AIR 1938 Bom 217, serves as an illustration.
Fact	In that case, a motor bus overturned because of negligent driving of the driver.
Fact	A passenger in the b us sustained injuries to which he succumbed.
Fact	The legal representative of the deceased passenger sued the owner, the driver and the insurance company.
Fact	The person who was driving the bus had obtained it on hire purchase.
Fact	The claim was dismissed on the ground that the indemnity undertaken by the insurer was for the benefit of the insured but not for the benefit of any passenger, and that the passenger or his legal representative being a stranger to the contract could not sue the insurer for money payable under the policy,  11.
Fact	For the sake of brevity, we shall hereinafter use the word "claimant" for "a third party" injured in an accident arising out of a motor accident, or his legal representative, in the case of his death,  12.
Fact	The provisions of Chapter VIII of the Act, particularly Sections 94 to 96 , were modelled on the English statute to meet social needs for which the three above said Acts were made In England.
Fact	The objects were: (1) to enable a claimant to recover whatever sum he is in law entitled, despit e the inability of the owner or the driver to pay; (2) to prevent the insurer from escaping liability on the ground of breach, on the part of the insured, of any term of the contract; and (3) to entitle the claimant to recover compensation directly from the insurer.

Fact	The law thus created compulsion for insurance against third party risks.

Fact	15.

RatioOfTheDecision	To this extent alone the right of the claimant is enlarged.
RatioOfTheDecision	Secondly, the section prescribes the limit of the insurer's liability which does not exceed the sum assured payable under the policy, as read with Section 95 .
RatioOfTheDecision	This point is fully discussed in Manjula Devi Bhuta v. Manjushri Raba, 1968 Ace CJ 1 (MP).

RatioOfTheDecision	But if the sum specified in the insuran ce policy as payable thereunder, exceeds the sum as fixed under Section 95(2) , the maximum liability shall be the sum specified in the policy.
RatioOfTheDecision	Thirdly, it exhaustively enumerates the grounds of defence available to the insurer to avoid his liability to satisfy a judgment against the insured.
RatioOfTheDecision	One thing to be particularly noted is that the provisions of Section 96 relate to the liability of the insurer which arises only when the insured has incurred liability but not independent of it.
RatioOfTheDecision	20.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is thus seen from the above provisions that: (1) A claimant is entitled to recover from the insurer the amount of compensation which he is in law entitled to obtain from, the insured, subject to the statutory limits of liability of the insurer.
RatioOfTheDecision	(2) Insurance against third party risks is compulsory which secures payment to the claimant against the insolvency or liquidation of the insured.
RatioOfTheDecision	(3) The insurer cannot escape liability under the policy on the ground that because of breach of any term of the contract on the part o f the insured, the claim of third party was not covered, or, otherwise, the insurer is entitled to avoid the policy.
RatioOfTheDecision	But these provisions do not go beyond this.
RatioOfTheDecision	They leave untouched the question what would make the insurer liable to pay the insurance money to the claimant.
RatioOfTheDecision	To put it differently, they lay down when the insurer is liable and to what extent he is liable, but not what makes him liable.
RatioOfTheDecision	We shall leave this topic here for a moment and first turn to the remaining sections of this Chapter,  21.
Statute	Sections 110 to 110-F were introduced in the Act by the Motor Vehicles (Amendment) Act (No.
Statute	100) of 1956.

RatioOfTheDecision	Now, to specify the limit of liability of the insurer, the <ORG> will have to go back, to the substantive provisions contained in Section 96 .
RatioOfTheDecision	It is clear that these provisions merely substitute a summary remedy for civil suits and for adjudication of claims before the Motor Accidents Claims <ORG>, instead of <ORG>, in respect of personal injury in accident arising out of the use of the motor vehicle.

Statute	23.
Statute	From the above resume of all the sections contained in Chapter VIII of the Motor Vehicles Act, it must be said, in short, that Sections 94 to 96 provide for compulsory insurance of a motor vehicle, before it can be used on a public road; lay down the liability of the insurer to pay to the claimant directly, when there is a decree or judgment of the Court and also fix the limits of pecuniary liability of the insurer in certain cases.
Statute	Sections 110 to 110-F lay down the procedure for enforcement of the substantive rights of the claimant as enlarged and circumscribed by Section 96 .
Statute	As provided in Section 110-B , <ORG> shall in its award determine the total amount of compensation and then specify the sum which shall be paid by the insurer.
RatioOfTheDecision	And, by virtue of Section 110-E , the latter sum is recoverable from the insurer as arrears of land revenue.
RatioOfTheDecision	However, the ingredients of the liability of the insurer are nowhere provided in this special statute.
RatioOfTheDecision	Necessarily, the refore, that aspect of the matter will be governed by the general substantive law, which remains untouched by this special law.
RatioOfTheDecision	We have already shown that it is on the contract of indemnity that the insurer is liable to pay compensation only if the insured is liable to pay damages to the claimant.
RatioOfTheDecision	If the insured is not liable, then the insurer is also not liable.
RatioOfTheDecision	In other words, the liability of the insurer depends upon the liability of the insured.
RatioOfTheDecision	Now, the liability, direct or vicarious, of the owner of a motor vehicle involved in an accident resulting in injury to, or death of, a third party, to pay damages arises from the law of, torts.
RatioOfTheDecision	Under that law, negligence of the owner or driver is the sine qua non for, such liability.
RatioOfTheDecision	So was held in <CITATION_251519> , AIR 1962 Punj 540; <CITATION_540215> , 1968 Acc CJ 401 = (AIR 1969 Punj 172); <NAME> v. <NAME>, 1969 Acc CJ 204 (Australia H. C.) and <NAME> v. General Manager, <ORG>, 1969 Ace CJ 327 (Orissa).

RatioOfTheDecision	We have already stated the object and effect of compulsory insurance.
RatioOfTheDecision	25.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is true that the provisions of the Motor Vehicles Act do not lay down affirmatively that negligence must be proved before the insurer is held liable to pay the claimant compensation, but this is because the Act is not intended to substitute the law of torts in its application to the case of an accident,  26.
RatioOfTheDecision	A flood of light is received In a comparison of the case of an accident in an area for which <ORG> has not been constituted under Section 110 of the Act, with the case of an accident in an area for which there is <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the former case, the claimant has to obtain a decree for damages against the insured from <ORG> to be able to recover the amount from the insurer; such a decree can be passed only if the insured is found liable, dir ectly or vicariously, on the ground of negligence, but not otherwise.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the latter case, although the claimant makes a claim before the <ORG>, the substantive law which governs the rights and liabilities of the parties is the same for both the proceedings.
RatioOfTheDecision	It cannot be accepted that in the former case damages cannot be decreed without proof of negligence because the suit is before the civil <ORG>, while in the latter case compensation will be awarded even without proof of negligence merely because the proceeding is before the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	The same substantive rights and liabilities must govern both the cases, although the procedures may differ.
RatioOfTheDecision	27.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is an argument that negligence of the insured is irrelevant in a claim before the <ORG> against the insurer because under Section 96(2) of the Act, the defences open to the insurer are limited to those enumerated in that section.
RatioOfTheDecision	In our view that is not so.
RatioOfTheDecision	The insurer may resist the claim against him in two ways: (1) That the insurer is not liable although  the insured may be liable; and (2) that the insurer is not liable because the insured is not liable.
RatioOfTheDecision	Under the first head, the insurer is entitled to escape liability by saying that the policy is void because it was obtained by the insured on a false representation or non-disclosure of a material fact; or that the policy was cancelled before the accident; or that there has been a breach of a specified condition of the policy being one of the conditions enumerated in the section.
RatioOfTheDecision	The insurer is debarred from raising any other ground of defence to avoid his liability, if the insured is found to have incurred the liability.
RatioOfTheDecision	Under the second head, the insurer can plead that there was no negligence on the part of the insured, to show that the insured incurred no liability so that the insurer's liability under the contract of indemnity does not arise.
RatioOfTheDecision	The insurer is entitled to raise all such pleas in defence as the insured can take to show that he (the insured) has not incurred the liability.
RatioOfTheDecision	This the insurer can  do, as of right, in the name of the insured, provided that right is reserved in the policy.
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 96(2) has nothing to do with the second head of defences.
RatioOfTheDecision	All that Section 96(2) does is that it debars the insurer from taking up any defence, other than those enumerated in it, to show that the insurer is not liable although the insured has incurred liability.
Argument	This position is plain enough and is fully supported by the following observations in AIR 1959 SC 1331 (supra):--  "The insurer has the right, provided he has reserved it by the policy, to defend the action in the name of the assured and if he does so, all defences open to the assured can then be urged by him and there is no other defence that he claims to be entitled to urge.
Argument	He can thus avoid all hardship, if any, by providing for a right to defend the action in the name of the assured and this he has full liberty to do."
