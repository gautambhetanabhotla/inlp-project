Fact	JUDGMENT <NAME>, J.
Fact	1.
Fact	The controversy that I am called upon to decide in the present case under Article 227 of the Constitution essentially relates to the situs of the matrimonial home of the Petitioner, who is the daughter in law of the Respondents.
Fact	2.
Fact	The Petitioner was married to <NAME> on 14th April 2000.
Fact	They have a male child born on 27th November 2001.
Fact	Soon after their marriage, as per the prevailing practice, the Petitioner shifted into the home of <NAME> and the Respondents, that is, in the ground floor of property No.
Fact	B-135, Ashok Vihar, Phase I, Delhi.
Fact	The entire property belongs to the Respondents but all of them lived together on the ground floor.
Fact	3.
Fact	It seems that sometime in early 2002, relations between the parties deteriorated.
Fact	According to the Petitioner, the Respondents treated her cruelly, and so the Petitioner and <NAME> later shifted to the second floor of the said property, which became their matrimonial home.
Fact	However, it appears that their shifting to the second  floor of the said property did not improve relations between the Petitioner and the Respondents.
Fact	On the contrary, relations deteriorated to such an extent that the Petitioner registered an FIR against the Respondents, <NAME> and other family members under the provisions of Sections 406 / 498A / 506 and 34 of the .
Fact	On the basis of her complaint, the Respondents, their son in law and <NAME> were arrested in January 2003.
Fact	4.
Fact	According to the Petitioner, these events made it even more difficult for her to stay in the matrimonial home, and so she shifted to her parents' residence.
Fact	Later, when she tried to enter the matrimonial home, she found that the main entrance was locked and so she was unable to enter her matrimonial home.
Fact	This led the Petitioner to file Suit No.
Fact	87 of 2003 for a mandatory injunction directing the Respondents (and their son in law and her <NAME> <NAME>) to open the main entrance to enable her to reside in the matrimonial home, that is, the second floor of property bearing No.
Fact	B- 135, Ashok Vihar, Phase I, Delhi and for a permanent injunction restraining the Respondents from breaking open the locks of her matrimonial home and removing her goods there from.
Fact	5.
Fact	The suit was contested by the Respondents, who also applied for a mandatory direction to the Petitioner to hand over possession of the second floor of the Ashok Vihar property.
Fact	6.
Fact	The Respondents alleged before the Trial Judge, as also before me, that from sometime in September, 2002 the Petitioner was not residing in the second floor property.
Fact	On the contrary, during the pendency of the suit, the Petitioner trespassed upon the property and on an inspection of the second floor, it was noted on 23rd February, 2003 that only one blanket belonging to the Petitioner was found therein.
Fact	It was further contended by the Respondents that their son <NAME> had purchased some property in Ghaziabad and had shifted there.
Fact	It was consequently contended that that was now the matrimonial home of the Petitioner.
Argument	It was submitted that, in law , the user of the second floor of the property by the Petitioner was merely permissive, in as much as the entire property was owned by the Respondents and the Petitioner really had no right to reside in their property, except with their permission.
Argument	7.
Argument	On these broad facts, the learned Trial Judge decided on 4th March, 2003 both the applications for ad interim injunction filed by the parties.
Argument	He held that the Petitioner was 'admittedly' in possession of the second floor of the said property and that it would be in the fitness of things if both the parties did not interfere in each others possession and each others right to have access to the common passage.
Argument	8.
Argument	Feeling aggrieved by the decision of the learned Trial Judge, the Respondents appealed to the Senior Civil Judge who, by his order dated 17th September, 2004 held that the Petitioner was not residing in the second floor premises since her goods (other than one blanket) were not there and the premises had no electricity and water.
Argument	Furthermore, it was he ld that since the Petitioner's <NAME> was not living in the property, but elsewhere, the matrimonial home could not be where only the wife (Petitioner) was residing.
Argument	It was also held that the Petitioner had no right over property belonging to persons other than her <NAME> and, therefore, the application for an ad interim injunction filed by the Petitioner deserved dismissal.
Argument	It was, however, directed that the Petitioner may visit the property "once or twice in a month along with local Police to see that the locks in the suit premises are intact or not."
Argument	The parties were also directed to maintain status quo.
Argument	9.
Argument	Both the parties are aggrieved by the order of the learned Senior Civil Judge and have, therefore, filed respective petitions under Article 227 of the Constitution.
Fact	The issues in the petitions being the same, they were heard together on 25th November, 2004 and are being disposed of by a common decision.
Fact	Both learned counsels reiterated their submissions made before the learned Trial Judge and the lea rned Senior Civil Judge.
RatioOfTheDecision	10.
RatioOfTheDecision	I proceed on the basis that the Petitioner is presently not residing in the second floor of property bearing No.
RatioOfTheDecision	B-135, Ashok Vihar, Phase I, Delhi.
RatioOfTheDecision	This is because, to my mind, whether she is presently residing there or not is inconsequential for deciding if her matrimonial home is on the second floor of that property.
RatioOfTheDecision	Moreover, her actual possession or at least her constructive possession of the second floor of the property is not seriously doubted.
RatioOfTheDecision	I also proceed on the basis that the Petitioner's <NAME> is now residing at a place other than the second floor property.
RatioOfTheDecision	The question still remains whether, notwithstanding these assumptions, the second floor of the property is the Petitioner's matrimonial home or not.
RatioOfTheDecision	If the answer is in the affirmative, then she has a right to reside there (subject to the objections of the Respondents) otherwise not.
RatioOfTheDecision	11.
RatioOfTheDecision	I am of the view that in the context of Indian society, a pragmatic view has to be taken while attempting to understand what  is meant by the matrimonial home of a married couple, because there is no statute in this respect unlike in England where the Matrimonial Homes Act, 1983 would govern the situation.
Precedent	It appears that in England, an innocent spouse who is in occupation of the matrimonial home as a joint owner has a right not to be evicted from there except by an order of a <ORG> and if that spouse is not in occupation, he or she has a right to obtain a <ORG> order to enter and occupy such a matrimonial home.
Precedent	A spouse who has no proprietary interest in the matrimonial home but is in occupation thereof has a right to remain in occupation of the matrimonial home as against the spouse who has left that matrimonial home.
Precedent	In this regard, it may be mentioned that in <CITATION_1642416> , the Supreme <ORG> made a reference to the Matrimonial Homes Act, 1967 in England and the rights of occupation of a spouse and observed:  "But such rights are not granted in India though it may be that with change of situation and complex problems arisin g it is high time to give the wife or the spouse a right of occupation in a truly matrimonial home, in case of marriage breaking up or in case of strained relationship between the <NAME> and the wife."
Precedent	12.
Precedent	We all know that the accepted practice in India is that immediately after marriage, the bride resides with her <NAME> usually in the parental home of the <NAME> and their place of residence then becomes their matrimonial home.
RatioOfTheDecision	Of course, now there are may instances where the couple reside separately since the joint family system is breaking up but, as in the present case, residence is usually along with the parents of the <NAME>.
RatioOfTheDecision	In either case, the place of residence of the couple becomes their matrimonial home.
RatioOfTheDecision	A woman, therefore, would have the right to remain in that matrimonial home as long as she is married and if she is "obliged" to leave that matrimonial home, I think she would be entitled to obtain an injunction from an appropriate Court protecting her right and preventing her from being throw n out.
RatioOfTheDecision	13.
RatioOfTheDecision	Consequently, going by the generally accepted practice in most parts of India, if not in the entire country, it can be said that the ground floor of property bearing No.
RatioOfTheDecision	B-135, Ashok Vihar Phase-I, Delhi was the matrimonial home of the Petitioner and her <NAME> <NAME> even though the entire property belonged to the parents of <NAME>.
RatioOfTheDecision	It can also be said that later on, the Petitioner and her <NAME> shifted their matrimonial home to the second floor of the said property, with the knowledge and consent of the Respondents, the owners thereof.
RatioOfTheDecision	14.
RatioOfTheDecision	There can be no doubt that there is a legal obligation on <NAME> to provide a residence to the Petitioner and their son.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is one thing for <NAME> and the Respondents to say that since <NAME> is now staying in Ghaziabad, that would be the matrimonial home and he is prepared to have the Petitioner reside with him there.
RatioOfTheDecision	The fact of the matter is that <NAME> applied for a divorce from the Petitioner (although the divorce petition is  now said to have been dismissed in default).
RatioOfTheDecision	Consequently, it is extremely unnatural to expect the Petitioner to treat <NAME> Ghaziabad residence as her matrimonial home.
RatioOfTheDecision	In other words, <NAME> shifting from the second floor of the said property to Ghaziabad would not ipso facto shift the Petitioner's matrimonial home to Ghaziabad.
RatioOfTheDecision	15.
RatioOfTheDecision	Reference may usefully be made to <CITATION_1620806> ., which clearly holds that there is a legal obligation on the <NAME> to provide a residence for his wife and that the wife also needs access to her matrimonial home.
RatioOfTheDecision	It has further been held that the mere fact that the <NAME> shifts out of the matrimonial home to set up a home somewhere else does not, in all cases, mean that his new place of residence becomes the matrimonial home.
RatioOfTheDecision	This is precisely what has happened in the present case, namely, that <NAME> shifted out of the second floor of the said property to set up what he and his parents call a new matrimonial home in Ghaziabad.
RatioOfTheDecision	The motive for setting  up a new matrimonial home in Ghaziabad may or may not be suspect (as contended by learned counsel for the Petitioner) - I am not concerned with that.
RatioOfTheDecision	But, given the antecedent facts that I have already adverted to including the fact that <NAME> had actually filed a petition seeking to divorce the Petitioner (since dismissed in default), it cannot reasonably be held that the Ghaziabad residence of <NAME> is now the matrimonial home of the Petitioner and her <NAME>.
RatioOfTheDecision	16.
RatioOfTheDecision	In view of the factual context given above, it can safely be said that the second floor of the Ashok Vihar property continues to be the Petitioner's matrimonial home being the place where she and <NAME> last resided together as <NAME> and wife and where they voluntarily shifted from the ground floor.
RatioOfTheDecision	A mere change of residence by <NAME>, for whatever reason, would not shift the matrimonial home away from Ashok Vihar, more particularly on the facts of the present case when <NAME> had applied for a divorce from the Petitione r.  17.
Argument	It was contended by learned counsel for the Respondents that his clients, who own the Ashok Vihar property, had only permitted the Petitioner to reside on the second floor of the said property.
Argument	This permissive user, according to him, did not give her any right to continue to reside in that property and she had no proprietary interest in the property.
RatioOfTheDecision	I am not impressed with this contention.
RatioOfTheDecision	I have already held that the second floor of the said property was the matrimonial home of the Petitioner and, therefore, she has a right to stay there as against <NAME>.
RatioOfTheDecision	As regards her proprietary interest, it cannot be forgotten that the Respondents are not complete strangers, in which case the position may be completely different - the Respondents are the parents of <NAME> and the parents in law of the Petitioner and I do not think the theory of permissive user can be advantageously used by them in the Indian context particularly when their son and the Petitioner actually lived together with them on the  ground floor and thereafter shifted to the second floor of the same property with their full knowledge and consent, with a view to shift their matrimonial home from the ground floor to the second floor.
RatioOfTheDecision	18.
RatioOfTheDecision	Under the circumstances, I am of the view that the learned Trial Judge was quite right in concluding, though for different reasons, that the Petitioner is entitled to continue to reside in the second floor of property bearing No.B-135, Ashok Vihar, Phase-I, Delhi.
RulingByPresentCourt	Consequently, the impugned order dated 17th September, 2004 passed by the learned Senior Civil Judge is set aside and it is held that the Petitioner is entitled to continue to reside with her son on the second floor of B-135, Ashok Vihar, Phase-I, Delhi, which is her matrimonial home and that the Respondents or anyone claiming under them cannot deny her access to her matrimonial home or interfere in her possession thereof.
RulingByPresentCourt	19.
RulingByPresentCourt	The writ petition is, therefore, allowed.
