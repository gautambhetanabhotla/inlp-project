Argument	5.
RatioOfTheDecision	There can be no question of a writ of prohibition being issued in this case, for it has not been alleged or proved that there has been any wrongful adjudication by an unauthorised body.
RatioOfTheDecision	6.
RatioOfTheDecision	A writ of certiorari lies when the inferior <ORG> or tribunal has exceeded its jurisdiction or when such <ORG> on tribunal has proceeded illegally and no appeal lies.
RatioOfTheDecision	It can be issued, only to restrain the exercise of judicial functions as distinguished from ministerial and executive acts.
Precedent	It can be issued to <ORG>s and to officers, boards and tribunals who possess judicial or quasi-judicial power s and have to act judicially -- 'Rex v. Electricity Commissioners', (1924) 1 KB 171 (A), and in extreme cases also to purely ministerial bodies when they usurp judicial functions--'Braizie V. Fayettee County', (1884) 25 W Va 213 (B).
Precedent	It cannot extend to the Questioning of annulling of acts which are ministerial, executive or legislative.
Precedent	It is the nature of the act sought to be restrained and not the general character of the tribunal or officer proceeded against which determines the propriety of this writ.,  7.
Precedent	Two questions at once arise for decision, namely--  (1) Whether an officer conducting a removal proceeding in accordance with the provisions of Art.
Precedent	311 acts in an executive or administrative capacity, and (2) Whether the statutory rules by which the 'conditions of service of the petitioners are regulated have provided another adequate remedy by way of appeal or revision or other appropriate proceeding.
RatioOfTheDecision	8.
RatioOfTheDecision	If both these questions are answered in the affirmative, it is obvious that the Enquiry Officer  is not amenable to the writ of certiorari and it is not within the competence of this <ORG> to intervene at this stage.
RatioOfTheDecision	9.
Precedent	'Prima facie' the power to remove from office is an executive function, (In re.
Precedent	Opinion of Justices, 118 Am LR 166 at p. 169 (C)), for if a public servant holds office at the pleasure of the <ORG>, it is open to the appointing power to remove him arbitrarily without cause, or to remove him for cause, or to remove him for such cause as it may think fit or proper or upon such enquiries as it may think fit to make or in the absence of any enquiry whatever.
RatioOfTheDecision	In such a case <ORG>s are powerless to interfere.
RatioOfTheDecision	10.
RatioOfTheDecision	If, however, the Constitution declares --as has been declared in Article 311 -- that no person shall be dismissed or removed or reduced in rank unless he has been afforded a reasonable opportunity of being heard, the question arises whether the officer who conducts a removal proceeding against a public servant who cannot be removed without notice and hearing acts in a ministerial or  Judicial capacity.
RatioOfTheDecision	Two different views, which are diametrically opposed to each other, have been expressed.
RatioOfTheDecision	According to one view this function is purely executive or administrative containing no element of judicial power.
RatioOfTheDecision	11.
RatioOfTheDecision	In 'People ex rel.
Precedent	<NAME> v. <ORG>', (1383) 6 Colo 534 (D), holding that prohibition would not lie against <ORG> to prevent it from hearing charges and continuing proceedings for the removal of a city solicitor, the <ORG> said:--  "It will be observed that the tribunal to which the writ Issues must be acting in a judicial and not merely an administrative or ministerial capacity .............. <ORG> is not a judicial body; and it is doubtful if the legislature, under the constitution, could invest it with judicial authority.
Precedent	In the case under consideration it was not acting or attempting to act in a judicial capacity.
RatioOfTheDecision	The examination of charges preferred to the city solicitor, finding him guilty of malfeasance in office and removing him therefrom, by the <ORG> y Council was not the exercise of judicial power.
RatioOfTheDecision	And this is true though the offences charged may constitute cause of action cognizable by the <ORG>."
RatioOfTheDecision	12.
RatioOfTheDecision	According to the other view the function is judicial or quasi-judicial.
Precedent	In -- '<NAME> v. Detroit', (1894) 39 Am St. Rep. 555 (E), holding that prohibition would lie to prevent a common council from proceeding to investigate charges preferred against a City Counsellor, the Court said:  "Under the Constitution, the legislature may provide for the removal of municipal officers.
Precedent	It certainly has never been regarded in this State that the officer or body upon whom this power is conferred acts in a purely political, administrative or legislative capacity.
Precedent	Such officer or body acts, and must of necessity act, in a quasi-judicial capacity, and the method of procedure must be of a quasi-judicial character ..............
Precedent	Such officer or body then becomes an inferior tribunal, amenable to the writ of prohibition when acting In excess of the jurisdiction conferred.
Precedent	In such cases it is of little consequence what name is given to the power conferred.
Precedent	The name cannot relieve it of its essential character."
Precedent	13.
Precedent	While the cases cannot be reconciled, the weight of judicial opinion in America, appears to favour the proposition that where the law indicates that the power of removal shall be exercised for cause only and declares that the officers whose conduct is under investigation shall be entitled to have a written statement of the charges against him, and to be heard in his defence, then the proceeding is judicial in character because the power to hear and determine is to be exercised, as in <ORG>s, only after notice and a hearing on the merits.
Precedent	It needs no argument" observed the <ORG> 'In matter of <NAME>', 6 Abb NO 474 (P), "to prove that any proceeding Initiated to remove a person from a public office before a person or body having jurisdiction to decide upon the existence of cause for removal is judicial in its character and the tribunal to which it is presented is a C ourt for that purpose."
Precedent	The same <ORG> proceeded to draw a distinction between removal for cause and arbitrary removal and to hold that the power to remove a person from office for cause means that a reason, must exist which is personal to the individual sought to be removed, which the law and a sound, public opinion will recognise as a good cause for his no longer occupying the place.
RatioOfTheDecision	14.
RatioOfTheDecision	It will be seen from the above that a removal proceeding acquires the character of a judicial proceeding only if the power of removal can be exercised for cause and only if the officer is entitled to notice and hearing as a condition precedent to his removal.
