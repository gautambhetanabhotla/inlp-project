RatioOfTheDecision	There is a marked distinction between a lease and a licence.
Statute	Section 105 of the Transfer of Property Act defines a lease of immoveable property as a transfer of a right to enjoy such property made for a certain time in consideration for a price paid or promised.
Statute	Under s. 108 of the said Act, the lessee is entitled to be put in possession of the property.
Statute	A lease is there-' fore a transfer of an interest in land.
RatioOfTheDecision	The interest, transferred is called the leasehold interest.
RatioOfTheDecision	The lessor parts with his right to enjoy the property during the term of the lease, and it follows from it that the lessee g ets that right to the exclusion of the lessor.
Statute	Whereas s. 52 of the Indian Easements Act defines a licence thus :  "Where one person grants to another, or to a definite number of other persons, a right to do or continue to do in or upon the immoveable property of the grantor, something which would, in the absence of such right, be unlawful, and such right does not amount to an easement or an interest in the property, the right is called a licence."
RatioOfTheDecision	Under the aforesaid section, if a document gives only a right to use the property in a particular way or under certain terms while it remains in possession and control of the owner thereof, it will be a licence.
RatioOfTheDecision	The legal possession, therefore, continues to be with the owner of the property, but the licensee is permitted to make use of the premises for a particular purpose'.
RatioOfTheDecision	But for the permission, his occupation would be unlawful.
RatioOfTheDecision	It does not create in his favour any estate or interest n the property.
RatioOfTheDecision	There is, therefore, cleat distinction between the two concept s. The dividing line is clear though sometimes it becomes very thin or even blurred.
RatioOfTheDecision	At one time it was thought that the test of exclusive possession was infalliable and if a person was given exclusive possession of a premises, it would conclusively establish that he was a lessee.
Precedent	But there was a change and the recent trend of judicial opinion is reflected in <NAME> v. <NAME> (1), wherein Lord <NAME> reviewing the case law on the subject summarizes the result of his discussion thus at p. 155:  "The result of all these cases is that, although a person who is let into exclusive possession is prima facie, to be considered to be tenant, nevertheless he will not be held to be so if the circumstances negative any intention to create a tenancy."
Precedent	<ORG> again in <NAME> v. <NAME> (2) considered the legal position and laid down that the intention of the parties was the real test for ascertaining the character of a document.
Precedent	At p. 1201, <NAME>.. <NAME> stated :  "................ the solution that wo uld seem to have been found is, as one would expect, that it must depend on the intention of the parties."
Precedent	<ORG> said much to the same effect at p. 1202:  "The question in all these cases is one of intention: Did the circumstances and the conduct of the parties show that all that was intended was that the occupier should have a personal privilege with no interest in the land ?"

RatioOfTheDecision	What is the construction of the words " a room in a hotel " ?
RatioOfTheDecision	The object of the Act as disclosed in the preamble is " to provide for the control of rents and evictions, and for the lease to <ORG> of premises upon their becoming vacant, in certain areas in the 49 Provinces of Delhi and Ajmer-Merwara".
RatioOfTheDecision	The Act was, therefore, passed to control exorbitant rents of buildings prevailing in t he said <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	But s. 2 exempts a room in a hotel from the operation of the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	The reason for the exemption may be to encourage running of hotels in the cities, or it may be for other reasons.
RatioOfTheDecision	Whatever may be the object of the Act, the scope of the exemption cannot be enlarged so as to limit the operation of the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	The exemption from the Act is only in respect of a room in a hotel.
RatioOfTheDecision	The collocation of the words brings out the characteristics of the exempted room.
RatioOfTheDecision	The room is part of a hotel.
RatioOfTheDecision	It partakes its character and does not cease to be one after it is let out.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is, therefore, necessary to ascertain the meaning of the word "hotel".
RatioOfTheDecision	The word " hotel " is not defined in the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	A hotel in common parlance means a place where a proprietor makes it his business to furnish food or lodging, or both to travellers or other persons.
RatioOfTheDecision	A building cannot be run as a hotel unless services necessary for the comfortable stay of lodgers and boarders are maintained.
RatioOfTheDecision	Services so maintained.
RatioOfTheDecision	vary with the standard of  the hotel and the class of persons to which it caters; but the amenities must have relation to the hotel business.
RatioOfTheDecision	Provisions for heating or lighting, supply of hot water, sanitary arrangements, sleeping facilities, and such others are some of the amenities a hotel offers to its constituents.
RatioOfTheDecision	But every amenity however remote and unconnected with the business of a hotel cannot be described as service in a hotel.
RatioOfTheDecision	The idea of a hotel can be better clarified by illustration than by definition and by giving examples of what is a room in a hotel and also what is not a room in a hotel.
RatioOfTheDecision	(1) A owns a building in a part whereof he runs a hotel but leases out a room to B in the part of the building not used as hotel; (2) A runs a hotel in the entire building but lets out a room to B for a purpose unconnected with the hotel business; (3) A runs a hotel in the entire building and lets out a room to B for carrying on his business different from that of a hotel, though incidentally the inmates of the hotel take advantage of  it because of its proximity; (4) A lets out a room in such a building to another with an express condition that he should cater only to the needs of the inmates of the hotel; and (5) A lets out a room in a hotel to a lodger, who can command all the services and amenities of a hotel.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the first illustration, the room has never been a part of a hotel though it is part of a building where a hotel is run.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the second, though a room was once part of a hotel, it ceased to be one, for it has been let out for a non-hotel purpose.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the fifth, it is let out as part of a hotel, and, therefore, it is definitely a room in a hotel.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the fourth, the room may still continue as part of the hotel as it is let out to provide an amenity or service connected with the hotel.
RatioOfTheDecision	But to extend the scope of the words to the third illustration is to obliterate the distinction between a room in a hotel and a room in any other building.
RatioOfTheDecision	If a room in a building, which is not a hotel but situated near a hotel, is let out to a tenant  to carry on his business of a hair-dresser, it is not exempted from the operation of the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	But if the argument of the appellants be accepted, if a similar room in a building, wherein a hotel is situated is let out for a similar purpose, it would be exempted.
RatioOfTheDecision	In either case, the tenant is put in exclusive possession of the room and he is entitled to carry on his business without any reference to the activities of the hotel.
RatioOfTheDecision	Can it be said that there is any reasonable nexus between the business of the tenant and that of the hotel.
RatioOfTheDecision	The only thing that can be said is that a lodger in a hotel building can step into the saloon to have a shave or haircut.
RatioOfTheDecision	So too, he can do so in the case of a saloon in the neighbouring house.
RatioOfTheDecision	The tenant is not bound by the contract to give any preferential treatment to the lodger.
RatioOfTheDecision	He may take his turn along with others, and when he is served, he is served not in his capacity as a lodger but as one of the general customers.
RatioOfTheDecision	What is more, under the document the tenant is not even  bound to carry on the business of a hair-dresser.
RatioOfTheDecision	His only liability is to pay the stipulated amount to the landlord.
RatioOfTheDecision	The room, therefore, for the purpose of the Act, ceases to be a part of the hotel and becomes a place of business of the respondent.
RatioOfTheDecision	As the rooms in question were not let out as part of a hotel or for hotel purposes, I must hold that they are' not rooms in a hotel within the meaning of s. 2 of the Act.
