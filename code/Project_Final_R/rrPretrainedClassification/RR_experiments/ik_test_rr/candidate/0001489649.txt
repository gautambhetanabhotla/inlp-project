Argument	7.
RatioOfTheDecision	Though allegations of <NAME> fide are made in the petition, no sufficient material is placed before us to substantiate that plea.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is also to be noted that we are not concerned here with an executive act of the Government but with legislation duly passed by the legislature.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is well settled that the validity of a legislation cannot be attacked on the grounds of <NAME> fides and it is not the province of the <ORG> to go into the motive of the legislature in enacting legislation, nor is it for the <ORG> to scan the wisdom or the reasonableness of the policy behind a statute.
RatioOfTheDecision	<NAME>, learned counsel for the petitioner, in the above circumstances very rightly in our opinion did not seriously press the contention that the legislation is vitiated by <NAME> fides.
RatioOfTheDecision	8.
RatioOfTheDecision	Article .
Statute	31-A(1)(b) of the Constitution was introduced by the Constitution Fourth Amendment in 1955 and reads:  "Notwithstanding anything contained in Article.
Statute	13 no law providing for  (a) ......................................................................................................................................................  (b) taking over of the management of any property by the <ORG> for a limited period either in public interest or in order to secure proper management of the property .....................................................................  (c) .......................................................................................................................................................  (d) ......................................................................................................................................................  (e) ........................................................................................................................................ ...............  shall be deemed to be void on the ground that it is inconsistent with or takes away or abridges any of the rights conferred by Article.
Fact	14, Article.
Fact	19 or Article.
Fact	31 of the Constitution."
RatioOfTheDecision	In order that Article.
RatioOfTheDecision	31-A (1)(b) may apply (a) the legislation must be one whereby the management of any property is taken over by the state; (b) it should be for a limited period; (c) it should be in the public interest or in order to secure proper management of the property.
RatioOfTheDecision	9.
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 4(1) of the Act provides at the management of the college shall in the Government and shall continue to so vest for a period not exceeding five years, or until the medical college is acquired, whichever is earlier.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is clear from this provision that the management is taken over for a limited period, namely, not exceeding five years or until the medical college is acquired whichever is earlier.
RatioOfTheDecision	Hence condition (B) is satisfied.
RatioOfTheDecision	Reference was made to the decision of <ORG> in <CITATION_1747969> , .
Argument	Under Sect ion 44 of the Bombay Tenancy and Agricultural Lands Act  <ORG> was entitled to assume management in certain circumstances of any land-holder's estate.
Argument	Section 61 provided that <ORG> may direct that the said management shall be terminated when it is of opinion that it is not necessary to continue the management of the estate.
Argument	Rule.
Argument	35 of the Rules made under the Act stated that the Manager of an estate of which management has been assumed shall, before, the 31st day of March following the year in which the management has been assumed, send a report and state whether it is necessary to continue the management.
Argument	After considering the report <ORG> shall decide whether the management should be terminated or continued and if so for what period, such period not being in excess of five years at a time.
Argument	If the management was to be continued beyond the expiry of ten years, the Collector has to hold a formal inquiry and send a report to <ORG> which had to be tak en into consideration by <ORG> before it decided to continue the management any further.
Argument	<ORG> held that protection of Article.
Statute	31-A (1)(b) is available only where there is a definite limit in the law for the period of management.
RatioOfTheDecision	Neither Section 61 alone nor read with the rule indicate any such limit.
RatioOfTheDecision	They did not accept the argument that so long as there was possibility of return of land to the original owner it must be construed that the management is of a limited period.
RatioOfTheDecision	If the management was likely to continue for an indefinite period it was not in any sense limited.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the present case however, a limited period is fixed, namely, either five years or until acquisition of the college whichever is earlier.
RatioOfTheDecision	If it was stated in the Act that the management was until acquisition it could have been argued on the strength of the decision of <ORG> that the period is indefinite and it cannot be stated definitely when the college would be acquired.
RatioOfTheDecision	But in this case the legisla ture has taken care to provide that the period is either five years of until the acquisition of the college whichever is earlier.
RatioOfTheDecision	Thus, even if there is no acquisition the management cannot extend over a period of five years.
RatioOfTheDecision	We are therefore of the view that condition in Article.
RatioOfTheDecision	31-A (1)(b) that the management should be for a limited period is fulfilled in this case.
RatioOfTheDecision	10.
Precedent	Reference was also made to <ORG> v. <ORG>, ILR (1974) 1 Delhi 311.
Precedent	In that case Section 4(2) of the Sick Textile Under taking (Taking Over of Management) Act (1972) provided that, in certain circumstances, <ORG> may make a declaration that the management of a textile undertaking ought to be taken over by it.
Precedent	The long title of the Act however recited that it was an Act to provide for taking over in the public interest of the management of the stick textile undertaking pending nationalisation of such undertakings.
RulingByLowerCourt	<ORG> held that Article.
RulingByLowerCourt	31-A (1)(b) was not applicable.
RulingByLowerCourt	It observed  that the specification for a limited period mentioned in the Article.
RulingByLowerCourt	necessarily assumes that after the expiry of the limited period the management will be returned to the old management but the impugned Act does not at all contemplate the return to private management and specifically provided for taking over of the management of sick textile undertakings pending nationalisation of such undertakings.
RulingByLowerCourt	Thus the take-over of management cannot be said to be for a limited period within Article.
RulingByLowerCourt	31-A (1)(b).
RatioOfTheDecision	It is to be noticed that in that case Section 4(2) did not specify any period during which the <ORG> would be in management.
RatioOfTheDecision	It was only in the long title that it was mentioned that the taking over of management was pending nationalisation.
RatioOfTheDecision	Hence in that case there was no limited period.
RatioOfTheDecision	As observed by <ORG> in <CITATION_1747969> (supra) the period is indefinite as it could not be said with any degree of certainty when the undertaking would be nationalised.
RatioOfTheDecision	For the above reason we agree with  the conclusion of <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	We however do not agree with the observation that merely because he Act does not contemplate the return to private management and specifically provides for taking over of the management pending nationalisation it cannot be said to be for a limited period.
RatioOfTheDecision	Article 31-A (1)(b) does not require that the management should be handed over to the old management after the expiry of the period.
RatioOfTheDecision	If the period is limited, Art.
Argument	31-A (1)(b) would in our view be applicable even though at the end of the period the undertaking would be acquired and is not returned to the old management.
Argument	11.
Argument	It was also contended that the management was not taken in public interest or to secure proper management of the property.

RatioOfTheDecision	31-A (1)(b) and in such taking over of the management is coupled with taking of possession it is outside the purview of the said Article.
RatioOfTheDecision	Mr. <NAME> drew our attention to the various provisions of the Act.
Statute	Under S. 4(2) of the Act <ORG> shall be deemed to include all assets, rights and lease holds, powers, authorities and privileges and all propert y, movable and immovable, including lands, buildings, stores, instruments and vehicles, cash balances, reserve fund, investments and book debts and all other rights and interests arising out of such property as were immediately before the appointed day in the ownership, possession, power or control of the private management and all books of account, registers and all other documents of whatever nature relating thereto.
Statute	Under S. 4 (3) any contract, whether express or implied or other arrangements in so far as it relates to the management of the medical college and in force immediately before the appointed day shall be deemed to have terminated on the appointed day.
Statute	Under S. 4 (6) every person in whose possession or custody or under whose control the medical college or any part thereof or any property attached thereto may be immediately before the appointed day, shall deliver possession of the medical college or such part thereof or any properties attached thereto as the case may be to the special officer.
Argument	Sect ion 4(7) provides that liability incurred by the private management in relation to the medical college before the appointed day shall be enforceable against the private management and not against the <ORG> or the Special Officer.
Argument	Private management is defined in S. 2 (f) as meaning both the societies namely petitioners 1 and 2.
Argument	The learned counsel for the petitioners submit that these and other provisions make it clear that under the guise of taking over management, the <ORG> is practically acquiring the college or at any rate taking possession of the college.
RatioOfTheDecision	We are not inclined to agree with this submission.
RatioOfTheDecision	The preamble to that Act says that this is an Act to provide for the taking over of the management pending acquisition of the college.
RatioOfTheDecision	Under S. 4(1) it is only the management of the college it vests in the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is clear that these provisions that the legislation is one whereby the management of the college is taken over.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is no doubt true the college and its properties including ca sh balance etc., are also taken possession of by the Special Officer.
RatioOfTheDecision	But it is clear that such possession is taken only for the purpose of management.
RatioOfTheDecision	Without taking possession of the property, movable and immovable or the cash balance etc., it will be impossible to manage the college.
RatioOfTheDecision	Hence the mere fact that the act also provides for taking possession of the property of the college does not render the legislation any the less a legislation whereby the management of the college is taken over.
RatioOfTheDecision	The fact that possession is only taken for the purpose of management is made clear by S. 6 which provides that during the period when the management of the medical college remains vested in the Government it shall pay to the person interested in respect of any property such sum as is equal to the rent which would have been payable for its use and occupation if it is an immovable property, or for its use if it is a movable property had it been taken on lease for that period.
RatioOfTheDecision	Under the proviso it is stated that if the su m is not settled by any agreement, the matter shall be referred to the arbitrator for making an award.
RatioOfTheDecision	An appeal lies to <ORG> against such award under S. 7 .
RatioOfTheDecision	Again the preamble makes it clear that the taking over of management is pending the legislation.
RatioOfTheDecision	In other words acquisition is to follow at a future date and is not made under the Act.

RulingByLowerCourt	31 (2) of the C onstitution.

RatioOfTheDecision	Whereas in the case before <ORG> the proprietary and ownership rights apart form the rights pertaining to management and control were taken over by the Sections which were declared unconstitutional, in the present case it is only the management that is vested in the <ORG> under S. 4(1) of the Act.

Precedent	In <ORG> v. <ORG>, Hazipur, it was held that the appointment of a Managing Committee of a school does not amount to acquisition of the school and therefore the action of  the Government under the Bihar High School (Control and Regulation of Administration ) Act, 1960, does not involve infringement of Art.
Precedent	31 (2) of the Constitution.
Precedent	In this case also the effect of the Act is that the Special Officer steps into the shoes of the Managing Committee until the college is acquired.
Precedent	<CITATION_686466> , <ORG> had to consider the validity of the Kerala University Act .
Precedent	One of the grounds on which it was attacked was that it violated Art.
Precedent	31-a (1) (b) of the Constitution.

Precedent	31-A (1)(b) is not available as the assent of the President was required in order that the article is applicable and in that case the assent was not obtained.

RatioOfTheDecision	31 (2) and (2-A).

RatioOfTheDecision	The transfer of right to possession in that case was not merely an incident of right to management as in the present case.
