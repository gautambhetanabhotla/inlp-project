RatioOfTheDecision	4.
RatioOfTheDecision	We think it is impossible to say that there is absolutely no evidence of the purchase, although in our opinion that evidence is extremely slight, and had we to decide the point upon the evidence, we might have come to a different conclusion, but there being some sort of evidence, the lower <ORG> were justified in finding as a fact that the defendants had purchased the jote, and we cannot interfere with that finding of fact.
RatioOfTheDecision	5.
Precedent	Then as regards the other point raised by the appella nt, it appears to us that, whether or not the jote was an occupancy holding, Section 22, Sub-section (2) of the Bengal Tenancy Act does not operate to prevent the landlord from recovering the rent of the holding.
Precedent	Sub-section (2) runs as follows: "If the occupancy right in land is transferred to a person jointly interested in the land as proprietor or permanent tenure-holder, it shall cease to exist; but nothing in this sub-section shall prejudicially affect the rights of any third person."
Precedent	That is to say, the occupancy right will cease to exist, but it does not follow that the tenancy will be altogether extinguished.
Precedent	The third person mentioned in the clause must be held to include every person interested other than the transferor and transferee.
Precedent	So that the acquisition of an occupancy right by a proprietor would not affect the right of a co-sharer landlord to receive his share of the rent of the tenancy.
Argument	6.
Argument	That being so, the decree of <ORG> must be reversed, and that of the first Court r estored with costs in both <ORG>s.
Argument	The finding of <ORG> being that the defendants 1 to 3 purchased the jote the decree will be against them, and the suit will be dismissed as against defendant No.
