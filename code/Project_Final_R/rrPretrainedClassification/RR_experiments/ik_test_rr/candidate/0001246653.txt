Fact	2.
Fact	The basic question of law which arises in these matters is whether the report of the Inquiry Officer/authority who/which is appointed by the disciplinary authority to hold an inquiry into the charges against the delinquent employee is required to be furnished to the employee to enable him to make proper representation to the disciplinary authority before such authority arrives at its own finding with regard to the guilt or otherwise of the employe e and the punishment, if any, to be awarded to him.
Fact	This question in turn gives rise to the following incidental questions:  (i) Whether the report should be furnished to the employee even when the statutory rules laying down the procedure for holding the disciplinary inquiry are silent on the subject or are against it?
Fact	(ii) Whether the report of the Inquiry Officer is required to be furnished to the delinquent employee even when the punishment imposed is other than the major punishment of dismissal, removal or reduction in rank?
Fact	(iii) Whether the obligation to furnish the report is only when the employee asks for the same or whether it exists even otherwise?
Fact	(iv) Whether the law laid down in <NAME> case (AIR 1991 SC  471) (supra) will apply to all establishments- Government and non- Government, public and private sector undertakings?
Fact	(v) What is the effect of the non-furnishing of the report on the order of punishment and what relief should be granted to the employee in such cases?
Fact	(vi) Fro m what date the law requiring furnishing of the report should come into operation?
Fact	(vii) Since the decision in <NAME> case (AIR 1991 SC 471) (supra) has made the law laid down there prospective in operation, i.e., applicable to the orders of punishment passed after 20th November, 1990 on which day the said decision was delivered, this question in turn also raises another question, viz., what was the law prevailing prior to 20th November, 1990?
RatioOfTheDecision	3.
RatioOfTheDecision	In order to appreciate fully the significance of the basic question, it is necessary to refer briefly to the genesis of the law on the subject of furnishing the report of the Inquiry Officer/authority to the delinquent employee.
RatioOfTheDecision	In this country, the law on the subject has developed along two paths.
RatioOfTheDecision	viz., the statute and the principles of natural justice.
RatioOfTheDecision	We may first refer to the statutory development of the law.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is not necessary to refer to the law prior to the Public Servants (Inquiries) Act , 1850 which for the first time made uniform the law regulatin g inquiries into the behaviour of public servants who were not removable from their appointments without the sanction of the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	It provided for a formal and public inquiry into the imputations of misbehviour against the public servant.
RatioOfTheDecision	Either the <ORG>, if it thought fit conducted the prosecution or left it to the accuser to conduct it after requiring him to furnish reasonable security.
RatioOfTheDecision	The Act also provided that the inquiry may be committed either to the <ORG>, <ORG> or any other authority to which the accused public servant was subordinate, or to any other person or persons to be specially appointed as Commissioners for the purpose.
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 25 of the Act, however, saved the authority of the <ORG> for suspending or removing any such public servant for any cause without an inquiry under the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	While the said Act continued to be on the statute book, the <ORG> of India Act, 1919 was enacted and sub-sec.
RatioOfTheDecision	(2) of S. 96B of that Act authorised the Secretary of <ORG> in Council to make rule s for regulating the classification of the civil services, the methods of their recruitments, their conditions of service, pay and allowances and discipline and conduct.
RatioOfTheDecision	In pursuance of these powers, the Civil Services Classification Rules, 1920 were framed, and Rule XIV of the said Rules provided that without prejudice to the provisions of the Public Servants (Inquiries) Act , 1850, in all cases in which the dismissal, removal or reduction in rank of any officer is ordered, the order shall, except when it is based on facts or conclusions, established at a judicial trial, or when the officer concerned has absconded with the accusations hanging over him, be preceded by a properly recorded departmental inquiry.
RatioOfTheDecision	At such an inquiry, a definite charge in writing had to be framed in respect of each offence and explained to the accused.
RatioOfTheDecision	The evidence in support of it and any evidence which the accused may adduce in his defence had to be recorded in his presence and his defence had to be taken down in writing.
RatioOfTheDecision	Each of  the charges framed had to be discussed and the finding had to be recorded on each charge.
RatioOfTheDecision	However, there was no provision made in the <ORG> for hearing the delinquent officer against the action proposed to be taken on the basis of the finding arrived at in the inquiry.
RatioOfTheDecision	All that R. XVI of the <ORG> provided was that any officer against whom an order was passed and who thought himself wronged thereby would be entitled to prefer at least one appeal against such order.
RatioOfTheDecision	These rules were followed by the Civil Services (Classification, Control and Appeal) <ORG>, 1930 also framed under S. 96B of the Government of India Act, 1919.
RatioOfTheDecision	Rule 55 thereof contained the same provisions as those contained in R. XIV of 1920 <ORG> and made no difference to the earlier position of law on the subject.
RatioOfTheDecision	It cannot, therefore, be gainsaid that the seeds of the law on the subject were laid by S. 240(3) of the Government of India Act, 1935 (the 'GOI Act,).
RatioOfTheDecision	It stated that the civil servant shall not be dismissed or reduced in rank until h e had been given "reasonable opportunity to show cause against action proposed to be taken in regard to him".
RatioOfTheDecision	The expression "against action proposed to be taken" was uniformly interpreted by the courts to mean the stage at which the disciplinary authority had arrived at its tentative conclusion with regard to the guilt of and the punishment to be awarded to the employee.
RatioOfTheDecision	The expression "reasonable opportunity to show cause" was accordingly interpreted to mean an opportunity at that stage to represent to the authority against the tentative findings both with regard to the guilt and the proposed punishment.
RatioOfTheDecision	It was therefore, held that in order that the employee had an effective opportunity to show cause against the finding of guilt and the punishment proposed, he should, at that stage be furnished with a copy of the findings of the inquiring authority.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is in this context that the furnishing of the Inquiry Officer's report at that stage was held to be obligatory.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is, however, necessary to note that though  the provisions of S. 240(3) of the Government of India Act stated that they would apply only when the employee was sought to be dismissed or reduced in rank which were the major punishments, the same were interpreted to mean that they would also apply when the employee was sought to be removed.
RatioOfTheDecision	These provisions of S. 240(3) of the GOI Act were incorporated bodily in Art.
RatioOfTheDecision	311(2) of the Constitution with a specific addition of the case of "removal" of the employee to the cases of dismissal and reduction in rank.
RatioOfTheDecision	This addition did not make any difference to the prevailing law, since, as stated earlier, the <ORG> had already interpreted the provision to include the case of the removal of the employee as well.
RatioOfTheDecision	Probably the specific addition was on account of the interpretation placed by the courts.
RatioOfTheDecision	Article 311(2 ), however, underwent change with the Constitution (15th Amendment) Act of 1963 which came into force from 6th October, 1963.
RatioOfTheDecision	It explained and expanded the scope of "reasonable opportunity".
RatioOfTheDecision	For the ori ginal expression "until he has been given reasonble opportunity of showing cause against the action proposed to be taken in regard to him", the provision "except after an inquiry in which he has been informed of the charges against him and given a reasonable opportunity of being heard in respect of those charges and where it is proposed after such inquiry, to impose on him any such penalty, until he has been given reasonable opportunity of making representation on the penalty proposed, but only on the basis of the evidence adduced during such inquiry" was substituted.
RatioOfTheDecision	Consequent upon this Amendment, necessary changes were made in the proviso to clause (2) of Art.
RatioOfTheDecision	311 which changes need not detain us here.
RatioOfTheDecision	It would thus be apparent that the 15th Amendment for the first time in terms provided for holding of an inquiry into the specific charges of which information was given to the delinquent employee in advance and in which he was given reasonable opportunity to defend himself against those charges.
RatioOfTheDecision	The Amendme nt also provided for a second opportunity to the employee to show cause against the penalty if it was proposed as a result of the inquiry.
RatioOfTheDecision	The courts held that while exercising his second opportunity of showing cause against the penalty, the employee was also entitled to represent against the findings on charges, as well.
RatioOfTheDecision	What is necessary to note for our present purpose is that in spite of this change, the stage at which the employee was held to be entitled to a copy of the report, was the stage at which the penalty was proposed, as was the case prior to the said Amendment.
RatioOfTheDecision	The provisions of clause (2) of Art.
Statute	311 were further amended by the Constitution (42nd Amendment) Act of 1976.
Statute	It came into force from 1st January, 1977.
Statute	It expressly stated that "it shall not be necessary to give such person any opportunity of making representation on the penalty proposed".
RatioOfTheDecision	The words "such person" of course meant the person who was to be dismissed or removed or reduced in rank.
RatioOfTheDecision	In other words, the 42nd Amendment of the  Constitution while retaining the expanded scope of the reasonable opportunity at the first stage, viz., during the inquiry as introduced by the 15th Amendment of the Constitution, did away with the opportunity of making representation against the penalty proposed after the inquiry.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is this Amendment to Art.
RatioOfTheDecision	311(2) which has given rise to the controversy as to whether when the Inquiry Officer is other than the disciplinary authority, the employee is entitled to a copy of the findings recorded by him, before the disciplinary authority applies its mind to the findings and the evidence recorded, or whether the employee is entitled to the copy of the findings of the enquiry Officer only at the second stage, viz., when the disciplinary authority had arrived at its conclusions and proposed the penalty.
RatioOfTheDecision	Upon answer to this question depends the answer to the other question flowing from it, viz., whether the employee was entitled to make representation against such finding before the penalty was proposed even when  Art.
RatioOfTheDecision	311(2) stood as it was prior to the 15th Amendment of the Constitution.
RatioOfTheDecision	4.
RatioOfTheDecision	It will be instructive to refer briefly to certain authorities on this aspect of the matter.
RatioOfTheDecision	We may first refer to the decision of this Court in <CITATION_546415> , 1958 SCR 1080 : (AIR 1958 SC 300) where two questions squarely fell for consideration, viz., what is meant by the expression "reasonable opportunity of showing cause against the action proposed" and at what stage the notice against the proposed punishment was to be served on the delinquent employee.
RatioOfTheDecision	After referring to the decisions of <ORG> in <CITATION_70419> for India, (1937) 64 Ind App 55: (AIR 1937 PC 31) and of <ORG> in Secretary of <ORG> for <CITATION_134248> , 1945 FCR 103 : (AIR 1945 FC  47), the Court held that the reasonable opportunity envisaged by the provisions of Art.
RatioOfTheDecision	311(2) as originally enacted was at the following stages:  "(a) An opportunity to deny his guilt and establish his innocence, which he can only do if he is told  what the charges levelled against him are and the allegations on which such charges are based;  (b) an opportunity to defend himself by cross-examining the witnesses produced against him and by examining himself or any other witnesses in support of his defence; and finally  (c) an opportunity to make his representation as to why the proposed punishment should not be inflicted on him, which he can only do if the competent authority, after the inquiry is over and after applying his mind to the gravity or otherwise of the charges proved against the government servant tentatively proposes to inflict one of the three punishments and communicates the same to the government servant .........."  The Court further held that the substance of the protection provided by rules like R. 55 of the Civil Services (Classification, Control and Appeal) Rules promulgated on May 27, 1930 under S.96-B of the Government of India Act, 1915 (sic), was bodily lifted out of the said Rules and together with an additional opportunity emb odied in 5.
RatioOfTheDecision	240(3) of the GOI Act was incorporated in Art.
RatioOfTheDecision	311(2) S0 as to convert the protection into a constitutional safeguard.
RatioOfTheDecision	The Court also held that the opportunity to show cause against the penalty proposed should be given after a stage has been reached where the charges had been established and the competent authority had applied its mind to the gravity or otherwise of the proved charges tentatively and proposed a particular punishment.
RatioOfTheDecision	It was necessary to state so, since in that case no notice was served upon the appellant there when the competent authority accepted the report of the Inquiry Officer and confirmed the opinion that the punishment of dismissal should be inflicted on him, and no cause, therefore, could be shown by him.
RatioOfTheDecision	On the other hand, by the first notice itself which communicated the charges, the appellant was called upon to show cause as to why he should not be dismissed from service, although the notice further called upon the appellant to state in reply whether he wished to be hea rd in person and whether he would produce his defence.
RatioOfTheDecision	What are the duties of the Inquiry Officer appointed by the disciplinary authority to conduct the inquiry is the next question and this <ORG> in <CITATION_1876892> (1962) Supp 1 SCR 968 at p.977: (AIR 1962 SC 1130 at p. 1134) has in terms held that the question of imposing punishment can only arise after inquiry is made and the report of the Inquiry Officer is received.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is for the punishing authority to propose the punishment and not for the inquiring authority to do so.
RatioOfTheDecision	The latter has, when so required, to appraise the evidence, to record its conclusion and if it thinks proper to suggest the appropriate punishment.
RatioOfTheDecision	But neither the conclusion on the evidence nor the punishment which the inquiring authority may regard as appropriate is binding upon the punishing authority.
Argument	In that case, the charge served upon the delinquent officer by the Inquiry Officer itself incorporated the proposed punishment.
Argument	Hence it was also observed that in the communication a ddressed by the Inquiry Officer the punishment proposed to be imposed upon the appellant if he was found guilty of the charges could not properly be set out.
Argument	Two things, therefore, emerge from this decision, viz., that it is not the function of the inquiry Officer to propose any punishment even after he records findings of guilt against the delinquent employee.
Argument	Much less can the Inquiry Officer do so at the stage of serving the charges on the employee.
Argument	Secondly, it is for the disciplinary authority to propose the punishment after receipt of the report of the Inquiry Officer which suggests that before the authority proposes the punishment, it must have applied its mind to the evidence and the findings recorded by the Inquiry Officer.
Argument	Still further question that was required to be answered was whether when the disciplinary authority issued notice to the employee to show cause against the punishment proposed, the employee had the right also to represent that he was not guilty of the charge itself and the findin gs recorded against him were wrong.
Argument	This question was squarely answered by this <ORG> in <CITATION_1137632> (1964) 4 SCR 718:(AIR 1964 SC  364).
Argument	The <ORG> pointed out there that it was well settled that the public servant entitled to the protection of Art.
Argument	311 must get two opportunities to defend himself.
Argument	He must have a clear notice of the charge which he is called upon to meet before the departmental inquiry commences, and after he gets a notice and is given the opportunity to offer his explanation, the inquiry must be conducted according to the rules and consistently with the requirements of natural justice.
RatioOfTheDecision	At the end of the inquiry, the Inquiry Officer appreciates the evidence, records his conclusions and submits his report to the <ORG> concerned.
RatioOfTheDecision	That is the first stage of the inquiry.
RatioOfTheDecision	After the report is received by the <ORG>, the <ORG> is entitled to consider the report and the evidence laid against the delinquent public servant.
RatioOfTheDecision	The <ORG> may agree with the report or may differ, ei ther wholly or partially, from the conclusions recorded in the report.
RatioOfTheDecision	If the report makes a finding in favour of the public servant and the Government agrees with the said finding, nothing more remains to be done, and the public servant who may have been suspended is entitled to be reinstated with consequential reliefs.
RatioOfTheDecision	If the report makes findings in favour of the public servant and the Government disagrees with the said findings and holds that the charges framed against the public servant are prima facie proved, the Government should decide provisionally what punishment should be imposed on the public servant and proceed to issue a second notice against him in that behalf.
RatioOfTheDecision	If the Inquiry Officer makes findings, some of which are in favour of the public servant and some against him, the Government is entitled to consider the whole matter and if it holds that some or all the charges framed against the public servant are, in its opinion, prima facie established against him, then also the Government has to dec ide provisionally what punishment should be imposed on the public servant and give him notice accordingly.
RatioOfTheDecision	The Court then proceeded to observe that "it would thus be seen that the object of the second notice is to enable the public servant to satisfy, the <ORG> on both the counts, one that he is innocent of the charges framed against him and the other that even if the charges are held proved against him, the punishment proposed to be inflicted upon is unduly severe.
RatioOfTheDecision	This position under Art.
RatioOfTheDecision	311 of the Constitution is substantially similar to the position which governed the public servants under S.240 of the <ORG> of India Act, 1935".
RatioOfTheDecision	The Court also observed that the decisions in The Secretary of <ORG> for <CITATION_134248> , 1945 FCR 103 : (AIR 1945 FC 47), High Commr.
RatioOfTheDecision	for India and High Commissioner for <CITATION_1135972> , 75 Ind App 225: (AIR 1948 PC 121) and <CITATION_546415> , 1958 SCR 1080 : (AIR 1958 SC  300) would show that it had never been suggested that the findings recorded by the Inquir y Officer concluded the matter and the <ORG> which appoints the Inquiry Officer and directs the inquiry is bound by the said finding and must act on the basis that the said findings are final and cannot be reopened.
RatioOfTheDecision	lt is obvious that the Inquiry Officer holds the inquiry against the employee as a delegate of the disciplinary authority.
RatioOfTheDecision	The object of the inquiry is plain.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is to enable the <ORG> to hold an investigation into the charges framed against the employee so that the <ORG> can in due course consider the evidence adduced and decide whether the said charges are proved or not.
RatioOfTheDecision	The interposition of the inquiry which is held by a duly appointed Inquiry Officer does not alter the true legal position that the charges are framed by the <ORG> and it is the <ORG> which is empowered to impose punishment on the deliquent public servant.
Precedent	Repelling the contention that the <ORG> is bound to accept the findings of the Inquiry Officer, the Court pointed out that if that argument was va lid, the second notice would serve very little purpose.
Precedent	For at the second stage, the opportunity which is intended to be given to the public servant is to show cause not only against the proposed punishment but also against the finding recorded against him and that opportunity would be defeated because the Government cannot alter the said finding even if the employee shows that the findings are incorrect.
Precedent	The Court then went on to add that unless the statutory rule or the specific order under which the officer is appointed to hold an inquiry so required, the Inquiry Officer need not make any recommendations as to the punishment to be imposed.
Precedent	If, however, the Inquiry Officer makes any recommendations in that behalf, the said recommendations like his findings on the merits are intended merely to supply appropriate material for the consideration of the Government.
Precedent	Neither the findings nor the recommendations are binding on the Govemment.
Precedent	In <NAME> v. Inspector General of Police, Punjab, 1968 Serv LR 131 ( SC) admittedly the findings of the Inquiry Officer were not communicated to the delinquent employee and he was only orally told that it was proposed to dismiss him.
Precedent	The Court in this context held that every public servant is entitled to have the whole of the matter brought to his notice before he was asked to show cause why particular punishment should not be meted out to him.
Precedent	The Court has explained what it meant by "the whole of the matter" by stating that it is the findings on the charges against him which should be made known to him.
Precedent	<CITATION_1554866> (1970) 1 SCR 251 : (AIR 1969 SC 1294) this Court held that the requirement of a reasonable opportunity would not be satisfied unless the entire report of the Inquiry Officer including his views in the matter of punishment were disclosed to the delinquent public servant.
Argument	The Inquiry Officer is under no obligation or duty to make any recommendations in the matter of punishment and his function merely is to conduct the inquiry in accordance with law, and to s ubmit the records along with his findings.
Argument	But if he has also made recommendations in the matter of punishment "that is likely to affect the mind of the punishing authority with regard to penalty or punishment to be imposed" it must be disclosed to the delinquent officer.
Argument	Since such recommendations form part of the record and constitute appropriate material for consideration of the Government it would be essential that that material should not be withheld from him so that he could, while showing cause against the proposed punishment, make a proper representation.
Argument	The entire object of supplying a copy of the report of the Inquiry Officer is to enable the delinquent officer to satisfy the punishing authority that he is innocent of the charges framed against him and that even if the charges are held to have been proved, the punishment proposed to be inflicted is unduly servere".
RatioOfTheDecision	<CITATION_1232209> (1970)3 SCR 271 : (AIR 1970 SC 1095) it is reiterated that the duty of the Inquiry Officer ends with the making of  the report.
RatioOfTheDecision	The disciplinary authority has to consider the record of the inquiry and arrive at its own conclusion on each charge.
RatioOfTheDecision	Even if the inquiry committee makes a report absolving the employee of the charges against him, the disciplinary authority may on considering the entire record come to a different conclusion and impose a penalty.
Precedent	A reference is made in this connection to <NAME> case (AIR 1964 SC 364) (supra).
Precedent	<CITATION_1797599> (1975) Supp SCR 354: (AIR 1975 SC 2045), it was held that in the absence of furnishing the copy of the report of the Inquiry Officer, the plaintiff had been denied a reasonable opportunity of showing cause against his removal.
Precedent	It was also held that although S. 240(3) of the GOI Act did not cover a case of "removal", it did not mean that the protection given by the said section did not cover the case of "removal".
Precedent	From the Constitutional stand-point "removal" and "dismissal" stand on the same footing except as to future employment.
Precedent	In the context of S. 240(3) .
Precedent	removal  and dismissal are synonymous terms-the former being only species of the latter.
Precedent	The broad test of "reasonable opportunity" is whether in the given case the show cause notice issued to the delinquent servant contained or was accompanied by so much information as was necessary to enable him to clear himself of the guilt, if possible, even at that stage or in the alternative to show that the penalty proposed was much too harsh and disproportionate to the nature of the charge established against him.
RulingByLowerCourt	<CITATION_1134697> (1985) Supp 2 SCR 131 :(AIR 1985 SC 1416), this <ORG> had specifically to consider the legal position arising out of the 42nd Amendment of the Constitution by which clause (2) of Art.
RulingByLowerCourt	311 was amended and the part of the said clause, viz., "and where it is proposed, after such inquiry, to impose on him any such penalty until he has been given reasonable opportunity of making representation on the penalty proposed, but only on the basis of the evidence adduced during such inquiry" was deleted.
RulingByLowerCourt	In t hat decision, this Court has not dealt with the procedure to be followed by the disciplinary authority after the Inquiry Officer's report is received by it.
RatioOfTheDecision	The question whether the delinquent employee should be heard by the disciplinary authority to prove his innocence of the charges levelled against him when they are held to have been proved by the Inquiry Officer, although he need not be heard on the question of the proposed penalty was neither raised nor answered.
RatioOfTheDecision	This decision, therefore, is not helpful for deciding the said question.
RatioOfTheDecision	<CITATION_412675>  : (AIR 1987 SC 1919), again the question did not arise as to whether the report of the Inquiry Officer should be furnished to the delinquent employee as a part of the reasonable opportunity at the first stage, Viz., before the disciplinary authority took its decision on the said report and came to its own conclusions with regard to the guilt or innocence of the employee.
RatioOfTheDecision	The contention raised there was with regard to the non-supply of the report to show  cause against the penalty proposed.
RatioOfTheDecision	Since it was raised in ignorance of the 42nd Amendment of the Constitution, this <ORG> rejected the said contention.
Precedent	<CITATION_1295850> (1986)3 SCC 103 : (AIR 1986 SC 1173) which is a decision of two learned Judges of this <ORG>, it was lamented that after the 42nd Amendment of the Constitution, the question still remained as to the stage when the delinquent Government servant would get the opportunity of showing that he had not been guilty of any misconduct so as to deserve any punishment or that the charges proved against him were not of such a character as to merit the extreme penalty of dismissal or even of removal or reduction in rank and that any of the lesser punishments ought to have been sufficient in his case.
Precedent	The <ORG>, however, felt that it was bound by the majority decision in <NAME> case (AIR 1985 SC 1416) (supra).
Precedent	The <ORG> further went on to observe that in view of the constitutional change and the decision of the majority in <NAME> case  (supra), the only stage at which now a civil servant can exercise the said valuable right was by enforcing his remedy by way of a departmental appeal or revision or by way of judicial review.
Argument	<CITATION_690923> (1988) 3 SCR 209 : (AIR 1988 SC 1000), the question squarely arose before a Bench of two learned Judges of this <ORG> as to whether the failure to supply a copy of the report of the Inquiry Officer to the delinquent employee before the disciplinary authority makes up its mind and records the finding of guilt would constitute violation of Art.
Argument	311(2) of the Constitution and also of the principles of natural justice.
Argument	It was opined that in the event of failure to furnish the report of the Inquiry Officer, the delinquent employee is deprived of crucial, and critical material which is taken into account by the real authority which holds him guilty, viz., the disciplinary authority.
Argument	According to the <ORG>, it is the real authority because the Inquiry Officer does no more than act as a delegate and furnishes  the relevant material including his own assessment regarding the guilt, to assist the disciplinary authority who alone records the effective finding.
Argument	The non-supply of the copy of the report would, therefore, constitute violation of the principles of natural justice and accordingly will be tantamount to denial of reasonable opportunity within the meaning of Art.
Precedent	311(2) of the Constitution.
Precedent	It was observed that there could be glaring errors and omissions in the report or it may have been based on no evidence or rendered in disregard to or by overlooking evidence.
Precedent	If the report is not made available to the delinquent employee, this crucial material which enters into the consideration of the disciplinary authority never comes to be known to the delinquent and he gets no opportunity to point out such errors and omissions and to disabuse the mind of the disciplinary authority before he is held guilty.
Precedent	The Court then specifically pointed out that serving a copy of the inquiry report on the delinquent employee to e nable him to point out anomaly, if any, before finding of guilt is recorded by the disciplinary authority, is altogether a different matter from serving a second show cause notice against the penalty to be imposed which has been dispensed with by virtue of the amendment of Art.
Precedent	311(2) by the 42nd Amendment of the Constitution.
Precedent	The <ORG> then found that the said point required consideration by a larger <ORG> and referred the matter to Hon'ble the Chief Justice for placing it before a larger <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	5.
RatioOfTheDecision	Since it is contended that in <NAME> v. State of U. P., (1988) 3 SCC 600: (AIR 1988 SC 1338), a <ORG> of three learned Judges has taken a view that it is not necessary to furnish the report of the Inquiry Officer to the delinquent employee before the disciplinary authority arrives at its conclusions, it is necessary to consider the said authority a little closely.
Fact	In that case, pursuant to the direction of the High <ORG>, an inquiry was conducted by <ORG> under <ORG> plinary Proceedings [Administrative Tribunal] Rules, 1947 against the petitioner who was a Munsiff Magistrate.
Fact	The charge against him was that he had demanded bribe from a plaintiff in a suit pending before him.
Fact	After completion of the inquiry, the entire matter was considered by <ORG> of the High Court which approved the findings of <ORG> holding the writ petitioner guilty.

Precedent	What emerges from the above survey of the law on the subject is as follows:  Since the Government of India Act, 1935 till the 42nd Amendment of the Constitution, the Government servant had always the right to receive report of the Inquiry Officer/authority and to represent against the findings recorded in it when the Inquiry Officer/authority was not the disciplinary authority.

Precedent	The right to receive the Inquiry Officer's report and to show cause against the findings in the report was independent of the right to show cause against the penalty proposed.
Precedent	The two rights came to be confused with each other bacause as the law stood prior to the 42nd Amendment of the Constitution, the two rights arose simultaneously only at the stage when a notic e to show cause against the proposed penalty was issued.

Precedent	The right to receive the report and to represent against the findings recorded in it was thus inextricably connected with the acceptance of the report by the disciplinary authority and the nature of the penalty proposed.
Precedent	Since the 42nd Amendment of the Constitution dispensed with the issuance of the notice to show cause against the penalty proposed even if it was dismissal, removal or reduction in rank, some courts took the view that the Government servant was deprived of his right to represent ag ainst the findings of guilt as well.
Precedent	The error occurred on account of the failure to distinguish the two rights which were independent of each other.
Precedent	While the right to represent against the findings in the report is part of the reasonable opportunity available during the first stage of the inquiry viz., before <ORG> takes into consideration the findings in the report.
Precedent	the right to show cause against the penalty proposed belongs to the second stage when <ORG> has considered the findings in the report and has come to the conclusion with regard to the guilt of the employee and proposes to award penalty on the basis of its conclusions.
Precedent	The first right is the right to prove innocence.
Precedent	The second right is to plead for either no penalty or a lesser penalty although the conclusion regarding the guilt is accepted.
Precedent	It is the second right exercisable at the second stage which was taken away by the 42nd Amendment.
Precedent	The reason why the right to receive the report of the Inquiry O fficer is considered an essential part of the reasonable opportunity it the first stage and also a principle of natural justice is that the findings recorded by the Inquiry Officer form an important material before <ORG> which along with the evidence is taken into consideration by it to come to its conclusions.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is difficult to say in advance, to what extent the said findings including the punishment, if any, recommended in the report would influence <ORG> while drawing its conclusions.
RatioOfTheDecision	The findings further might have been recorded without considering the relevant evidence on record, or by misconstruing it or unsupported by it.
RatioOfTheDecision	If such a finding is to be one of the documents to be considered by <ORG>, the principles of natural justice require that the employee should have a fair opportunity to meet, explain and controvert it before he is condemned.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is the negation of the tenets of justice and a denial of fair opportunity to the employee to co nsider the findings recorded by a third party like the Inquiry Officer without giving the employee an opportunity to reply to it.
RatioOfTheDecision	Although it is true that the disciplinary authority is supposed to arrive at its own findings on the basis of the evidence recorded in the inquiry, it is also equally true that the disciplinary authority takes into consideration the findings recorded by the Inquiry Officer along with the evidence on record.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the circumstances, the findings of the Inquiry Officer do constitute an important material before the disciplinary authority which is likely to influence its conclusions.
RatioOfTheDecision	If the Inquiry Officer were only to record the evidence and forward the same to the disciplinary authority, that would not constitute any additional material before the disciplinary authority of which the delinquent employee has no knowledge.
RatioOfTheDecision	However, when the Inquiry Officer goes further and records his findings, as stated above, which may or may not be based on the evidence on record or are contrary to the  same or in ignorance of it, such findings are an additional material unknown to the employee but are taken into consideration by the disciplinary, authority while arriving at its conclusion.
RatioOfTheDecision	Both the dictates of the reasonable opportunity as well as the principles of natural justice, therefore, require that before the disciplinary, authority comes to its own conclusions, the delinquent employee should have an opportunity to reply to the Inquiry Officer's findings.
RatioOfTheDecision	The disciplinary authority is then required to consider the evidence, the report of the Inquiry Officer and the representation of the employee against it.
RatioOfTheDecision	It will thus be seen that where the Inquiry Officer is other than the disciplinary authority, the disciplinary proceedings break into two stages.
RatioOfTheDecision	The first stage ends when the disciplinary authority arrives at its conclusions on the basis of the evidence, Inquiry Officer's report and the delinquent employee's reply to it.
RatioOfTheDecision	The second stage begins when the disciplinary authority decides to impose  penalty on the basis of its conclusions.
RatioOfTheDecision	If the disciplinary authority decides to drop the disciplinary proceedings, the second stage is not even reached.
RatioOfTheDecision	The employee's right to receive the report is thus, a part of the reasonable opportunity of defending himself in the first stage of the inquiry.
Precedent	If this right is denied to him, he is in effect denied the right to defend himself and to prove his innocence in the disciplinary proceedings.
Precedent	The position in law can also be looked at from a slightly different angle.
Precedent	Article 311(2) says that the employee shall be given a "reasonable opportunity of being heard in respect of the charges against him".
