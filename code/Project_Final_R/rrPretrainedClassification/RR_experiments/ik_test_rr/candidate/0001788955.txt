RatioOfTheDecision	5.1 At this stage, we may, with advantage, notice some of the relevant provisions.
Fact	By Customs Notification No.
RulingByLowerCourt	5/76, dated 31-1-1976, <ORG> had exempted goods imported against an Advance Licence, being materials required to be imported for the purpose of manufacturing goods for execution of an export order, from duty of customs subject to the conditions laid down therein.
RulingByLowerCourt	One condition was that the materials should be covered by a <ORG>.
RulingByLowerCourt	Another was that the exempt materials should be used in the manufacture of such resultant products and in such factories as were specified in the <ORG>.
RulingByLowerCourt	They should not be sold or otherwise transferred to any other person or utilised in any other manner without the previous permission of the competent authority.
RulingByLowerCourt	5.2 Notification No.
RulingByLowerCourt	117/78, dated 9-6-1978 exempted the goods specified in the schedule to the notification, imported against an Advance Licence, being materials required to be imported for the purpose of manufacture of goods, or r eplenishment of the materials used in the manufacture of goods or both for executing one or more export orders, from customs duty.
RulingByLowerCourt	There were several conditions laid down including the one about DEEC.
RulingByLowerCourt	One of the conditions was that the exempt materials shall not be sold or otherwise transferred to any other person, or utilised or permitted to be utilised or disposed of in any other manner without the previous permission of the competent authority.
RulingByLowerCourt	However, this condition was not applicable to exempt materials which are imported for replenishment of the materials used in the manufacture of goods.
RatioOfTheDecision	5.3 Thus two important differences are noticeable in the 1978 scheme as compared to the earlier scheme.
RatioOfTheDecision	One is that the imported materials were allowed to be imported duty free not only for the manufacture of specified goods for export but also for replenishment of the materials used in the manufacture of such goods.
RatioOfTheDecision	Secondly, the prohibition on the disposal of the goods by sale or otherwise without the permission of  the competent authority was not made applicable to the materials imported for replenishment purposes.
RatioOfTheDecision	6.
Argument	At the outset, the learned Counsel for the appellants made certain submissions challenging the validity of the show cause notice issued to the appellants and the jurisdiction of the Collector of <ORG>, Cochin to adjudicate upon the proceedings initiated thereunder.
Argument	With respect to the show cause notice, the submission was that it was issued by the Assistant Collector on 30-6-1986, i.e., after the amendment to Section 28 of the <ORG> Act by Section 3 of the <ORG> (Amendment) Act, 1985 on 27-12-1985.
Argument	The effect of this amendment was two fold.
Argument	First, the show cause notice for payment of duties not levied, short-levied or erroneously refunded, in a case involving collusion or any wilful misstatement or suppression of facts by the importer or exporter or his agent or employee, enabling the <ORG> to invoke an extended period of five years instead of the usual period of six months, was to be issued by t he Collector.
Argument	Second, the order of adjudication must in such a case be passed by the Collector.
Fact	In the present instance, the show cause notice was issued by the Assistant Collector though cause was required to be shown to the Collector.
Argument	Further, neither the notice nor, for that matter, the order passed by the Collector did allege any suppression of facts, or mis-statement or collusion on the part of the appellants.
Argument	Therefore, urged <NAME>, the show cause notice was barred by time, the import having been effected on 19-1-1984 and the last shipment having been effected on 13-4-1984.

RatioOfTheDecision	10.
RatioOfTheDecision	Since the two preliminary submissions of <NAME> are fundamental to the validity of the impugned order, we propose to deal with them first.
RatioOfTheDecision	Taking first the question of the Cochin Collector's jurisdiction to demand duty in the present case, we have to note that the import of the goods took place at Madras.
Fact	Their clearance duty free in terms of <ORG> notification was granted at Madras.
Fact	In terms of the said notification, a claim was required to be made to the Collector of <ORG>, Madras for exemption from duty.
Fact	A bond/legal undertaking for complying with the conditions specified in the notification was to be executed before such authority as may be approved by <ORG>.
Fact	A declaration was to be filed by the appellants before the Assistant Collector binding themselves to pay on demand an amount equal to the duty leviable, b ut for the exemption, on the imported materials in respect of which the conditions laid down in the notification had not been compiled with (this declaration was filed before the Assistant Collector of <ORG>, Madras).
Fact	There is, therefore, no manner of doubt that the Assistant Collector of <ORG> or the Collector of <ORG>, Madras was the proper authority who was to return a finding of non-compliance with the conditions laid down in the notification and to demand customs duty in respect of the quantity of goods in respect of which the conditions were not complied with.
Fact	It is, of course, true that the use or utilisation of the imported materials in the present case was not in the Madras Collector's jurisdiction.
Fact	If the Cochin Collector had reason to believe that the conditions laid down in the notification had been violated necessitating proceedings against the appellants which could have led to demand of duty, penalty, etc., the proper course would have been for him to report the matter to the Collector of  <ORG>, Madras to enable the latter to proceed against the appellants in accordance with law.
RatioOfTheDecision	The Cochin Collector, however, seems to have assumed jurisdiction to deal with the matter himself.
RatioOfTheDecision	The analogy with Central Excise Rule 196 as sought to be drawn by <NAME> is inapplicable.
RatioOfTheDecision	The rule itself provides for demand of duly by the proper officer having jurisdiction over the applicant-user who may have got his requirement of materials cleared duty-free or at concessional duty in terms of Rule 192 from a manufacturer situated in another officer's jurisdiction.
RatioOfTheDecision	Such is not the case here.
RatioOfTheDecision	The two decisions cited in this context are of no help to the <ORG>.
Argument	<NAME> has sought to justify the demand on the plea that what was demanded by the Cochin Collector was not really duty but only an amount equal to the duty leviable.
RatioOfTheDecision	This contention is, on the face of it, untenable.
RatioOfTheDecision	For one thing, what the notification makes the importer liable to pay on demand is an amount equal to the duty leviable but for the exem ption.
RatioOfTheDecision	No doubt the duty has to be quantified in an amount but, for that reason, it does not cease to be duty.
RatioOfTheDecision	For another, the Collector himself has no such illusion.
RatioOfTheDecision	He has quite clearly and categorically asked the appellants to pay the customs duty demanded in the show cause notice.
RatioOfTheDecision	If indeed the amount in question was not duty it could not have been demanded in terms of an adjudication order under the Customs Act but only in enforcement of the bond or legal undertaking executed by the appellants, if necessary through appropriate proceedings in <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	<NAME> contention has, therefore, to be, and is, rejected  11.
RatioOfTheDecision	The <ORG> has not produced any notification or other legal provision (none has been placed before us) under which the Cochin Collector was conferred jurisdiction to demand duty in respect of goods imported at Madras and cleared by the Madras customs either generally or in this particular case.
RatioOfTheDecision	<ORG> has issued notifications under Section 4 of the Customs Act appo inting Collectors of <ORG> and lower officers for different jurisdictions.
RatioOfTheDecision	In terms of these notifications, the respective jurisdiction of the Collectors have been clearly spelt out (see, for example, Notification No.
RatioOfTheDecision	36-Cus., dated 1-2-1963, as amended).
RatioOfTheDecision	Notification No.
RatioOfTheDecision	37, dated 1-2-1963, as amended, appoints the Collector of <ORG> and Central Excise, Cochin, as the Collector of <ORG> in his jurisdiction.
RatioOfTheDecision	Evidently, it does not extend to Madras Port for which the Collector is the Collector of <ORG>, Madras, vide Notification No.
RatioOfTheDecision	36, dated 1-2-1963.
RatioOfTheDecision	There are, of course, a few officers appointed as Collectors with all-India jurisdiction such as the Director of Revenue Intelligence but the Collector of <ORG>, Cochin is not one among them.
RatioOfTheDecision	In these circumstances, we hold that the Collector of <ORG>, Cochin had no jurisdiction to demand duty in the present case and, consequently, we set aside the demand.
RatioOfTheDecision	12.
RatioOfTheDecision	Thus, we are now left with only the penalty imposed by the Collector on the appellants.
RatioOfTheDecision	Th e order does not in terms spell out the provision of the <ORG> Act under which the penalty has been imposed.
RatioOfTheDecision	However, the show cause notice called upon the appellants to explain why, in the circumstances stated in the notice, a penalty should not be imposed on them under Section 114 of the <ORG> Act, 1962.
RatioOfTheDecision	We have, therefore, to proceed on the basis that penalty has in fact been imposed under Section 114 , though it is not clearly stated so.
RatioOfTheDecision	The Cochin Collector, in any event, could not have imposed any penalty for alleged violation of the <ORG> notification in question since, as we have already held, the demand for duty could not have been made by him and it follows that any adjudication leading to imposition of penalty in connection with any alleged violation of the notification could not also have been made by the Cochin Collector.
RatioOfTheDecision	Now, Section 114 provides that any person who, in relation to any goods, does or omits to do any act, which act or omission would render such goods liable to confiscation  under Section 113 or abets the doing or omission of such an act, shall be liable to the penalties prescribed therein.
RatioOfTheDecision	Separate penalties are prescribed in the case of different types of goods.
RatioOfTheDecision	The first type is goods in respect of which any prohibition is in force under the Customs Act or any other law for the time being in force.
RatioOfTheDecision	It has not been shown before us that in respect of the exported goods, there was any such prohibition.
RatioOfTheDecision	This provision has no application to the present case.
RatioOfTheDecision	The Second type is dutiable goods other than prohibited goods.
RatioOfTheDecision	"Dutiable goods" in this context evidently means goods liable to export duty.
RatioOfTheDecision	The <ORG>'s case is not that the exported goods were dutiable or that they were exported without payment of duty.
RatioOfTheDecision	This provision again has no application.
RatioOfTheDecision	The third and the last type is goods that are exported under claim for drawback.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the present case, we are not concerned with goods which were exported under claim for drawback.
RatioOfTheDecision	In fact, the admitted position is that exports of th e goods covered by the 38 shipping bills, including the four relating to goods shipped after the import of materials at Madras, were effected without claiming any drawback thereon.
RatioOfTheDecision	If this is so, then, the provision in Section 114 enabling the Collector to levy penalty in the case of goods exported under claim for drawback is also not applicable to the present case.
RatioOfTheDecision	What appears to have happened (though we do not wish to pronounce any verdict on this) is that the appellants seem to have claimed duty drawback on cartons made out of non-duty paid imported materials in certain shipments which, however, are not the subject matter of the present dispute because the Collector, in his order, has not demanded repayment of the drawback amount which he should have done if the present dispute was in relation to such shipments.
RatioOfTheDecision	Perhaps, there are separate proceedings in this regard.
RatioOfTheDecision	Be that as it may, it is obvious that in the case before us, the penalty has not been imposed on the ground that the appellants had exported  goods under claim for drawback though they had been made out of non-duty paid imported materials.
RatioOfTheDecision	13.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the above view of the matter, the penalty levied by the Collector is also not sustainable and is set aside.
RatioOfTheDecision	14.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the view we have taken, there is no need to go into the other submissions made before us.
RatioOfTheDecision	15.
RulingByPresentCourt	The result is that the impugned order is set aside and the appeal is allowed.
