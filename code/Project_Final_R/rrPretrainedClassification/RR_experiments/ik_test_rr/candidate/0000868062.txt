Statute	5.
Statute	Under Section 4(1)(a) and (b)(i) and (ii) of the Act, subject to the provisions of the Act, the total income of any previous year of any person who is a resident and an ordinary resident of British India during such year includes all income, profits and gains from what ever source derived which are re ceived or deemed to be received in British India in such year or which accrued or arisen to him in British India during such year or which accrued or arose to him without British India during such year.
Statute	When the assessee is a partner in a firm, his share income or share loss has to be computed in the manner provided by Section 16(1)(b) and included in his total income; and under the proviso to that section, if the share so computed is a loss such loss may be set off or carried forward and set off in accordance with the provision of Section 24 .
Statute	Under Section 14(2)(a) tax is not payable by an assessee if he is a partner of an unregistered firm in respect of his income computed in the manner laid down in Section 16(1)(b) on which tax has already been paid by the firm.
RatioOfTheDecision	But if such income is from an unregistered firm carrying on business wholly out of British India, it would not be so exempt from taxation in the hands of the partner under this provision.
RatioOfTheDecision	To such income Section 14(2)(c) will apply and the tax shal l not be payable in respect of such income unless it is received or deemed to be received in or brought into British India in the previous year by or on behalf of the assessee or is assessable under Section 42 ; but the income so exempt has to be included in the total income of the assessee under Section 16(1)(a) .
RatioOfTheDecision	It is thus clear that the share income from British India and out of British India are both liable to tax subject to certain exemptions.

RatioOfTheDecision	7.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is difficult to appreciate the submission of the learned counsel for the Commissioner, who pressed the argument which is to be found in the second reference of <ORG>, that the share income from a firm carrying on trade is not income from business.
RatioOfTheDecision	Under S ection 4 of the Indian Partnership Act, partnership is a relation between persons who have agreed to share profits of a business carried on by them or by any of them acting for all; persons who have entered into partnership with one another are called individually "partners" and collectively "a firm" and the name under which their business is carried on is called the "firm name".
RatioOfTheDecision	A firm is not a juristic person, distinct from partners.
RatioOfTheDecision	The crucial test of partnership is the carrying on of business with a view to profit by all the partners or any of them acting for all.
RatioOfTheDecision	Every partner is under law an agent of the other partners.
RatioOfTheDecision	Common interest, mutual agency and division of profits are thus the essential conditions for the existence of a partnership.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is not clear from the statement of the case that the assessee was taking any active part in the conduct of the business.
RatioOfTheDecision	Nor is it to be found that the assessee received any salary, interest, commission or other remuneration from the Jaipur firm.
RatioOfTheDecision	We will theref ore assume for the purpose of this case that the partners residing at Jaipur were carrying on the business acting for the assessee.
RatioOfTheDecision	It follows that the assessee was carrying on the business at Jaipur in the firm name through his agents and that the income he would have received from that business would be the profits of that business earned out of British India.

RatioOfTheDecision	9.
RatioOfTheDecision	The learned counsel for the Commissioner has drawn our attention to the second proviso to Section 24 , sub-section (1), which say :-  "Provided further that where the assessee is an unregistered firm .......any such loss shall be set off only against the income, profits and gains of the firm and not against the income, profits and gains of any of the partners of the firm;..."  In our view, this proviso has no application as it is an exception to the general provision for set-off in Section 24(1) which refers to a set-off of losses under one head against the income under any other head mentioned in Section 6 of the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	This is what has been recognized by their Lordship of <ORG> in the case cited supra.
RatioOfTheDecision	10.
RatioOfTheDecision	We are unable to accept the argument of the learned counsel that the second proviso should be read as an indep endent provision.
RatioOfTheDecision	According to him the proviso is applicable to a set-off of the loss of an unregistered firm against the income, profits and gains of any of its partners, whether that firm in British India or outside and whether the set-off is claimed against another head of income or against the same head.
RatioOfTheDecision	As observed by <NAME> in his Interpretation of Statutes, 9th edition, page 165 :-  "The true principle undoubtedly is, that the sound interpretation and meaning of the statute, on a view of the enacting clause, saving clause and proviso, taken and construed together, is to prevail."
RatioOfTheDecision	"There is no rule that the first or enacting part is be construed without reference to the proviso.
RatioOfTheDecision	The proper course is to apply the broad general rule of construction, which is that a section or enactment must be construed as whole each portion throwing light if need be, on the rest.
RatioOfTheDecision	There is no other rule even in the case of a proviso in the strictest or narrowest sense" : (<NAME> v. <NAME>).
RatioOfTheDecision	As stated by their Lordship s of <ORG> in <CITATION_361854> :-  "The proper function of a proviso is to except and deal with a case which would otherwise fall within the general language of the main enactment, and its effect is confined to that case."
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 24(1) of the Act refers to the set-off of loss under one head against the income of the assessee under another head under Section 6 .
RatioOfTheDecision	To this general rule the second proviso provides an exception that in the case of an unregistered firm any loss of that firm under one head must be set off against its income, profits and gains under another head and not against the income, profits and gains of any of its partners.
RatioOfTheDecision	If the unregistered firm still returns a loss, that loss can be carried forward under sub-section (2).

RatioOfTheDecision	We now advert to the observations of the <ORG> on the powers of <ORG> in dealing with references made to it under Section 66(1) of the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	We have already set out the question of law referred to us under that section and the question of law that ought to have been referred as arising on the same facts.
RatioOfTheDecision	The Appellate <ORG> seems to think that it is not bound to decide an appeal before it by applying the appropriate law to the facts found by it simply because the assessee has erroneously railed on wrong provisions in support of his relief; and that it is not bound to refer to <ORG> the question of law that really arises on the facts found by it if the assessee does not raise the question of law in a particular form at the hearing of the appeal before the tribunal or in his application under Section 66(1) of the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the view of  the <ORG>, <ORG> has no jurisdiction to allow the assessee to present the case back to <ORG> under sub-section (4) to re-submit the case with appropriate question for decision by <ORG>; and that to pursue this course would be to permit the assessee to set up an entirely new case which he had not set up before the Income-tax authorities.
RatioOfTheDecision	In this view <ORG> pointed out that this Court had no jurisdiction to refer back the case to re-submit it with the question of law that this Court thought arose on the facts of the case.
RatioOfTheDecision	15.
RatioOfTheDecision	This is a serious misconception about the duties of <ORG> in deciding appeals and the applications under Section 66(1) of the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is a fundamental principle of administration of justice that a litigant has a right to present at any stage any question of law arising on the facts found by a <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	He has to plead facts an not law, and the <ORG> is always under the obligation to apply the appropriate law to the fact s found by it.
RatioOfTheDecision	There is no provision either in the Indian Income-tax Act or in the rules framed by the <ORG> to warrant a contrary view.
RatioOfTheDecision	16.
RatioOfTheDecision	Under Section 66(1) the assessee can require the Appellate <ORG> to refer to <ORG> any question of law arising out of such order, and the Appellate <ORG> shall, if it finds that a question of law does arise, draw up a statement of the case and refer it to <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	If the <ORG> is of the opinion that a question of law does not arise out of the order, the assessee can move <ORG> to require the Appellate <ORG> to state the case and refer it and if <ORG> is not satisfied of the correctness of the decision of the Appellate <ORG>, it shall require the <ORG> to state the case.
RatioOfTheDecision	In interpreting the expression "the question of law arising out of such order" <NAME>, C.J., summed up the correct legal position very tersely thus;  "........looking at the plain language of the section apart from any authority, I should have stated  that a question of law arose out of the order of the <ORG> if such a question was apparent on the order itself or it could be raised on the facts found by there <ORG> and which were stated in the order.
RatioOfTheDecision	I see no reason to confine the jurisdiction of this <ORG> to such questions of law as have been argued before the <ORG> or are dealt with by <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	The section does not say so and there is no reason why we should construe the expression arising out of such order in a manner unwarranted by the ordinary grammatical construction of that expression" : <CITATION_1097513> .
RatioOfTheDecision	There cannot thus be any reasonable doubt that this <ORG> had jurisdiction to refer the case back to the <ORG> to make such additions to or alterations in the statement of the case as it may direct.
RatioOfTheDecision	17.
RatioOfTheDecision	The statement of a case which the Appellate <ORG> is required to submit to a High <ORG> should not be coloured by the expression of opinion of the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	We have noticed in this case and several other cases that the <ORG> l does not state all the facts found by it which are necessary for answering the question that it referred to this Court, but it proceeds as if it is required to decide that question of law.
RatioOfTheDecision	In our view, this is outside the jurisdiction of <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	Prior to the amendment of Section 66 in 1939, the Commissioner in referring the case was required to submit his opinion on the case that he referred to <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	The section as now stands does not require <ORG> to express its opinion.
RatioOfTheDecision	That opinion can be found in the order under Section 33(4) of the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is unfair to the assessee that <ORG>, which may not have given full consideration to his case in the appellate order, should supplement that order by giving additional reasons, and sometimes additional facts, to the prejudice of the assessee.
RatioOfTheDecision	In our view, the <ORG> should state all the facts which were considered by it in coming to the conclusion it did under Section 33(4) , whether those facts are referr ed to in its order or in the orders of the Income-tax authorities and to refer to any document which may have been used in deciding the case.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is also necessary to state the case in restrained language even though the <ORG> considers an order of <ORG> under Section 66(4) erroneous.
RatioOfTheDecision	The <ORG> is not called upon by the Act to express its opinion on the correctness or otherwise of the order which under law it is bound to carry out.
