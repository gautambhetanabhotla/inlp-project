Fact	Mr. <NAME> for the appellant contends that <ORG> was in error in coming to the conclusion that the appellant's suit was incompetent because he argues that <ORG>  has misjudged.the effect of the provisions of s. 18-A In dealing With the question wether civil courts jurisdiction to entertain suit is barred or not it is necessary to bear in mind the fact that there is a general presumption that there must be a remedy in the ordinary civil courts to a citizen claiming that an amount has been recovered from him illegally and that such a remedy can be held to be barred only on very clear and unmistakable indications to the contrary.
RatioOfTheDecision	The exclusion of the jurisdiction of Civil Courts to entertain civil causes will not be assumed unless the relevant statute contains an express provision to that effect, or leads to a necessary and inevitable implication of that nature .The mere fact that a special statute provides for certain remedies may not by itself necessarily exclude the jurisdiction of the , civil courts to deal with a case brought before it in respect of some of the matters covered by the said statute.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is, therefore,necessary to enquire whether s.18- Aexpressly or by  necessary implication excludes the jurisdiction of the civil court to entertain a suit like the present.
Statute	Section 18-A provides that no suit or other Proceeding shall, except as expressly provided in this Act.
Statute	be instituted in any Court to set aside or modify any assessment made under this Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is common ground that there is no express provision made in the Act under which the present suit can be said to have been filed, and so, it falls under the prohibition contained in this section.
RatioOfTheDecision	The prohibition is express and unambiguous and there can be no doubt on a fair construction of the section that a suit cannot be entertained by a civil court if, by instituting the suit, the plaintiff wants to set a side or modify any assessment made under this Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	There is therefore, no difficulty in holding that this section excludes the jurisdiction of the civil courts in respect of the suits covered by it.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is, however, urged by Mr. <NAME> that if an order',of assessment has been made illegally by the appropriate autho rity purporting to exercise its powers under the Act, such an assessment cannot be said to be an assessment made under this Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	He contends that the words used are "any assessment made under this Act" and the section does not cover cases of assessment which are purported to have been made under this Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	In support of this argument he has referred us to the provisions of s. 17 (1) and s. 18 where any act done or purporting to be done under this Act is referred to.
RatioOfTheDecision	It would, however, be noticed that having regard to the subject-matter of the provisions contained in ss.
RatioOfTheDecision	17 (1) and 18 it was obviously necessary to refer not only to acts done, but also to acts purporting to be done under this Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 17 (1) is intended to bar certain proceedings and s. 18 is intended to afford an indemnity and that is the reason why the legislature had to adopt the usual formula by referring to acts done or porting to be done.
RatioOfTheDecision	It was wholly unnecessary purl to refer to cases of assessment purporting to have been made under  this Act while enacting s. 18-A , because all assessments made under this Act would attract the provisions of s. 18-A and that is all that the legislature intends s. 18-A to cover.
RatioOfTheDecision	The expression "'any assessment made under this Act" is, in our opinion, wide enough to coverall assessments made by the appropriate authorities under this Act whether the said assessments are correct or not.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is the activity of the assessing officer acting as such officer which is intended to be protected and as soon as it is shown that exercising his jurisdiction and authority under this Act, an assessing officer has made an order of assessment that clearly falls within the scope of s.18-A.
RatioOfTheDecision	The fact that the order passed by the assessing authority may in fact be incorrect or wrong does not affect the position that in law, the said order has been passed by an appropriate authority and the assessment made by it must be treated as made under this Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	Whether or not an assessment has been made under this Act will not depend on the  correctness or the accuracy of the order passed by the assessing authority.
RatioOfTheDecision	In determining the applicability of s.18-A.
RatioOfTheDecision	the only question to consider is: "Is the assessment sought to be set aside or modified by the suit instituted an assessment made under this Act or not?"
RatioOfTheDecision	It would be extremely anomalous, to hold that it is only an accurate and correct order of assessment which falls under s.18-A.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, it seems to us that the orders of assessment challenged by the appellant in its suit fall under s.18-A.
RatioOfTheDecision	In this connection, it is necessary to emphasise that while providing for a bar to suits in ordinary civil courts in respect of matters covered by s.18-A, the legislature has taken the precaution of safeguarding the 'citizens' rights by providing for adequate alternative remedies.
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 11 of the Act provides for appeals to such authority as may be prescribed; s. 12 confers revisional jurisdiction on the authorities specified by it; s.12-A allows an appeal to the appellate <ORG>; s.12-B provides  for a provision by <ORG> under the cases specified in it; s.12-C provides for an appeal to <ORG>; and s. RatioOfTheDecision	The matter can even be brought to this Court by way of a petition under Art.
RatioOfTheDecision	130 of the Constitution.
RatioOfTheDecision	It would thus be seen that and dealer who is aggrieved by an order of assessment passed in respect of his transactions, can avail him self of the remedies provided in that behalf by these sections of the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is in the light of these elaborated alternative remedies provided by the Act that the scope and effect of s.18-A must be judged.
RatioOfTheDecision	Thus considered, there can be no doubt that where and order of assessment has been made by an appropriate authority the provisions of this Act, any challenge to its correctness and any attempt either to have it set aside or modified must be made before the appellate or the revisional forum prescribed by the relevant provisions o f the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	A suit instituted for that purpose would be barred under s. 18-A .
RatioOfTheDecision	The facts alleged by the appellant in this case are somewhat unusual.

RatioOfTheDecision	The question about the exclusion of the jurisdiction of the civil courts to entertain civil actions by virtue of specific provisions contained in special statutes has been judicially considered on several occasions.

RatioOfTheDecision	Similarly, if an appropriate authority has acted in violation of the fundamental principles of judicial procedure, that may also tend to make the proceedings illegal and void and this infirmity may affect the validity of the order passed by the authority in question.

RatioOfTheDecision	In what cases such a plea would succeed it is unnecessary for us to decide in the present appeal because we have no doubt that the contention of the appellant that on the merits, the decision of the assessing authority was wrong, cannot be the subject- matter of a suit because s. 18-A clearly bars such a claim in the civil courts.
