RatioOfTheDecision	One thing may.be noticed that the seniority in <ORG> is not governed by the date of joining.
RatioOfTheDecision	If one looks at the Principles, as approved by the President, principle (i) is - "the seniority of officers, appointed to various <ORG> (Class 1) shall be determined on the basis of the 'date for increment on time-scale' to be specifically determined in each case in accordance with these principles".
RatioOfTheDecision	It is not an invariable rule that seniority should be determined only on the basis of the respective dates of appointment to the post  and that any departure from it would be unreasonable and illegal.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is open to <ORG> to take a note of the relevant circumstances obtaining in relation to each department and determine objectively the rules that should govern the inter se seniority and ranking.
RatioOfTheDecision	The dates of increment of the appellants in time-scale having been specifically determined by the authorities before Respondent 1 joined service, he has to rank junior to the appellants.
RatioOfTheDecision	Besides, principle  (vii), which is the relevant principle, may be extracted for understanding its scope:  "Principle (vii) In the case of Class II Officers permanently promoted to Class 1 Services, if two or more than two officers are promoted on the same date their relative seniority will be in the order of selection.
RatioOfTheDecision	Subject to the aforesaid provisions the seniority of officers, permanently promoted from Class 11 to Class 1 services, shall be determined by giving weightage based on-  (a) the year of service connoted by the initial pay on permanen t promotion to Class 1 Service; or  (b) half the total number of years of continuous service in Class II, both officiating and permanent; whichever is higher, subject to a maximum weightage of five years."
RatioOfTheDecision	12.
RatioOfTheDecision	It was in accordance with principle (vii)(a) that the date of increment on time-scale for the appellants was determined.
RatioOfTheDecision	Once this position becomes clear, there is no difficulty in applying principle (vii).
RatioOfTheDecision	13.
RatioOfTheDecision	The principle of granting seniority on the basis of weightage of past service and lower service to the category of promoting (sic promoted) officers is well known and well recognised in the service jurisprudence.
RatioOfTheDecision	14.
RatioOfTheDecision	We may mention that the appellants were initially members of <ORG> and were thus actively associated in personnel matters and it was because of this that <ORG> thought it fit to provide avenue for promotion to these officers in <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	The nature of working being done by them while working for <ORG> was similar to the work which they were required to perform in superior se rvice, viz., IRPS.

RatioOfTheDecision	The principle for conferment of limited benefit or weightage was held to be not unreasonable or illegal by this Court in the case of <CITATION_492752> .

Precedent	It is settled law that the appointing authority or the appropriate <ORG> can frame rules governing seniority which are reasonable keeping in mind the divergent claims that can be put forward by the various categories of the members of the service.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is, however, necessary that there should be no discrimination, that is, persons placed in the same group must be treated similarly and, further, that any principle which is made the basis of determination of seniority should, if applicable to others, be applied to them also.
RatioOfTheDecision	In other words, if seniority is to depend purely upon the date of confirmation or the date of appointment, that rule should be applicable to all; but if the promotees and special recruits are being given weightage,  the principles applicable to the members of the service should be kept in mind while determining the weightage to be given or while laying down rules for determination of seniority.
RatioOfTheDecision	18.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the case of <CITATION_282592> this Court considered the rules of seniority contained in the Regulation of Seniority Rules and the Special Recruitment Seniority Regulations, 1960 to be valid not offending Articles 14 and 16 of the Constitution.
RatioOfTheDecision	Under Rule 3(3)(b) of the Regulation of Seniority Rules the year of allotment of a promotee was to be determined by his continuously officiating on a senior scale post included in <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	Under Regulation 3(3) of the Special Recruitment Seniority Regulations, 1960, the year of allotment of special recruits was to be determined by the formula.
RatioOfTheDecision	While upholding the validity of these rules and not regarding them to be arbitrary or discriminatory, it was observed that the promotees can claim that total length of service in <ORG> be consid ered for determining seniority.
RatioOfTheDecision	On the other hand, the direct recruits can say that the seniority should depend upon their entry in <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	This Court took the view that the rule contained in clauses (b) and (c) of Rule 3(3) was a mean between these extreme views and was regarded to be just and fair.
RatioOfTheDecision	Similarly, the rule contained in Regulation 3(3) of the Special Recruitment Seniority Regulations, 1960 was considered to be fair and equitable.
