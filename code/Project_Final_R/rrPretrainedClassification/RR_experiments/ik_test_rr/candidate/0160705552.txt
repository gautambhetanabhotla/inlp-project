Precedent	Our attention has been drawn to a decision of <ORG> in <CITATION_56215> under Urban Land Ceiling and Regulation Act , 1976 and Others [1988 Mh.

Fact	The plots cannot be sub-divided nor, thus, can be given to any other person.
RatioOfTheDecision	The lands in question are, therefore, not available for distribution, equitable or otherwise.
RatioOfTheDecision	The respondents being tenants in common, their right, title and interest in the land would be half and half.
RatioOfTheDecision	The definition of the word 'person' as contained in Section 2(i) although merits liberal construction, but the respondents would not come within the purview thereof.
RatioOfTheDecision	It would, therefore, be not correct to contend that they together would be entitled only to one unit.
RatioOfTheDecision	So far as the submission of Mr. <NAME> to the effect that as on the appointed day, no construction had been made on the land in question and only a building plan therefor has been sanctioned, the exception contained in Section 2(q) of the Act would not be applicable is concerned, we may notice that clause (i) of Section 2(q) excludes the land on which construction of a building is not permissible under the building regulations in force in the area in which such  land is situated from the definition of 'vacant land'.
RatioOfTheDecision	The area where there are building regulations, the land occupied by any building which has been constructed before, or is being constructed on, the appointed day with the approval of the appropriate authority and the land appurtenant to such building is also excluded.
RatioOfTheDecision	A plain and literal meaning attributed to clause (i) of Section 2(q) leaves no manner of doubt that for the purpose of applicability thereof, it is not necessary that constructions must exist on the appointed day.
RatioOfTheDecision	What is necessary is as to whether construction of a building is permissible or not.
RatioOfTheDecision	The scheme of the Act particularly Section 29 thereof clearly shows that regulation of construction of building with dwelling units was contemplated by the makers of the legislation.
RatioOfTheDecision	As regard the space which is to be left vacant for the purpose of construction of building, a restriction of construction of building with dwelling units having been provided for in the Act, it is idle to suggest that  for the purpose of exclusion of land in terms of clause (i) of Section 2(q) , constructions must have existed on the land on the appointed day.
RatioOfTheDecision	Had the intention of the <ORG> been to exclude only such lands which have been directed to be left vacant only on the constructed buildings in terms of the building regulations, the same would have been stated expressly.
RatioOfTheDecision	Indisputably the respondents had applied for sanction of the building plan and the same had been granted.
RatioOfTheDecision	They, thus, on the appointed day in terms of the building regulations having regard to the purport and object of the Act were, thus, in our opinion entitled to get the vacant land required to be kept in terms of the building plan excluded.
RatioOfTheDecision	The only question which survives for our consideration is as to whether for the purpose of determination of ceiling area, the land over which the flats of the respondents situated at Bombay were required to be taken into consideration for the purpose of sub-section (9) of Section 4 of the Act.
Precedent	So far as  those flats in Bombay are concerned, the respondents did not hold any vacant land appurtenant thereto.
RatioOfTheDecision	They were entitled, as a matter of right, to exclusively possess and own the structures alone.
RatioOfTheDecision	No land appurtenant to the said structure exclusively belongs to them.
RatioOfTheDecision	The said Act being expropriatory legislation is required to be construed strictly.
Precedent	[ <CITATION_1119469> .
Precedent	[2003 (2) SCALE 145 para 41].
Precedent	In <ORG> Vs. <ORG> and Others [(2003) 2 SCC 111], this Court held:  "An owner of a property, subject to reasonable restrictions which may be imposed by the <ORG>, is entitled to enjoy the property in any manner he likes.
Precedent	A right to use a property in a particular manner or in other words a restriction imposed on user thereof except in the mode and manner laid down under statute would not be presumed.
Precedent	The statutory interdict of use and enjoyment of the property must be strictly construed.
Precedent	It is well-settled that when a statutory authority is required to do a thing in  a particular manner, the same must be done in that manner or not at all.
Precedent	The <ORG> and other authorities while acting under the said Act are only creature of statute.
Precedent	They must act within the four-corners thereof."
Precedent	In terms of the provisions of the Act, land in excess of the ceiling area was to vest in the <ORG> Government.
Precedent	By reason of the provisions contained in Section 2(q) of the Act, the <ORG> has defined the term 'vacant land'.
Precedent	Strict meaning has to be attributed to the said words as expression 'means' has been used.
Precedent	From the definition of 'vacant land', land which is not mainly used for the purpose of agriculture has been excluded.
Precedent	Further thereto, what is required to be excluded would be those lands as are specified in clauses (i), (ii) and (iii) thereof.
Precedent	The exclusionary clauses contained in the definition of 'vacant land' must, therefore, receive a liberal construction.
Precedent	Section 2(q) of the Act keeping in view the fact that expression 'means' has been used would be prima facie restrictive and  exhaustive.
Precedent	The said provision is neither vague nor ambiguous.
Precedent	It cannot also be said that sub-section (9) of Section 4 provides a contrary context.
Precedent	It is trite that when a statutory enactment defines its terms, the same should govern what is proposed, authorised or done under or by reference to that enactment.
Precedent	[See <ORG> vs. <ORG> (1990 (1) All.
Precedent	E.R.
Precedent	780 at 785].
Precedent	It is also trite that all statutory definitions have to be read subject to the qualification variously expressed in the interpretation clause which created them particularly when the definition is exhaustive.
Precedent	The only exception to the aforementioned rule would be where there exist provisions, the meaning therefor is required to be determined in the context in which the word has been used.
Precedent	The words 'vacant land' have been defined as land subject to certain exception.
Precedent	Those exclusionary clauses must be interpreted liberally.
Precedent	The charging section is Section 3 which provides that persons shall  not be entitled to hold any vacant land in excess of the ceiling limit in the territory to which it applies.
Precedent	Ceiling limit has been provided in terms of Section 4 but the same is subject to other provisions contained therein.
Precedent	The scheme of the Act in general and the purport and object thereof in particular do not lead to a conclusion that what has been excluded from the definition of 'vacant land' should be included for another purpose.
RatioOfTheDecision	There does not exist any reason as to why the plain and unequivocal meaning cannot be given to the said definition.
RatioOfTheDecision	For the purpose of determination of the ceiling limit as stated in sub-section (9) of Section 4 of the Act, a person must not only hold a vacant land but also must hold any other land on which there is a building with a dwelling unit therein which clearly goes to show that such other land on which there is a building for the purpose of sub- section (9) of Section 4 must be a land other than a vacant land.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is well-settled that the provisions of the statute are  to be read in the text and context in which they have been enacted.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is well-settled that in construction of a statute an effort should be made to give effect to all the provisions contained therein.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is equally well-settled that a statute should be interpreted equitably so as to avoid hardship.
RatioOfTheDecision	So interpreted the decision of this <ORG> in <CITATION_1186610> [] commends to us in preference of the decision of this <ORG> in <CITATION_1293650> [].
Precedent	<NAME> case (supra) has been followed by this <ORG> in <NAME> and Others vs. State of U.P.
Precedent	and Others [(1993) Supp.
Precedent	(1) SCC 1] and <NAME> vs. District Judge, Gorakhpur and Others [].
RatioOfTheDecision	We are not unmindful of the observations made by a two-Judge Bench of this <ORG> in <CITATION_1340845> [] stating that the decisions of this <ORG> in <NAME>'s case (supra) and <NAME> case (supra) are in conflict with each other and <NAME>'s case should hold the field.
RatioOfTheDecision	However, in <NAME> case (supra), the conflict was not resolved by <ORG> stitution Bench to which a reference was made by a three-Judge Bench in <CITATION_1340845> ].
RatioOfTheDecision	In view of our discussions aforementioned, it must be held that – (1) that the respondents having independent title to the property in question, are entitled to the two separate units under the said Act;  (2) despite the fact that no construction had been raised on appointed day, they are entitled to the benefit under sub-clause  (i) of clause (q) of sub-section (2) of the Act; and (3) for the purpose of determination of ceiling limit, the area of the flats belonging to the respondents in Bombay would not be taken into consideration.
