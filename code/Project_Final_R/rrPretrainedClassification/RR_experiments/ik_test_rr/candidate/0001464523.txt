Argument	The neat and terse definition of tax which has been given by <ORG>, in <NAME> v. <ORG> (1) is often cited as a classic on this subject.

RatioOfTheDecision	If specific services are rendered to a specific area or to a specific class of persons or trade or business in any local area, and as a condition precedent for the said services or in return for them cess is levied against the said area or the said class of persons or trade or business the cess is distinguishable from a tax and is described as a fee.
RatioOfTheDecision	Tax recovered by public authority invariably goes into the consolidated fund which ultimately is utilised for all public purposes, whereas a cess levied by way of fee is not intended to be, and does not become, a part of the consolidated fund.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is earmarked and set apart for the purpose of services for which it is levied.
RatioOfTheDecision	The re is, however, an element of compulsion in the imposition of both tax and fee.
RatioOfTheDecision	When the <ORG> decides to render a specific service to any area or to any class of persons, it is not open to the said area or to the said class of persons to plead that they do not want the service and therefore they should be exempted from the payment of the cess.
RatioOfTheDecision	Though there is an element of quid pro quo between the tax- payer and <ORG> there is no option to the tax-payer in the matter of receiving the service determined by public authority.
RatioOfTheDecision	In regard to fees there is, and must always be, co-relation between the fee collected and the service intended to be rendered.
RatioOfTheDecision	Cases may arise where under the guise of levying a fee <ORG> may attempt to impose a tax; and in the case of such a colourable exercise of legislative power courts would have to scrutinise the scheme of the levy very carefully and determine whether in fact there is a co-relation between the service and the levy, or whether the levy is eit her not co-related with service or is levied to such an excessive extent as to be a presence of a fee and not a fee in reality.

RatioOfTheDecision	It is not disputed that under Entry 23 in List II read with Entry 66 in the said List <ORG> can levy a fee in respect of mines and mineral development.

RatioOfTheDecision	The argument is that though <ORG> is competent to levy a fee in respect of mines and mineral development, if the statute passed by <ORG> in substance and in effect imposes a duty of excise it is travelling outside its jurisdiction and is trespassing on the legislative powers of <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	This argument is based on two considerations.
RatioOfTheDecision	The first relates to the form in which the levy is imposed, and the second relates to the extent of the levy authorised.
RatioOfTheDecision	The extent of the  levy authorised would always depend upon the nature of the services intended to be rendered and the financial obligations incurred thereby.
RatioOfTheDecision	If the services intended to be rendered to the notified mineral areas require that a fairly large cess should be collected and co- relation can be definitely established between the proposed services and the impost levied, then it would be unreasonable to suggest that because the rate of the levy is high it is not a fee but a duty of excise.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the present case, if the development of the mining areas involves con- siderable expenditure which necessitates the levy of the prescribed rate it only means that the services being rendered to the mining areas are very valuable and the rate- payer in substance is compensating the <ORG> for the services rendered by it to him.

RatioOfTheDecision	If the co-relation between the levy and the services was not genuine or real, or if the levy was disproportionately higher than the requirements of the services intended to be rendered it would have been another matter.
RatioOfTheDecision	Then as to the form in which the impost is levied, it is difficult to appreciate how the method adopted by the <ORG> in recovering the impost can alter its character.

RatioOfTheDecision	All that it has to do is then to create a special fund out of the amounts collected and to attach rendering of certain services to the fund.
RatioOfTheDecision	All that would be necessary would be to define the services to be rendered so widely that the amount required for the purpose would be practically limitless.
RatioOfTheDecision	In that case there would be no difficulty in levying any amount of tax on income, for the amount collected would always be insufficient for the large number of services to be rendered.
RatioOfTheDecision	What has to be done is to find out a number of items in Lists II and III of the Seventh Schedule in respect of which fees can be levied by the State <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	These fees can be levied on a total basis for a large number of services under various  entries of Lists II and III.
RatioOfTheDecision	A fund can be created, say, for rendering services of various kinds to residents of one district.
RatioOfTheDecision	In order to meet the expenses of tendering such services, suppose, the legislature imposes a tax on every one in the district at 10 per centum of the net total income (other than agricultural income); the amount so collected is put in a separate fund and ear-marked for such special services to be rendered to the residents of that district.
RatioOfTheDecision	Can it be said that such a levy is a fee justified under various entries of Lists II and III, and not a tax on income, on the ground that this is merely a mode of quantification?
RatioOfTheDecision	As an instance, take, item 6 of List II, "Public health and sanitation, hospitals and dispensaries"; item 9, "Relief of the disabled and unemployable"; item II, Education; item 12, Libraries, museums and similar institutions"; item 13, communications, that is to say, roads, bridges and other means of communications; item 17, "Water, that is to say, water supplies, irrigati on and canals, drainage and embankments, water storage and water power"; and item', 25, "Gas and gas-works"; item 23 of List III, "Social security and social insurance, employment and unemployment"; item 24, "Welfare of labour including conditions of work, provident funds, employers' liability workmen's compensation, invalidity and old age pensions and maternity benefits"; item 25, "Vocational and technical training of labour"; and item 38, "Electricity".
RatioOfTheDecision	Assume that a fund is created for rendering, these services to the residents of a district.
RatioOfTheDecision	<ORG> is entitled to impose fees for rendering these services to the residents of the district; the costs of these services would obviously be limitless and in order to meet these costs, the State legislature levies a consolidated fee for all these purposes at 10 per centum of the total net income on the residents of the district (excluding his agricultural income) as a measure of quantification of the fee.
RatioOfTheDecision	Can it be said in the circumstances that such  a levy would not be Income-tax, simply because a fund is created to be used in the district where collections are made and these services have to be rendered out of the fund so created to the residents of that district and to no others?
RatioOfTheDecision	The answer can only be one, viz., that the nature of the impost is to be seen in its pith and substance; and if in pith and substance it is income-tax within item 82 of List I of the Seventh Schedule it will still remain income-tax in spite of the creation of a fund and the attaching of certain services to the monies in that fund to be rendered in a particular area.
RatioOfTheDecision	Such an impost can never be justified as a consolidated fee on the ground that it is merely a method of quantification.

RatioOfTheDecision	There can be no doubt in the circ umstances that the levy of a cess as a fee in this case is a colourable piece of legislation.
RatioOfTheDecision	I do not say that <ORG> did this deliberately.
RatioOfTheDecision	The motive of the legislature in such cases is irrelevant and it is the effect of the legislation that has to be seen.
RatioOfTheDecision	Looking at that, the cess in this case is in pith and substance nothing other than a duty of excise under item 84 of List I and therefore the State legislature was incompetent to levy it as a fee.
Fact	The next contention on behalf of the State of Orissa is that if the cess is not justified as a fee, it is a tax under item 50 of List II of the Seventh Schedule.
RulingByLowerCourt	Item 50 provides for taxes on mineral rights subject to any limitations imposed by <ORG> by law relating to mineral development.
RulingByLowerCourt	This raises a question as to what are taxes on mineral rights.
RulingByLowerCourt	Obviously, taxes on mineral rights must be different from taxes on goods produced in the nature of duties of excise.
RulingByLowerCourt	If taxes on mineral rights also include taxes on minerals produced,  there would be no difference between taxes on mineral rights and duties of excise under item 84 of List I.
RulingByLowerCourt	A comparison of Lists I and II of the Seventh Schedule shows that the same tax is not put in both the Lists.
RulingByLowerCourt	Therefore, taxes on mineral rights must be different from duties of excise which are taxes on minerals produced.
RulingByLowerCourt	The difference can be understood if one sees that before minerals are extracted and become liable to duties of excise somebody has got to work the mines.
RulingByLowerCourt	The usual method of working them is for the owner of the mine to grant mining leases to those who have got the capital to work the mines.
RulingByLowerCourt	There should therefore be no difficulty in holding that taxes on mineral rights are taxes on the right to extract minerals and not taxes on the minerals actually extracted.
RulingByLowerCourt	Thus tax on mineral rights would be confined, for example, to taxes on leases of mineral rights and on premium or royalty for that.
RulingByLowerCourt	Taxes on such premium and royalty would be taxes on mineral rights while taxes on the minerals ac tually extracted would be duties of excise.
RulingByLowerCourt	It is said that, there may be cases where the owner himself extracts minerals and does not give any right of extraction to somebody else and that in such cases in the absence of mining leases or sub-leases there would be no way of levying tax on mineral right,-,.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is enough to say that these cases also, rare though they are, present no difficulty.
RatioOfTheDecision	Take the case of taxes on annual value of buildings.
RatioOfTheDecision	Where there is a lease of the building, the annual value is determined by the lease-money; but there are many cases where owners themselves live in buildings.
RatioOfTheDecision	In such cases also taxes on buildings are levied on the annual value worked out according to certain rules.
RatioOfTheDecision	There would be no difficulty where an owner himself works the mine to value the mineral rights on the same principles on which leases of mineral rights are made and then to tax the royalty which, for example, the owner might have got if instead of working the mine himself he had leased it out to somebody el se.
RatioOfTheDecision	There can be no doubt therefore that taxes on mineral rights are taxes of this nature and not taxes on minerals actually produced.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore the present case is not a tax on mineral rights; it is a tax on the minerals actually produced and can be no different in pith and substance from a tax on goods produced which comes under Item 84 of List I, as duty of excise.
RatioOfTheDecision	The present levy therefore under s. 4 of the Act cannot be justified as a tax on mineral rights.

RatioOfTheDecision	I would therefore allow the petition, and declare that the Orissa Mining Areas Development Fund Act, 1952, is beyond the constitutional competence of the Orissa <ORG> to pass it.
RatioOfTheDecision	The whole Act must be struck down because there will be very little left in the Act if s. 4 falls as it must.
