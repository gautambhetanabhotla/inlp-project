Statute	Therefore, Section 13(6) inter alia provides that once <ORG> takes possession of the secured asset, then the rights, title and interest in that asset can be dealt with by <ORG> as if it is the owner of such an asset.
Statute	In other words, the asset will vest in <ORG> free of all encumbrances and the secured creditor would be entitled to give a clear title to the transferee in respect thereof.
Statute	Section 13(7) refers to recovery of all costs, charges and expenses incurred by <ORG> for taking action under Section 13(4) .
Statute	Section 13(7) provides for priority in the matter of recovery of dues from the borrower.
Statute	It inter alia provides for payment of surplus to the person entitled thereto.
Statute	Section 13(8) inter alia states that if the dues of the secured creditor together w ith all costs, charges and expenses incurred are tendered to the secured creditor before the debt fixed for sale/transfer, the secured asset shall not be sold or transferred by the bank/FI to the asset reconstruction company and no further steps shall be taken in that regard.
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 13(9) inter alia states that where a financial asset is funded by more than one bank/FI or in case of joint financing by a consortium, no single secured creditor from that consortium shall be entitled to exercise right under Section 13(4) unless exercise of such right is agreed upon by all the secured creditors.
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 13(9) provides for one more instance when permission of <ORG> may be required under the first proviso to Section 19(1) of the <ORG> Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	The agreement between the secured creditors in such cases is required to be placed before the <ORG> not as a fetter on the rights of the secured creditors but out of abundant caution.
RatioOfTheDecision	Generally, such agreements are complex in measure, particularly because rights of each of the secured  creditor in the consortium may be required to be looked into.
RatioOfTheDecision	However, if before the <ORG>, all the secured creditors in such consortium enter into an agreement under Section 13(9) then no such further inquiry is required to be made by the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	In such cases, the <ORG> has only to see that all the secured creditors in the consortium are represented under the agreement.
RatioOfTheDecision	The point to be noted is that the scheme of the NPA Act does not deal with disputes between the secured creditors and the borrower.
RatioOfTheDecision	On the contrary, the NPA Act deals with the rights of the secured creditors inter se.
RatioOfTheDecision	The reason is that the NPA Act proceeds on the basis that the liability of the borrower has crystallized and that his account is classified as non-performing asset in the hands of the bank/FI.
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 13(9) also deals with pari passu charge of the workers under Section 529-A of the Companies Act, 1956, apart from banks and financial institutions, who are secured creditors.
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 13(10) inter alia states that where the dues of the se cured creditor are not fully satisfied by the sale proceeds of the secured assets, the secured creditor may file an application to <ORG> under Section 17 of the NPA Act for recovery of balance amount from the borrower.
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 13(10) , therefore, shows that the bank/ FI is not only free to move under NPA Act with or without leave of <ORG> but having invoked NPA Act , liberty is given statutorily to the secured creditors (banks/ FIs.)
RatioOfTheDecision	to move the <ORG> under the <ORG> Act once again for recovery of the balance in cases where the action taken under Section 13(4) of the NPA Act does not result in full liquidation of recovery of the debts due to the secured creditors.
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 13(10) fortifies our view that the remedies for recovery of debts under the <ORG> Act and the NPA Act are complementary to each other.
RatioOfTheDecision	Further, Section 13(10) shows that the first proviso to Section 19(1) of <ORG> Act is an enabling provision and that the said provision cannot be read as a condition precedent to taking recourse to NPA Act .
Statute	Section 13(1 1) of the NPA Act inter alia states that, without prejudice to the rights conferred on the secured creditor under Section 13 , the secured creditor shall be entitled to proceed against the guarantor/pledgor; that the secured creditor shall be entitled to sell the pledged assets without taking recourse under Section 13(4) against the principal borrower in relation to the secured assets under the NPA Act .
Statute	Section 13(13) states that, no borrower shall, after receipt of notice under Section 13(2) , transfer by way of sale, lease or otherwise any of his secured assets referred to in the notice, without prior written consent of the secured creditor.
RatioOfTheDecision	Thus, Section 13(13) further fortifies our view that notice under Section 13(2) is not merely a show cause notice.
RatioOfTheDecision	In fact, Section 13(13) indicates that the notice under Section 13(2) in effect operates as an attachment/ injunction restraining the borrower from disposing of the secured assets and, therefore, such a notice, which in the present case is dated 6.1.2003,  is not a mere show cause notice but it is an action taken under the provision of the NPA Act .
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 17 of NPA Act confers right to appeal.
Statute	It inter alia states that any person including borrower, aggrieved by exercise of rights by the secured creditor under Section 13(4) , may make an application to the <ORG> as an appellate authority within forty-five days from the date on which action under Section 13(4) is taken.
Statute	That application should be accompanied by payment of fees prescribed by the 2002 Rules made under the NPA Act .
Statute	A proviso is added to Section 17(1) by amending Act 30 of 2004.
Statute	It states that different fees may be prescribed for making the application by the borrower and the person other than the borrower.
Statute	By way of abundant caution, an Explanation is added to Section 17(1) saying that the communication of the reasons to the borrower by the secured creditor rejecting his representation shall not constitute a ground for appeal to the <ORG>.
Statute	However, under Section 17(2) , the <ORG> is required to consi der whether any of the measures referred to in Section 13(4) taken by the secured creditor for enforcement of security are in accordance with the provisions of the NPA Act and the Rules made thereunder.
Statute	If the <ORG>, after examining the facts and circumstances of the case and the evidence produced by the parties, comes to the conclusion that any of the measures taken under Section 13(4) are not in accordance with the NPA Act , it shall direct the secured creditor to restore the possession/ management to the borrower (vide Section 17(3) of NPA Act).
RatioOfTheDecision	On the other hand, after the <ORG> declares that the recourse taken by the secured creditor under Section 13(4) is in accordance with the provisions of the NPA Act then, notwithstanding anything contained in any other law for the time being in force, the secured creditor shall be entitled to take recourse to any one or more of the measures specified under Section 13(4) to recover his secured debt.
RatioOfTheDecision	In our view, Section 17(4) shows that the secured creditor is free to t ake recourse to any of the measures under Section 13(4) notwithstanding anything contained in any other law for the time being in force, e.g., for the sake of argument, if in the given case the measures undertaken by the secured creditor under Section 13(4) comes in conflict with, let us say the provision under the <ORG> land revenue law, then notwithstanding such conflict, the provision of Section 13(4) shall override the local law.
RatioOfTheDecision	This position also stands clarified by Section 35 of the NPA Act which states that the provisions of NPA Act shall override all other laws which are inconsistent with the NPA Act .
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 35 is also important from another angle.
RatioOfTheDecision	As stated above, the NPA Act is not inherently or impliedly inconsistent with the DRT Act in terms of remedies for enforcement of securities.
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 35 gives an overriding effect to the NPA Act with all other laws if such other laws are inconsistent with the NPA Act .
RatioOfTheDecision	As far as the present case is concerned, the remedies are complimentary to each other  and, therefore, the doctrine of election has no application to the present case.

Argument	Learned advocates submitted that actual physical possession of immovable assets can be taken under Rule 8(3), in cases where there is a vacant plot or a property which is lying unatte nded, but where the immovable property is in actual physical possession of any person, the person in possession cannot be dispossessed by virtue of a notice under Rule 8(1); that actual physical possession is to be delivered only after confirmation of sale under Rule 9(6) read with Appendix V under which the authorised officer is empowered to deliver the property to the purchaser free from all encumbrances in terms of Rule 9(9) of the 2002 Rules.
Argument	Learned advocates, therefore, submitted that <ORG> was right in holding that the borrower or any other person in possession of the immovable property cannot be physically dispossessed at the time of issuing notice under Section 13(4) of the NPA Act so as to defeat the adjudication of his claim by the <ORG> under Section 17 of NPA Act, and that, physical possession can be taken only after the sale is confirmed in terms of Rule 9(9) of the 2002 Rules.
RatioOfTheDecision	We do not find any merits on the above contentions for the following reasons.
RatioOfTheDecision	The word possession is a relativ e concept.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is not an absolute concept.
RatioOfTheDecision	The dichotomy between symbolic and physical possession does not find place in the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	As stated above, there is a conceptual distinction between securities by which the creditor obtains ownership of or interest in the property concerned (mortgages) and securities where the creditor obtains neither an interest in nor possession of the property but the property is appropriated to the satisfaction of the debt (charges).
RatioOfTheDecision	Basically, the NPA Act deals with the former type of securities under which the secured creditor, namely, the bank/FI obtains interest in the property concerned.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is for this reason that the NPA Act ousts the intervention of the courts/ tribunals.
RatioOfTheDecision	Keeping the above conceptual aspect in mind, we find that Section 13(4) of the NPA Act proceeds on the basis that the borrower, who is under a liability, has failed to discharge his liability within the period prescribed under Section 13(2) , which enables the secured creditor to take recourse to one of the  measures, namely, taking possession of the secured assets including the right to transfer by way of lease, assignment or sale for realizing the secured assets.
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 13 (4-A) refers to the word "possession" simpliciter.
RatioOfTheDecision	There is no dichotomy in sub-section (4-A) as pleaded on behalf of the borrowers.
Statute	Under Rule 8 of the 2002 Rules, the authorised officer is empowered to take possession by delivering the possession notice prepared as nearly as possible in Appendix IV to the 2002 Rules.
Statute	That notice is required to be affixed on the property.
Statute	Rule 8 deals with sale of immovable secured assets.
Statute	Appendix IV prescribes the form of possession notice.
Statute	It inter alia states that notice is given to the borrower who has failed to repay the amount informing him and the public that the bank/FI has taken possession of the property under Section 13(4) read with Rule 9 of the 2002 Rules.
Statute	Rule 9 relates to time of sale, issue of sale certificate and delivery of possession.
Statute	Rule 9(6) states that on confirmation of sale, if the  terms of payment are complied with, the authorised officer shall issue a sale certificate in favour of the purchaser in the form given in Appendix V to the 2002 Rules.
Statute	Rule 9(9) states that the authorised officer shall deliver the property to the buyer free from all encumbrances known to the secured creditor or not known to the secured creditor.
Statute	(emphasis supplied).
Statute	Section 14 of the NPA Act states that where the possession of any secured asset is required to be taken by the secured creditor or if any of the secured asset is required to be sold or transferred, the secured creditor may, for the purpose of taking possession, request in writing to the District Magistrate to take possession thereof.
Statute	Section 17(1) of NPA Act refers to right of appeal.
Statute	Section 17(3) states that if the <ORG> as an appellate authority after examining the facts and circumstances of the case comes to the conclusion that any of the measures under Section 13(4) taken by the secured creditor are not in accordance with the provisions of the  Act, it may by order declare that the recourse taken to any one or more measures is invalid, and consequently, restore possession to the borrower and can also restore management of the business of the borrower.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, the scheme of Section 13(4) read with Section 17(3) shows that if the borrower is dispossessed, not in accordance with the provisions of the Act, then the <ORG> is entitled to put the clock back by restoring the status quo ante.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, it cannot be said that if possession is taken before confirmation of sale, the rights of the borrower to get the dispute adjudicated upon is defeated by the authorised officer taking possession.
RatioOfTheDecision	As stated above, the NPA Act provides for recovery of possession by non-adjudicatory process, therefore, to say that the rights of the borrower would be defeated without adjudication would be erroneous.
RatioOfTheDecision	Rule 8, undoubtedly, refers to sale of immovable secured asset.
Statute	However, Rule 8(4) indicates that where possession is taken by the authorised officer before issuance  of sale certificate under Rule 9, the authorised officer shall take steps for preservation and protection of secured assets till they are sold or otherwise disposed of.
Statute	Under Section 13(8) , if the dues of the secured creditor together with all costs, charges and expenses incurred by him are tendered to the creditor before the date fixed for sale or transfer, the asset shall not be sold or transferred.
Statute	The costs, charges and expenses referred to in Section 13(8) will include costs, charges and expenses which the authorised officer incurs for preserving and protecting the secured assets till they are sold or disposed of in terms of Rule 8(4).
RatioOfTheDecision	Thus, Rule 8 deals with the stage anterior to the issuance of sale certificate and delivery of possession under Rule 9.
RatioOfTheDecision	Till the time of issuance of sale certificate, the authorised officer is like a court receiver under Order XL Rule 1 CPC.
RatioOfTheDecision	The court receiver can take symbolic possession and in appropriate cases where the court receiver finds that a third party interest  is likely to be created overnight, he can take actual possession even prior to the decree.
RatioOfTheDecision	The authorized officer under Rule 8 has greater powers than even a court receiver as security interest in the property is already created in favour of the banks/FIs.
RatioOfTheDecision	That interest needs to be protected.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, Rule 8 provides that till issuance of the sale certificate under Rule 9, the authorized officer shall take such steps as he deems fit to preserve the secured asset.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is well settled that third party interests are created overnight and in very many cases those third parties take up the defence of being a bona fide purchaser for value without notice.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is these types of disputes which are sought to be avoided by Rule 8 read with Rule 9 of the 2002 Rules.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the circumstances, the drawing of dichotomy between symbolic and actual possession does not find place in the scheme of the NPA Act read with the 2002 Rules.
RatioOfTheDecision	(iii) On Point No.
Argument	3, on question of court fee:  Whether ad valorem court fee prescribed under R ule 7 of the <ORG> (Procedure) Rules, 1993 is payable on an application under Section 17(1) of the NPA Act in the absence of any rule framed under the NPA Act .
Argument	Mr. <NAME> supplemented by Mr. <NAME>, learned advocates appearing on behalf of the borrower submitted that by virtue of the amending Act 30 of 2004 with effect from 11.11.2004, the persons aggrieved against the action of the bank or FI initiated under Section 13(4) of the NPA Act have a right to adjudication by way of an application to the <ORG> under Section 17(1) of the NPA Act.
Argument	It is submitted that in exercise of powers conferred under Section 40(1) of the NPA Act, <ORG> has issued an Order called the "Securitisation and Reconstruction of Financial Assets and Enforcement of Security Interest (Removal of Difficulties) Order, 2004 ("Order 2004") making the provision for levying of fees for filing of appeals.
Argument	This Order 2004 was issued on 6.4.2004.
Argument	It is further pointed out that on 8.4.2004, this Court delivered its judgment i n the case of <ORG> (supra).
Argument	Clause (3) of the Order 2004 provides that the fee for filing of an appeal to <ORG> under Section 17(1) of the NPA Act shall be mutatis mutandis as provided for filing of an application to <ORG> under Section 19 of the <ORG> Act read with Rule 7 of the Debts Recovery Tribunal (Procedure) Rules, 1993 ("1993 Rules").
Argument	Learned advocates urged that after the amending Act 30 of 2004 which came into force with effect from 11.11.2004 by which amendment was made to Section 17(1) of NPA Act, the Order 2004 dated 6.4.2004 issued by <ORG> has become redundant because the amending provision stipulates filing of an application by the borrower under Section 17(1) of NPA Act to the <ORG> challenging the action under Section 13(4) by filing an application along with payment of fees as may be prescribed.
Argument	Learned advocates submitted that under Section 17(1) of NPA Act, as amended, a proviso is added which states that different fees may be prescribed for making an application by t he borrower.
Argument	It is further submitted that the word "prescribed" has been defined under Section 2(s) to mean prescribed by rules made under the NPA Act .
Argument	It is urged that in the judgment of <ORG> (supra), this <ORG> held that the remedy under Section 17 of NPA Act is not an appellate remedy.
Argument	Clause (3) of the Order 2004 providing for fees for filing an appeal under the unamended provisions cannot, therefore, be made applicable to any application filed after 11.11.2004.
Argument	Learned advocates submitted that NPA Act vide Section 17(1) of NPA Act read with Rule 7 of the 1993 <ORG> under DRT Act cannot form the basis to claim ad valorem court fee in terms of Rule 7 of the 1993 <ORG>, particularly after 11.11.2004 because, as stated above, this <ORG> has held in <ORG> (supra) that the remedy under Section 17(1) of NPA Act is the original remedy and not an appellate remedy.
Argument	It is further submitted that after 11.11.2004, fees could be levied only vide <ORG> and not by an Order removing Difficulties.
RatioOfTheDecision	We do not find any merits in the above contentions, for the following reasons.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is true that Section 17(1) of the NPA Act states inter alia that a borrower aggrieved by action taken under Section 13(4) may make an application along with fees, as may be prescribed to the <ORG> having jurisdiction in the matter.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is true that, the marginal note states that Section 17(1) is a right to appeal.
RatioOfTheDecision	In our view, the marginal note to Section 17(1) cannot control the text and the content of Section 17(1) which, as stated above, states that the borrower aggrieved by any of the measures in Section 13(4) may make an application to the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	The judgment of this Court in <ORG> (supra) states that the <ORG> acts in an Original Jurisdiction under Section 17 of the NPA Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	In our opinion, as far as the levy of fee is concerned, the terminology makes no difference.
RatioOfTheDecision	In fact, the proviso to Section 17(1) indicates that different fees may be prescribed for making an application by the borrower.
RatioOfTheDecision	The reason is obvious.
RatioOfTheDecision	C ertain measures taken under Section 13(4) like taking over the management of the fee vis-a-vis the secured creditor taking possession of financial assets have to bear different fees.
RatioOfTheDecision	Each measure is required to be separately charged to the borrower (applicant) for which different fees could be prescribed.
RatioOfTheDecision	The said proviso indicates that the tribunal under Section 17(1) exercises Original Jurisdiction and, therefore, as far as the fees are concerned, the terminology of original or appellate jurisdiction in the context of fees is irrelevant.
RatioOfTheDecision	Secondly, under the Order 2004 issued by <ORG> under Section 40 of the NPA Act, it is provided that the fee for filing an appeal to the <ORG> under Section 17(1) of NPA Act shall be mutatis mutandis as provided for filing an application to the <ORG> under Rule 7 of the 1993 Rules.
RatioOfTheDecision	The word mutatis mutandis indicates that a measure is adopted for assessing the fees required to be paid by the borrower when he applies by way of application to the <ORG> under Section  17(1) of NPA Act challenging the action taken under Section 13(4) of NPA Act by the secured creditor.
RatioOfTheDecision	Lastly, we do not find any merit in the argument advanced on behalf of the borrowers that since fees have not been prescribed by the rules after 11.11.2004, fees cannot be levied on the basis of the Order 2004 which was there prior to 11.11.2004.
RatioOfTheDecision	The contention of the borrowers is that since Section 17(1) of NPA Act, as amended, provides for prescribing fees for an application under Section 17(1) and since no rule has been framed under the NPA Act after 11.11.2004 fees cannot be levied under the Order 2004 dated 6.4.2004 which, according to the borrower, has come to an end after 11.11.2004 with the enactment of the amending Act 30 of 2004.
RatioOfTheDecision	We do not find any merit in this last argument also.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the case of <CITATION_755547> .
Fact	reported in , one of the questions which arose for determination was whether <ORG> in the exercise of its power to remove difficulties under the Income Tax Act similar  to Section 40 of the NPA Act was competent to supply a deficiency in the Act.
Fact	Answering the above question, this Court held as follows:  "36.
Fact	This raises two questions: (1) Is this a 'difficulty' within the contemplation of clause (7) of the Regulation?
Fact	(2) Is <ORG> in the exercise of its power under that clause competent to supply a deficiency or casus omissus of this nature ?
Precedent	38.
Precedent	For a proper appreciation of the points involved, it is necessary to have a general idea of the nature and purpose of a "removal of difficulty clause" and the power conferred by it on the <ORG>.
Precedent	39.
RatioOfTheDecision	To keep pace with the rapidly increasing responsibilities of a welfare democratic State, the <ORG> has to turn out a plethora of hurried legislation, the volume of which is often matched with its complexity.
RatioOfTheDecision	Under conditions of extreme pressure, with heavy demands on the time of the <ORG> and the endurance and skill of the draftsman, it is well nigh impossible to foresee all the circumstances to deal  with which a statute is enacted or to anticipate all the difficulties that might arise in its working due to peculiar local conditions or even a local law.
RatioOfTheDecision	This is particularly true when <ORG> undertakes legislation which gives a new dimension to socio-economic activities of the <ORG> or extends the existing Indian laws to new territories or areas freshly merged in <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	In order to obviate the necessity of approaching the <ORG> for removal of every difficulty, howsoever trivial, encountered in the enforcement of a statute, by going through the time-consuming amendatory process, the legislature sometimes thinks it expedient to invest the <ORG> with a very limited power to make minor adaptations and peripheral adjustments in the statute, for making its implementation effective, without touching its substance.
RatioOfTheDecision	That is why the "removal of difficulty clause", once frowned upon and nick-named as "Henry VIII Clause" in scornful commemoration of the absolutist ways in which that English  <NAME> got the "difficulties" in enforcing his autocratic will removed through the instrumentality of a servile <ORG>, now finds acceptance as a practical necessity, in several Indian statutes of post independence era.
RatioOfTheDecision	40.
RatioOfTheDecision	Now let us turn to Clause (7) of the Regulation.
RatioOfTheDecision	It will be seen that the power given by it is not uncontrolled or unfettered.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is strictly circumscribed, and its use is conditioned and restricted.
RatioOfTheDecision	The existence or arising of a "difficulty" is the sine qua non for the exercise of the power.
RatioOfTheDecision	If this condition precedent is not satisfied as an objective fact, the power under this Clause cannot be invoked at all.
RatioOfTheDecision	Again, the '"difficulty" contemplated by the clause must be a difficulty arising in giving effect to the provisions of the Act and not a difficulty arising aliunde, or an extraneous difficulty.
RatioOfTheDecision	Further, <ORG> can exercise the power under the clause only to the extent it is necessary for applying or giving effect to the Act, etc., and no further.
RatioOfTheDecision	It may slightly  tinker with the Act to round off angularities, and smoothen the joints or remove minor obscurities to make it workable, but it cannot change, disfigure or do violence to the basic structure and primary features of the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	In no case, can it, under the guise of removing a difficulty, change the scheme and essential provisions of the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	41.
RatioOfTheDecision	The above principles, particularly the distinction between a 'difficulty' which falls within the purview of the Removal of Difficulty Clause and one which falls outside it, finds ample illustration in the 1949 Order and the impugned provision of the 1962 Order which came up for consideration in <ORG> case (1968) 2 SCR 1.
RatioOfTheDecision	Excepting the reference to the corresponding provision of the 1922 Act, the language of the 1949 Order was the same as that of the unimpugned part of clause (3) of Order 2 of 1970 in the present case.
RatioOfTheDecision	The 1949 Order related to the removal of a difficulty which had arisen in giving effect to the provisions of Section 10(2)(vi) Proviso (c) and Se ction 10(5)(b) of the 1922 Act, corresponding to Section 34(2)(i) and Section 43(6)(b) of the Act of 1961.
RatioOfTheDecision	This difficulty had arises because the income-tax laws of the merged States were not repealed by the Indian Income-tax Act but by the Taxation Laws (Extension to Merged States and Amendment) Act 67 of 1949.
RatioOfTheDecision	Owing to this, the depreciation actually allowed under the laws of the merged States could not be taken into account in computing the aggregate depreciation allowance referred to in sub-section (2)(vi), proviso (c) or the written down value under clause (b) of sub-section (5) of Section 10 of the 1922 Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	If this difficulty had not been removed, anomalous results would have followed.
RatioOfTheDecision	The written down value of the assets acquired before the previous year would have been taken as the original cost of the assets without deduction of the depreciation actually allowed in the past under the State laws.
RatioOfTheDecision	This would have given to the assessees in the merged States, a benefit, inconsistently with the scheme of  Section 10 of the 1922 Act, exceeding in the aggregate even the original cost of the assets.
RatioOfTheDecision	42.
RatioOfTheDecision	The 1949 Order removed this difficulty.
RatioOfTheDecision	In terms, it did no more than directing that if under the income-tax laws of a merged <ORG> any depreciation was actually allowed, it was to be taken into account in ascertaining the written down value of the assets.
RatioOfTheDecision	Far from supplanting or changing the essence of the essential provisions of the Act relating to depreciation and written- down value, it gave effect, life and meaning to them."
RatioOfTheDecision	In view of the above judgment of this Court in Madeva Upendra Sinai, we are of the view that the 2004 Order, in the present case, was issued with the object of supplying a deficiency, namely, levy of fees.
RatioOfTheDecision	By such levy of fees, the nature and scope of the NPA Act is not altered.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is not in dispute that the 2004 Order has been issued after the enactment of NPA Act .
RatioOfTheDecision	After the amending Act 30 of 2004, certain amendments have been made in Section 17(1) of NPA Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	However, the 2004 Orde r dated 6.4.2004 does not, in any way, alter the scheme of the amended Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	It merely fills in the deficiency and, therefore, the 2004 Order will continue to operate even after the amending Act 30 of 2004 and till rules are prescribed in terms of Section 2(s) of the NPA Act.
