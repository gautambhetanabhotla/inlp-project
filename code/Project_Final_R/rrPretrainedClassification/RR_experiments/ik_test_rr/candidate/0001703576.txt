Fact	1.
Fact	In this case, the petitioner <NAME> has moved <ORG> for the issue of a writ in the nature of certiorari to quash an order of <ORG> dated 23-7-1953 discharging the petitioner from <ORG> service as Probationary Sub-Deputy Collector.
Fact	2.
Fact	The petitioner <NAME> was appointed as a temporary Sub-Deputy Collector in the year 1944 and in 1946 the petitioner was vested with the powers of a first class Magistrate.
Fact	On 8-12-1947 the petitioner was appointed to a Substantive post in <ORG> and was placed on probation.
Fact	On 4-11-1952 <ORG> issued a notice to the petitioner asking him to show cause why his services should not be terminated.
Fact	The notice is annexure 'A' to the application.
Argument	In this notice the <ORG> say that the petitioner had earned notoriety as a corrupt officer while he was posted at Jamshedpore, and that his judicial work was carefully scrutinized by the <ORG> and in eigh t criminal cases the orders of acquittal passed by the petitioner were found to be perverse.
Argument	The notice further states that from May 1949 to March 1951 the petitioner was posted at Nawada and there also his reputation was bad.
Argument	The petitioner was transferred later on to Gaya and his work at Gaya was also found unsatisfactory.
Argument	The petitioner made a representation to the Government in response to the notice.
Argument	The petitioner denied the charges of corruption and bribery and requested the permission of the Government to inspect the records of the cases which he had decided at Jamshedpore.
Argument	The petitioner also prayed for an opportunity to cross-examine the Collector and the Commissioner who had sent adverse reports against him.
Argument	The petitioner further submitted that he should be given a copy of the confidential reports of the Collector and the Commissioner upon which the notice of the Government was based.
Argument	But <ORG> permitted the petitioner only to examine the records of the criminal cases which he h ad tried at Jamshedpore.
Argument	The <ORG> did not permit the petitioner to inspect the confidential reports sent by the Collector and the Commissioner and the <ORG> also declined the request of the petitioner that he should be permitted to lead evidence or to cross-examine the Collector and the Commissioner upon whose report the <ORG> had taken action.
Argument	On 23-7-1953 <ORG> passed an order terminating the services of the petitioner as a Probationary Sub-Deputy Collector.
Argument	It was alleged on behalf of the petitioner that <ORG> had no justification for withholding the confidential reports from being shown to the petitioner during the proceedings.
Argument	It is further alleged that the procedure contemplated by Article 311(2) of the Constitution was not followed by the <ORG> and the petitioner was entitled to a second notice in terms of the language employed in that article.
Argument	It was also submitted that the petitioner should have been allowed to examine witnesses on his behalf  and also to cross-examine the Collector and the Commissioner upon whose confidential reports the notice of discharge was based.
Argument	It was stated on petitioner's behalf that Rule 55 of the Civil Services (Classification, Control and Appeal) Rules applied to his case and the full procedure contemplated by that article should have been followed.
Argument	It was therefore argued that the order of discharge passed by the <ORG> dated the 23rd July 1953 was illegal and ultra vires on the ground of all these irregularities of procedure.
Argument	(3) The first submission made on petitioner's behalf is that the procedure contemplated by Rule 55 has not been followed and the order of discharge passed by the <ORG> was therefore illegal and void.
Argument	In support of his argument Mr. <NAME> referred to Rule 55 which states--  "Without prejudice to the provisions of the Public Servants Inquiries Act , 1850, no order of dismissal, removal or reduction shall be passed on a member of a Service (other than an order based on facts which h ave led to his conviction in a criminal Court) unless he has been informed in writing of the grounds on which it is proposed to take action, and has been afforded an adequate opportunity of defending himself.
Statute	The ground on which it is proposed to take action shall be reduced to the form of a definite charge or charges, which shall be communicated to the person charged together with a statement of the allegations on which each charge is based and of any other circumstances which it is proposed to take into consideration in passing orders on the case.
Statute	He shall be required, within a reasonable time, to put in a written statement of his defence and to state whether he desires to be heard in person.
Statute	If he so desires or if the authority concerned so direct, an oral inquiry shall be held.
Statute	At that inquiry, oral evidence shall be heard as to such of the allegations as are not admitted, and the person charged shall be entitled to cross-examine the witnesses, to give evidence in person and to have such witnesses called,  as he may wish, provided that the officer conducting the inquiry may, for special and sufficient reasons to be recorded in writing, refuse to call a witness.
RatioOfTheDecision	The proceedings shall contain a sufficient record of the evidence and a statement of the findings and the grounds thereof........"  Counsel submitted on the basis of this rule that the petitioner should have been heard in person, that the allegations should have been reduced to the form of definite charge or charges and that oral evidence should have been taken on behalf of the <ORG> and the petitioner should have been given opportunity to cross-examine the Collector or the Commissioner or the other persons who made confidential reports.
RatioOfTheDecision	In my opinion the argument addressed on behalf of the petitioner is not well founded.
Fact	Rule 55 was amended on 15-11-1947 by notification No.
Statute	8184-A of the <ORG> of Bihar.
Statute	Rule 49 was also amended by the same notification.
Statute	Before the amendment Rule 55 did not apply to the case of a probationer for the explanatio n of Rule 49 stated that the discharge of a person appointed on probation would not amount to removal or dismissal within the meaning of that rule.
Statute	But Rule 49 was amended on 15-11-1947 by the notification, already referred to, and an explanation was added to Rule 49 to the following effect:  "The discharge of a probationer, whether during or at the end of the period of probation, for some specific fault or on account of his unsuitability for the service, amounts to removal or dismissal within the meaning of this rule."
Statute	At the same time, the following sub-paragraph was added to Rule 55:  "The full procedure prescribed in this rule need not be followed in the case of a probationer discharged in the circumstances described in explanation II to Rule 49.
Statute	In such cases, it will be sufficient if the probationer is given an opportunity to show cause in writing against the discharge after being apprised of the grounds on which it is proposed to discharge him and his reply duly considered before orders are passed."
RatioOfTheDecision	It is clear in view of the amendments of Rules 49 and 55 that the petitioner was not entitled to a full inquiry as contemplated by the first paragraph of Rule 55.
RatioOfTheDecision	The argument of Mr. <NAME> on this point must therefore be rejected.
RatioOfTheDecision	4.
RatioOfTheDecision	The next submission made by the learned counsel for the petitioner is that in any event the confidential reports upon which the notice of discharge was made should have been given to the petitioner.
RatioOfTheDecision	It was contended that the petitioner should have been given a fair opportunity of meeting the allegations before the government decided to pass the orders of discharge.
RatioOfTheDecision	It was argued that there was a violation of natural justice in this case and the order of discharge must therefore be quashed by a writ of certiorari.
RatioOfTheDecision	In my opinion the argument addressed by the counsel is correct It appears from Annexure "A" of the application that the notice of discharge was based upon certain reports received by the <ORG> while the petitioner was employed as Sub-Deputy Collector at Jams hedpur.
RatioOfTheDecision	Annexure "C" also states that "confidential enquiries were made and it was found that while employed at Jamshedpur Mr. <NAME> had the reputation of being a corrupt officer".
RatioOfTheDecision	It was argued by the Government Pleader on behalf of the respondent that the annual confidential reports for the years 1949 to 1952 were communicated to the petitioner and in support of his contention the Government Pleader referred to annexure 'D'.
RatioOfTheDecision	It may be true to say that the annual confidential reports were communicated to the petitioner bat the notice of discharge dated 4-11-1952 is not based merely on the annual confidential <ORG> but also on certain inquiries made at Jamshedpur and the confidential reports submitted by the officers making these inquiries.
RatioOfTheDecision	The complaint of the learned counsel for the petitioner is that these special confidential reports were not made available to the petitioner before he was asked to show cause against the notice of discharge.
RatioOfTheDecision	The Government Pleader produced during the heari ng of this case the original record of the proceedings in the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	It appears from these proceedings that the order of discharge was passed by the Government not merely upon the annual confidential reports of the Collector and the Commissioner but also upon two special reports made by the Commissioner of Chotanagpur Division dated 31-5-1952 and by the Deputy Inspector General, <ORG>, dated 16-6-1951.
RatioOfTheDecision	These two special reports contain matters which are highly relevant and prejudicial to the petitioner and in my opinion, these reports should have been shown to the petitioner or their contents communicated to him before he was asked to show cause against the notice of discharge.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is a matter of general principle that a person should not be condemned on ex parte statements and no order of removal or discharge should be passed against a Government servant unless he has been given a real and effective opportunity of refuting the statements upon which his notice of discharge is  based.
RatioOfTheDecision	This follows from the principle of law embodied in the maxim 'audi alteram partem'.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is true that the final order of discharge of a probationer is an administrative act but the proceedings leading to that final order are quasi judicial in character, and the principle embodied in the maxim 'audi alteram partem' therefore applies to such proceedings.
Precedent	This view is borne out by the judgment of Lord <NAME> in -- ' <CITATION_1798267> ', (1911) AC 179 (A).
Precedent	"Comparatively recent statutes have extended if they have not originated, the practice of imposing upon departments or officers of <ORG> the duty or deciding or determining questions of various kinds.
Precedent	In the present instance as in many others, what comes for determination is sometimes a matter to be settled by discretion involving no law.
Precedent	It will, I suppose, usually be of an administrative kind; but sometimes it will involve matter of law as well as matter of fact, or even depend upon matter of law alone.
Precedent	In such cases <ORG> will have  to ascertain the law and also to ascertain the facts, I need not add that in doing either they must act in good faith and fairly listen to both sides, for that it is a duty lying upon every one who decides anything.
Precedent	But I do not think they are bound to treat such a question as though it were a trial.
Precedent	They have no power to administer an oath, and need not examine witnesses.
Precedent	They can obtain information in any way they think best always giving a fair opportunity to those who are parties in the controversy for correcting or contradicting any relevant statement prejudicial to their view."
Precedent	The principle has been reiterated by <ORG> in--'Errington v. Minister of Health', (1935) 1 KB 249 (B).
Precedent	In that case a local authority had made a clearance order under Section 1 of the Housing Act, 1930, and had submitted the order to the Minister of Health for confirmation.
Precedent	Objections were made by the owners.
Precedent	The Act required that a public inquiry should be held and the Minister should consider the objections made  by the owners and the report of the person who held the inquiry before he passed the order of confirmation.
Precedent	It was held by <ORG> that in deciding whether or not he shall confirm the clearance order the Minister occupied a quasi judicial function and must not take into consideration any matters other than those mentioned in paragraph 4 of the first schedule of the Act, If the Minister holds a private inquiry to which the owners are not invited or takes into consideration ex parte statements with which the owners have had no opportunity of dealing he was not acting in accordance with correct principles of justice and his confirmation of the clearance order would be ultra vires and the owners would be entitled to get the confirmation order quashed by a writ of certiorari.
Precedent	To the same effect also is the decision of <ORG> in -- 'R.
Precedent	v. Architects' Registration Tribunal', (1945) 2 All ER 131 (C).
Precedent	<ORG> has also laid down a similar principle in -- ' <CITATION_1461059 > ', AIR 1955 SC 65 (D).
Precedent	It was observed by <NAME> in that case that the <ORG> had violated the fundamental rule of justice firstly by not disclosing to the assessee what information had been supplied to it by the <ORG> representative, and secondly by not giving an opportunity to the assessee to rebut the material furnished to it by the <ORG>.
Precedent	It was held for these reasons that the assessee had not obtained a fair hearing, and the assessment made by the Income-tax authorities and affirmed by the <ORG> should be set aside.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is clear in the present case that the petitioner was not shown the special reports of the Commissioner of Chotanagpur Division and of the Deputy Inspector General, <ORG> and there has been a violation of the principle of natural justice.
RatioOfTheDecision	It follows that the order of discharge made by the <ORG> dated 23-7-1953 is illegal and ultra vires.
RatioOfTheDecision	5.
RatioOfTheDecision	The next question presented for determination is whether the petitioner should have been given  a second notice under Article 311(2) of the Constitution before the final order of discharge was passed against him.
Argument	It was argued on behalf of the petitioner that even though the amended Rule 55 contemplated only a summary inquiry in the case of a probationer officer, the provision of Article 311 (2) of the Constitution required that a second notice should be given before the final order of discharge was made.
Argument	It was however pointed out on behalf of the respondents that a probationer was always liable to be discharged in case his conduct was not satisfactory and there was no necessity of giving a second notice as contemplated by Article 311 (2) of the Constitution.
RatioOfTheDecision	The point is not free from difficulty but upon the whole I am satisfied that the petitioner is entitled to a second notice after summary inquiry had been made into the allegations of bribery and corruption made in the first notice.
Precedent	This view is based upon the interpretation placed by <ORG> upon the language of Section 240(3) of t he Government of India Act in --'High Commr.
Precedent	for <CITATION_134248> ', AIR 1948 PC 121 (E).
Precedent	Section 240(3) of the Government of India Act was in the following terms:  "(1) Except as expressly provided by this Act, every person who is a member of <ORG> in India, holds office during His Majesty's pleasure............. (3) No such person as aforesaid shall be dismissed................until he has been given a reasonable opportunity of showing cause against the action proposed to be taken in regard to him..........."  It should be noticed that Section 240(3) of the Government of India Act was couched in the same language as Article 311(2) of the Constitution.
Precedent	It was held by <ORG> in I, M. <NAME>'s case (E) that no action was proposed within the meaning of Section 240(3) until the authority had come to a definite conclusion upon the charges brought against a civil servant and the actual punishment to follow was provisionally determined upon.
RatioOfTheDecision	Before that stage was reached, the c harges were unproved and the suggested punishments were merely hypothetical.
RatioOfTheDecision	It was on that stage being reached that the Act gave the civil servant the opportunity for which Sub-section (3) made provision.
RatioOfTheDecision	At page 242 of the report Lord <NAME> states:  "Their Lordships agree with the view taken by the majority of <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	In their opinion, Sub-section (3) of the Section 240 was not intended to be, and was not a re-production of Rule 55 which was left unaffected as an administrative rule.
RatioOfTheDecision	Rule 55 is concerned that the civil servant shall be informed of the grounds on which it is proposed to take action', and to afford him an adequate opportunity of defending himself against charges which have to be reduced to writing; this is in marked contrast to the statutory provisions of 'a reasonable opportunity of showing cause against the action proposed to be taken in regard to him'.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the opinion of their Lordships, no action is proposed within the meaning of the sub-section until a definite conclusion  has been come to on the charges, and the actual punishment to follow is provisionally determined on.
RatioOfTheDecision	Before that stage, the charges are unproved and the suggested punishments are merely hypothetical.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is on that stage being reached that the statute gives the civil servant the opportunity for which Sub-section (3) makes provision.
RatioOfTheDecision	Their Lordships would only add that they see no difficulty in the statutory opportunity being reasonably afforded at more than one stage.
RatioOfTheDecision	If the civil servant has been through an inquiry under Rule 55, it would not be reasonable that he should ask for a repetition of that stage, if duly carried out, but that would not exhaust his statutory right, and he would still be entitled to represent against the punishment proposed as the result of the findings of the inquiry."
RatioOfTheDecision	It was pointed out by the Government Pleader in this case that a probationer was not entitled to a full inquiry contemplated by the first paragraph of Rule 55 of the Civil Services (Classification, Control and Appeal ) Rules.
RatioOfTheDecision	I do not think that the argument has any bearing on the interpretation of Article 311(2).
RatioOfTheDecision	In the case of a probationer the inquiry contemplated by Rule 55 is a summary inquiry but nevertheless Article 311(2) of the Constitution applies to the case for the explanation to the amended Rule 49 states that "the discharge of a probationer for some specific fault or on account of his unsuitability for the service, amounted to removal or dismissal within the meaning of this rule."
RatioOfTheDecision	Article 311(2) of the Constitution therefore applies to this case and a probationer is entitled under that article to a second notice requiring him to show cause against the action to be taken, viz., against the order of discharge from Government service.
Precedent	A similar view of the law has been expressed by <ORG> of <ORG> in --'Tribhuwannath v. <ORG>', AIR 1953 Nag 138 (F) and -- 'M.
Precedent	A. Waheed v. State of Madhya Pradesh', AIR 1954 Nag 229 (G).
RatioOfTheDecision	In my opinion, the petitioner was entitled t o a second notice after the <ORG> had reached the finding that the allegations were proved against the petitioner, and in the absence of such a notice it must be held that the final order of discharge passed on 23-7-1953 is void and inoperative.
RatioOfTheDecision	6.
RatioOfTheDecision	In view of these considerations I hold that a writ in the nature of certiorari should be issued to quash the order of <ORG> dated 23-7-1953 discharging the petitioner from <ORG> service.
RatioOfTheDecision	I would accordingly allow this application.

RatioOfTheDecision	The question whether two notices have to be given to a probationer before he can be discharged is one of considerable difficulty.
RatioOfTheDecision	A view that can reasonably be taken is that two notices need not be given to such an officer.
RatioOfTheDecision	8.
RatioOfTheDecision	As my learned brother has pointed out, Rules 49 and 55 of the Civil Services (Classification, Control and Appeal) Rules were amended by Notification No.
Statute	8184-A, dated 15-11-1947.
Statute	Before this amendment, the explanation to Rule 49 provided that the discharge of a pers on during the period of probation did not amount to removal or dismissal within the meaning of the rule.
Statute	Rule 55, as it then stood, did not, therefore, apply to a person on probation.
Statute	By the amendment, the explanation to Rule 49 has been renumbered as explanation I and Explanation II has been added.
RatioOfTheDecision	This explanation provides that the discharge of a probationer even during the period of probation "for some specific fault or on account of his unsuitability for the service" amounts to removal or dismissal within the meaning of this rule.
RatioOfTheDecision	The third sub-paragraph which has been added to Rule 55 by the amendment lays down that the full procedure prescribed in the first sub-paragraph of that Rule need not be followed in the case of a probationer, and that in the case of a probationer who is to be discharged in the circumstances described in explanation II to Rule 49, "it will be sufficient if the probationer is given an opportunity to show cause in writing against the discharge after being apprised of the grounds on  which it is proposed to discharge him......."  9.AIR 1948 PC 121 (E) was the case of a confirmed officer.
RatioOfTheDecision	Their Lordships of <ORG> were concerned in that case with Section 240 (3) of the Government of India Act, and what is now the first sub-paragraph of Rule 55.
RatioOfTheDecision	The language in Article 311(2) of the Constitution is the same as used in Section 240(3) , Government of India Act, 1935. he present sub-paragraph 1 of Rule 55 clearly Contemplates the issue of a notice to an officer and aft open enquiry in his presence after he has put in a written statement of his defence.
RatioOfTheDecision	Such a notice was given to <NAME>.
RatioOfTheDecision	Their Lordships held m view of the words used in Section 240(3) of the Government of India Act that a second notice was necessary after the enquiry had been concluded and, the charges having been proved, dismissal of the officer had been provisionally decided upon.
RatioOfTheDecision	There is no doubt that even under Article 311(2) of the Constitution, a notice has to be given to an officer when it is provisi onally decided that he should be dismissed or removed.
RatioOfTheDecision	It may be argued with considerable force, however, that the notice contemplated under the third sub-paragraph of Rule 55 as added by the notification referred to above is also a notice which has to be given after the discharge of the probationer has been provisionally decided upon.
RatioOfTheDecision	This appears to follow from the fact that sub-paragraph (3) of Rule 55 requires a notice to be given "to show cause in writing against the discharge."
RatioOfTheDecision	The words "after being apprised of the grounds on which it is proposed to discharge him" as used in that sub-paragraph strongly tend to show that discharge of the probationer must be provisionally decided upon before notice is given.
RatioOfTheDecision	It may, therefore, be reasonably concluded that the notice has to be issued under both the provisions at the same stage and that one notice will, therefore, be sufficient.
