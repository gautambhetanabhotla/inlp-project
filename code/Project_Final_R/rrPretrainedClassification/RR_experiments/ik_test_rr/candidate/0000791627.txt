RatioOfTheDecision	It is well-settled that if an earlier legislation is incorporated into a later legislation, the provisions of earlier law which are incorporated into the later law become a part and parcel of the later law.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore,  amendments made in the earlier law after the date of incorporation cannot, by their own force, be read into the later law.
RatioOfTheDecision	That is because the legislature, which adopts by incorporation the existing provisions of another law, cannot be assumed to intend to bind itself to all future amendments or modifications which may be made in the earlier law.
RatioOfTheDecision	In other words, the incorporating Act does nothing more than borrow certain provisions of an existing Act and instead of setting out, verbatim, those provisions in its own creation, refers to them as a matter of convenience in the mode of drafting.
RatioOfTheDecision	(See Secretary of <ORG> for India in <CITATION_183510> ; Craies on Statute Law, 7th Edition, pages 360-361.)
RatioOfTheDecision	The principle, broadly, is that where a statute is incorporated by reference into a second statute, the repeal of the first statute by a third does not affect the second (see <NAME> v. <NAME>).
RatioOfTheDecision	Likewise, logically, where certain provisions from an existing Act have been incorporated into a subsequent Act, no addi tion to the former Act, which is not expressly made applicable to the subsequent Act, can be deemed to be incorporated in it.
RatioOfTheDecision	(see Secretary of <ORG> for India in <CITATION_183510> ).
RatioOfTheDecision	(supra) But these rules are not absolute and inflexible.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the case last cited, <ORG> qualified its statement of the law by saying that the principle, that an amendment of the first law which is not expressly made applicable to the subsequent incorporating Act cannot be deemed to be incorporated into the second Act, applies "if it is possible for the subsequent Act to function effectually without the addition" (page 267).
RatioOfTheDecision	Besides, as held by <ORG> of this Court in the <CITATION_1193965> .
RatioOfTheDecision	the decision of <ORG> could not be extended too far so as to cover every case in which the provisions of another statute are adopted by absorption (see page 837).
RatioOfTheDecision	Finally, in <CITATION_1721191> this Court held, after an examination of the relevant decisions, that the broad principle that where a subsequen t <ORG> incorporates provisions of a previous <ORG> then the borrowed provisions become an integral and independent part of the subsequent <ORG> and are totally unaffected by any repeal or amendment in the previous <ORG>, is subject to four exceptions, one of which is that the principle will not apply to cases "where the subsequent <ORG> and the previous <ORG> are supplemental to each other".
RatioOfTheDecision	Applying these principles, we are of the opinion that in the instant case, subsequent amendments made to the Municipal Corporation <ORG> and the Municipalities <ORG> will also apply to the power of taxation provided for in section 69(d) of the <ORG> of 1973.
Statute	The <ORG> of 1973 did not, by section 69(d) , incorporate in its true signification any particular provision of the two earlier <ORG>s.
Statute	It provides that, for the purpose of taxation, <ORG> shall have the powers which <ORG> or <ORG> has under the Madhya Pradesh Municipal Corporation <ORG>, 1956 or the Madhya Pradesh Municipali ties Act, 1961.
RatioOfTheDecision	The case therefore is not one of incorporation but of mere reference to the powers conferred by the earlier <ORG>.
Statute	As observed in Nathella Sampathu Chetty, there is a distinction between a mere reference to or a citation of one statute in another and an incorporation which in effect means the bodily lifting of the provisions of one enactment and making them part of another, so much so that the repeal of the former leaves the latter wholly untouched.
Statute	14  Section 69(d) of the Act of 1973 must accordingly be read to mean that respondent 1 shall have all the powers of taxation which <ORG> or <ORG> has for the time being, that is to say, at the time when respondent 1 seeks to exercise those powers.
Statute	The Act of 1973 does not provide for any independent power of taxation or any machinery of its own for exercising the power of taxation.
Statute	It rests content by pointing its finger to the provisions contained in the two Municipal <ORG>.
Statute	The three <ORG> are therefore supplement al, from which it must follow that amendments made to the earlier <ORG> after the enactment of section 69(d) shall have to be read into that section.
Statute	Without recourse to such a construction, the power of taxation conferred by that section will become ineffectual.
RatioOfTheDecision	A reading of the reference to the two earlier Municipal <ORG> as a reference to those <ORG> as they stand at the time when the power of taxation is sought to be exercised by respondent 1, will not, possibly, cause repugnancy between the two earlier <ORG> on one hand and the Act of 1973 on the other, nor indeed will it cause any confusion in the practical application of the earlier <ORG>, because the Act of 1973 does not contain any independent provision or machinery for exercising the power of taxation.
RatioOfTheDecision	The first contention of the Attorney General must therefore fail.
RatioOfTheDecision	The second contention is that assuming that section 127A of the Municipalities Act or section 135 of the Municipal Corporation Act, which were introduced by an amendment made after the enac tment of section 69(d) , can be invoked for levying the property tax, respondent 1 cannot impose that tax without following the procedure prescribed by sections 129 and 133 of the aforesaid Acts, respectively.
RatioOfTheDecision	This contention is devoid of substance.
RatioOfTheDecision	Sections 127A and 135 create, by their own force, the liability to be brought to property tax and the right to levy that tax.
Statute	They provide:  Notwithstanding anything contained in this chapter, as and from the financial year 1976-77, there shall be charged, levied and paid for each financial year a tax on the lands or buildings or both.......at the rate specified in the table below:"  Nothing further is required to be done by the <ORG> or <ORG> in order to impose the property tax and therefore the procedure preliminary to the imposition of other taxes which is prescribed by sections 129 and 133 of the two Acts, can have no application to the imposition of the property tax.
RatioOfTheDecision	Apart from this the position is put beyond doubt by the language o f sections 129 and 133 of two <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 129 of the Madhya Pradesh Municipalities <ORG> prescribes the procedure for "the imposition of any tax under section 127 ".
Statute	Similarly section 133 of the Madhya Pradesh Municipal Corporation <ORG> prescribes the procedure for "the imposition of any tax under section 132 ".
Statute	The property tax is imposed by respondent 1 under section 127A of the Municipalities <ORG> and section 135 of the Municipal Corporation <ORG>.
Statute	It is not imposed under section 127 of the former <ORG> or section 132 of the latter <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is therefore not necessary to follow the procedure prescribed by sections 129 and 133 of the respective <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	This position is made clear, out of abundant caution, by clause (4) of section 133 of the Municipal Corporation <ORG>, which provides that nothing contained in section 133 shall apply to the tax mentioned in clause (a) of sub-section (1) of section 132 , which shall be charged and levied in accordance with section 135 .
Statute	Section 132(1)(a) refers to property tax.
Argument	The lear ned Attorney General contends that the taxing authority must all the same apply its mind to the question whether it wants to bring to tax the land or the building or both.
Argument	It is not possible to accept this submission because sections 127A and 135 of the two Acts in question leave no such choice open to the taxing authority.
RatioOfTheDecision	The obligation which the statute places upon it is to impose tax on lands where there are lands only and they can be taxed, on buildings where buildings alone can be brought to tax and on both lands and buildings where lands are built upon and both can be brought to tax.
RatioOfTheDecision	This is not, as said by the Attorney General rationalising the taxing power.
RatioOfTheDecision	What we have said is the plain meaning of the taxing provision.
Argument	The third contention of the Attorney General flows from the provisions of article 285(1) of the Constitution which says that the property of the <ORG> shall, save in so far as <ORG> may by law otherwise provide, be exempt from all taxes imposed by a <ORG> or by any authority wit hin a State.
Argument	Section 127A(2) of the Madhya Pradesh Municipalities Act and section 136 of the Madhya Pradesh Municipal Corporation Act also provide that the property tax shall not be leviable, inter alia, on "buildings and lands owned by or vesting in <ORG>".
Argument	Relying on these provisions, it is contended by the Attorney General that since the appellant companies are wholly owned by <ORG>, the lands and buildings owned by the companies cannot be subjected to property tax.
Argument	The short answer to this contention is that even though the entire share capital of the appellant companies has been subscribed by <ORG>, it cannot be predicated that the companies themselves are owned by <ORG>.
Argument	The companies, which are incorporated under the Companies Act , have a corporate personality of their own, distinct from that of <ORG>.
Argument	The lands and buildings are vested in and owned by the companies: <ORG> only owns the share capit al.
Argument	In <NAME> v. <ORG> (The Banks Nationalisation case) it was held:  "A company registered under the Companies Act is a legal person, separate and distinct from its individual members.
Argument	Property of the <ORG> is not the property of the shareholders.
Argument	A shareholder has merely an interest in the <ORG> arising under its Articles of Association, measured by a sum of money for the purpose of liability, and by a share in the profit."
Argument	In <ORG> v. The State of Bihar & Ors., <ORG> was incorporated under the Companies Act and its entire share capital was contributed by <ORG>.
Argument	It was therefore a Government <ORG> under section 617 of the Companies Act.
Argument	On the question as to whether the <ORG> carried on an industry under the authority of <ORG> within the meaning of section 2(a) of the Industrial Disputes Act, 1947, it was held by this Court that an incorporated company has a separate existenc e and the law recognises it as a juristic person, separate and distinct from its members.
Argument	The mere fact that the entire share capital of the respondent company was contributed by <ORG> and the fact that all its shares were held by the President and certain officers of <ORG> did not make any difference to that position.
Argument	The decision of this <ORG> in the <CITATION_64804> .
Argument	puts the matter beyond all doubt.
Argument	In that case, <ORG> claimed exemption from taxation under article 289 of the Constitution by which, the property and income of a <ORG> is exempt from union taxation.
Argument	This <ORG>, while rejecting the <ORG>'s claim, held that though it was wholly controlled by the <ORG> Government it had a separate entity and its income was not the income of the <ORG> Government.
Argument	<NAME>, C. J., while speaking for the <ORG>, referred to the judgment of Lord <NAME> in <NAME> v. <NAME> in which the learned Judge observed:  "In the eye of th e law, the corporation is its own master and is answerable as fully as any other person or corporation.
Argument	It is not the <ORG> and has none of the immunities or privileges of the <ORG>.
Argument	Its servants are not civil servants, and its property is not <ORG> property.
Argument	It is as much bound by Acts of Parliament as any other subject of the King.
Argument	It is, of course, a public authority and its purposes, no doubt, are public purposes, but it is not a government department nor do its powers fall within the province of government".
Argument	In Pennington's Company Law, 4th Edition, pages 50-51, it is stated that there are only two decided cases where the court has disregarded the separate legal entity of a company and that was done because the company was formed or used to facilitate the evasion of legal obligations.
Argument	The learned author, after referring to English and American decisions, has summed up the position in the words of an American Judge, <NAME>, J. to the effect that as a general rule, a corporation will be looked upon as a  legal entity and an exception can be made "when the notion of legal entity is used to defeat public convenience, justify wrong, protect fraud, or defend crime", in which case, "the law will regard the corporation as an association of persons".
RatioOfTheDecision	In cases such as those before us, there is no scope for applying the doctrine of lifting the veil in order to have regard to the realities of the situation.
RatioOfTheDecision	The appellant companies were incorporated under the Companies Act for a lawful purpose.
RatioOfTheDecision	Their property is their own and it vests in them.
RatioOfTheDecision	Under section 5(1) of the Coal Mines (Nationalisation) Act, 26 of 1973, which applies in the instant case, the right title and interest of a nationalised coal mine vest, by direction of <ORG>, in the Government company.
RatioOfTheDecision	If the lands and building on which respondent 1 has imposed the property tax cannot be regarded as the property of <ORG> for several other purposes like attachment and sale, there is no reason why, for taxing purposes, the property  can be treated as belonging to that Government as distinct from the company which has a juristic personality.
Argument	The learned Attorney General resisted the taxation on the lands by contending that they belong to <ORG> and were taken on lease for a period of 30 years by the appellant companies.
Argument	It is urged that if at all the lands can be subjected to property tax, it is <ORG> and not the appellant companies who can be called upon to pay that tax.
RatioOfTheDecision	This contention does not appear to have been taken before the assessing authority.
RatioOfTheDecision	No documents seem to have been filed before it to bear out facts which are sought to be placed before us nor indeed have we evidence before us to show that the lands belong to <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	The appellants may, if so advised, raise this particular point in future assessments.
RatioOfTheDecision	We would, however, like to draw attention to the Explanation to section 147 of the Madhya Pradesh Municipalities Act which says that though the property tax has to  be paid by the owner of the land or building, as the case may be, for the purposes of that section a tenant of land or building or both, who holds the same under a lease for an agreed period with a convenant for its renewal thereafter, shall be deemed to be the owner thereof.
Fact	Section 141(1) of the Madhya Pradesh Municipal Corporation Act provides that the property tax shall be paid primarily by the owner.
Fact	By sub-section (2) of section 141 , the property tax levied on the owner can also be recovered from the occupier of the land or the building.
Fact	These provisions shall have to be borne in mind by the appellants before any attempt is made before the assessing authority to transfer or avoid the impost of the property tax.
Fact	Finally, the learned Attorney General raised a contention of fundamental importance which was not raised in <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	The lands and buildings on which respondent 1 has imposed the property tax are used for the purposes of and are covered by coal mines.
Argument	Basing himself on that consideration  the Attorney General argues:  (1) By virtue of the declaration contained in section 2 of Mines and Minerals (Development and Regulation) Act , 1957, the legislative field covered by Entry 23, List II passed on the <ORG> by virtue of Entry 54, List I.
Statute	(2) The <ORG> enacted the Coal Mines Nationalisation Act , 1973 for acquisition of coal mines with a view to reorganising and reconstructing such coal mines so as to ensure the rational, coordinated and scientific development and utilisation of coal resources as best to subserve the common good.
Fact	(3) Under section 5 of the Nationalisation Act, the acquired properties were vested in a Government Company in order to carry out more conveniently the object of that Act, and for that purpose, the properties were freed from all encumbrances by section 6 of the Act.
Fact	(4) The taxing power of the State legislature must be construed as limited in its scope so as not to come in conflict with the power and function of the <ORG> to regulate and develop the mines as  envisaged by the Nationalisation Act .
RatioOfTheDecision	(5) The impugned tax is manifestly an impediment in the discharge of the aforesaid function since it substantially increases the cost of the developmental activities.
RatioOfTheDecision	The tax is not in the nature of a fee.
RatioOfTheDecision	Apart from the fact that there is no data before us showing that the property tax constitutes an impediment in the achievement of the goals of the Coal Mines Nationalisation Act , the provisions of the M.P.
RatioOfTheDecision	Act of 1973, under which <ORG> are constituted afford an effective answer to the Attorney General's contention.
Statute	Entry 23 of List II relates to "Regulation of mines and mineral development subject to the provisions of List I with respect to regulation and development under the control of the <ORG>".
Statute	Entry 54 of List I relates to "Regulation of mines and mineral development to the extent to which such regulation and development under the control of the <ORG> is declared by <ORG> by law to be expedient in the publ ic interest".
Statute	It is true that on account of the declaration contained in section 2 of the Mines and Minerals (Development and Regulation) Act , 1957, the legislative field covered by Entry 23 of List II will pass on to <ORG> by virtue of Entry 54, List I.
Statute	But in order to judge whether, on that account, the State legislature loses its competence to pass the Act of 1973, it is necessary to have regard to the object and purpose of that Act and to the relevant provisions thereof, under which <ORG> are given the power to tax lands and buildings within their jurisdiction.
RatioOfTheDecision	We have set out the objects of the Act at the commencement of this judgment.
RatioOfTheDecision	one of which is to provide for the development and administration of Special Areas through <ORG>, Section 64 of the Act of 1973, which provides for the constitution of the special areas, lays down by sub-section (4) that: Notwithstanding anything contained in the Madhya Pradesh Municipal Corporation Act, 1 956, the Madhya Pradesh Municipalities Act, 1961, or the Madhya Pradesh <ORG>s Act, 1962, the Municipal Corporation, <ORG>, <ORG> or a <ORG>, as the case may be, shall, in relation to the special area and as from the date <ORG> undertakes the functions under clause  (v) or clause (vi) of section 68 cease to exercise the powers and perform the function and duties which <ORG> is competent to exercise and perform under the Act of 1973.
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 68 defines the functions of <ORG>, one of which, as prescribed by clause (v), is to provide the municipal services as specified in sections 123 and 124 of the Madhya Pradesh Municipalities Act, 1961.
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 69 , which defines the powers of the <ORG>, shows that those powers are conferred, inter alia, for the purpose of municipal administration.
RatioOfTheDecision	Surely, the functions, powers and duties of Municipalities do not become an occupied field  by reason of the declaration contained in section 2 of the Mines and Minerals (Development and Regulation) Act , 1957.
RatioOfTheDecision	Though, therefore, on account of that declaration, the legislative field covered by Entry 23, List II may pass on to the <ORG> by virtue of Entry 54, List I, the competence of <ORG> to enact laws for municipal administration will remain unaffected by that declaration.
RatioOfTheDecision	Entry 5 of List II relates to "Local Government, that is to say, the constitution and powers of municipal corporations and other local authorities for the purpose of local self-Government".
RatioOfTheDecision	It is in pursuance of this power that <ORG> enacted the Act of 1973.
RatioOfTheDecision	The power to impose tax on lands and buildings is derived by <ORG> from Entry 49 of List II: "Taxes on lands and buildings".
RatioOfTheDecision	The power of the municipalities to levy tax on lands and buildings has been conferred by <ORG> on <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	Those authorities have the power to  levy that tax in order effectively to discharge the municipal functions which are passed on to them.
RatioOfTheDecision	Entry 54 of List I does not contemplate the taking over of municipal functions.
RatioOfTheDecision	<NAME>, who appears on behalf of the respondents, has drawn our attention to the judgment of <ORG> of this Court in <CITATION_21773> , which provides a complete answer to the Attorney General's contention.
RatioOfTheDecision	In that case, under the terms of a mining lease, the lessee worked the mines and bound himself to pay a dead rent if he used the leased land for the extraction of iron ore and to pay surface rent in respect of the surface area occupied or used by him.
RulingByLowerCourt	Demands were made upon the lessee for successive years for the payment of land cess under sections 78 and 79 of the Madras District Boards Act, 1920.
RulingByLowerCourt	Those demands were challenged by the lessee on the ground, inter alia, that the provision imposing the land cess quoad royalty under the mining leases must be held to have been repealed by the Central Act v iz.
RulingByLowerCourt	the Mines and Minerals (Regulation and Development) Act , 1958, and the Mines and Minerals (Regulation and Development) Act , 1957.
RulingByLowerCourt	This contention was repelled by this <ORG> by holding that sections 78 and 79 of the Madras District Boards Act had nothing to do with the development of mines and minerals or their regulation.

RulingByLowerCourt	It was further held by the <ORG> that the land cess was not a tax on mineral rights but was in truth and substance a "tax on lands" within the meaning of Entry 49 of the State List.

RatioOfTheDecision	The decision of this Court in <CITATION_1752601> ., on which the learned Attorney General relies, is distinguishable.
RatioOfTheDecision	In that case, <ORG> demanded dead rent, royalty and surface rent from the appellant contrary to the terms of his lease on the strength of the amended section 10(2) of the Bihar Land Reforms Act, 1950, and the amended Rule 20 of the Bihar Rules.

RatioOfTheDecision	By the aforesaid declaration and the enactment of section 15 , the whole of the field relating to minor minerals had come within the jurisdiction of <ORG> and no scope was left for the enactment of the second proviso to section 10 of the Bihar Land Reforms Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	The second sub-rule added to Rule 20 was held to be without jurisdiction for the same reason.
RatioOfTheDecision	That the declaration in section 2 of the Mines and Minerals (Regulation and Development) Act , 1957 does not result in invalidation of every State legislation relating to mines and minerals is demonstrated effectively by the decision in <CITATION_1201636> .
Precedent	<ORG> passed the Haryana Minerals (Vesting of Rights) Act, 1973, under which two notifications were issued for  acquisition of right to saltpeter, a minor mineral, and for auctioning certain saltpeter bearing areas.
