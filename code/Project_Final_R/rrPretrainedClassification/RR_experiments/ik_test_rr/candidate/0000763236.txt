RatioOfTheDecision	We have referred to these two documents out of several of them available on the record to show that the <ORG> was aware of the position that these trainee engineers formed a special class and very peculiar circumstances warranted a definitely special treatment in regard to them.
RatioOfTheDecision	Yet it is unfortunate that a statutory body like the <ORG> has failed to stand up to its representations made from time to time to a group of engineers who had spent years of their valuable life for qualifying themselves as engineers and who believing the representation of the <ORG> and acting upon the same continued to remain in the employment of the <ORG> as trainee engineers foregoing opportunities available to seek other employments and in the process have become age-barred for any public employment.
RatioOfTheDecision	This Court almost a score of years back in cle ar language indicated in <CITATION_1882267> :  Under our jurisprudence, the Government is not exempt from liability to carry out the representation made by it as to its future conduct and it cannot some undefined and undisclosed ground of necessity or expediency fail to carry out the promise solemnly made by it, nor claim to be the judge of its own obligation to the citizen on an ex parte appraisement of the circumstances in which the obligation has arisen."
RatioOfTheDecision	<NAME>, <NAME> as the learned Judge then was, quoted with approval what <NAME> <NAME> had in <CITATION_561287> :  "Whether it is the equity recognised in <NAME>'s case, or it is some other form of equity, is not of much importance.
Argument	<ORG>s must do justice by the promotion of honesty and good faith, as far as it lies in their power."
Argument	The legal position was reiterated by this <ORG> in <CITATION_113707> ., where it was said:  "Public bodies are as much bound as private individuals to carry out representations of facts and promises made by them, relyin g on which other persons have altered their position to their prejudice.
Argument	The obligation arising against an individual out of his representation amounting to a promise may be enforced ex contractu by a person who acts upon the promise; when the law requires that contract enforceable at law against a public body shall be in certain form or be executed in the manner prescribed by statute, the obligation may be, if the contract to be not in that form, enforced against it in appropriate cases in equity..."  612  This <ORG> added a pithy observation:  "If our nascent democracy is to thrive different standards of conduct for the people and the public bodies cannot be permitted.
Precedent	A public body is, in our judgment, not exempt from liability to carry out its obligations arising out of representations made by it relying upon which a citizen has altered his position to his prejudice."

Argument	this <ORG> went ahead to state that the doctrine of promissory estoppel is not really based on the principle of estopp el, but it is a doctrine evolved by equity in order to prevent injustice and it can be the basis of cause of action.
RatioOfTheDecision	In our view, the principle relied upon in these cases has full application to the facts before us.
RatioOfTheDecision	The <ORG> is a statutory authority and is 'State' within the meaning of Article 12 of the Constitution.
RatioOfTheDecision	The <ORG> has tried to seek shelter under a set of rules framed by it in exercise of the powers vested under section 79 of the Electricity (Supply) Act of 1948.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the peculiar facts of the case we are of the view that the defence is ill-placed and cannot hold as a shield against the application of the equitable doctrine.
RatioOfTheDecision	Admittedly, the trainee engineers before us formed a specific class and from time to time the <ORG> treated them as members of class and in its resolution of April 26, 1979, recognised this fact and swore to the position that such treatment should never be repeated even if apprentice engineers were appointed.
RatioOfTheDecision	Learned counsel appearing for the <ORG> indicated to us that the <ORG> ard was prepared to regularise the employment of the appellants belonging to the category of the Assistant Engineers or Junior Engineers subject to their qualifying in the examination and being formally recruited as required under the rules.
RatioOfTheDecision	They further emphasised that the appellants would not be entitled to seniority above those who have already been regularly employed under the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	So far as the first aspect is concerned, we have sufficiently pointed out already that the <ORG> had waived the requirement of examination and had, while taking advantage of the services of the appellants when it was in need, delayed the implementation of its representations.
RatioOfTheDecision	But it appears that several engineers have also been recruited either on permanent or temporary basis against regular vacancies and they are not parties to these appeals.
RatioOfTheDecision	The appellants, therefore, cannot have seniority above those people and we would not be justified in making any direction which would prejudice their seniority behind their back.
RatioOfTheDecision	It ap pears that there have been requirements even during the pendency of these appeals.
RatioOfTheDecision	While granting leave and while disposing of miscellaneous petitions for directions, this <ORG> has already made it clear that appointments pendente lite would be subject to the result of the appeals.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, the recruits of 1983 are bound to be subject to our directions.
RatioOfTheDecision	We are inclined to take the view that the appellants being already in employment of the <ORG> much prior to 1983 on being taken into regular appointment of the <ORG> have to rank above the recruits of 1983 and in the years thereafter.
RatioOfTheDecision	The <ORG> in our view is, therefore, bound to regularise the appointments of the appellants who had been taken as trainee engineers initially and have continued to be in the employment of the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	In this view of the matter after the hearing was over we issued a mandamus to the <ORG> to offer regular appointment to the appellants within three months from that day, i.e.
