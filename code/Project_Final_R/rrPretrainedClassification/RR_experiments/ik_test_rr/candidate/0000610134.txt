Statute	Section 96 (2) of the Act provides :--  "No sum shall be payable by an insurer under Sub-section (1) in respect of any judgment unless before or after the commencement of the proceedings in which the judgment is given the insurer had notice through the court of the bringing of the proceedings, or in respect of a ny judgment so long as execution is stayed thereon pending an appeal; RatioOfTheDecision	The statutory provision is clear that an insurer who has been made a party  to a proceeding for recovery of compensation can resist the claim only on those grounds mentioned in Sub-section (2) and it is not open to it to raise any other plea.
RatioOfTheDecision	The consensus of judicial opinion is to the same effect.
Precedent	<CITATION_1363897> , AIR 1959 Punj 297; <ORG>.

RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, the object of providing for a notice to the insurance company is really two-fold.
RatioOfTheDecision	One is to enable it to defend the action in its own right and in its own name if it is challenging the claim on any of the grounds mentioned in Section 96 (2).

RatioOfTheDecision	We may now refer to the decision of <ORG> in the case of <ORG> v. <NAME>, (AIR 1959 SC 1331) (Supra).
RatioOfTheDecision	This was a case where in the policy bond there was a condition as in this case.
Argument	First dealing with the provision of Section  96 of the Act, the Court observed :--  "To start with it is necessary to remember that apart from the statute an insurer has no right to be made a party to the action by the injured person against the insured causing the injury.
Argument	Sub-section (2) of Section 96 however gives him the right to be made a party to the suit and to defend it.
RatioOfTheDecision	The right therefore is created by statute and its content necessarily depends on the provisions of the statute.
RatioOfTheDecision	The question then really is, what are the defences that Subsection (2) makes available to an insurer ?
RatioOfTheDecision	That clearly is a question of interpretation of the sub-section.
RatioOfTheDecision	Now the language of Sub-section (2) seems to us to be perfectly plain and to admit of no doubt or confusion.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is that an insurer to whom the requisite notice of the action has been given 'shall be entitled to be made a party thereto and to defend the action on any of the following grounds, namely', after which comes an enumeration of the grounds.
RatioOfTheDecision	It would follow that an insurer is entitled to defend on  any of the grounds enumerated and no others.
RatioOfTheDecision	If it were not so, then of course no grounds need have been enumerated.
RatioOfTheDecision	When the grounds of defence have been specified, they cannot be added to.
RatioOfTheDecision	To do that would be adding words to the statute."
RatioOfTheDecision	Coming to deal with the terms of the policy bond the <ORG> further observed :--"We think that this contention is without foundation.
RatioOfTheDecision	Sub-section (2) in fact deals with defences other than those based on the conditions of a policy.
RatioOfTheDecision	Thus Clause (a) of that sub-section permits an insurer to defend an action on the ground that the policy has been duly cancelled provided the conditions set out in that clause have been satisfied.
RatioOfTheDecision	Clause (c) gives him the right to defend the action on the ground that the policy is void as having been obtained by non-disclosure of a material fact or a material false representation of fact.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, it cannot be said that in enacting Sub-section (2) the legislature was contemplating only those defences which were based on the conditions of the  policy, It also seems to us that even if, Sub-section (2) and Sub-section (3) were confined only to defences based on the conditions of the policy that would not have led to the conclusion that the legislature thought that other defences not based on such conditions, would be open to an insurer.
RatioOfTheDecision	If that was what the legislature intended, then there was nothing to prevent it from expressing its intention.
RatioOfTheDecision	What the legislature has done is to enumerate in Sub-section (2) the defences available to an insurer and to provide by Sub-section (6) that he cannot avoid his liability excepting by means of such defences.
RatioOfTheDecision	In order that Sub-section (2) may be interpreted in the way the learned Solicitor-General suggests we have to add words to it.
RatioOfTheDecision	The learned Solicitor-General concedes this and says that the only word that has to be added is the word 'also' after the word 'grounds'.
RatioOfTheDecision	But even this the rules of interpretation do not permit us to do unless the section as it stands is meaningless or of doubtful meaning, neither  of which we think it is.
RatioOfTheDecision	The addition suggested will, in our view, make the language used unhappy and further effect a complete change in the meaning of the words used in the sub-section."
RatioOfTheDecision	Next comes the observation on which full reliance has been placed by the insurer.
RatioOfTheDecision	".....
RatioOfTheDecision	The Statute has no doubt created a liability in the insurer to the injured person but the statute has also expressly confined the right to avoid that liability to certain grounds specified in it It is not for us to add to those grounds and therefore to the statute for reasons of hardship.
RatioOfTheDecision	We are furthermore not convinced that the statute causes any hardship.
RatioOfTheDecision	First, the insurer has the right, provided he has reserved it by the policy, to defend the action in the name of the assured and if he does so, all defences open to the assured can then be urged by him and there is no other defence that he claims to be entitled to urge.
RatioOfTheDecision	He can thus avoid all hardship, if any, by providing for a right to defend the action in the name of the assured  and this he has full liberty to do....."  From this decision of <ORG>, three propitions seem to have become settled; (i) an insurer is not entitled to resist a claim on a ground not enumerated under Section 96(2) of the Act, (ii) Section 96 (2) of the Act deals with defences other than those based on conditions of a policy, and (iii) where conditions of the policy so provide, an insurer has the right to defend the action in the name of the assured and if he does so, all defences open to the assured can then be urged by the insurer.
RatioOfTheDecision	There seems to have been some dispute as to what exactly was meant by <ORG> when it observed :--  "..... the insurer has the right, provided he has reserved it by the policy, to defend the action in the name of the assured and if he does so, all defences open to the assured can then be urged by him....."  On a plain reading of the lines quoted above from the judgment of <ORG>, it appears to us that the <ORG> has said that the insurer must have r eserved to it by the policy the right to defend the action in the name of the assured and the insurer must defend in the name of the assured in order that the restriction put under Section 96 (2) of the Act goes and all the defences available to the assured are available to the insurer.
RatioOfTheDecision	The phrase 'if he does so' does not have the narrow interpretation, as given to it by Counsel in support of the appeal, namely, that the insurer has reserved to it the right to defend in the name of the assured.
RatioOfTheDecision	If that was the real meaning intended to be given to the phrase, after it has already been said that the insurer has the right, provided he has reserved it by the policy, to defend the action in the name of the assured there was no necessity to add.
RatioOfTheDecision	What was really intended to be said was that such a condition was there under the terms of the policy and the insurer was out to raise the defence in exercise of the right.
RatioOfTheDecision	Most of the cases cited to us have followed the dictum of <ORG> in this sense though in o ne or two Courts a different view has been taken.
RatioOfTheDecision	Each of the cases that was cited to us at the <ORG>, however, seems to be one where the insured had not entered contest, i. e. both the insurer and the insured were not contesting simultaneously.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is unnecessary to give a catalogue of such cases.
RatioOfTheDecision	Where the insured appears and defends the claim and the insurer intends to defend in his own name, the condition in the policy bond does not provide shelter to the insurer.
