Fact	HEADNOTE: By the Constitution (Fifty-Second Amendment) Act, 1985 (popularly known as the Anti-defection law) the Tenth Schedule was inserted in the constitution of India providing for disqualification of a Member of either <ORG> or of <ORG> found to have defected from continuing as a Member of the <ORG>.
Fact	Paragraph 2 of the Tenth Schedule states that a Member of a <ORG> would incur disqualification if he voluntarily gives up his membership of the party by which he was set up as a candidate at the election, or if he without obtaining prior permission of the political part y to which he belongs votes or abstains from voting in the <ORG> contrary to "any direction" issued by such political party and such voting or abstention has not been condoned by such political party within 15 days from the date of such voting or abstention; or if a Member elected otherwise than as a candidate set up by any political party joins a political party after the 688 election; or, if a nominated Member joins any political party after expiry of six months from the date he took his seat.
Fact	Paragraph 6(1) states that the question of disqualification shall be referred for decision of the chairmen/Speaker of the <ORG> and his decision shall be final.
Fact	It further provides that such question in respect of Chairman/Speaker shall be referred for decision of such Member of the <ORG> as the <ORG> may elect in this behalf.
Fact	according to Paragraph 6(2) all proceedings under para 6(1) shall be deemed to be proceedings in <ORG>/Legislature of a <ORG> within the meaning of Article 122 /212.
Fact	Paragraph 7 states that  no court shall have jurisdiction in respect of any matter connected with the disqualification of a Member of a <ORG>.

RatioOfTheDecision	<ORG> is, therefore, distinguishable.
RatioOfTheDecision	Apart from inapplicability of the Doctrine of Severability to a Bill to which the proviso to clause (2) of Article 368 applies, for the reasons given, it does not apply in the present case to strike down para 7 alone retaining the remaining part of the Tenth Schedule.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the first place, the discipline for exercise of the constituent power was consciously and deliberately adopted instead of resorting to the mode of ordinary legislation in accordance with sub-clause (e) of clause (1) of Articles 102 and 191, which would render the decision on the question of disqualification on the ground of defection also amenable to judicial review as in the case of decision on questions relating to other disqualifications.
RatioOfTheDecision	Moreover, even the test applicable for applying the Doctrine of Severability to ordinary legislation as summarised in <CITATION_725224> ,  S.C.R.
RatioOfTheDecision	930, indicates that para 7 alone is not severable to permit retention o f the remaining part of the Tenth Schedule as valid legislation.
RatioOfTheDecision	The settled test whether the enactment would have been made without para 7 indicates that the legislative intent was to make the enactment only with para 7 therein and not without it.
RatioOfTheDecision	This intention is manifest throughout and evident from the fact that but for para 7 the enactment did not require the discipline of Article 368 and exercise of the constituent power.
RatioOfTheDecision	Para 7 follows para 6 the contents of which indicate the importance given to para 7 while enacting the Tenth Schedule.
RatioOfTheDecision	The entire exercise, as reiterated time and again in the debates, particularly the Speech of the Law Minister while piloting the Bill in <ORG> and that of the Prime Minister in <ORG>, was to emphasise that total exclusion of judicial review of the Speaker's decision by all courts including <ORG>, was the prime object of enacting the Tenth Schedule.
RatioOfTheDecision	The entire legislative history shows this.
Precedent	How can the Doctrine of Severability be applied i n such a situation to retain the Tenth Schedule striking down para 7 alone ?
Precedent	This is further reason for inapplicability of this doctrine.
Precedent	Point `F'- Violation of basic features The provisions in the Tenth Schedule minus para 7, assuming para 7 to be severable as held in the majority opinion, can be sustained only if they do not violate the basic structure of the Constitution or damage any of its basic features.
Precedent	This is settled by <NAME> [1973] Supp.
Precedent	S.C.R.
Precedent	1.
Precedent	The question, therefore, is whether there is violation of any of the basic features of the Constitution by the remaining part of the Tenth Schedule, even assuming the absence of ratification in accordance with the proviso to clause (2) of Article 368 results in invalidation of para 7 alone.
RatioOfTheDecision	Democracy is a part of the basic structure of our Constitution; and rule of law, and free and fair elections are basic features of democracy.
RatioOfTheDecision	One of the Postulates of free and fair elections is provision for resolution of election disputes as also adjudi cation of disputes relating to subsequent disqualifications by an independent authority.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is only by a fair adjudication of such disputes relating to validity of electrons and subsequent disqualifications of members that true reflection of the electoral mandate and governance by rule of law essential for democracy can be ensured.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the democratic pattern adopted in our Constitution, not only the resolution of election dispute is entrusted to a judicial tribunal, but even the decision on questions as to disqualification of members under Articles 103 and 192 is by the President/Governor in accordance with the opinion of <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	The constitutional scheme, therefore, for decision on questions as to disqualification of members after being duly elected, contemplates adjudication of such disputes by an independent authority out side the <ORG>, namely, President/Governor in accordance with the opinion of <ORG>, all of whom are high constitutional functionaries with security of te nure independent of the will of the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	Sub-clause (e) of clause (1) in Articles 102 and 191 which provides for enactment of any law by the <ORG> to prescribe any disqualification other than those prescribed in the earlier sub-clauses of clause (1), clearly indicates that all disqualifications of members were contemplated within the scope of Articles 102 and 191.
RatioOfTheDecision	Accordingly, all disqualifications including disqualification on the ground of defection, in our constitutional scheme, are different species of the same genus, namely, disqualification, and the constitutional scheme does not contemplate any difference in their basic traits and treatment.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is undisputed that the disqualification on the ground of defection could as well have been prescribed by an ordinary law made by the <ORG> under Articles 102 (1) (e) and 191 (1) (e) instead of by resort to the constituent power of enacting the Tenth Schedule.
RatioOfTheDecision	This itself indicates that all disqualifications of members according to the constitutional  scheme were meant to be decided by an independent authority outside the <ORG> such as the President/Governor, in accordance with the opinion of another similar independent constitutional functionary,, <ORG>, who enjoys the security of tenure of tenure of a <ORG> judge with the same terms and conditions of office.
RatioOfTheDecision	Thus, for the purpose of entrusting the decision of the question of disqualification of a member, the constitutional scheme envisages an independent authority outside the <ORG> and not within it, which may be dependent on the pleasure of the Majority in the <ORG> for its tenure.
RatioOfTheDecision	The Speaker's office is undoubtedly high and has considerable aura with the attribute of impartiality.
RatioOfTheDecision	This aura of the office was even greater when the Constitution was framed and yet the farmers of the Constitution did not choose to vest the authority of adjudicating disputes as to disqualification of members to the Speaker; and provision was made in Articles 103 and 192 for decision of s uch disputes by the President/Governor in accordance with the opinion of <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	To reason is not far to seek.
RatioOfTheDecision	The Speaker being an authority within the <ORG> and his tenure being dependent on the will of the majority therein, likelihood of suspicion of bias could not be ruled out.
RatioOfTheDecision	The question as to disqualification of a member has adjudicatory disposition and, therefore, requires the decision to be rendered in consonance with the scheme for adjudication of disputes.
RatioOfTheDecision	Rule of law has in it firmly entrenched, natural justice, of which, rule against Bias is a necessary concomitant; and basic postulates of Rule against Bias are; Nemo judex in causa sua - `A Judge is disqualified from determining any case in which he may be, or may fairly be suspected to be, biased'; and `it is of fundamental importance that justice should not only be done, but should manifestly and undoubtedly be seen to be done.'
RatioOfTheDecision	This appears to be the underlying principle adopted by the framers of the Constitution in not desi gnating the Speaker as the authority to decide election disputes and questions as to disqualification of members under Articles 103, 192 and 329 and opting for an independent authority outside the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	The framers of the Constitution had in this manner kept the office of the Speaker away from this controversy.
RatioOfTheDecision	There is nothing unusual in this scheme if we bear in mind that the final authority for removal of a Judge of the Supreme Court and <ORG> its outside the judiciary in the <ORG> under Article 124(4).
RatioOfTheDecision	On the same principle the authority to decide the question of disqualification of a member of legislature is outside the <ORG> as envisaged by Articles 103 and 192.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the Tenth Schedule, the Speaker is made not only the sole but the final arbiter of such dispute with no provision for any appeal or revision against the Speaker's decision to any independent outside authority.
RatioOfTheDecision	This departure in the Tenth Schedule is a reverse trend and violates a basic feature of the Constitution since the speaker  cannot be treated as an authority contemplated for being entrusted with this function by the basic postulates of the Constitution, notwithstanding the great dignity attaching to that office with the attribute of impartiality.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is the Vice-President of India who is ex-officio chairman of <ORG> and his position, being akin to that of the President of India, is different from that of the Speaker.
RatioOfTheDecision	Nothing said herein relating to the office of the Speaker applies to the Chairman of <ORG>, that is, the Vice-President of India.
RatioOfTheDecision	However, the only authority named for <ORG> and <ORG> is the Speaker of the <ORG> and entrustment of this adjudicatory function fouls with the constitutional scheme and, therefore, violates a basic feature of the Constitution.
RatioOfTheDecision	Remaining part of the Tenth Schedule also is rendered invalid notwithstanding the fact that this defect would not apply to <ORG> alone whose Chairman is the Vice-President of India, since the Tenth Schedule  becomes unworkable for <ORG> and <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	The Statutory exception of Doctrine of Necessity has no application since designation of authority in the Tenth Schedule is made by choice while enacting the legislation instead of adopting the other available options.
RatioOfTheDecision	Since the conferment of authority is on the Speaker and that provision cannot be sustained for the reason given, even without para 7, the entire Tenth Schedule is rendered invalid in the absence of any valid authority for decision of the dispute.
RatioOfTheDecision	Thus, even if the entire Tenth Schedule cannot be held unconstitutional merely on the ground of absence of ratification of the Bill, assuming it is permissible to strike down para 7 alone, the remaining part of the Tenth Schedule is rendered unconstitutional also on account of violation of the aforesaid basic feature.
RatioOfTheDecision	Irrespective of the view on the question of effect of absence of ratification, the entire Tenth Schedule must be struck down as unconstitutionally.
RatioOfTheDecision	Point `G' - Other cont entions We have reached the conclusion that para 7 of the Tenth Schedule is unconstitutional; that the entire Tenth Schedule is constitutionally invalid in the absence of prior ratification in accordance with the proviso to clause (2) of Article 368; that the Doctrine of Severability does not apply in the present case of a constitutional amendment which suffers from the defect of absence of ratification as required by the proviso to clause (2) of Article 368; that the remaining part of the Tenth Schedule minus para 7 is also unconstitutional for violation of a basic feature of the Constitution; and that the entire Tenth Schedule is, therefore, constitutionally invalid rendering the Constitution (Fifty-Second Amendment) Act, 1985 still born and an abortive attempt to amend the constitution.
RatioOfTheDecision	In view of this conclusion, it is not necessary for us to express our concluded opinion on the other grounds of challenge to the constitutional validity of the entire Tenth Schedule urged at the hearing on the basis of alle ged violation of certain other basic features of the Constitution including the right of members based on Article 105 of the Constitution.
RatioOfTheDecision	These are our detailed reasons for the operative conclusions pronounced by us earlier on November 12, 1991.
