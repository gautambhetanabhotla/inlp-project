RatioOfTheDecision	Subject to reconciliation of the two objectives, any regulation accompanying affiliation or recogni tion must satisfy the triple tests: (i) the test of resonableness and rationality, (ii) the test that the regulation would be conducive to making the institution an effective vehicle of education for the minority community or other persons who resort to it, and (iii) that there is no in-road on the protection conferred by Article 30(1) of the Constitution, that is, by framing the regulation the essential character of the institution being a minority educational institution, is not taken away.
RatioOfTheDecision	(para 122, <ORG>)  (iii) Minority educational institutions receiving <ORG> aid Conditions which can normally be permitted to be imposed on the educational institutions receiving the grant must be related to the proper utilization of the grant and fulfillment of the objectives of the grant without diluting the minority status of the educational institution, as held in <ORG> (See para 143 thereof).
RatioOfTheDecision	As aided institutions are not before us and we are not called upon to deal with their cases, we leave the di scussion at that only.
RatioOfTheDecision	So far as appropriation of quota by the <ORG> and enforcement of its reservation policy is concerned, we do not see much of difference between non-minority and minority unaided educational institutions.
RatioOfTheDecision	We find great force in the submission made on behalf of the petitioners that the <ORG>s have no power to insist on seat sharing in the unaided private professional educational institutions by fixing a quota of seats between the management and the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	The <ORG> cannot insist on private educational institutions which receive no aid from the <ORG> to implement <ORG>'s policy on reservation for granting admission on lesser percentage of marks, i.e.
RatioOfTheDecision	on any criterion except merit.
RatioOfTheDecision	As per our understanding, neither in the judgment of <ORG> nor in the <ORG> decision in <ORG>, which was approved by <ORG>, there is anything which would allow the <ORG> to regulate or control admissions in the unaided professional educational institutions so as to  compel them to give up a share of the available seats to the candidates chosen by the <ORG>, as if it was filling the seats available to be filled up at its discretion in such private institutions.
RatioOfTheDecision	This would amount to nationalization of seats which has been specifically disapproved in <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	Such imposition of quota of <ORG> seats or enforcing reservation policy of the <ORG> on available seats in unaided professional institutions are acts constituting serious encroachment on the right and autonomy of private professional educational institutions.
RatioOfTheDecision	Such appropriation of seats can also not be held to be a regulatory measure in the interest of minority within the meaning of Article 30(1) or a reasonable restriction within the meaning of Article 19(6) of the Constitution.
RatioOfTheDecision	Merely because the resources of the <ORG> in providing professional education are limited, private educational institutions, which intend to provide better professional education, cannot be forced by the <ORG> to make admissions availab le on the basis of reservation policy to less meritorious candidate.
RatioOfTheDecision	Unaided institutions, as they are not deriving any aid from <ORG> funds, can have their own admissions if fair, transparent, non-exploitative and based on merit.
RatioOfTheDecision	The observations in paragraph 68 of the majority opinion in <ORG>, on which the learned counsel for the parties have been much at variance in their submissions, according to us, are not to be read disjointly from other parts of the main judgment.
RatioOfTheDecision	A few observations contained in certain paragraphs of the judgment in <ORG>, if read in isolation, appear conflicting or inconsistent with each other.
RatioOfTheDecision	But if the observations made and the conclusions derived are read as a whole, the judgment nowhere lays down that unaided private educational institutions of minorities and non-minorities can be forced to submit to seat sharing and reservation policy of the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	Reading relevant parts of the judgment on which learned counsel have made comments and counter comments and rea ding the whole judgment (in the light of previous judgments of this <ORG>, which have been approved in <ORG>) in our considered opinion, observations in paragraph 68 merely permit unaided private institutions to maintain merit as the criterion of admission by voluntarily agreeing for seat sharing with the <ORG> or adopting selection based on common entrance test of the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	There are also observations saying that they may frame their own policy to give free-ships and scholarships to the needy and poor students or adopt a policy in line with the reservation policy of the state to cater to the educational needs of weaker and poorer sections of the society.
RatioOfTheDecision	Nowhere in <ORG>, either in the majority or in the minority opinion, have we found any justification for imposing seat sharing quota by the <ORG> on unaided private professional educational institutions and reservation policy of the <ORG> or <ORG> quota seats or management seats.
RatioOfTheDecision	We make it clear that the observations in <ORG> i n paragraph 68 and other paragraphs mentioning fixation of percentage of quota are to be read and understood as possible consensual arrangements which can be reached between unaided private professional institutions and the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	In <ORG>, it has been very clearly held at several places that unaided professional institutions should be given greater autonomy in determination of admission procedure and fee structure.
RatioOfTheDecision	<ORG> regulation should be minimal and only with a view to maintain fairness and transparency in admission procedure and to check exploitation of the students by charging exorbitant money or capitation fees.
RatioOfTheDecision	For the aforesaid reasons, we cannot approve of the scheme evolved in <ORG> to the extent it allows <ORG>s to fix quota for seat sharing between management and the <ORG>s on the basis of local needs of each <ORG>, in the unaided private educational institutions of both minority and non-minority categories.
RatioOfTheDecision	That part of the judgment in <ORG>, in our considered opi nion, does not lay down the correct law and runs counter to <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	NRI seats Here itself we are inclined to deal with the question as to seats allocated for Non-Resident Indians ('NRI', for short) or NRI seats.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is common knowledge that some of the institutions grant admissions to certain number of students under such quota by charging a higher amount of fee.
RatioOfTheDecision	In fact, the term 'NRI' in relation to admissions is a misnomer.
RatioOfTheDecision	By and large, we have noticed in cases after cases coming to this Court, neither the students who get admissions under this category nor their parents are NRIs.
RatioOfTheDecision	In effect and reality, under this category, less meritorious students, but who can afford to bring more money, get admission.
RatioOfTheDecision	During the course of hearing, it was pointed out that a limited number of such seats should be made available as the money brought by such students admitted against NRI quota enables the educational institutions to strengthen its level of education and also to enlarge its educational activities.
RatioOfTheDecision	I t was also pointed out that people of Indian origin, who have migrated to other countries, have a desire to bring back their children to their own country as they not only get education but also get reunited with Indian cultural ethos by virtue of being here.
RatioOfTheDecision	They also wish the money which they would be spending elsewhere on education of their children should rather reach their own motherland.
RulingByPresentCourt	A limited reservation of such seats, not exceeding 15%, in our opinion, may be made available to NRIs depending on the discretion of the management subject to two conditions.
RulingByPresentCourt	First, such seats should be utilized bona fide by the NRIs only and for their children or wards.
RulingByPresentCourt	Secondly, within this quota, the merit should not be given a complete go-by.
RatioOfTheDecision	The amount of money, in whatever form collected from such NRIs, should be utilized for benefiting students such as from economically weaker sections of the society, whom, on well defined criteria, the educational institution may admit on subsidized payment of their fee.
RatioOfTheDecision	To prev ent misutilisation of such quota or any malpractice referable to <ORG> quota seats, suitable legislation or regulation needs to be framed.
RatioOfTheDecision	So long as the <ORG> does not do it, it will be for the Committees constituted pursuant to <ORG> direction to regulate.
RatioOfTheDecision	Our answer to the first question is that neither the policy of reservation can be enforced by the <ORG> nor any quota or percentage of admissions can be carved out to be appropriated by the <ORG> in a minority or non-minority unaided educational institution.
RatioOfTheDecision	Minority institutions are free to admit students of their own choice including students of non-minority community as also members of their own community from other <ORG>s, both to a limited extent only and not in a manner and to such an extent that their minority educational institution status is lost.
RatioOfTheDecision	If they do so, they lose the protection of Article 30(1).
RatioOfTheDecision	Q.2.
RatioOfTheDecision	Admission procedure of unaided educational institutions.
RatioOfTheDecision	So far as the minority unaided institutions are concerned to admit stu dents being one of the components of "right to establish and administer an institution", the <ORG> cannot interfere therewith.
RatioOfTheDecision	Upto the level of undergraduate education, the minority unaided educational institutions enjoy total freedom.
RatioOfTheDecision	However, different considerations would apply for graduate and post-graduate level of education, as also for technical and professional educational institutions.
RatioOfTheDecision	Such education cannot be imparted by any institution unless recognized by or affiliated with any competent authority created by law, such as a <ORG>, <ORG>, <ORG> or <ORG> Government or the like.
RatioOfTheDecision	Excellence in education and maintenance of high standards at this level are a must.
RatioOfTheDecision	To fulfill these objectives, the <ORG> can and rather must, in national interest, step in.
RatioOfTheDecision	The education, knowledge and learning at this level possessed by individuals collectively constitutes national wealth.
RatioOfTheDecision	<ORG> has already held that the minority status of educational institutions is to be determined by treating the <ORG> s as units.
RatioOfTheDecision	Students of that community residing in other States where they are not in minority, shall not be considered to be minority in that particular State and hence their admission would be at par with other non-minority students of that State.
RatioOfTheDecision	Such admissions will be only to a limited extent that is like a 'sprinkling' of such admissions, the term we have used earlier borrowing from <ORG>, 1957.
RatioOfTheDecision	In minority educational institutions, aided or unaided, admissions shall be at the State level.
RatioOfTheDecision	Transparency and merit shall have to be assured.
RatioOfTheDecision	Whether minority or non-minority institutions, there may be more than one similarly situated institutions imparting education in any one discipline, in any State.
RatioOfTheDecision	The same aspirant seeking admission to take education in any one discipline of education shall have to purchase admission forms from several institutions and appear at several admission tests conducted at different places on same or different dates and there may be a clash of dates.
RatioOfTheDecision	If the sam e candidate is required to appear in several tests, he would be subjected to unnecessary and avoidable expenditure and inconvenience.
RatioOfTheDecision	There is nothing wrong in an entrance test being held for one group of institutions imparting same or similar education.
RatioOfTheDecision	Such institutions situated in one <ORG> or in more than one <ORG> may join together and hold a common entrance test or the <ORG> may itself or through an agency arrange for holding of such test.
RatioOfTheDecision	Out of such common merit list the successful candidates can be identified and chosen for being allotted to different institutions depending on the courses of study offered, the number of seats, the kind of minority to which the institution belongs and other relevant factors.
RatioOfTheDecision	Such an agency conducting Common Entrance Test (<ORG>, for short) must be one enjoying utmost credibility and expertise in the matter.
RatioOfTheDecision	This would better ensure the fulfillment of twin objects of transparency and merit.
RatioOfTheDecision	<ORG> is necessary in the interest of achieving the said objectives and also for sa ving the student community from harassment and exploitation.
RatioOfTheDecision	Holding of such common entrance test followed by centralized counseling or, in other words, single window system regulating admissions does not cause any dent in the right of minority unaided educational institutions to admit students of their choice.
RatioOfTheDecision	Such choice can be exercised from out of list of successful candidates prepared at the <ORG> without altering the order of merit inter se of the students so chosen.
RatioOfTheDecision	<ORG> has held that minority unaided institutions can legitimately claim unfettered fundamental right to choose the students to be allowed admissions and the procedure therefor subject to its being fair, transparent and non- exploitative.
RatioOfTheDecision	The same principle applies to non-minority unaided institutions.
RatioOfTheDecision	There may be a single institution imparting a particular type of education which is not being imparted by any other institution and having its own admission procedure fulfilling the test of being fair, transparent and non-exploitative .
RatioOfTheDecision	All institutions imparting same or similar professional education can join together for holding a common entrance test satisfying the abovesaid triple tests.
RatioOfTheDecision	The <ORG> can also provide a procedure of holding a common entrance test in the interest of securing fair and merit-based admissions and preventing mal- administration.
RatioOfTheDecision	The admission procedure so adopted by private institution or group of institutions, if it fails to satisfy all or any of the triple tests, indicated hereinabove, can be taken over by the <ORG> substituting its own procedure.
RatioOfTheDecision	The second question is answered accordingly.
RatioOfTheDecision	It needs to be specifically stated that having regard to the larger interest and welfare of the student community to promote merit, achieve excellence and curb mal-practices, it would be permissible to regulate admissions by providing a centralized and single window procedure.
RatioOfTheDecision	Such a procedure, to a large extent, can secure grant of merit based admissions on a transparent basis.
RatioOfTheDecision	Till regulations are framed, the admission  committees can oversee admissions so as to ensure that merit is not the casualty.
RatioOfTheDecision	Q.
RatioOfTheDecision	3 Fee, regulation of To set up a reasonable fee structure is also a component of "the right to establish and administer an institution" within the meaning of Article 30(1) of the Constitution, as per the law declared in <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	Every institution is free to devise its own fee structure subject to the limitation that there can be no profiteering and no capitation fee can be charged directly or indirectly, or in any form (Paras 56 to 58 and 161 [Answer to Q.5(c)] of <ORG> are relevant in this regard).
RatioOfTheDecision	Capitation Fees Capitation fee cannot be permitted to be charged and no seat can be permitted to be appropriated by payment of capitation fee.
RatioOfTheDecision	'Profession' has to be distinguished from 'business' or a mere 'occupation'.
RatioOfTheDecision	While in business, and to a certain extent in occupation, there is a profit motive, profession is primarily a service to society wherein earning is secondary or incidental.
RatioOfTheDecision	A student who gets a p rofessional degree by payment of capitation fee, once qualified as a professional, is likely to aim more at earning rather than serving and that becomes a bane to the society.
RatioOfTheDecision	The charging of capitation fee by unaided minority and non-minority institutions for professional courses is just not permissible.
RatioOfTheDecision	Similarly, profiteering is also not permissible.
RatioOfTheDecision	Despite the legal position, this <ORG> cannot shut its eyes to the hard realities of commercialization of education and evil practices being adopted by many institutions to earn large amounts for their private or selfish ends.
RatioOfTheDecision	If capitation fee and profiteering is to be checked, the method of admission has to be regulated so that the admissions are based on merit and transparency and the students are not exploited.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is permissible to regulate admission and fee structure for achieving the purpose just stated.
RatioOfTheDecision	Our answer to Question-3 is that every institution is free to devise its own fee structure but the same can be regulated in the interest of preventing  profiteering.
Fact	No capitation fee can be charged.
Fact	Q.4.
Fact	Committees formed pursuant to <ORG> Most vehement attack was laid by all the learned counsel appearing for the petitioner-applicants on that part of <ORG> which has directed the constitution of two committees dealing with admissions and fee structure.

RatioOfTheDecision	Such measures have also not been questioned as violative of the educational rights of either minorities or non- minorities.
RatioOfTheDecision	The two committees for monitoring admission procedure and determining fee structure in the judgment of <ORG>, are in our view, permissive as regulatory measures aimed at protecting the interest of the student community as a whole as also the minorities themselves, in maintaining required standards of professional education on non- exploitative terms in their institutions.
RatioOfTheDecision	Legal provisions made by the State <ORG> or the scheme evolved by the <ORG> for monitoring admission procedure  and fee fixation do not violate the right of minorities under Article 30(1) or the right of minorities and non-minorities under Article 19(1)(g).
RatioOfTheDecision	They are reasonable restrictions in the interest of minority institutions permissible under Article 30(1) and in the interest of general public under Article 19(6) of the Constitution.

RatioOfTheDecision	Non-minority unaided institutions can also be subjected to similar restrictions which are fo und reasonable and in the interest of student community.
RatioOfTheDecision	Professional education should be made accessible on the criterion of merit and on non-exploitative terms to all eligible students on an uniform basis.
RatioOfTheDecision	Minorities or non-minorities, in exercise of their educational rights in the field of professional education have an obligation and a duty to maintain requisite standards of professional education by giving admissions based on merit and making education equally accessible to eligible students through a fair and transparent admission procedure and on a reasonable fee-structure.
RatioOfTheDecision	In our considered view, on the basis of judgment in <ORG> and various previous judgments of this Court which have been taken into consideration in that case, the scheme evolved of setting up the two Committees for regulating admissions and determining fee structure by the judgment in <ORG> cannot be faulted either on the ground of alleged infringement of Article 19(1)(g) in case of unaided professional educationa l institutions of both categories and Article 19(1)(g) read with Article 30 in case of unaided professional institutions of minorities.
