Fact	JUDGMENT <NAME>, C.J.
RatioOfTheDecision	1.
RatioOfTheDecision	This is an appeal by an employee of <ORG> whose services were dispensed with, and he filed a suit from which this appeal arises contending that the order discharging him was not a valid order and that he continued to be in service of the <ORG>.
Fact	The appellant passed the entrance examination in June 1941, obtained a medical certificate of fitness on 24-10-1941 and he was appointed a clerk on the same date in <ORG>.
Fact	In January 1948 he was transferred to Bombay and he served in the office of the Controller of Naval Accounts.
Fact	He continued to serve in this <ORG> till 8-12-1948 when he was dis-charged.
Fact	The order of discharge states :  "Mr. <NAME> temporary U. D. Clerk of this office, having been declared medically unfit for further service in <ORG> by <ORG>, is discharged from service with effect from 6th December 1948."
Argument	It is this order which is being challenged by the a ppellant.
Argument	2.
Argument	The first question that we have to consider is whether the appellant was a temporary or a permanent servant of Government.
Argument	There is a large volume of evidence which the learned Judge has considered which leaves no doubt that the appellant was a temporary servant and was never confirmed.
Argument	In his own plaint he avers that the appointment of the plaintiff had become a permanent one in pursuance of the relevant rules and other rules, This averment clearly implies that when the plaintiff was appointed he was appointed as a temporary servant, and the plaintiff has failed to adduce any evidence in support of his contention that at ,a later date he was made permanent or confirmed in his post.
Argument	In his evidence also he has referred to his expectation that he would be confirmed in his post in or about the year 1947 and he has admitted that during the course of his service he did not receive any order or letter from the department confirming him or making him permanent.
Argument	In the certificate that was issued to h im on his being discharged he is referred to as a temporary clerk and in the service register which he has signed he has throughout been referred to as a temporary clerk.
Argument	In the letter which he wrote on 10-12-1948 after he was discharged, he states that he had more than seven years service in the department and hence he was expecting confirmation in the department, and he makes a grievance of the fact that he asked for bread but got a stone and that instead of getting confirmation he was discharged from service.
Argument	On this evidence we agree with the learned Judge that the appellant was a temporary servant of <ORG> who was at no time confirmed in his post, nor at any time made permanent.
Argument	3.
Argument	The interesting question that has been argued by Mr. <NAME> is as to what are the rights of a temporary <ORG> servant vis-avis the Constitution, and Mr. <NAME>'s contention is that this order of discharge was passed against his client without the constitutional safeguards guaranteed to him being complied with.
Argument	The constitutional safeguards on which reliance is placed are not the constitutional safeguards, given by Article 311 of the Constitution but Section 240(3).
Argument	Government of India Act which was in force, but as is well known the section and the Article are practically in 'pari materia'.
Argument	What is urged is that the appellant was dismissed from service without any reasonable opportunity being given to him to show cause against the charge that was levelled against him that he was permanently incapacitated.
RatioOfTheDecision	In this Court we have held in several decisions that a temporary Government servant cannot claim the rights provided in Article 311 of the Constitution, and the same would be the position with regard to Section 240(3).
RatioOfTheDecision	What Mr. <NAME> wanted to urge before us today was that even in the case of a temporary Government servant if the Government wishes to dismiss him or remove him or reduce him in rank, he is entitled to the same rights as a permanent servant.
RatioOfTheDecision	Mr. <NAME>'s contention is that the tenure of servic e has nothing whatever to do with the rights that a Government servant has under Section 240(3) or Article 311.
RatioOfTheDecision	4.
Precedent	Our attention was drawn by Mr. <NAME> to a decision of <ORG> in -- ' <CITATION_288855> .
Precedent	', 1954 SC 369 (AIR V 41) (A).
Precedent	That was a case of a permanent Government servant who was asked to retire compulsorily and his grievance was that, that constituted removal and he had a right to the constitutional safeguards under Article 311 ., At p. 374 of the judgment <NAME>, J. as he then was, observes :  "Removal, like dismissal, no doubt brings about a termination of service but every termination of service does not amount to dismissal of removal."
Precedent	And this is how the learned Judge poses the question:  "The question then is whether a tennination of service brought about by compulsory retirement is tantamount to dismissal or removal from service so as to attract the provisions of Article 311 of the Constitution.
Precedent	The answer to the question, will depend on whether the nature and incidents of the  action resulting in dismissal or removal are to be found in the action of compulsory retirement.
RatioOfTheDecision	There can be no doubt that removal -- I am using the term synonymously with dismissal --generally implies that the officer is regarded as in some manner --blameworthy or deficient, that, is to say, that he has been guilty of some misconduct or is-- lacking in ability or capacity or the will to discharge his duties as he should do.
RatioOfTheDecision	The action of removal taken against him in such, circumstances is thus founded and justified on some ground personal to the officer.
RatioOfTheDecision	Such grounds, therefore, involve the levelling of some imputation or charge against the officer which may conceivably be controverted or explained by the officer."
RatioOfTheDecision	Mr. <NAME> says that what we have to consider is whether in this order of discharge the nature and incidents of dismissal or removal are present and if those are present then it is irrelevant whether the Government servant was permanent or temporary.
RatioOfTheDecision	There is considerable force in Mr. <NAME> ria's contention because if <ORG> does not choose, to dispense with the services of a temporary servant under its contract with him but wishes to inflict a punishment upon him and wishes to dismiss him or remove him, then it may be said that his case would stand on the same footing as the case of a permanent servant that he is being punished by the <ORG> which may result in serious consequences, and such punishment should not be inflicted unless he is afforded the safeguard provided by Article 311.
RatioOfTheDecision	But <ORG> does not lay down with respect, that it is not open to the <ORG> to dispense with the services of a temporary servant.
RatioOfTheDecision	If a <ORG> servant is made permanent or is confirmed in his post, the tenure of his service would be regulated by the rules framed in that behalf and his services cannot be terminated before that period.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the case of temporary servant the tenure of his service may be regulated by a written contract, it may be regulated by rules, and in the absence of a cont ract or the rules it would be regulated by the principles of common law.
RatioOfTheDecision	If the contract between the <ORG> and the temporary servant permits the <ORG> to dispense with his services without notice and at any time, the <ORG> is as much entitled to do so as any other private employer.
RatioOfTheDecision	What has got to be borne in mind is that it is only in cases falling within Article 311 or Section 240(3) that the <ORG> as an employer is bound to conform to certain rules of natural justice indicated in that Article and that section.
RatioOfTheDecision	But if the case does not fall either within the ambit of Article 311 or Section 240(3), then the relationship of master and servant is governed by the rules if there are any which would constitute the contract of employment, or it would be governed by common law if the rules do not provide for the employment of the particular person.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, in order to succeed, Mr. <NAME> must satisfy us that <ORG>, which is the employer in this case, acted in such a manner th at its action came within the ambit of Section 240(3), <ORG> of India Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	In other words, its action was tantamount to dismissing or removing the plaintiff from service.
RatioOfTheDecision	If Mr. <NAME> satisfies us on that count, then undoubtedly if reasonable opportunity has not been afforded to the plaintiff, the plaintiff would be entitled to succeed.
RatioOfTheDecision	5.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is from that point of view that we must again look at the order discharging the appellant.
RatioOfTheDecision	It cannot be disputed that if the <ORG> had discharged the appellant without giving any reasons at all, that order could not have been challenged by the appellant because the appellant being a temporary servant he had no security of tenure, he could not claim to continue in <ORG> service for any length of time, and the <ORG> like any other employer would be entitled to dispense with his services at any time it thought proper.
RatioOfTheDecision	The question is whether the fact that the <ORG> has given a reason for dispensing with the services of the appellant alters an o rder of discharge into an order of dismissal or removal.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is true that we must look at the substance of the matter and we should not be influenced by the language used in the order.
RatioOfTheDecision	The mere fact that Government chooses to use the expression "discharge" is not conclusive of the matter, and notwithstanding the use of that expression it may still be an order of dismissal or removal.
RatioOfTheDecision	Both Section 240(3) and Article 311 are based on this essential principle that the Government is inflicting a punishment upon its employee and therefore before that punishment should be indicted certain rules of natural justice must be carried out.
RatioOfTheDecision	Again Section 240(3) and Article 311 are based on this principle that the employee is being proceeded against for something which he has done which involves moral turpitude or which calls for censure upon him.
RatioOfTheDecision	The very passage on which Mr. <NAME> relies in the judgment of <ORG> makes that perfectly clear.
RatioOfTheDecision	Mr. <NAME> only emphasises one part of the judgment of <NAME> d that is "or is lacking in ability or capacity or the will to discharge his duties as he should do", and Mr. <NAME> says that if the capacity is lacking and an employee is discharged for that reason, then it constitutes a dismissal.
RatioOfTheDecision	Mr. <NAME> says that in this case as the appellant was discharged for lacking the capacity to discharge his duties because he was medically unfit, the order must be read to be an order of dismissal.
RatioOfTheDecision	Now, the expression just referred to must be read in its own context and not divorced from its context, and when we read the whole of that paragraph which has been set out in extenso earlier, it is clear that <NAME> was referring to conduct which must involve an imputation or which would require a charge being drawn against the employee.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, the lack of ability or capacity of the lack of will to discharge ones duties must not be a lack which is involuntary or which is inflicted upon an unfortunate person through no fault of his, but it must be such as must involve an eleme nt of volition.
RatioOfTheDecision	No one suggests and no one can suggest that the unfortunate appellant who suffered from serious eye trouble and because of which he was declared medically unfit, had done anything which could possibly suggest either moral turpitude or conduct which may be considered blame-  worthy.
RatioOfTheDecision	As a matter of fact, the medical certificate on which this order was passed points out that the appellant was suffering from high myopia and lenticular opacities and in consequence thereof was completely and permanently incapacitated for further service of any kind in the clerical branch, and the certificate goes on to say: "His incapacity does not appear to us to have been caused by irregular or intemperate habits".
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, as good a certificate of character as is possible was given to the appellant.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is difficult to understand in face of this how, Mr. <NAME> could contend that the order he is complaining of was an order which dismissed him or removed him from service.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is an order which discharged him,  it is an order which could have been passed without giving any reasons.
RatioOfTheDecision	The reasons having been given, we have a right to scrutinize those reasons and even when we scrutinize those reasons, the reasons do not suggest that <ORG> looked upon the conduct of the appellant as unworthy conduct for which he was being punished by being asked to leave the service of <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	6.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, in our opinion, this order is not an order of dismissal or removal from service and therefore in no view of the case can the appellant contend that he is entitled to title rights given to an employee under Section 240(3), <ORG> of India Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	7.
RatioOfTheDecision	This should be sufficient to dispose of the appeal, but we will also consider some of the other arguments advanced by Mr. <NAME>.
Argument	It was argued that the order in question is based upon a medical certificate which is issued under Article 447(a) of the rules framed by <ORG>, which article refers to a form of medical certificate to be given respecting an officer applying fo r pension in India, and it is said that this certificate has not been properly issued because the appellant's case is not covered by the rules.
RatioOfTheDecision	We fail to understand what relevancy the issuing of the certificate under a particular article has on the rights of the appellant.
RatioOfTheDecision	Assuming the article has no application and assuming the certificate was issued in a form which was not applicable, the certificate contains certain facts and materials and the <ORG> has based its order of discharge on those facts and materials.
RatioOfTheDecision	Mr. <NAME> says' that if this certificate is not a proper certificate he was entitled to challenge the conclusion arrived at by <ORG> and which is embodied in the order of discharge.
RatioOfTheDecision	But if Section 240(3) has no application, then it is not open' to the employee to challenge the reasons given by his employer for dispensing with his services.
RatioOfTheDecision	Nor is it open to the employee to say that he should be given an opportunity to controvert the accuracy of the materials on which the employer cam e to a particular conclusion.
RatioOfTheDecision	8.
RatioOfTheDecision	Mr. <NAME> also sought to argue that the order passed by <ORG> was <NAME> fide.
RatioOfTheDecision	We do not understand how an employee is entitled to question the motives of his employer if the employer has the right to dispense with his services at any time.
RatioOfTheDecision	Whatever may be the motive which may influence the exercise of a legal right if the legal right exists then the motive becomes Irrelevant, and if in a case where Section 240(3) or Article 311 does not apply the <ORG> has the right to dispense with the services of a temporary servant, then it is not open to a temporary servant to say that his services were dispensed with for an ulterior motive or for a motive which was not a proper motive.
RatioOfTheDecision	9.
Argument	It was then urged by Mr. <NAME> that there are certain rules framed which form annexure "B" to Rules 216 to 221 of Office Manual Part I of <ORG>, 1930 Edition, which deal with cases of removal of a servant for physical incapacity, and Mr. <NAME> says that the  respondents should have dispensed with the services of the appellant after complying with these rules.
RatioOfTheDecision	When we look at annexture "B" it is headed thus; "Rules relating to the suspension, reduction, removal and dismissal of public servants", and Rule 1 says : "Permanent servants should not be removed or dismissed except for serious offences which have been clearly proved", and Rule 2 says : "Cases of physical incapacity should be treated according to rules."
RatioOfTheDecision	It is clear that these rules apply where Government wishes to pass an order of suspension, reduction, removal on dismissal.
RatioOfTheDecision	But when we have come to the conclusion that the order challenged is not an order which falls in this category, then ex-hypothesi these particular rules can have no application.
RatioOfTheDecision	Undoubtedly, the appellant would have been entitled to insist upon these rules being complied with if <ORG> had attempted to pass an order of suspension, reduction, removal or dismissal against the appellant.
RatioOfTheDecision	10.
RatioOfTheDecision	A rather ingenious argument wa s put forward by Mr. <NAME> that <ORG> has failed to point out any rule under which the Appellant was discharged, and according to Mr. <NAME> whenever there is a case of a temporary servant whose services are dispensed with either without notice or with notice, the case must fall under a particular rule which.
RatioOfTheDecision	<ORG> has framed which entitles <ORG> to dispense with the services of the temporary servant.
RatioOfTheDecision	In other words, according to Mr. <NAME> in the absence of any rule <ORG> has no power to dispense with the services of a temporary servant.
RatioOfTheDecision	In our opinion, that contention is entirely untenable.
RatioOfTheDecision	The law governing the relationship between an employee and an employer must apply equally to a case of an employee whose employer happens to be the <ORG> or the <ORG>, unless there is something in law or in the Constitution which has made a departure from that rule.
RatioOfTheDecision	Departure, and a rather serious departure, has been made to the extent-of Section 240(3), <ORG> of India Act a nd Article 311 of the Constitution.
RatioOfTheDecision	But when Government dispenses with the services of a temporary servant it is not for Government to justify that order by referring to any specific rule, but it is for the discharged servant to point out that there is something in the Constitution or in any provision of the law which constitutes a departure from the principles of common law.
RatioOfTheDecision	There is no doubt that under common law Government could have dispensed with the services of the appellant and at most it would have been compelled to pay salary for reasonable notice which it was bound to give.
RatioOfTheDecision	But apart from that there is no restriction on the right, of the employer to dispense with the services of his employee.
RatioOfTheDecision	In most cases there is a written contract when Government employs a temporary servant] or there are rules which govern the employment of temporary servants.
RatioOfTheDecision	We have a case here where there seems to be neither a written contract nor a rule, and in the absence of one or the other we must fall back upon principl es of common law and we cannot possibly take the view that Government was not entitled in law to dispense with the services of a temporary servant.
RatioOfTheDecision	11.
Precedent	Mr. <NAME> has relied on the case of -- ' <CITATION_979574> ', 1930 Bom 320 (AIR, V17) (B), for the proposition that when a Government Department or a Municipality dismisses a servant the Government department or the Municipality is constituted a domestic tribunal and that domestic <ORG> must observe the rules of natural justice.
RatioOfTheDecision	There is no doubt that when an authority is constituted a domestic <ORG> and has got to hold an inquiry into the conduct of a person, it must observe the rules of natural justice.
RatioOfTheDecision	But the question is whether on the facts of; this case the Government is a domestic <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	The Government would only be a domestic <ORG> if it was inquiring and investigating into any charge levelled against the appellant.
RatioOfTheDecision	In that case any grievance that the appellant might make with regard to the violation of the rules of natural justice wou ld be perfectly in order.
RatioOfTheDecision	But as we have said before, the difficulty and the insurmountable difficulty in the way of the appellant is that this is not a case where Government has levelled any charges against the appellant.
RatioOfTheDecision	There is not a case where any inquiry has to be held against the appellant, and therefore neither the question of a domestic <ORG> nor the question of the rules of natural justice can arise In this case.
RatioOfTheDecision	12.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the circumstances we agree with the view taken by the learned trial Judge that the plaintiff's suit was not well founded.
RulingByPresentCourt	The result is that the appeal fails and must be dismissed.
