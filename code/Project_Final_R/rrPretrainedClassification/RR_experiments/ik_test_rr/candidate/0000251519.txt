Argument	The appellant's counsel has very forcefully contended that but for the negligence of the driver, the bus could not have skidded and that on the facts and circumstances of this case, it is for <ORG> to explain want of negligence on the part of the driver.
Argument	The counsel, in other words, contends that the doctrine of res ipsa loquitur is fully attracted by the facts and circumstances of this case.
Argument	(4) It is next argued that under Section 110 of  the Motor Vehicles Act, in order to claim compensation it is not necessary to establish that the accident involving bodily injury to persons arising out of the use of the motor vehicle in question was the result of negligence on the part of the owner or the driver of the vehicle.
Argument	In support of this contention, reference is made to the wording of this section and it is emphasised that the section does not speak of negligence on the part of the driver or the owner of the vehicle.
Argument	(5) On behalf of the Punjab Roadways respondents, <NAME> has contended that the onus of establishing his claim is always on the claimant (appellant in this case) and that even if there was any onus resting on his client, the same has been fully discharged.
Argument	It has also been emphasised that in the vehicle in question, the governor had been fixed at the speed of 30 miles per hour with the result that the vehicle could not possibly be going at a speed higher than 30 miles per hour.
Argument	It is further pointed out that in the complaint mad e by the appellant, there was no mention of the driver's negligence; nor was there any such mention made in the report to the Superintendent of Police, <NAME>.
Argument	It is submitted that no interference on appeal on the basis of the argument that the incident is prima facie independent of the driver's negligence is called for.
Argument	The respondent's counsel has in this connection relied on section 110-F of the Motor Vehicles Act which excludes the jurisdiction of Civil Courts from entertaining questions relating to claims for compensation.
Argument	(6) On the second point, <NAME> has contended that only a person who has been at fault can be held liable and burdened with the liability and that no innocent party should in fairness be called upon to compensate the injured person.
RatioOfTheDecision	(7) Dealing with the second point first, it is true that section 110-B of the Motor Vehicles Act does not in terms lay down that it is only when negligence on the part of the driver of the vehicle concerned is established that compensation can be awa rded, but then it should be borne in mind that this bunch of Secs.
RatioOfTheDecision	(110 to 110-F) merely deal with the subject of the substitution of <ORG> in place of civil <ORG> for the purpose of adjudicating on claims for compensation in respect of accidents involving the death or bodily injury to person arising out of the use of motor vehicles.
RatioOfTheDecision	They do not deal with the question as to who is to be held liable and in what circumstances, if any injury results from an accident.
RatioOfTheDecision	In order, therefore to discover the criterion or test for fixing liability, we have, in the absence, of any statutory provision fixing liability irrespective of negligence, to turn to the law of Torts according to which indisputably negligence in causing the accident in question is generally speaking essential to hold the negligent person liable.
RatioOfTheDecision	Nothing has been stated at the bar to persuade me to hold that the bunch of sections mentioned above in any way override the law of Torts.
RatioOfTheDecision	The cardinal principle of liability in  tort, when death or bodily injury has been caused to a person, is negligence or failure to take the requisite amount of care required by law.
RatioOfTheDecision	It has not been argued, and, indeed not even a suggestion has been thrown, that the claim in question before me is based on any scheme of insurance, contractual or statutory.
RatioOfTheDecision	I have, therefore, no hesitation in repelling this contention.
RatioOfTheDecision	(8) In so far as the first point is concerned, the contention raised is really based on the maxim res ipsa loquitur.
RatioOfTheDecision	This maxim, as I understand its purport, suggests that on the circumstances of a given case the Res speaks and is eloquent because the facts stand unexplained, with the result that the natural and reasonable inference from the facts--not a conjectural inference--shows that the act is attributable to some person's negligent conduct.
RatioOfTheDecision	The effect of this maxim, however, depends on the cogency of the inferences to be drawn and must, therefore very in each case.
RatioOfTheDecision	Its effect appears to be to shift the burden or onus of proof on  to the defendant who is expected to show as to how the accident may have occurred without his negligence.
RatioOfTheDecision	There is undoubtedly a divergence of judicial opinion as to the extent of the onus but in the case in hand that question does not arise and, therefore, it is unnecessary for me to advert to it.
RatioOfTheDecision	<ORG> has found that, the bus in question skidded on account of the wet condition of the road, as originally mentioned by the claimant in the complaint book.
RatioOfTheDecision	This finding has not been successfully challenged on behalf of the appellant and as a matter of fact no argument has been addressed in respect of this finding on the merits.
RatioOfTheDecision	(9) The contention raised, however, is that it was not possible for the vehicle to skid if there had been no negligence on the part of the driver; in other words the vehicles could only skid if there was some serious defect in it.
RatioOfTheDecision	I would grant that a skid in itself does not excuse the accident and it may not suffice, by itself, to displace the prima facie inference of negli gence arising from the vehicle being where it has no right to be, for a skid may be caused by bad, careless or fast driving.
RatioOfTheDecision	The skid is thus by itself a neutral factor and it may or may not be due to the driver's negligence.
RatioOfTheDecision	But in the instant case the finding of the Court below based on the earliest representation made by the claimant in the complaint book has not been successfully displaced on behalf of the appellant.
RatioOfTheDecision	It was, I may here mention, for the claimant it to be clearly erroneous in which attempt he does not seem to have succeeded.
RatioOfTheDecision	I have, therefore, no option but to repel this contention as well.
RatioOfTheDecision	(10) In the result this appeal falls and is hereby dismissed.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the circumstances of the case, I would not like to burden the appellant with costs of this appeal.
RatioOfTheDecision	Before concluding, however, I should like to observe that the learned counsel for the appellant put his client's case with considerable fairness and clarity of thought and nothing that could reasonably be urged has been left out.
RulingByPresentCourt	(11) Appeal  dismissed.
