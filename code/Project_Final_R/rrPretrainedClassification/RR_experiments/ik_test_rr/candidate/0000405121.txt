RatioOfTheDecision	(8) It is nto disputed before us that none of the grounds mentioned in clauses (a), (b) and (c) of sub-section (2) of section 96 exists in the present case.
RatioOfTheDecision	(9) Bare perusal of the relevant part of sub-section (2), which has been reproduced above, makes it plain that an insurer, upon whom liability is sought to be fastened in respect of any amount regarding which legal proceedings are taken against the insured, has to be served with a notice of the proceedings through the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	The insurer on receipt of that notice has a right to be imp leaded as a party to the proceedings.
RatioOfTheDecision	The grounds, which the insurer can however take to d efend the action in case the insurer is imp leaded as a party, have to be those confined to clauses (a), (b) and (c) of sub-section (2) of section 96 .
RatioOfTheDecision	It may also be mentioned that apart from the statute the insurer has no right to be made a party to the action by the injured person or the heirs of the deceased against the insured causing the injury.
RatioOfTheDecision	Sub-section (2) of section 96 , however, gives the right to the insurer to be made a party.
RatioOfTheDecision	The right is thus created by statute- and its scope and extent would necessarily be governed by the provisions of the statute.
Argument	Dealing with sub-section (2) of section 96 of the Act, <NAME>, I, (as he then was) speaking for the <ORG>, observed in the case of <CITATION_135147> , (1) :-  "To start with it is necessary to remember that apart from the statute an insurer has no right to be made a party to the action by the injured person against the insured causing the injury.
Argument	Sub-section (2) of section 96 however gives him the right to be made a party to the suit and to defend  it.
RatioOfTheDecision	The right therefore is created by statute and its content necessarily depends on the provisions of the statute.
RatioOfTheDecision	The question then really is, what are the defenses that sub-section (2) makes available to an insurer ?
RatioOfTheDecision	That clearly is a question of interpretation of the sub-section.
RatioOfTheDecision	(6)Now the language of sub-section (2) seems to us to be perfectly plain and to admit of no doubt or confusion.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is that an insurer to whom the requisite notice of the action has been given 'shall be entitled to be made a party thereto and to defend the action on any of the following grounds, namely', after which comes an enumeration of the grounds.
RatioOfTheDecision	It would follow that an insurer is entitled to defend on any of the grounds enumerated and no others.
RatioOfTheDecision	If it were nto so, then of course no grounds need have been enumerated.
RatioOfTheDecision	When the grounds of defense have been specified, they cannto be added to.
RatioOfTheDecision	To do that would be adding words to the statute."
RatioOfTheDecision	(10) Mr. <NAME> on behalf of <NAME> has argued that the insurer has a  right to be imp leaded as a party only if the insurer takes up any of the defenses mentioned in clauses (a), (b) and (c) of sub-section (2) of section 96 of the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	If the insurer does nto take those defenses the name of the insurer, according to the submission of Mr. <NAME>, would be liable to be struck out from the list of respondents.
RatioOfTheDecision	This contention, in our opinion, is nto well-founded.
RatioOfTheDecision	An insurer to whom notice under sub-section (2) of section 96 of the Act is given has a right to be imp leaded as party to the proceedings.
RatioOfTheDecision	The <ORG> at that stage is only concerned with the question of impleading the insurer as a party.
RatioOfTheDecision	It cannto at that stage compel the insurer to disclose his defense.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is only after the insurer is imp leaded as a party that he can be called upon to file his written statement.
RatioOfTheDecision	If the written statement discloses defense, which is nto covered by clauses (a), (b) and (c) of sub-section (2) of section 96 , the defense to that extent would be liable to be struck off.
RatioOfTheDecision	The insurer would, how ever, continue to remain a party to the proceedings.
RatioOfTheDecision	(11) In the present case the insurance company was initially imp leaded as a respondent to the proceedings.
RatioOfTheDecision	Although the <ORG> was justified in striking off the defense of the insurance company to so far as it was nto covered by clauses (a), (b) and (c) of subjection (2) of section 96 , the <ORG>, in our view, was in error in striking off the name of the insurance company from the list of respondents in the case.
Precedent	Mr. <NAME> has referred to the case of <CITATION_528920> , (2).
Precedent	In that case <ORG> of <ORG> (<ORG>, <ORG> and <ORG>) held :- "If the ex-parte decree was passed against the defendant and if <ORG> through the defendant satisfied the <ORG> that <ORG> did nto have reasonable opportunity to defend the action, then the <ORG> acting under its inherent jurisdiction would set aside the ex-parte decree, because although the defendant might have had sufficient cause to defend the action, if the defen se was being conducted by <ORG> and the defendant was only a nominal defendant, then it is nto sufficient that the defendant should have had ample opportunity to defend the action, but <ORG> should also be given ample opportunity to defend the action.
RatioOfTheDecision	(12) There is no question of setting aside an ex-parte decree at the instance of an insurer in the present case.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the circumstances, the observations made by the learned Judges, which were in the context of the facts of that case, cannto be of any material assistance in the present case.
RatioOfTheDecision	(13) Reference has also been made by Mr. <NAME> to the case of <CITATION_1363897> (3).
RatioOfTheDecision	There is, however, nothing in that case which militates against the view we have taken in the matter.
RatioOfTheDecision	(14) Mr. <NAME> on behalf of <ORG> has argued that the provisions of section 96 of the Act apply only to proceedings in a regular suit before a civil court and nto to proceedings before <ORG> (herein referred to as <ORG> aims <ORG>).
RatioOfTheDecision	Reference in this context has been made to the case of <CITATION_1109883> <NAME> and others, (4), wherein <ORG> of <ORG> observed:- "Reading of Section 96 clearly show that it was nto intended to govern enquiry before a Claims <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 96 contemplates proceedings in a <ORG> and nto a proceeding before a <ORG>."
RatioOfTheDecision	(15) We have given the matter our consideration and are of the view that the provisions of section 96 of the Act are intended to govern all proceedings in which question arises about the liability of the insurer to satisfy judgment against persons insured in respect of third party risks.
RatioOfTheDecision	There is nothing in that section which restricts the applicability of its provision to proceedings in a regular suit in a civil court and which excludes from their ambit the proceedings before the Claims <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	it may be mentioned that prior to the enactment of section 96 of the Act the remedy of the victim of the accident was only to proceed against the assured  for damages and the insurer could nto intervene nor could the plaintiff implead the insurer as a defendant.
RatioOfTheDecision	The policy of insurance could be enforced only by the parties to the insurance and the third person being nto a party associated with the insurance could nto sue on its basis.
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 96 and the other provisions of Chapter Viii of the Act were enacted with a view to remove that difficulty and mitigate the harshness of the general law.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is, in our opinion, difficult to believe that the legislature intended to relieve the parties of that hardship only in proceedings in a regular suit and nto those before <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is no doubt true that section 96 makes reference to <ORG> and to judgment and decree.
RatioOfTheDecision	In that respect we are of the view that the word "court" 'used in that section is comprehensive enough to include <ORG> and the words "judgment" and "decree" would include the award of that <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	<ORG> have been established in pursuance of the provisions of Section 110  of the Act, and if it had been the intention of the legislature to exclude the proceedings before such <ORG> from the operation of section 96 a clear indication to that effect would have been given.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is significant that according to sub-section (2) of section 110 of the Act the Claims <ORG> shall have all the powers of <ORG> for the purpose of taking evidence on oath and of enforcing the attendance of witnesses and of compelling the discovery and production of documents and material objects and for such other purposes as may be prescribed.
RatioOfTheDecision	It has further been provided that the Claims <ORG> shall be deemed to be <ORG> for all the purposes of section 195 and Chapter xxxv of the Code of Criminal Procedure.
