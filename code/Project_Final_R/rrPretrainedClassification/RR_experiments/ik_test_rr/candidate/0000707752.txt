Argument	All that the learned Judge has done in his judgment is to state that "after a careful consideration of all the issues that arise for decision in this Second Appeal, 1 am of the opinion that the best form in which a decree could be given to the plaintiffs is in the following terms," and then the learned Judge has proceeded to set out the t erms of his decree in clauses (1), (2) & (3), the 3rd clause being sub- divided into clauses (a), (b) & (c).

Argument	Before dealing with this contention, however, it is neces- sary to refer to a preliminary objection raised by Mr. <NAME> on behalf of the respondents.

Argument	In resisting this preliminary objection, Mr. <NAME> for the appellants has relied on the decision of this <ORG> in  <CITATION_1098114> (1).

Argument	It appears that a preliminary objection had been raised in that case by the respondents similar to the one which is raised in the present appeal, and in rejecting that preliminary objection, this <ORG> observed that "since leave has been -ranted, we do not think we can or should virtually revoke the leave by accepting the preliminary objection."
RatioOfTheDecision	It is because of this observation that this appeal has been referred to a larger <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is true that the statement on which Mr. <NAME> relies does seem to support his contention; but we are satisfied that the said statement should no, be interpreted as laying down a general proposition that if special leave is -ranted in a given case, it can never be revoked.
RatioOfTheDecision	On several occasions, this <ORG> has revoked special leave when facts were brought to its notice to justify the adoption of that course, and so we do not think Mr. <NAME> is justified in contending that leave granted to the appellants under Art.
RatioOfTheDecision	136. as in the present case, can never be revoked.
RatioOfTheDecision	The true position is that in a given case, if the respondent brings to the notice of this <ORG> facts which would justify the <ORG> in revoking the leave already granted, this <ORG> would, in the interests of justice, not hesitate to adopt that course.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, the question which falls to be considered is whether the present appeal should be dismissed solely on the ground that the appellants did not apply for leave under the r elevant clause of the Letters Patent of the Madras High Court.
RatioOfTheDecision	There is no doubt that if a party wants to avail himself of the remedy provided by Art.
RatioOfTheDecision	136 in cases where the decree of <ORG> under appeal has been passed under s. 100 C. P. C., it is necessary that the party must apply for leave under the Letters Patent, if the relevant clause of the Letters Patent provides for an appeal to <ORG> against the ,decision of a single Judge.
RatioOfTheDecision	Normally, an application for special leave against a second appellate decision would not be granted unless the remedy of a Letters Patent Appeal has been availed of.
RatioOfTheDecision	In fact, no appeal against second appellate decisions appears to be contemplated by the Constitution as is evident from the fact that Art.
RatioOfTheDecision	133(3) expressly provides that normally an appeal will not lie to this Court from the judgment, decree, or final order of one Judge of <ORG>, It is only where an application for special leave against a second appellate judgment raises issues of law of  general importance that the <ORG> would grant the application and proceed to deal with the merits of the contentions raised by the appellant.
RatioOfTheDecision	But even in such cases, it is necessary that the remedy by way of a Letters Patent Appeal must be resorted to before a party comes to this <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	Even so, we do not think it would be possible to lay down an unqualified rule that leave should not be granted if the party has not moved for leave under the Letters Patent and it cannot be so granted, nor is it possible to lay down an inflexible rule that if in such a case leave has been granted it must always and necessarily be revoked.
RatioOfTheDecision	Having regard to the wide scope of the powers conferred on this <ORG> under Art.
RatioOfTheDecision	136 , it is not possible and, indeed, it would not be expedient, to lay down any general rule which would govern all cases.
RatioOfTheDecision	The question as to whether the jurisdiction of this <ORG> under Art.
RatioOfTheDecision	136 should be exercised or not, and if yes, on what terms and conditions, is a matter which this <ORG> has to decide on  the facts of each case.
RatioOfTheDecision	In dealing with the respondents' contention that the special leave granted to the appellant against a second appellate decision should be revoked on the ground that the appellant had not applied for leave under the relevant clause of the Letters Patent it is necessary to bear in mind one relevant fact.
RatioOfTheDecision	If at the stage when special leave is granted, the respondent caveator appears and resists the grant of special leave on the ground that the appellant has not moved for Letters Patent Appeal, and it appers that the said ground is argued and rejected on the merits and consequently special leave is granted, then it would not be open to the respondent to raise the same point over again at the time of the final hearing of the appeal.
RatioOfTheDecision	If, however, the caveator does not appear, or having appeared, does not raise this point, or even if he raises the point the <ORG> does not decide it before granting special leave, the same point can be raised at the time of final hearing.
RatioOfTheDecision	In such a case, there  would be no technical bar of res judicata, and the de- cision on the point will depend upon a proper consideration of all the relevant facts.
RatioOfTheDecision	Reverting then to the main point raised by the appellants in this appeal, we do not think we would be justified in refus- ing to deal with the merits of the appeal solely on the  -round that the appellants did not move the learned single Judge for leave to prefer an appeal before <ORG> of <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	The infirmity in the judgment under appeal is so glaring that the ends of justice require that we should set aside the decree and send the matter back to <ORG> for disposal in accordance with law.
RatioOfTheDecision	The limitations placed by s. 100 , C.P.C., on the jurisdiction and powers of <ORG> in dealing with second appeals are well-known and the procedure which has to be followed by <ORG> in dealing with such appeals is also well-established.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the present case, the learned Judge has passed an order which reads more like an awa rd made by an arbitrator who, by terms of his reference, is not under an obligation to give reasons for his conclusions embodied in the award.
RatioOfTheDecision	When such a course is adopted by <ORG> in dealing with second appeals, it must obviously be corrected and <ORG> must be asked to deal with the matter in a normal way in accordance with law.
RatioOfTheDecision	That is why we think we cannot uphold the preliminary objection raised by Mr. <NAME>, even though we disapprove of the conduct of the appellants in coming to this Court without attempting to obtain the leave of the learned single Judge to file a Letters Patent Appeal before <ORG> of the Madras <NAME>h Court.
