Fact	2.1 Eligibility of an item to tax or deduction can hardly be made to depend on the label given to it by the parties.
Fact	An assessee cannot claim deduction under section 80-O in respect of certain receipts merely on the basis that they are described as royalty, fee or commission in the contract between the parties.
RatioOfTheDecision	By the same token, the absence of a specific label cannot be destructive of the right of an assessee to claim a deduction, if in fact, the consideration for the receipts can be attributed to the sources indicated in the section.
Statute	[p. 100BC] 2.2 The receipts by way of royalty, fees, commissions and `similar payments' envisaged by section 80-O may be derived in the course of a business or profession and constitute part of the profits and gains of such business or profession.
Statute	For instance, the fees received by a consulting scientist, an architect or an engineer for providing technic al services to others will nevertheless be assessable as part of the profits and gains from such profession.
Statute	[p. 90DE] 2.3 The essence of the exemption under section 80-O lies, not in consigning the receipt to one of the pigeonholes or `royalty', `commission' or `fees' but in examining whether the receipt is a payment in consideration of one of the two situations envisaged in the section : e.g., where the assessee is the owner of a patent or invention, he may generally permit another to make use of patent or invention, in consideration of a `royalty' payment; or, where the assessee is in possession or technical know-how, he may be prepared to allow another to make use thereof in consideration of a `fee' to the assessee; or he may stipulate a consideration in the form of a commission based on the sales of the products the other party is able to manufacture with the aid of such invention or know-how, or an assessee may have achieved some speciality and he may agree to lend his services to some other person and  stipulate a consideration therefor which may be variously described.

RatioOfTheDecision	In the circumstances, Sri <NAME> contends that the <ORG> should not be allowed to take up the stand that the approval of the <ORG> had no value at all and could be completely ignored by the assessing officer because, In his opinion, it did not fulfill the requirements of section 80- O.  we have considered the contentions urged on behalf of both parties.
RatioOfTheDecision	Since we have already expressed our conclusion that the contract in the present case does come within the fold of section 80-O and that the <ORG> acted rightly in granting approval to the contract, it may not be quite necessary for us to express any opinion on this issue.
RatioOfTheDecision	However, since the matter has been fully debated before us, and is of s ome general importance we may indicate our views on this issue.
RatioOfTheDecision	At the outset, it may be pointed out that, earlier section 80-O (and certain other sections in the statute) had provided for the approval of <ORG> as a condition precedent for the grant of relief or concessions thereunder, where the relief or concession was in relation to a contract with a foreign party.
RatioOfTheDecision	At that stage, it was possible to take a view that the provision was intended only as a safeguard to monitor contracts with foreigners as such contracts may involve several aspects of policy, finance, foreign exchange and other elements vital to the country's interests.
RatioOfTheDecision	But this power of approval has since been shifted to the <ORG> which is the highest administrative authority under the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	This is a very significant change.
RatioOfTheDecision	No doubt, even after the change, the approval acts as a safety valve and enables the Government to decline its approval for various reasons the effect of which, inter alia, would be that no relief be sought  for under the relevant provisions.
RatioOfTheDecision	But there is a change in the content and purpose of the approval.
RatioOfTheDecision	The <ORG> has to grant the approval "in this behalf" that is for the purposes of this section.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is true that, even earlier, the approval of <ORG> was to be granted "in this behalf" but when the power is vested in the apex authority under the Income-tax Act , it is clear that the scope of the <ORG>'s powers is more extensive and should bear upon the terms of the agreement vis-a-vis the claim for relief under the section in relation to which relief is sought.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is also interesting to see that this power of approval has since been de-centralised and vested in the Director-General and Chief Commissioner which are authorities at a lower rung than the <ORG> but at a higher rung than the assessing officer.
RatioOfTheDecision	While, at one time, the Income-tax Officer was described as the king-pin of the tax administration and was the sole repository of all functions pertaining to assessment, the recent tendency has  been to vest powers of assessment even in officers above the rank of the Income- tax officer either because of the amount involved or for other reason.
RatioOfTheDecision	Here again, there is good reason, over and above the general need to have a surveillance over foreign contracts, why the power to grant approval is vested in a higher authority in the Income-tax hierarchy itself.
RatioOfTheDecision	The first is that the Board is considered better equipped, both on considerations of time as well as the technical knowledge needed to examine the ramifications of technical international contracts and decide how far the contract in question and the receipts thereunder are of the nature intended to be covered by the exemption clause; The second is that, with such a provision, the applicant is sure to take steps to obtain necessary approval at a stage earlier to the implementation of the contract and it will be possible to require the party, if modification or changes are called for, to modify the contract even at the outset so as to bring it within t he range of contracts for which relief is intended.
RatioOfTheDecision	The third and perhaps and most important reason is that such contracts are generally likely to be long-term contracts and it is of the essence for an applicant to know well beforehand where he stands in the matter of tax exemption and whether he can proceed to execute the contract on the basis that he would be eligible for the relief he feels he is eligible for.
RatioOfTheDecision	It would result in chaos if an assessee's contracts were left to be scrutinised at the time of assessment several years after they have been implemented and the availability of an exemption provision which the assessee was banking upon and on the basis of which he had entered into the contract, denied to him for one reason or another whereas, duly forewarned by a disapproval, he could have backed out of the contract, if necessary, and saved his skin.
RatioOfTheDecision	In this situation, we find it difficult to accept the plea of Sri <NAME> that the approval is nothing but a measure for screening the cases which an asse ssing officer may have to consider.
RatioOfTheDecision	111  We are also reinforced in this conclusion by the manner in which the provision has been understood and implemented by the <ORG> since its introduction.
RatioOfTheDecision	The <ORG> had issued circulars earlier when the relief had been introduced originally by the insertion of section 85-C and, again, later in 1972.
RatioOfTheDecision	But, after the power of approval was vested in the <ORG>, elaborate guidelines were drawn up as pointed out by Sri <NAME>.
RatioOfTheDecision	These guidelines clearly envisage a detailed examination, by the <ORG>, of the terms of the contract submitted to it for scrutiny from all angles relevant for a decision as to eligibility for exemption under section 80-O .
RatioOfTheDecision	The proforma calls for details of the analysis of the receipts under the contract.
RatioOfTheDecision	An examination whether the receipts can be said to be by way of royalty, commission, fee or similar payment is undertaken.
RatioOfTheDecision	The receipts are analysed under the three headings, as earlier referred to us, set out in paras 5(a)(i), 5(a)(ii) and 5(b) of the p roforma.
RatioOfTheDecision	Even the situation where the contract is a composite one has been dealt with by the guidelines and this may be referred to here in a little greater detail.
RulingByLowerCourt	In the circular of 23.12.75 (supra), the <ORG> decided that it would decline approval in cases where the consolidated consideration could not be legitimately attributed to know- how, services etc.
RulingByLowerCourt	envisaged in the section but that in cases where such apportionment was considered permissible, it would grant approval to the agreement and have the quantification of the exemption to be decided by the assessing officer.
RulingByLowerCourt	It said :  "(ix) In the case of a composite agreement specifying a consolidated amount as consideration for purposes which include matters outside the scope of section 80-O (e.g.
RulingByLowerCourt	use of trade marks, supply of equipment etc.)
RulingByLowerCourt	the amount of the consideration relating to the provision of technical know-how or technical services, etc.
RulingByLowerCourt	qualifying for purposes of section 80-O will have to be determined by the Income-tax Officer separately at  the time of assessment after due appreciation of the relevant facts.
RulingByLowerCourt	Where, however, in the opinion of the <ORG>, it will not be possible to properly ascertain and determine the amount of the consideration relatable to the provisions of the know-how or the technical services, etc., qualifying of section 80-O , the <ORG> may not approve such an agreement for the purposes of section 80-O of the Act."
RatioOfTheDecision	It had also taken the view that a consideration for the use of the assessee's trade-mark would be outside the purview of section 80-O .
RatioOfTheDecision	Subsequently, however, the <ORG> changed its line of approach on these two issues .
RatioOfTheDecision	In its circular No.

RatioOfTheDecision	The <ORG> has had occasion to re-examine the aforesaid guidelines and it has been decided to modify the guidelines to the extent indicated below :-  XXX XX X XXX  (ii) In para (ix) of the said circular, it was mentioned that consideration for use of trade mark would be outside the scope of section 80-O .

RatioOfTheDecision	(iii) It was also stated in para 3(ix) of the circular dated 23.12.1975 that in the case of a composite agreement which specified a consolidated amount as consideration for purposes which included matters outside the scope of section 80-O , the Board may not approve such an agreement for the purposes of section 80-O of the Act if it' was not possible to properly ascertain and determine the amount of the consideration relatable to the provision of the know-how or technical services etc., qualifying for section 80-O .
RatioOfTheDecision	Thus the benefits of section 80-O could be denied to the entire amount of royalty, commission, fees etc., receivable under such an agreement.
RatioOfTheDecision	It has since been decided that in such cases approval w ould be granted by the <ORG> subject to a suitable disallowance for the non-qualifying services after taking into consideration the totality of the agreement so that balance of the royalty'fees etc.

RatioOfTheDecision	It is thus clear that the <ORG> has chalked out for itself, we think quite legitimately and properly, a very detailed and dominant rule as to the availability of exemptions under section 80-O .
RatioOfTheDecision	The guidelines are of general nature, fully sanctioned by the provisions of section 119(1) of the Act and, being instructions enuring to the benefit of the assessee, cannot be gone back upon by the Departmental Officer subordinate to the <ORG>, particularly in a case where no steps have been taken - or even suggested as necessary to be taken - to cancel or revoke the approval already accorded.
Precedent	This is, indeed, a proposition well- settled by the series of judicial decisions starting from <NAME> case (1955) 56 I.T.R.
Precedent	198 S.C.
RatioOfTheDecision	In fact also, the <ORG> ard has followed only its own guidelines.
RatioOfTheDecision	Elaborate reference to the correspondence, discussions and hearing is unnecessary.
RatioOfTheDecision	The <ORG> had reached its decision to approve the contract and the basis of claim for exemption after full consideration and analysis.
RatioOfTheDecision	We may, in this context, also point out that while the <ORG>, in the present case, simply approved of some of the contracts on the basis of the application filed, it has, in the case of some other contracts modified that basis also.
RatioOfTheDecision	For instance, in regard to the <ORG> and <ORG> projects, the letter of approval states that approval is granted subject to the condition or clarification that only the profits relating to rendering of technical services will qualify for the benefit of section 80-O of the I.T.
RatioOfTheDecision	Act and not the profits relating to the supply of material/equipment.
RatioOfTheDecision	These guidelines have also since attained statutory recognition as the proforma earlier prescribed by the <ORG> has virtually been incorporated in Rule 11E and Form prescribe d thereunder.
RatioOfTheDecision	In fact Sri <NAME> wants to utilise certain columns in the statutory form to support his contentions that an approval under section 80-O is effective even after section 80-HHB was introduced but to this argument, we shall advert a little later.
RatioOfTheDecision	We have, in view of the above discussion, no doubt at all that, while granting the approval under section 80-O , the <ORG> has not only the jurisdiction but also the responsibility of examining the agreement submitted for approval from all angles relevant to the deduction provided for under section 80-O and that it is not competent to the <ORG> to question the maintainability of the claim for deduction under section 80-O of the aspects gone into and decided upon by the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	We should, however, make it clear that our conclusion does not mean the deprivation of all functions of the assessing officer while making the assessment on the applicant.
RatioOfTheDecision	The Officer has to satisfy himself (i) that the amounts in respect of which the relief is claimed are am ounts arrived at in accordance with the formula, principle or basis explained in the assessee's application and approved by the <ORG>; (ii) that the deduction claimed in the relevant assessment year relates to the items and is referable to the basis on which application for exemption was asked for and granted by the <ORG>; (iii) that the receipts (before the 1975 amendment) were duly certified by an accountant or that, thereafter, the amounts have been received in or brought into India in convertible foreign exchange within the specified period.
RatioOfTheDecision	The second of these functions is, particularly, important as the approval for exemption granted in principle has to be translated into concrete figures for the purposes of each assessment.
RatioOfTheDecision	Neither the introduction of the words "in accordance with and subject to the provisions of this sections" nor the various "conditions" outlined in the letter of approval add anything to or detract anything from the scope of the approval As already mentioned, <NAME> also contend ed that, even after the insertion of S-HHB, the assessee would be entitled to claim the deduction under section 80-O in view of the <ORG>'s amendment to the letter of approval that the approval will be operative for assessment year 1982-83 onwards, rescinding the qualification in the earlier letter that the provisions of S. 80-HHB will apply for assessment year 1983-84 onwards.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is true that the earlier restriction was lifted by the <ORG> after considering the contentions raised by the assessee in its letter of 2-12- 1983 :  (a) that the two section operate in different fields for exemption;  (b) that the approval once granted under section 80-O , the exemption to which the assessee became eligible should ensure for the directions for the entire contract; and  (c) that s. 80-HHB should be restricted to agreements entered into before 1-4-1983.
RatioOfTheDecision	But we are unable to give effect to the <ORG>'s decision of 31-7-1985 in the same way as we have given effect to the <ORG>'s earlier approval letter of 28-10-1983 for  a number of reasons.
RatioOfTheDecision	The first is that the jurisdiction of a <ORG> is to grant approval to a contract only for the purposes of section 80-O ; it has no jurisdiction to pronounce on the availability or otherwise of an exemption under section 80-HHB and the <ORG>'s opinion as to this, even if expressly stated by the <ORG>, cannot bind the Officer.
RatioOfTheDecision	The relief under section 80-HHB is not dependent on the approval of the <ORG> and is for a totally different type of transaction.
RatioOfTheDecision	The letter of 31.7.85 is also a decision in an individual case and cannot be treated as a general circular incorporating a policy decision by the <ORG> that in all cases of a particular type governed by both section relief may be given under section 80-O in which event perhaps it could have been implemented by applying the principle of the <NAME> case (supra).
RatioOfTheDecision	The second is that the <ORG>, in the 1985 letter, has only stated that the approval under section 80-O will enure for 1982-83 onwards.
RatioOfTheDecision	This is quite a correct statement of, as we hav e explained earlier, the approval by the <ORG> is to the contract and so long as the contract subsists the relief should be granted on the term of section 80-O .
RatioOfTheDecision	Thus the assessee is entitled to deduction under section 80-O on the terms of that section even for 1983-84 and subsequent years.
RatioOfTheDecision	It becomes disentitled to the relief not because it does not fulfill the requirements of section 80-O but only because section 80-HHB(5) stands in the way and mandates that in cases to which both provisions will apply relief under section 80-HHB will alone be available.
RatioOfTheDecision	The argument that the applicability of section 80-HHB should be excluded from contracts entered into, or those approved of under section 80-O , before 1.4.1983, is patently untenable.
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 8 comes into force on 1.4.1983 and should be applicable for assessment year 1983-84 onwards in all cases.
RatioOfTheDecision	It does not contain even a reference to section 80-O and so its applicability cannot depend on the formation of the contract subsequent to that date or to the date  of its approval under the latter section being after that date.
RatioOfTheDecision	Thirdly, the approval which otherwise qualifies the assessee for relief is no doubt still effective but its power to "qualify" for relief is taken away by the new statutory provision.
RatioOfTheDecision	The argument that the assessee could not have anticipated the insertion of section 80-HHB and is put to a hardship if that section is applied is no doubt correct.
RatioOfTheDecision	But one cannot decline to give effect to the applicability of the statutory provision on the ground of hardship or on the ground that it restricts the relief which, but for the insertion of the section, would have been available to the assessee, particularly when the section itself envisages the possibility of the assessee being also eligible for relief under another section and makes special provision of that eventuality.
RatioOfTheDecision	<NAME> submitted that we should not favour the above interpretation as it would lead to an anomalous result.
RatioOfTheDecision	He says that the whole idea of section 80-HHB was to enlarge the benef its to contractors working abroad and earning foreign exchange but that, by reason of our decision, the assessee will now get relief only to the extent of 25% in respect of a contract for which it got 100% benefit in earlier years.
RatioOfTheDecision	On the other hand, the department would no doubt say that our conclusion that the assessee was entitled, in earlier assessment year, to 100 % relief on this type of contract is anomalous in the light of the fact that subsequently the legislature specifically provided that only 25 % of the earnings on foreign projects should be exempted.
RatioOfTheDecision	In our view, there is no force in these contentions.
RatioOfTheDecision	The anomaly, if it is one, arises because of the specific language of the statute and the nature of the contract we have to consider.
RatioOfTheDecision	S. 80-HHB does not confer an additional benefit; sub-section (5) in no uncertain terms states that the benefit thereunder will take away the benefit, if any, under any other provision.
RatioOfTheDecision	This has to be given effect to.
RatioOfTheDecision	Equally, the assessee was able to get 100 % relie f in earlier years only because the contract here is of such nature that it consists only of the rendering of technical services so that the fields of the two exemptions completely overlap.
RatioOfTheDecision	On the other hand, as discussed earlier, it is possible to conceive of foreign projects wherein the construction and installation aspect and information or technical services aspect are kept separate.
RatioOfTheDecision	Equally there can be cases falling under section 80-O which do not all relate to a "foreign project" as defined in section 80-HHB .
RatioOfTheDecision	In such cases, the two provisions will continue to operate independently.
RatioOfTheDecision	There is, therefore, no anomaly or absurdity in the conclusion we have reached.
RatioOfTheDecision	116  For the reasons discussed above, we hold that the assessee was entitled to the relief under section 80-O for assessment years earlier to 1983-84 and that the approval granted by the <ORG> under that section was right and proper.
