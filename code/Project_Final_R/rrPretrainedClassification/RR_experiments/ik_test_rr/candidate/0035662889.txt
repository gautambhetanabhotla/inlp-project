Fact	The first question to be considered is whether the notification of <ORG> under s. 4 of the Act dated October 4, 1950 is liable to be quashed on the ground that the acquisition of the land was not for a public purpose.
Argument	It was alleged for the appellant that the lime-stone extracted from quarries situated in the land was used by <ORG> for the manufacture of cement which was sold for profit in open market and was not used for any public work of construction.
Argument	It was contended that  the manufacture of cement for being sold for profit will not amount to a public purpose and the notification of <ORG> under s. 4 of the Act must therefore be held to be illegal.
RatioOfTheDecision	In our opinion, the argument put forward on behalf of the appellant cannot be accepted.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is manifest that the declaration made by <ORG> in the notification under s. 6(1) of the Act, that the land was required for a public purpose, is made conclusive by sub-s. (3) of s. 6 and it is, therefore, not open to a court to go behind it any try to satisfy itself whether in fact the acquisition was for a public purpose.
RatioOfTheDecision	It was pointed out by this Court in <CITATION_1536600> that it was for the <ORG> to be satisfied, in a particular case, that the purpose for which the land was needed was a public purpose and the declaration of the <ORG> under s. 6(1) of the Act will be final subject, however, to one exception, namely in the case of colourable exercise of the power, the declaration is open to challenge  at the instance of the aggrieved party.
RatioOfTheDecision	The power conferred on the <ORG> by the Act is a limited power in the sense that it can be exercised only where it is for a public purpose (leaving aside, for the moment, where the acquisition is for a company under Part VII of the Act).
Argument	If it appears that what the <ORG> is satisfied about is not a public purpose but a private purpose or no purpose at all, the action of the <ORG> would be colourable as being outside the power conferred upon it by the Act and its declaration under s. 6 of the Act will be a nullity.
Argument	On behalf of the respondents the argument was stressed that the lime-stone was utilised for being used in the cement factory established in the Public Sector at Churk.
Argument	It was argued that the production of cement was important in national interest, particularly when the cement was used in the construction of the Rihand dam.
Argument	It is conceded on behalf of the respondents that the allegation of the appellant that cement was being sold in market for pr ofit was not clearly controverted by the counter-affidavit by the <ORG> but it was said that even on the assumption that the cement was sold for profit the use of the lime-stone in the production of the cement was in public interest, because the profit from the sale of cement benefited the General Revenues of the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is not necessary for us to express any concluded opinion as to whether the production of cement as a commercial enterprise is a public purpose within the meaning of the Act for we consider that the principle of the decision of this Court in <CITATION_1536600> applies to this case and the argument of the appellant must be rejected because he has not been able to show that the action of the Government in issuing the notification under s. 6 of the Act is a colourable exercise of power.

Precedent	According to <ORG> "arable land" is "land which is capable of being ploughed or fit for tillage."
Precedent	In the context of s. 17(1) of the Act the expression must b e construed to mean "lands which are mainly used for ploughing and for raising crops" and therefore the land acquired in this case is not arable land.

Precedent	According to <ORG> the expression "waste" is defined as follows :  "Waste - (from Latin.
Precedent	vastus - waste, desert, unoccupied; Uncultivated, incapable of cultivation or habitation; producing little or no vegetation; barren, desert."
