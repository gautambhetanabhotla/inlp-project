RatioOfTheDecision	10.
RatioOfTheDecision	That, briefly, is a summary of relevant facts.
Argument	11.
Argument	Mr. <NAME> contends that the order in this case is an order of dismissal and violates the provisions of Article 311(2) of the Constitution.
Argument	He complains that the petitioner had no reasnoable opportunity of showing cause against the action taken.
RatioOfTheDecision	I have come to the conclusion that neither in the facts of this case nor in law such objections can be sustained.
RatioOfTheDecision	I shall state my reasons very briefly.
RatioOfTheDecision	12.
RatioOfTheDecision	Article 311 of the Constitution deals with dismissal, removal or reduction in rank I am of the opinion that in the present case it is neither dismissal nor removal nor reduction.
RatioOfTheDecision	The present case is one of due termination of service under the Rules and contract of Service in natural course of events and therefore Article 311 of the Constitution cannot be  attracted to such a termination.
RatioOfTheDecision	13.
RatioOfTheDecision	<ORG> in <CITATION_1270113> ) has explained the legal position on this point.
RatioOfTheDecision	The learned Chief Justice of <ORG> at p. 48 of the report Says:  "But if the servant has no right to the post as where he is appointed to a post, permanent or temporary, either on probation or on an officiating basis and whose temporary service has not ripened into a quasi permanent service as defined in the Temporary Service Rules, the termination of his employment does not deprive him of any right and cannot, therefore, by itself be a punishment."
RatioOfTheDecision	Here in the present case also the applicant's temporary service has not matured into a "quasi-permanent" service as defined by the Temporary Service Rules.
RatioOfTheDecision	14.
Precedent	Thereafter, the learned Chief Justice of India goes on to observe in his Lordship's judgment in the above case:  "To put it in another way if the Government has by contract, express or implied, or, under the Rules, the right to terminate the employment at any time, th en such termination in the manner provided by the contract or the Rules is, prima facie and per se, not a punishment and does not attract the provisions of Article 311."
RatioOfTheDecision	15.
RatioOfTheDecision	I consider that these observations are a complete answer to Mr. <NAME>'s argument in this case.
RatioOfTheDecision	Here also the Rules of Service expressly stipulate for the termination of service for unauthorised absence from duty without any leave.
RatioOfTheDecision	Rule 14(c) of the Revised Leave Rules, 1933 provides:  "Where a Government servant who is not in permanent employ or quasi-permanent employ fails to resume duty on the expiry of the maximum period of extraordinary leave granted to him, or where such a Government servant, who is granted a lesser amount of extraordinary leave than the maximum amount admissible, remains absent from duty for any period which together with the extraordinary leave granted exceeds the limit up to which he could have been granted such leave under Sub-rule (b), he shall be deemed to have resigned his appointment and shall, accordingly,  cease to be in <ORG> employ."
RatioOfTheDecision	Now this is the rule and part of the contract of service of the petitioner with the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	In order to get out of the rigour and operation of this Rule, Mr. <NAME> tried to contend that this Rule was introduced in September 1953, subsequently to the appointment of the petitioner in service, and, therefore, was no part of the contract of the petitioner's service with the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	I am unable to agree with this contention.
RatioOfTheDecision	The service in this case means that the <ORG> servant accepts as part of his conditions of service these Rules as they from time to time are made and modified.
RatioOfTheDecision	In fact, Rule 2 of the Revised Leave Rules says expressly that these Rules shall apply with retrospective effect.
RatioOfTheDecision	A part of the obligations of a <ORG> servant, as I understand under these Rules, is that he takes the risk of the amendments made in these Rules from time to time.
RatioOfTheDecision	So long these Rules or their amendments do not violate any provisions of the Constitution or of any relevan t Statute, mutability of these Rules forms part of the Contract and conditions of service, and are to be deemed as engrafted therein as and when they or their amendments are made, unless expressly or by necessary implication, they say otherwise.
Argument	16.
Argument	Then Mr. <NAME> argued that there was no grant of extraordinary leave to the petitioner in this case and, therefore, Rule 14 (c)5 would not apply.
Argument	His argument is that the extraordinary leave was only granted by the Assistant Collector's order dated 17-4-1956 and he had 10 days time thereunder to join.
Argument	Therefore he contends that the order of the Collector of Central Excise dated 7-5-1956 saying that his extraordinary leave expired on 28-1-56 must be wrong.
RatioOfTheDecision	This argument has a good deal of plausibility about it.
RatioOfTheDecision	I have, however, come to the conclusion that it is essentially unsound and cannot be upheld.
RatioOfTheDecision	Here again I shall state my reasons briefly.
RatioOfTheDecision	The order of the Assistant Collector of Excise, <NAME> in directing the entire period of the petitioner's absence  from duty from 11-8-1955 to the date of his rejoining as extraordinary leave, was in fact beyond and in excess of the rules.
RatioOfTheDecision	Extraordinary leave is granted under Rule 14 of the Revised Leave Rules.
RatioOfTheDecision	It can be granted to an officer only in special circumstances and the duration of such extraordinary leave under the rules cannot exceed 3 or 18 months on any one occasion, the longer period being only admissible in case of:  (i) pulmonary tuberculosis in a recognised sanatorium, or  (ii) tuberculosis of any other part of the body by a qualified tuberculosis specialist or a civil surgeon, or  (iii) leprosy The order of the Assistant Collector, therefore, was clearly beyond the rules as the applicant not satisfying the above conditions could never have more than three months' extraordinary leave.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, the Collector of <ORG> by his order of 7-5-1956 being the appellate authority over the Assistant Collector rightly gave the maximum extraordinary leave permissible, that is three months.
RatioOfTheDecision	Those three months expi red on 28-1-1956 on the facts of this case.
RatioOfTheDecision	17.
RatioOfTheDecision	Mr. <NAME> realised the force of this!
RatioOfTheDecision	argument.
RatioOfTheDecision	He, therefore, contended that if that was the position, then the Collector of <ORG> was retrospectively granting extraordinary leave with which the petitioner could not possibly comply in order to come under rule 14(c) of the Revised Leave Rules.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is quite true that the Assistant Collector on 17-4-1956 by making the entire period of unauthorised absence from 11-8-1955 to the future date of joining ten days ahead was putting forward a prospective date with which the applicant could comply.
RatioOfTheDecision	But then as I have said this was illegal and not permissible under the rules.
RatioOfTheDecision	The Collector of Excise took the only permissible part of this as the maximum, three months of extraordinary leave.
RulingByLowerCourt	It is true that as the Collector was making the order on 7-5-1956 his order naturally became retrospective because he stated that the three months' extraordinary leave expired on 28-1-1956.
RulingByLowerCourt	But then the short answer to this is that by  rule 14(d) it is expressly provided that periods of absence without leave may be commuted to extraordinary leave retrospectively.
RulingByLowerCourt	Rule 14(d) of the Revised Leave Rules, 1933 states as follows:  "The authority empowered to grant leave may commute retrospectively periods of absence without leave into extraordinary leave."
RatioOfTheDecision	I am, therefore, of the opinion that the Collector of Excise's order of 7-5-1956 was justified under this rule.
RatioOfTheDecision	This inevitably brings into operation Rule 14(c) whereby the service must be deemed to be terminated and the Collector had no other option but to declare that fact by his order.
RatioOfTheDecision	18.
RatioOfTheDecision	This disposes of the objections made on behalf of the applicant in this case.
RatioOfTheDecision	On the facts I find that the petitioner had ample opportunities to show cause against the two charges (1) of disobedience to orders and (2) of unauthorised absences.
RatioOfTheDecision	He was found guilty of both these charges.
RatioOfTheDecision	What thereafter happened by the order of 7-5-1956 by the Collector of Central Excise is only a declaration of the fact  that the applicant had ceased to be in service under the Rules of Service.
RatioOfTheDecision	According to the decision of <ORG> which I have quoted it is not a case of dismissal, removal or reduction.
RatioOfTheDecision	I need only point out here that the applicant is not even in "quasi-permanent service".
RatioOfTheDecision	Under rule 3 of the Central Civil Service Temporary Service Rules, 1949 a Government servant can be deemed to be in quasi-permanent service only if there has been a declaration to that effect.
RatioOfTheDecision	No such declaration was issued ini this case for the reasons which I have already mentioned.
RatioOfTheDecision	In those circumstances the observations made by the learned Chief Justice of India in the case quoted above apply with all the vigour to the facts of the present case.
RatioOfTheDecision	In that view of the matter Article 311 of the Constitution does not at all apply to the facts of the present case and Rule 14(c) of the Revised Leave Rules 1933 being part of the Contract and Rules of Service providing for termination of service in due course under its very terms and co ntract cannot be regarded as ultra vires Article 311 of the Constitution as contended by Mr. <NAME>.
RatioOfTheDecision	19.
RatioOfTheDecision	Before I conclude it is necessary to state that the petitioner has not at all been vigilant.
RatioOfTheDecision	The order declaring that he had ceased to be in the employment of the <ORG> is dated 7-5-1956.
RatioOfTheDecision	The petitioner does not move for this Rule until 12-2-1957.
RatioOfTheDecision	He knew that the vacancy also had been filled up when he was served with that order of 7-5-1956 because it stated that the vacancy had been filled up.
RatioOfTheDecision	Administration, in my view, cannot be allowed to wait indefinitely in this manner because an aggrieved person does not choose to move the court with reasonable diligence for redress of his alleged grievance.
RatioOfTheDecision	Even if I were satisfied on the merits of this application, which I am not, to allow this application after this long delay would not only prejudice the administration but the new appointment which has since been made, and would therefore be unjust An effort was made by Mr. <NAME> to explain the delay by sh owing that his client wrote a solicitor's letter.
Fact	But then that solicitor's letter was not written until 14-8-1956.
Fact	In fact even the letter from <ORG> which directed the petitioner to come through the proper channel, namely, the Collector of Central Excise, Calcutta, was dated 5-7-1956 and no step was taken thereafter until 12th February of the following year.
RatioOfTheDecision	Finally, I am satisfied that this is an unmeritorious application.
RatioOfTheDecision	A Government servant who has consistently refused to obey orders of transfer and who has consistently absented himself from duty without being granted leave does not discharge the minimum obligations of a Government servant; and when even under the formal order of the Assistant Collector of Excise dated 17-4-1956 he did not even offer to rejoin within the time of 10 days given to him and does not move the Court until long after the vacancy has been filled up then he is not in my opinion a fit, proper and deserving case to be helped and aided with a constitutional w rit of mandamus or certiorari from this Court.
RatioOfTheDecision	20.
RulingByPresentCourt	I, therefore, dismiss this application and discharge the Rule.
