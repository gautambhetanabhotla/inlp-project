RatioOfTheDecision	It is pointed out that the Act, by s. 3 , empowers the appropriate <ORG> in certain eventualities to appoint <ORG> for the purpose of making an inquiry into any definite matter of public importance and for no other purpose.
RatioOfTheDecision	The contention is that the conduct of an individual person or co <ORG> cannot possibly be a matter of public importance and far less a definite matter of that kind.
RatioOfTheDecision	We are unable to accept this argument as correct.
RatioOfTheDecision	Widespread floods, famine and pestilence may quite easily be a definite matter of public importance urgently calling for an inquiry so as to enable the Government to take appropriate steps to prevent their recurrence in future.
RatioOfTheDecision	The conduct of villagers in cutting the bunds for taking water to their fields during the dry season may cause floods during the rainy season and we can see no reason why such unsocial conduct of villagers of certain villages thus causing floods should not be regarded as a definite matter of public importance.
RatioOfTheDecision	The failure of a big bank resulting in the loss of the life savings of a multitude of men of moderate means is certainly a definite matter of public importance but the conduct of the.
RatioOfTheDecision	persons in charge -and management of such a bank which brought about its collapse is equally a definite matter of public importance.
RatioOfTheDecision	Widespread dacoit ies in parti- cular parts of the country is, no doubt, a definite matter of public importance but we see no reason why the conduct, activities and modes operandi of particular dacoits and thugs notorious for their cruel depredations.
RatioOfTheDecision	should not be regarded as definite matters of public importance urgently requiring a sifting inquiry.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is needless to multiply instances.
RatioOfTheDecision	In each case the question is: is there a definite matter of public importance which calls for an inquiry ?
RatioOfTheDecision	We see no warrant for the proposition that a definite matter of public importance must necessarily mean only some matter involving the public benefit or advantage in the abstract, e. g., public health, sanitation or the like or some public evil or prejudice, e. g., floods, famine or pestilence or the like.
RatioOfTheDecision	Quite conceivably the conduct of an individual person or company or a group of individual persons or companies may assume such a dangerous proportion and may so prejudicially affect or threaten to affect the public well-being as to mak e such conduct a definite matter of public importance urgently calling for a full inquiry.
RatioOfTheDecision	Besides, s. 3 itself authorises the appropriate <ORG> to appoint <ORG> not only for the purpose of making an inquiry into a definite matter of public importance but also for the purpose of performing such functions as, may be specified in the notification.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, the notification is well within the powers conferred on the appropriate <ORG> by s. 3 of the Act and it cannot be questioned on the ground of its going beyond the provisions of the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	Learned counsel for the petitioners immediately replies that in the event of its being held that the notification is within the terms of the Act, the Act itself is ultra vires the Constitution.
RatioOfTheDecision	The validity of the Act is called in question in two ways.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the first place it is said that it was beyond the legislative competency of <ORG> to enact a law conferring such a wide sweep of powers.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is pointed out that <ORG> enacted the Act in  exercise of the legislative powers conferred on it by Art.
RatioOfTheDecision	246 of the Constitution read with entry 94 in List I and entry 45 in List III of the Seventh Schedule to the Constitution.
RatioOfTheDecision	The matters enumerated in entry 94 in List 1, omitting the words not necessary for our purpose, are " inquiries......... for the purpose of any of the matters in this List ", and those enumerated in entry 45 in List III, again omitting the unnecessary words, are " inquiries............ for the purposes of any of the matters specified in List II or List  111. "
RatioOfTheDecision	Confining himself to the entries in so far as they relate to " inquiries ", learned counsel for the petitioners urges that <ORG> may make a law with respect to inquiries but cannot under these entries make a law conferring any power to perform any function other than the power to hold an inquiry.
RatioOfTheDecision	He concedes that, according to the well recognised rule of construction of the provisions of a Constitution, the legislative heads should be construed very liberally and that i t must be assumed that the Constitution intended to give to the appropriate legislature not only the power to legislate with respect to the particular legislative topic but also with respect to all matters ancillary thereto.
RatioOfTheDecision	Indeed the very use of the words " with respect to in Art.
RatioOfTheDecision	246 supports this principle  of liberal interpretation.
RatioOfTheDecision	He, however, points out that the law, which the appropriate legislature is empowered to make under these entries must be with respect to inquiries for the purposes of any of the matters in the relevant lists and it is urged that the words " for the purpose of " make it abundantly clear that the law with respect to inquiries to be made under these two entries must be for the purpose of future legislation with respect to any of the legislative heads in the relevant lists.
RatioOfTheDecision	In other words, the argument is that under these two entries the appropriate legislature may make a law authorising the constitution of a <ORG> or <ORG> to inquire into and ascertain facts so a s to enable such legislature to undertake legislation with respect to any of the legislative topics in the relevant lists to secure some public benefit or advantage or to prevent some evil or harm befalling the public and thereby to protect the public from the same.
RatioOfTheDecision	But if an inquiry becomes necessary for, say, administrative purposes, a law with respect to such an inquiry cannot be made under these two entries.
RatioOfTheDecision	And far less can a law be made with respect to an inquiry into any wrongs alleged to have been committed by an individual person or company or a group of them for the purpose of punishing the suspected delinquent.
RatioOfTheDecision	This argument has found favour with <ORG>, but we are, with great respect, unable to accept this view.
RatioOfTheDecision	To adopt this view will mean adding words to the two entries so as to read " inquiries for the purpose of future legislation with respect to any of the matters in the List or Lists mentioned therein.
RatioOfTheDecision	The matter, however, does not rest here.
RatioOfTheDecision	A careful perusal of the language used in  entry 45 in List III does, in our view, clinch the matter.
RatioOfTheDecision	Entry 45 in List III, which is the Concurrent List, speaks, inter alia, of " inquiries for the purpose of any of the matters in List II or List 111.
RatioOfTheDecision	Under Art.
RatioOfTheDecision	246 read with this entry, <ORG> as well as <ORG> may make a law with respect to " inquiries for the purpose of any of the matters in List II."
RatioOfTheDecision	<ORG>, under Art.
RatioOfTheDecision	246 , has no power to make a law with respect to any of the matters enumerated in List 11.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, when <ORG> makes a law under Art.
RatioOfTheDecision	246 read with., entry 45 in List III with respect to an inquiry for the purposes of any of the matters in List 11, such law can never be one for inquiry for the purpose of future legislation by <ORG> with respect to any of those matters in List 11.
RatioOfTheDecision	Clearly <ORG> can make a law for inquiry for the purpose of any of the matters in List 11 and none the less so though <ORG> cannot legislate with respect to such matters and though none of <ORG> ures wants to Legislate on such matters.
RatioOfTheDecision	In our opinion, therefore, the law to be made by the appropriate legislature with respect to the two legislative entries referred to above may cover inquiries into any aspect of the matters enumerated in any of the lists mentioned therein and is not confined to those matters as mere heads of legislative topic.
RatioOfTheDecision	Quite conceivably the law with respect to inquiries for the purpose of any of the matters in the lists may also be for administrative purposes and the scope of the inquiry under such a law will cover all matters which may properly be regarded as ancillary to such inquiries.
RatioOfTheDecision	The words " for the purposes of " indicate that the scope of the inquiry is not necessarily limited to the particular or specific matters enumerated in any of the entries in the list concerned but may extend to inquiries into collateral matters which may be necessary for the purpose, legislative or otherwise, of those particular matters.
RatioOfTheDecision	We are unable, therefore,to hold that the Inquiry which  may be set up by a law made under these two entries is, in its scope or ambit, limited to future legislative purposes only.
RatioOfTheDecision	Learned counsel then takes us through the different heads of inquiry enumerated in the notification and urges that the inquiry is neither for any legislative nor for any administrative purpose, but is a clear usurpation of the functions of the judiciary.
