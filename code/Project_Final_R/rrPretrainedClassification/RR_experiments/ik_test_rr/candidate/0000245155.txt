Precedent	The relevant portion of that rule is in the following terms:  "<ORG> or <ORG>, if it is satisfied with respect to any particular person that with a view to preventing him from acting in any manner prejudicial to the defence of India, the public safety, the maintenance of public order, His Majesty's relations with foreign powers or Indian States, the maintenance of peaceful conditions in tribal areas or the efficient prosecution of the war it is necessary so to do, may make an order  (a) ........ Precedent	The present cases are quite different.
Precedent	The Bengal Criminal Law Amendment Act , Section 2 (1) does not say that the Government may detain a person with a view to prevent him from doing one or all of several Acts.

RatioOfTheDecision	Before you can detain a person under such a provision of the law you must be satisfied that he belongs to one or other of those categories.

RatioOfTheDecision	In this rule it was not necessary for the Government to form an opinion definitely as to which act such person may do.
RatioOfTheDecision	He may be a person wh o is prone to do one or other of such acts.

Precedent	19.
Precedent	We have already dealt with these Rules on the footing that the Bengal Criminal Law Amendment Act is an Act providing for preventive detention and have shown that even if it were so, the detention orders which are the subject-matter of these Rules are bad.
RatioOfTheDecision	We are of opinion, however, that the Bengal Criminal Law Amendment Act does not deal with preventive detention.
RatioOfTheDecision	The law regarding preventive detention, as we understand it is directed to wards preventing a person from doing something which the Legislatur e thinks should not be done.
RatioOfTheDecision	Nowhere in the Act do we find any expression which indicates that the law is designed for the purpose of preventing a person from doing any thing.
RatioOfTheDecision	We have set out the preamble in the earlier part of the judgment and it contains nothing which would indicate that the law is a preventive measure.
Precedent	Section 2 of the Act, which is the most important section in the Act and which gives powers of detention to <ORG> does not say that these powers are given for the purpose of preventing anything being done.
Precedent	It merely lays down that a person may be detained or dealt with in certain other ways if he is or was a member of a certain kind of association or if he is or was being instigated, or controlled by a member of a certain kind of association or if he is doing or did at any time any act to assist the operation of such association.
Precedent	It nowhere says that a person may be detained or otherwise dealt with in order to pre vent him from doing anything.
Precedent	If you detain a person if he  is a member of an association you are not detaining him in order to prevent him from being a member of the association but you are restricting his liberty because he is a member of such association, a fortiori, if you detain or otherwise restrict the liberty of a person because he was a member of an association, you are not doing anything in order to prevent him from being such member.
Precedent	The same arguments would apply to the provisions contained in Section 2 (1) (ii) and (iii).
Precedent	The law does not lay down that the authority detaining a person or other wise restricting his liberty should be satisfied that the detention is necessary for the prevention of anything.
Precedent	That being so, we are of opinion that the Bengal Criminal Law Amendment Act is not a law of preventive detention.
Precedent	The learned Advocate-General drew our attention to a certain passage in <NAME>'s <ORG>, Edn.
Precedent	IX at page 231 where a certain Irish Act was described as an Act which gave the executive absolute-power of arbitrary and preventive  arrest.
Precedent	We do not think it will serve any useful purpose to discuss this passage of the Irish law referred to.
Precedent	It is dangerous to interpret one law by reference to another unless the terms are identical or so similar as to justify a common deduction.
Precedent	It would be safe and more correct to ascertain the nature of the Bengal Criminal Law Amendment Act by reference to its own provisions.
Precedent	Having regard to those provisions we have no hesitation in saying that the law is not a law providing for preventive detention.
Precedent	20.
Precedent	Now, if it is not such a law, then it is impossible to justify the orders passed in these cases.

RatioOfTheDecision	Thus the detenus have been deprived of their personal liberty without complying with one of th e most important items of procedure laid down in the Constitution Act regarding such deprivation.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the circumstances the order of detention cannot be upheld.
RatioOfTheDecision	21.
RatioOfTheDecision	We shall now deal with the two cases in which persons have been detained under the West Bengal Security Ordinance, 1949.
RatioOfTheDecision	This Ordinance is clearly an Ordinance providing for preventive detention, but in our opinion this Ordinance is void because it is inconsistent with the provisions of the Constitution Act .
RatioOfTheDecision	The Ordinance in Section 22 provides for the detention of a person for a period not exceeding nine months.

RatioOfTheDecision	It says that the Pr ovincial <ORG> may place before a Judge of <ORG> the grounds on which the order of preventive detention is made.

RatioOfTheDecision	The <ORG> if it so chooses need not place before the aforesaid Judge its order of detention.
RatioOfTheDecision	22.
RatioOfTheDecision	Next, we would point out that reference to a Judge of <ORG> is only to be made where an order is passed under Clause (a) of Sub-section (1) of Section 21 of the aforesaid Ordinance, that is to say, in the case of an order directing detention.

RatioOfTheDecision	We shall now deal with the Preventive Detention (Extension of Duration) Order, 1950, made by the President of the Indian Republic.
