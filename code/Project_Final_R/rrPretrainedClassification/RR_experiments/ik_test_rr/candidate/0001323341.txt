Statute	Section 30 of the Act no doubt provides that the Financial <ORG> may by notice in writing demand the payment of full amount advanced to any industrial concern in the event of the happening of certain event s, and the clauses of this section provide for all those circumstances which will entitle the <ORG> to demand the whole amount.
Statute	Section 30 .
RatioOfTheDecision	therefore confers power on the <ORG> to recover the whole amount in addition to the term of the contract itself.
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 31 of the Act provides the procedure for the recovery of the amount.
RatioOfTheDecision	It was contended, on reference to the statement of objects and reasons which was attached to the Bill of this Act when it was presented for enactment, that the scheme of Section 31 provides a special procedure for recovery of the amounts due to a <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	It may not be necessary to go into the obiects and reasons stated when the Bill was introduced.
RatioOfTheDecision	But a perusal of Section 31 itself clearly indicates that instead of the <ORG> being called upon to file a regular civil suit, a special procedure has been prescribed for enforcement of the claims.
Statute	Section 31 provides for an application by the <ORG> on the basis of which proceedings for recovery can bp launched.
Statute	Section 31 is as under:--  "31.
Statute	(1) Where an industrial concern, in breach of any agreement makes any default in repayment of any loan or advance or any instalment thereof or otherwise fails to comply with the terms of its agreement with <ORG> or where <ORG> requires an industrial concern to make immediate repayment of any loan or advance under Section 30 and the industrial concern fails to make such repayment, then, without prejudice to the provisions of Section 29 of this Act and of Section 69 of the Transfer of Property Act.
Statute	1882. any officer of <ORG>, generally or specially authorized by the Board in this behalf may apply to the District Judge within the limits of whose jurisdiction the industrial concern carries on the whole or a substantial part of its business for one or more of the following reliefs, namely:--  (a) for an order for the sale of the property pledged, mortgaged, hypothecated or assigned to <ORG> as security  for the loan or advance; or  (b) for transferring the management of the industrial concern to <ORG>; or  (c) for an ad interim injunction restraining the industrial concern from transferring or removing its machinery or plant or equipment from the premises of the industrial concern without the permission of the Board, where such removal is apprehended.
Statute	(2) An application under Sub-section (1) shall state the nature and extent of the liability of the industrial concern to <ORG>, the ground on which it is made and such other particulars as may be prescribed."
Statute	The three clauses of Sub-section (1) provide for various reliefs which the <ORG> can seek when it makes an application under this section.
Statute	Clauses (a) and (b) of Sub-section (11 are the two reliefs which a <ORG> may claim as final reliefs in the application, where-as Clause (c) provides for certain interim orders in the nature of attachment or injunction.
Statute	Section 32 of the Act prescribes the procedure af ter such an application has been filed before the District Judge.
Statute	Sub-sections (1) and (2) of Section 32 pertain to Passing of interlocutory orders.
Statute	Sub-section (3) provides that if the District Judge so thinks fit he may even examine the officer who makes the application, before the issue of an interlocutory order, Sub-section (4) of Section 32 and the rest of its sub-sections provide for the procedure for final determination Subsections (4), (5) and (6) of Section 32 are as under :  "32.
Statute	..................  (4) At the same time as he passes an order under Sub-section (1).
Statute	the District Judge shall issue to the industrial concern a notice accompanied by copies of the order, the application and the evidence, if any.
Statute	recorded by him calling upon it to show cause on a date to be specified in the notice why the ad interim order of attachment should not be made absolute or the injunction confirmed.
Statute	(5) If no cause is shown on or before the date specified in the notice under Sub-sections (2) and (4).
Statute	the District  Judge shall forthwith make the ad interim order absolute and direct the sale of the attached property or transfer the management of the industrial concern to <ORG> or confirm the injunction, (6) If cause is shown, the District Judge shall proceed to investigate the claim of <ORG> in accordance with the provisions contained in the Code of Civil Procedure.
Statute	1908. in so far as such provisions may be applied thereto."
Statute	Sub-section (4) of Section 32 provides that a notice shall be issued to the industrial concern to show cause why the order passed should not be made absolute, and along with this notice a copy of the order will also be sent.
Statute	Subsection (51 provides that if no cause is shown, the District Judge shall forthwith make the interim order absolute.
Statute	Sub-section (6) provides for the procedure in case cause is shown and it is provided that the District Judge shall proceed to investigate the claim of <ORG> in accordance with the provisions contained i n the Code of Civil Procedure.
Argument	It is on the basis of Sub-section (6) of Section 32 that it is contended that the District Judge was bound to investigate the defence set up by the petitioner because in absence of any investigation about it it could not be said that the claim of the <ORG> has been investigated.
RatioOfTheDecision	The word 'claim', used in Sub-section (6), cannot be said to mean only the monetary part of the claim which the <ORG> is entitled to recover.
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 31 refers to the word 'claim' stating "Special provisions for enforcement of claims by Financial <ORG>."
RatioOfTheDecision	Consequently the word "claim" has been used in these provisions to mean the sum-total of the facts alleged by the <ORG> with demand for a relief under any of the three clau-es of Sub-section (1) of Section 31 .
RatioOfTheDecision	Consequently the question of default and the justification sought by the petitioner would also fall within, the ambit of investigation into the claim.
Statute	This sub-section also brings in the Code of Civil Procedure, and it has  been provided that the District Judge shall proceed to investigate the claim of <ORG> in accordance with the provisions contained in the Civil P. C,  7.
Statute	A similar language has been used in Sub-section (8) of Section 32 , which is as under :  "(8) An order of attachment or sale of property under this section shall be carried into effect as far as practicable in the manner provided in the Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, for the attachment or sale of property in execution of a decree as if <ORG> were the decree-holder."
RatioOfTheDecision	This language was considered by <ORG> in AIR 1972 SC 801 where it was held as under-  "The question which really arises is whether by using the words "in the manner provided in the Code of Civil Procedure" in Section 32(8) the <ORG> intended to include the provisions in the Code dealing with appeals.
RatioOfTheDecision	There is no doubt that under the Code of Civil Procedure an order setting aside or refusing to set aside a sale in execution of a decree is ap pealable under O. XLIII, Rule 1 (i).
RatioOfTheDecision	It is difficult to understand why the scope of the language should be cut down by not including appeals provided tinder the Code of Civil Procedure within the ambit of the words 'in the manner provided in the Code of Civil Procedure'.
RatioOfTheDecision	'Manner' means method of procedure and to provide for an appeal is to provide for a mode of procedure.
RatioOfTheDecision	<ORG> lends huge amounts and we cannot for a moment imagine that it was the intention of the <ORG> to make the order of sale of properly, passed by the District Judge, final and only subject to an appeal to <ORG> under Article 136 of the Constitution.''
RatioOfTheDecision	It cannot, therefore, be doubted that when the <ORG> used this language to bring in the Code of Civil Procedure, the relevant provisions of the Code were made applicable.
Statute	In AIR 1972 Pat 83 a question arose about the jurisdiction of the District Judge under Section 30 of the Industrial Finance Corporation Act, 1948, which is more or less analo gous to Section 32 of the present Act, and it was observed as under-  "The question, therefore, that arises for our consideration, is whether the proceedings under Section 30 of the Act are the proceedings contemplated by Section 141 of the Code.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the proceeding under Section 30 of the Act, the <ORG> in the circumstances mentioned therein has to apply to the District Judge for the relief or reliefs stated above.
RatioOfTheDecision	In a suit also, a party has to make out a cause of action and has to claim a certain relief and, after the trial, the Court passes a decree in favour of the plaintiff or dismisses the suit.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, the special provisions for enforcement of claims by the <ORG> are very similar to the proceedings in the nature of the suit.
RatioOfTheDecision	I am, therefore, of opinion that a proceeding under this section is a proceeding of the kind contemplated by Section 141 of the Code, and, as such, the District Judge, while acting under that section, had the power to appoint a receiver under Order 40, Rule 1 of the C ode."
RatioOfTheDecision	Consequently it cannot be doubted that while enquiring into the claim as provided in subsection (6), the District Judge ought to have investigated into the circumstances pleaded by the petitioner as he had jurisdiction under Order 20, Rule 11, Civil P. C. to consider the case of the petitioner.

Statute	Sub-section (7) of Section 32 , which provides the reliefs which the District Judge can grant, is as under-  "(7) After making an investigation under Sub-section (6), the District Judge may-  (a) confirm the order of attachment and direct the sale of the attached property;  (b) vary the order of attachment so as to release a portion of the property from attachment and direct the sale of the remainder of the attached property;  (c) release the property from attachment;  (d) confirm or dissolve the injunction; or  (e) transfer the management of the industrial concern to <ORG> or reject the claim made in this behalf;  Provided that when making an order under Clause (c), the District Judge may  make such further orders as he thinks necessary to protect the interests of <ORG> and may apportion the costs of the proceedings in such manner as thinks fit:  Provided further that unless <ORG> intimates to the District Judge that it will not appeal against any order releasing any property from attachment, such order shall not be given effect to, until the expiry of the period fixed under Sub-section (9) within which an appeal may be preferred or, if an appeal is preferred, unless <ORG> otherwise directs until the appeal is disposed of."
RatioOfTheDecision	These reliefs which a District Judge can grant while deciding an application under Section 31 of the Act clearly go to show that the District Judge can also release a property from attachment, and while so doing he can also pass orders for protecting the interests of <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	This also shows that the District Judge can even pass orders which do not fall within any one of the reliefs that may be claimed by t he <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	As already discussed, the application of the provisions of the Code of Civil Procedure has already been discussed by <ORG> in the decision referred to above.
RatioOfTheDecision	In this view of the matter, it cannot be doubted that the District Judge ought to have investigated the objections raised by the petitioner to the claim of the respondent <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	9.
Argument	It was contended that as the alternative remedy of appeal, which was available to the petitioner, was not availed of by him, relief under Article 226 of the Constitution could not be granted.
Argument	In AIR 1971 SC 33, this question was considered by <ORG> and it was observed that-  "An order under Section 35 of the Income-tax Act is not appealable.
Argument	It is true that a petition to revise the order could be moved before the Commissioner of Income-tax.
Argument	But <NAME> moved a petition in <ORG> and <ORG> entertained that petition.
Argument	If <ORG> had not entertained his petition, <NAME> could have move d the Commissioner in revision, because at the date on which the petition was moved the period prescribed by Section 33A of the Act had not expired.
Argument	We are unable to hold that because a revision application could have been moved for an order correcting the order of the Income-tax Officer under Section 35 , but was not moved, <ORG> would be justified in dismissing as not maintainable the petition, which was entertained and was heard on the merits."
Argument	In this view of the matter, it would not be right to reject this petition at this stage.
Argument	<NAME>, appearing for the respondent-Corporation, contended that in the <ORG> case referred to above on the date, on which the writ petition was filed in <ORG> a revision could be filed, and as <ORG> admitted the petition the <ORG> felt that thereafter the petition could not be dismissed on that ground alone.
RatioOfTheDecision	But, according to the learned counsel, in the present case the time for appeal had already run out, and, therefore, the alte rnative remedy having not been availed of, this petition could not be entertained.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is no doubt true that the time provided for an appeal had run out when this petition was filed.
RatioOfTheDecision	But if the petitioner had so chosen, he could have preferred an appeal and sought condonation.
RatioOfTheDecision	In that view of the matter, the principles laid down by <ORG> will fully apply to the present case also, and no distinction can be drawn only on that ground.
RatioOfTheDecision	It appears that what <ORG> felt was that if at the time of motion hearing the petition had not been entertained, the petitioner could have sought the alternative remedy, but that not having been done, after lapse of long time it would not be fit to refuse to grant relief as now it would not be possible for the petitioner to avail of the alternative remedy also.
RatioOfTheDecision	Apart from that, the question involved in the present case is about the jurisdiction of the District Judge, and by refusing to investigate into the objections raised by the petitioner the District Judg e had refused to exercise jurisdiction in him by law.
RatioOfTheDecision	Consequently this is a ground on which a petition under Article 226 or even under Article 227 of the Constitution would be maintainable.
RatioOfTheDecision	Consequently the petition cannot also be thrown out on the objection of maintainability.
