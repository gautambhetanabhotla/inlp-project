Argument	Rejecting the argument <NAME>, <NAME>, observes as follows :-  No doubt if the, Act does affect agricultural land in the Governors' Provinces, it was beyond the competence of the <ORG> to enact it; and whether or not it does so must depend upon the meaning which is to be given to the word 'property' in the Act.
Argument	If that word necessarily and inevitably comprises all forms of property, including agricultural land, then clearly the Act went beyond the powers of the L egislature ; but when a <ORG> with limited and restricted powers makes use of a word of such wide and general import, the presumption must surely be that it is using it with reference to that kind of property with respect to which it is competent to legislate, and to no other.

RatioOfTheDecision	The legal proposition, therefore, is well settled that the presumption is in favour of the enactments being within the constitutional limits of the enacting authority.
RatioOfTheDecision	8.
RatioOfTheDecision	But the revision petitioners' learned Advocate overlooks that the presumption is about  the enactment being within all constitutional limitations on <ORG> and such limitations would cover Article 304(a) as well.
RatioOfTheDecision	There under, <ORG>s may, by law, impose on goods imported from other States or the Union territories, any tax, to which similar goods manufactured or produced in that State are subject, so, however, as not to discriminate between goods so imported and goods so manufactured or produced.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is therefore clear that <ORG>, in passing <CITATION_8778749> of the Act, cannot be presumed to have enacted something that would be violative of Article 304(a) ; and yet that would exactly be the position we are asked to place on the word "land" in <CITATION_8778749> ; for, the produces from rubber estates within the Madras State would not be charged to sales tax if sold at Fort Cochin ; but the produces from the rubber estates within Travancore-Cochin area would be liable, because they would not be produces of land that had been intended to be excluded.
Argument	Indeed the same owner would get the benefit under the provision if the produce be from plantations within the Madras State, but would be liable should his goods from estates in the adjoining Travancore-Cochin State come to be sold at Fort Cochin.
Argument	When confronted with such a position and the presumption of the constitutionality precluding the <ORG> having such an intention, the petitioners' Advocate was compelled to argue that the restriction under Article 304(a) is against levying tax at the point of import, and not when the imported goods are being sold in the market.
Argument	We are afraid that such an interpretation is not justified; and, there are overwhelming decisions declining to place such a restricted interpretation on Article 304(a).
Argument	9.
Argument	The petitioners in <CITATION_1319737> A.I.R.
Argument	1956 M.B.
Argument	214 at p. 218 were carrying on the business of sale and purchase of pugrees, which were mainly manufactured in Uttar Pradesh and Rajasthan ; and the complaint was that the State of Madhya Bharat, having exempted  similar goods under a notification, pugrees manufactured by similar process in other <ORG>s cannot be subjected to a tax, as that would offend Article 304.
Argument	<NAME>, J., upholding the objection, observes as follows:-  The wording of Article 304 of the Constitution indicates that the taxing power of the <ORG> is only valid so far as it does not work discrimination as against similar goods coming from other <ORG>s.
Argument	The moment it begins to work discrimination, the taxing power is rendered invalid to the extent of discrimination.
Argument	Learned Advocate-General contended that the law imposing sales tax is valid.
RatioOfTheDecision	What is invalid is the discriminatory nature of exemption granted.
RatioOfTheDecision	The result will be that the exemption will cease to operate and the similar goods made at the aforesaid places in Madhya Bharat will be subjected to tax.
RatioOfTheDecision	The tax on pugrees imported from Uttar Pradesh or Rajasthan will not be free from taxation.
RatioOfTheDecision	I am unable to agree with this contention.
RatioOfTheDecision	The Constitution permits taxation upon goods imported fro m <ORG>s other than taxing <ORG>s within certain defined limits as laid down in Article 304 of the Constitution.
RatioOfTheDecision	The moment the limits are crossed by whatever means, whether by the provision of the <ORG> law passed by the <ORG>, or under any power vesting in an authority by reason of power delegated to it the taxing power is gone.
RatioOfTheDecision	And when the taxing power to the extent of discrimination is gone, the tax becomes invalid and cannot be recovered.
