RatioOfTheDecision	Before adverting to the submissions made before us we would broadly refer to a few fundamental principles regarding the competence of the respective legislatures to enact laws and as to which law would prevail in case of inconsistency between the laws made by the <ORG> and the laws made by the State L egislature.
RatioOfTheDecision	The principles are being referred to in the context of the controversy involved in the present appeals.
RatioOfTheDecision	India being <ORG> has <ORG> (<ORG>) and <ORG> for framing laws.
RatioOfTheDecision	Legislative fields in which the union or the <ORG> legislatures can frame laws are prescribed in the three lists contained in the Seventh Schedule to the Constitution of India  Union List (List I); <ORG> List (List II); and Concurrent List (List III ).
Precedent	Source of power for enacting laws relating to the three lists is to be found in Articles 245 and 246 of the Constitution of India.
Precedent	Article 245 provides that subject to the provisions of the Constitution, <ORG> may make laws for the whole or any part of the territory of India, and the <ORG> of a <ORG> may make laws for the whole or any part of the <ORG>.
Statute	Law made by the <ORG> shall not be deemed to be invalid on the ground that it would have extra- territorial operation.
Statute	Article 246 reads:  "246.
Statute	Subject-matter of laws mad e by <ORG> and by the <ORG>s of States.- (1) Notwithstanding anything in clauses (2) and (3), <ORG> has exclusive power to make laws with respect to any of the matters enumerated in List I in the Seventh Schedule (in this Constitution referred to as the "Union List").
Statute	(2) Notwithstanding anything in clause (3), <ORG> and, subject to clause (1), the <ORG> of any State also, have power to make laws with respect to any of the matters enumerated in List III in the Seventh Schedule (in this Constitution referred to as the "Concurrent List").
Statute	(3) Subject to clauses (1) and (2), the <ORG> of any State has exclusive power to make laws for such State or any part thereof with respect to any of the matters enumerated in List II in the Seventh Schedule (in this Constitution referred to as the 'State List').
Statute	(4) <ORG> has power to make laws with respect to any matter for any part of the territory of India not included in a State notwithstanding that such matter is a matter enumerat ed in the State List."

RatioOfTheDecision	In a federal Constitution, in which there is a division of legislative powers between the <ORG> and the Provincial <ORG>s, controversies often arise as to whether one or the other legislature is not exceeding its legislative power, and encroaching on the other's constitutional legislative power.
RatioOfTheDecision	To resolve the dispute as to which law would prevail in a case where both the <ORG> as well as <ORG> have the competence to enact laws, Article 254 provides that if any provision of a law made by the <ORG> of a State is repugnant to any provision of law made by <ORG> which <ORG> is competent t o enact, or to any provision of an existing law with respect to one of the matters enumerated in the Concurrent List, then, subject to the provisions of clause (2), the law made by <ORG> shall prevail and the law made by the <ORG> of the State shall to the extent of the repugnancy be void.
RatioOfTheDecision	Clause (2) provides that where a law made by the <ORG> of a State with respect to one of the matters enumerated in the Concurrent List contains any provisions repugnant to the provisions of an earlier law made by the <ORG> or an existing law with respect to the matters, then, the law so made by the <ORG> of such State shall, if it has been reserved for the consideration of the President and has received his assent, prevail in that State.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is likely to happen from time to time that enactment though purporting to deal with a subject in one list touches also on a subject in another list and prima facie looks as if one legislature is impinging on the legislative field of the another <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	T his may result in large number of statutes being declared unconstitutional because the legislature enacting law may appear to have legislated in a field reserved for the other legislature.
RatioOfTheDecision	To examine whether a legislation has impinged in the field of other legislatures, in fact or in substance, or is incidental, keeping in view the true nature of the enactment, the <ORG> have evolved the doctrine of "pith and substance" for the purpose of determining whether it is legislation with respect to matters in one list or the other.
RatioOfTheDecision	Where the question for determination is whether a particular law relates to a particular subject mentioned in one list or the other, the courts look into the substance of the enactment.
RatioOfTheDecision	Thus, if the substance of enactment falls within Union List then the incidental encroachment by the enactment on the State List would not make it invalid.
RatioOfTheDecision	This principle came come to be established by <ORG> when it determined appeals from Canada or Australia involving the question of legislati ve competence of the federation or the <ORG> in those countries.
RatioOfTheDecision	This doctrine came to be established in India and derives its genesis from the approach adopted by the <ORG> including <ORG> in dealing with controversies arising in other federations.
RatioOfTheDecision	For applying the principle of "pith and substance" regard is to be had (i) to the enactment as a whole, (ii) to its main objects, and (iii) to the scope and effect of its provisions.
Precedent	For this see: <ORG> Vs. State of Kerala, <ORG> 1981 SC 1863; State of Rajasthan Vs. <NAME>, <ORG> 1959 SC 544; <NAME> Vs. State of Rajasthan, 1955 (2) SCR 303, Delhi Cloth and General Mills C o. Ltd.

RulingByLowerCourt	A combined reading of Sections 2(4A) and 2(6) makes it clear that even if the appellant No.

RatioOfTheDecision	The impugned Act and the Central Act s in the instant case operate in two different fields without encroaching upon each other's field in as much as the true nature and character of the impugned State Act is to acquire the undertaking and pay compensation as provided in the Act whereas both the Central Act s (Acts of 1910 and 1948) have made general provisions with regard to supply and use of electrical energy.
