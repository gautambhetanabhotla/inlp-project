RatioOfTheDecision	It is well settled by a long course of decisions of this Court that in the case of a probationer or a temporary employee, who has no right to the post, such a termination of his services is valid and does not attract the provisions of Art.
RatioOfTheDecision	311 of the Constitution.
Statute	In the case of <CITATION_1382698> , the matter was considered in all its aspect s by <ORG> comprising seven Judges of this <ORG> and the <ORG> adumbrated the following pro- positions:-  "Before a probationer is confirmed the authority concerned is under an obligation to consider whether the work of the probationer is satisfactory or whether he is suitable for the post.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the absence of any Rules governing a probationer in this respect the authority may come to the conclusion that on account of inadequacy for the job or for any temperamental or other object not involving moral turpitude the probationer is unsuitable for the job and hence must be discharged.
RatioOfTheDecision	No punishment is involved in this.
RatioOfTheDecision	The fact of holding an inquiry is not always conclusive.
RatioOfTheDecision	What is decisive is whether the order is really by way of punishment.
RatioOfTheDecision	A probationer whose terms of service provided that it could be terminated without any notice and without any cause being assigned could hot claim the protection of Article 311 (2) ....   An order terminating the services of a temporary servant or probationer un der the Rules of Employment and without anything more will not attract Article 311.
RatioOfTheDecision	Where a departmental enquiry is contemplated and if an enquiry is not in Act proceeded with Article 311 will not be attracted unless it can he shown that the order though unexceptionable in form is made following a report based on misconduct."
RatioOfTheDecision	Similarly, the matter was previously considered in <CITATION_1270113> where the following observations were "Shortly put, the principle is that when a servant has right to a post or to a rank either under the terms of the contract of employment, express or implied, or under the rules governing the conditions of his service, the termination of the service of such a servant or his reduction to a lower post is by itself and prima facie a punishment, for it operates as a forfeiture of his right to hold that post or that rank and to get the emoluments and other benefits attached thereto.
RatioOfTheDecision	But if the servant has no right to the post, as where he is appointed to a post, permanent or temporary e ither on probation or on an officiating basis and whose temporary service has not ripened into a quasi permanent service as defined in the Temporary Service Rules, the termination of his employment does not deprive him of any right and can not, therefore, by itself be a punishment.
RatioOfTheDecision	One test for deter mining whether the termination of the service of a <ORG> men servant is by way of punishment is to ascertain whether the servant, but for such termination, had the right to hold the post.
RatioOfTheDecision	If he had a right to the post as in the three cases hereinbefore mentioned, the termination of his service will by itself be a punishment and he will be entitled to the protection of Article 311.
RatioOfTheDecision	In other words and broadly speaking Art.
RatioOfTheDecision	311(2) will apply to those cases where the <ORG>ment servant, had he been employed by a private employer, will be entitled to maintain an action for wrongful dismissal, removal or reduction in rank.
RatioOfTheDecision	To put it in another way, if the <ORG>ment has, by contract, express or implied, or, under the  rules, the right to terminate the employment at any time, then such termination in the manner provided by the contract or the rules, is, prima facie and per se, not a punishment and does not attract the provisions of Art.
Argument	311."

RatioOfTheDecision	The matter was again considered at great length by a recent decision of this <ORG> in the case of <CITATION_443992> ., where <ORG> observed thus:  "Ordinarily and generally the rule laid down in most of the cases by this <ORG> is that you have to look to the order on the face of it and find whether it casts any stigma on the Government servant.

RatioOfTheDecision	Applying the principles enunciated by this <ORG> in various cases to the facts of the present case, the position is that the order impugned is prima facie an order of termination simpliciter without involving any stigma.
RatioOfTheDecision	The order does not in any way involve any  evil consequences and is an order of discharge simpliciter of the respondent who was a probationer and had no right to the service.
RatioOfTheDecision	The respondent has not been able to make out any strong case for this Court to delve into the documents, materials in order to determine a case of victimisation or one of punishment.
RatioOfTheDecision	612  Reliance was, however, placed by the respondent on a decision of this Court in the case of <CITATION_32468> , where it was held that although termination of the service of a person holding the post on probation cannot be said to deprive him of any right to the post and is no punishment but where instead of terminating a person's service the employer choose to hold an enquiry into his alleged misconduct and proceeds by way of a punishment, such a course involves a stigma and an order of termination is bad.

RatioOfTheDecision	The short history of the service of the respondent clearly shows that his work had never been satisfactory and he was not found suitable for being retaine d in service and that is why even e though some sort of an enquiry was started, it was not proceeded with and no punishment was inflicted on him.

RatioOfTheDecision	311 of the Constitution.

RatioOfTheDecision	For these reasons, therefore, we are satisfied that the order terminating the services of the respondent was valid and did not involve any stigma and was fully justified in the facts and circumstances of the present case.
