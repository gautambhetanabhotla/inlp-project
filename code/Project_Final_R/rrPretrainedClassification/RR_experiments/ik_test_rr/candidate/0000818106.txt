Precedent	In directing the jury as to the meaning and effect of the Explanation in Section 124A , Mr. Justice <NAME> said:  Observe first that, as I have already said, while the first clause shows affirmatively what the offence made punishable by the section is, the explanation states negatively what it is not.

RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore its object is to protect from the condemnation pronounced by the first clause certain acts which it distinguishes from the disloyal attempts which the first clause deals with.
RatioOfTheDecision	The next and most important point for y ou to bear in mind is that the thing protected by the explanation is 'the making of comments on the measures of the <ORG> with a certain intention.'
RatioOfTheDecision	This shows that the explanation has a strictly defined and limited scope.
RatioOfTheDecision	Observe that it has no application whatever unless you come to the conclusion that the writings in question can fairly and reasonably be construed as 'the making of comments on the measures of the <ORG>.'
RatioOfTheDecision	It does not apply to any sort of writing except that.
RatioOfTheDecision	It does not apply to any writing which consists not merely of comments upon <ORG> measures, but of attacks upon the <ORG> itself.

RatioOfTheDecision	In the next place, supposing that you are satisfied that these writings can fairly and reasonably be construed as 'comments on the measures of the Government' and not as attacks upon the Government itself, still you cannot apply the explanation unless you believe that such comments were made with the intention of exciting only 'such a disapprobation of the measures of the Government as is compatible with a disposition to render obedience to the lawful authority of the Government and to support the lawful authority of the Government against unlawful attempts to subvert or resist that authority.'
RatioOfTheDecision	This, you will see, draws a distinction between attempting to excite feelings of 'disaffection' to the Government, and intending to excite only a certain species of 'disapprobation' of Government measures; and protects the latter only.
RatioOfTheDecision	What is the mean ing of 'disapprobation' of <ORG> measures as contrasted with 'disaffection' to the <ORG>?
RatioOfTheDecision	I agree with Sir <NAME> that while disaffection means the absence of affection, or enmity, disapprobation means simply disapproval; and that it is quite possible to like or be loyal to anyone, whether an individual or a <ORG>, and at the same time to disapprove strongly of his or its measures.

RatioOfTheDecision	It shows clearly what a public speaker or writer may do, and what he may not do.

RatioOfTheDecision	The object of the explanation is to protect honest journalism and bond fide criticism of public measures and institutions with a view to their improvement, and to the remedying of grievances and abuses, and to distinguish this from attempts, whether open or disguised, to make the people hate their rulers.
RatioOfTheDecision	So long as a journalist observes this distinction he has nothing to fear.
RatioOfTheDecision	It seems to me that this view of the law secures all the liberty which any reasonable man can desire , and that to allow more would be culpable weakness, and fatal to the interest not only of the <ORG> but of the people.
RatioOfTheDecision	But now there are other words in the explanation which we have still to consider.
RatioOfTheDecision	To come within the protection of the explanation, a writing must not only be the making of comments on <ORG> measures with the intention of exciting only disapprobation of them as distinguished from disaffection to the <ORG>, but the disapprobation must be 'compatible' with a disposition to render obedience to the lawful authority of the <ORG> and to support the lawful authority of the <ORG> against unlawful attempts to subvert or resist that authority.
RatioOfTheDecision	What that means is that even exciting disapprobation of <ORG> measures may be carried too far.
RatioOfTheDecision	For instance, if a man published comments upon <ORG> measures which were not merely severe, unreasonable or unfair, but so violent and bitter, or accompanied by such appeals to political or religious fanaticism, or addressed to ignor ant people at a time of great public excitement, that persons reading those comments would carry their feelings of hostility beyond the <ORG> measures to their author, the <ORG>, and would become indisposed to obey and support the <ORG>, and if it could fairly be gathered from the writing as a whole that the writer or publisher intended these results to follow, then he would be guilty under the section and would not be protected by the explanation.

Precedent	And lastly, it is a disposition to support the Government against all such unlawful attempts whenever occasion may arise, not only against any particular unlawful attempt, proceeding or impending at the time of the publication.
