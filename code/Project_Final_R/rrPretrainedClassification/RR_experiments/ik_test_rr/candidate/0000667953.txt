RatioOfTheDecision	5.
RatioOfTheDecision	I shall deal with the point of sanction in the last.
RatioOfTheDecision	In my opinion, there is no substance in the first point urged on behalf of the appellant.
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 5A of Act II of 1947, broadly speaking, merely modifies the provisions of Section 156 of the Code of Criminal Procedure and provides that notwithstanding anything contained in the said Code, no police officer below the rank of the officers specified in Clauses (a) to (c) of Section 5A "shall investigate any offence punishable under Section 161 , Section 185 or Section 165 A of the 1.
Fact	P. C. (Act XLV of 1860) or under Sub-section (2) of Section 5 of this Act, without the order of a Presidency Magistrate or a Magistrate of the first class, as the case may be, or make any arrest therefor without a warrant."
Fact	The prohibitory command of the <ORG> contained in Section 6 of Act II of 1947 does not say that the <ORG> shall not take cognizance of an offence except upon a police report submitted after investigation in accordance with the p rovisions of Section 5A .
Precedent	Sub-section (1) of Section 8 of Act XLVI of 1952 states:  "8(1) A special Judge may take cognizance of offences without the accused being committed to him for trial and in trying the accused persons, shall follow the procedure prescribed by the Code of Criminal Procedure, 1898 (Act V of 1898) for the trial of warrant cases by Magistrates."
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 8(1) does not provide as to in what manner and on what material the Special Judge may take cognizance of the offences referred to therein.
RatioOfTheDecision	For this one has to go to the provisions of Sub-section (1) of Section 190 of the Code of Criminal Procedure.
RatioOfTheDecision	I find no sufficient reason to import the provisions of Clause (b) of Sub-section (1) of Section 190 and to hold that the Special Judge may take cognizance of the offence only upon a report in writing of the facts of the offence made by any police officer and not otherwise.
RatioOfTheDecision	It, therefore, follows that he can take cognizance upon receiving a complaint of the facts which constitute the offence or  even upon information received from any person other than a police officer or upon his own knowledge of suspicion that the offence has been committed.
RatioOfTheDecision	That being so, the taking of the cognizance by the Special Judge upon the complaint filed by the Circle Inspector cannot be held to be illegal or without jurisdiction.
RatioOfTheDecision	Learned counsel when confronted with the various aspects of the matter fairly conceded that no other reasonable view of the law was possible to be taken except the one I have expressed above.
Precedent	Some support may be lent to this view from the judgment of <NAME>, in <CITATION_173056> , AIR 1955 Pat 317, wherein cognizance seems to have been taken by the Special Judge in accordance with the provisions of law contained in Clause (c) of Sub-section (1) of Section 190 of the Code of Criminal Procedure.

RatioOfTheDecision	On the point of sanction, however, the appeal has got to succeed and it has got to he held that the sanction is invalid.
Fact	The sanction (Ext.

Fact	The note and recommendation of the Additional Collector dated 27th December, 1959, spoken of in the order of sanction of the District Magistrate and Collector has not been produced by the prosecution.
Fact	The evidence of the Circle Officer is that after the 12th December, "1959, when the appellant failed to file his explanation, he had discussed the matter with the Additional Collector but he has not deposed to the fact as to what papers were prepared by him and what facts were stated in the note which was placed before the Collector.
RatioOfTheDecision	The "gist of evidence against the <NAME>," referred to in the sanction order, has also not been connected with the evidence of three witnesses recorded by the Circle Officer which has been produced in the case as Exhibits 7 series.
RatioOfTheDecision	Nobody on behalf of the prosecution has deposed to the fact that the "gist of evidence" contained in Exhibits 7 series was placed before the Collector at t he time when he made the order of sanction for the prosecution of the appellant.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is no doubt true that in the order the Collector has said that he was "fully satisfied after due consideration of the facts of the case" that there existed a prima facie case for the grant of sanction, and, therefore, he was according sanction but the prosecution has failed to adduce any evidence in Court to show as to what were the facts which were placed before the Collector.
RatioOfTheDecision	17.
RatioOfTheDecision	<CITATION_1841408> , AIR 1948 P C 82, Sir <NAME>, delivering the judgment of the Board, has pointed out, with reference to the provisions of Clause 23 of the Cotton Cloth and Yarn (Control) Order which are in pari materia with the provisions of Section 6 of Act II of 1947, that:  "It is plainly desirable that the facts should be referred to on the face of the sanction, but this is not essential, since Clause 23 does not require the sanction to be in any particular form, nor even to be in writing.
RatioOfTheDecision	But if the facts constituting the offence cha rged are not shown on the face of the sanction, the prosecution must prove the extraneous evidence that those facts were placed before the sanctioning authority."
RatioOfTheDecision	It has further been pointed out:  "The sanction to prosecute is an important matter; it constitutes a condition precedent to the institution of the prosecution and the Government have an absolute discretion to grant or withhold their sanction.
RatioOfTheDecision	They are not, as <ORG> seems to have thought, concerned merely to see that the evidence discloses a prima facie case against the person sought to be prosecuted.
RatioOfTheDecision	They can refuse sanction on any ground which commends itself to them, for example, that on political or economic grounds they regard a prosecution as inexpedient."
RatioOfTheDecision	<CITATION_1440006> AIR 1954 SC 637 following the Morarka's case, AIR 1948 PC 82 decided by <ORG>, it has been pointed out:  "The burden of proving that the requisite sanction has been obtained rests on the prosecution, and such burden includes proof that the sanctionin g authority had given the sanction in reference to the facts on which the proposed prosecution was to be based; and these facts might appear on the face of the sanction or might be proved by extraneous evidence."
RatioOfTheDecision	The case in hand is slightly distinguishable from the two cases referred to above in that in the sanction orders of those cases it was not stated that <ORG> had considered the facts of those cases.
RatioOfTheDecision	None the less, I am of the view that in absence of the statement of any fact in the order of sanction it was incumbent upon the prosecution to satisfy the court that the facts upon which the sanction order was obtained by <ORG> were those in respect of which the delinquent public servant has been charged with in court.
RatioOfTheDecision	In absence of any evidence to that effect it is difficult to hold that the sanction order was made by the Collector after all the facts concerning this case had been placed before him.
RatioOfTheDecision	From a layman's point of view, one is tempted to think that the fa cts considered by the Collector must have been the facts in relation to this case.
RatioOfTheDecision	But a court of law has got to take the strict legal view of the matter specially in regard to the order of sanction which is a condition precedent, as pointed out by <ORG> in <NAME>'s case, AIR 1948 P C 82 for launching prosecution against the delinquent public servant.
RatioOfTheDecision	In absence of any evidence as to the missing links either in the form of the note and recommendation of the Additional Collector or the gist of evidence or any other paper showing that the facts concerning this particular case were placed before the Collector before his order of sanction was obtained, I find it difficult to hold that the order of sanction was validly obtained after placing all the facts of the case.
Argument	18.
Argument	Reliance was placed on behalf of the <ORG> on an unreported decision of this <ORG>, to which I was a party, in <ORG> v. <ORG>, Criminal Appeal No.
Argument	262 of 1958 D/-9-7-1958 (Pat), and to another <ORG> decision of this <ORG>,  to which also I was a party, in <CITATION_142176939> , 1961 (1) Cri L J 544 (Pat).
RatioOfTheDecision	With reference to the facts of both those cases, it would be noticed that the documents on the basis of which sanction orders had been obtained in those cases were produced and got exhibited by the prosecution in c6urt and those documents did contain the facts of the case.
RatioOfTheDecision	The test, therefore, laid down by <ORG> and <ORG> in the two cases, referred to above, was satisfied in both those cases, but in my opinion it is not satisfied in the case in hand.
RatioOfTheDecision	I would, therefore, hold that the sanction (Exhibit 10) was invalid and taking cognizance by the learned Special Judge on the basis of such a sanction was without jurisdiction.
RatioOfTheDecision	The trial was, therefore, vitiated.
RatioOfTheDecision	Upon this ground alone the conviction of the appellant has got to be set aside.
RatioOfTheDecision	19.
RulingByPresentCourt	In the result, I allow the appeal and set aside the conviction of and the sentence imposed upon the appellant.
