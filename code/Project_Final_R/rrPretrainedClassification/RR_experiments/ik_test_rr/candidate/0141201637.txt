Argument	6.
RatioOfTheDecision	The fact that the issue relating to increase of compensation is pending in appeals before <ORG> in pursuance of the order of remand by this <ORG>, is not in dispute.
RatioOfTheDecision	The quantum of compensation will have to be decided in those appeals and not in a writ petition.
RatioOfTheDecision	As on date, there is no order either in the appeal or the writ petition determining any amount (other than what was awarded by the Reference <ORG>) as due to the respondents.
RatioOfTheDecision	The contention and prayer of the respondents in the writ petition that fresh notifications should be issued regarding the acquisitions and the compensation should be determined with reference to the current rates as on the date of such fresh notification and not as on 18.8.1981, is a matter that is yet to be decided in the writ petitio n. As both the writ petition and the appeals are pending, it cannot be said that there is any delay on the part of the state government or its officers in effecting payment of compensation.
RatioOfTheDecision	The delay at present is in fact on account of the pendency of the matters before <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	If <ORG> was of the view that the matter was getting unnecessarily delayed, or that any injustice had been caused to the land owners, it ought to have heard the writ petition finally and decided the dispute on merits instead of listing the matter on several days and asking different senior officers of the state government to be present and virtually intimidate them to agree for a settlement by paying compensation at current market value instead of with reference to 18.8.1981.
RatioOfTheDecision	The procedure and method adopted by <ORG> of <ORG>, to say the least, is improper and requires to be deprecated.
RatioOfTheDecision	7.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is a matter of concern that there is a growing trend among a few Judges of <ORG> to routinely  and frequently require the presence, in court, of senior officers of the government and local and other authorities, including officers of the level of Secretaries, for perceived non-compliance with its suggestions or to seek insignificant clarifications.
RatioOfTheDecision	The power of <ORG> under Article 226 is no doubt very wide.
RatioOfTheDecision	It can issue to any person or authority or government, directions, orders, writs for enforcement of fundamental rights or for any other purpose.
RatioOfTheDecision	<ORG> has the power to summon or require the personal presence of any officer, to assist the court to render justice or arrive at a proper decision.
RatioOfTheDecision	But there are well settled norms and procedures for exercise of such power.
RatioOfTheDecision	8.
RatioOfTheDecision	This court has repeatedly noticed that the real power of courts is not in passing decrees and orders, nor in punishing offenders and contemnors, nor in summoning the presence of senior officers, but in the trust, faith and confidence of the common man in the judiciary.
RatioOfTheDecision	Such trust and confidence should not be fritt ered away by unnecessary and unwarranted show or exercise of power.
RatioOfTheDecision	Greater the power, greater should be the responsibility in exercising such power.
RatioOfTheDecision	The normal procedure in writ petitions is to hear the parties through their counsel who are instructed in the matter, and decide them by examining the pleadings/affidavit/evidence/documents/material.
RatioOfTheDecision	Where the court seeks any information about the compliance with any of its directions, it is furnished by affidavits or reports supported by relevant documents.
RatioOfTheDecision	Requiring the presence of the senior officers of the government in court should be as a last resort, in rare and exceptional cases, where such presence is absolutely necessary, as for example, where it is necessary to seek assistance in explaining complex policy or technical issues, which the counsel is not able to explain properly.
RatioOfTheDecision	The court may also require personal attendance of the officers, where it finds that any officer is deliberately or with ulterior motives withholding any specific information requ ired by the court which he is legally bound to provide or has misrepresented or suppressed the correct position.
RatioOfTheDecision	10    9.
RatioOfTheDecision	Where the <ORG> has a definite policy or taken a specific stand and that has been clearly explained by way of affidavit, the court should not attempt to impose a contrary view by way of suggestions or proposals for settlement.
RatioOfTheDecision	A court can of course express its views and issue directions through its reasoned orders, subject to limitations in regard to interference in matters of policy.
RatioOfTheDecision	But it should not, and in fact, it cannot attempt to impose its views by asking an unwilling party to settle on the terms suggested by it.
RatioOfTheDecision	At all events the courts should avoid directing the senior officers to be present in court to settle the grievances of individual litigants for whom the court may have sympathy.
RatioOfTheDecision	The court should realize that the state has its own priorities, policies and compulsions which may result in a particular stand.
RatioOfTheDecision	Merely because the court does not like such a stand, it cannot summ on or call the senior officers time and again to court or issue threatening show cause notices.
RatioOfTheDecision	The senior officers of the government are in-charge of the administration of the <ORG>, have their own busy schedules.
RatioOfTheDecision	The court should desist from calling them for all and sundry matters, as that would amount to abuse of judicial power.
RatioOfTheDecision	Courts should guard against such transgressions in the exercise of power.
RatioOfTheDecision	Our above observations do not of course apply to summoning of contemnors in contempt jurisdiction.
RatioOfTheDecision	11    10.
RatioOfTheDecision	We have made the above observations rather reluctantly.
RatioOfTheDecision	Our observations should not be construed as restricting or limiting the exercise of the extraordinary jurisdiction of <ORG> under Article 226 of the Constitution of India.
RatioOfTheDecision	The observations are intended to be guidance for self- regulation and self-restriction by courts.
RatioOfTheDecision	It became necessary as we have noticed that the learned Presiding Judge of the Bench has been frequently making such orders directing senior officers of the Government to be  present and settle claims.
Precedent	It is a coincidence that another case where a similar procedure was adopted by the learned Presiding Judge of the bench, came up before us today - <ORG>, Nainital vs. <NAME> (CA No.10087-10090 of 2010 decided on 26.11.2010).
RatioOfTheDecision	We have no doubt that the learned Judge bona fide believes that by requiring the presence of senior officers, he could expedite matters and render effective justice.
RatioOfTheDecision	But it is not sufficient that the object of the Judge is noble or bonafide.
RatioOfTheDecision	The process of achieving the object should be just and proper, without exceeding the well recognised norms of judicial propriety.
RatioOfTheDecision	11.
RatioOfTheDecision	In this context we may refer to the following observations of this court in <CITATION_592341> - 2007 (14) SCC 94 :   "A large number of cases have come up before this Court where we find that learned Judges of various <ORG> have been summoning the Chief Secretary, Secretaries to the Government (Central and state), Directors General of Police, Director-CBI or B <ORG> or other senior officials of the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	There is no doubt that <ORG> has power to summon these officials, but in our opinion that should be done in very rare and exceptional cases when there are compelling circumstances to do so.
RatioOfTheDecision	Such summoning orders should not be passed lightly or as a routine or at the top of a hat.
RatioOfTheDecision	Judges should have modesty and humility.
RatioOfTheDecision	They should realize that summoning a senior official, except in some very rare and exceptional situation, and that too for compelling reasons, is counterproductive and may also involve heavy expenses and valuable time of the official concerned.
RatioOfTheDecision	The judiciary must have respect for the executive and the legislature.
RatioOfTheDecision	Judges should realize that officials like the Chief Secretary, Secretary to <ORG>, Commissioners, District Magistrates, senior police officials, etc.
Precedent	are extremely busy persons who are often working from morning till night."
