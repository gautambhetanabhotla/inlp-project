Fact	The point taken before us in this Second Appeal for the defendant-appellant is, that the plaintiff's suit is barred by Section 47 Civil Procedure Code, his only remedy being an application under that section.

RatioOfTheDecision	The facts relevant for the argument are these.
Fact	One <NAME> obtained a mortgage decree against the plaintiff; the properties were sold in Court auction and one of the items was purchased by the present defendant who was a stranger to the mortgage suit and to the decree.
Fact	A sale certificate was issued to him and he obtained a delivery order.
Fact	In execution of that order he got possession of plots A, B and C. This was about Hi years ago.
Argument	It is the plaintiff's case that plot C alone was included in the decree and in the sale certificate, and was delivered to the defendant, and that the defendant subsequently trespassed on the plots A and B which are  the subject matter of the present suit; he now sues to recover them.
Argument	The defendant pleaded that all the plots were included in the <ORG> sale and in his certificate, and that he got possession of them all properly through court and that he was entitled to them.
Argument	The Lower <ORG>s have found that the alleged trespass is untrue and that the plaint plots were delivered to the defendant by the <ORG> amin.
Argument	They have however also found that plots A and B were not really included in the mortgage deed or in the decree or in the sale certificate or in the delivery order, which all contained the same description of the property as to boundaries and measurements.
Argument	The amin's mistake in delivering them to the defendant arose from a mistake in the measurements in the documents and the plots really belonged to the plaintiff.
Argument	Applying Article 144 of the Limitation Act , the lower <ORG>s held that the plaintiff was not barred by limitation and gave him a decree for the plots.
Argument	3.
Argument	Mr. <NAME> for the defendant-appellant contends  that on the finding, this is a claim by the judgment debtor under a mortgage decree for the recovery of property overdelivered in execution to the court auction-purchaser, and, as such, this is a case to which Section 47 Civil Procedure Code applies even though the purchaser was a stranger to the mortgage suit.
Argument	He urges that the present suit is thus barred by the section and should have been dismissed.
Argument	If the section applies there can be no doubt that the plaintiff's claim must fail whatever the merits may be, for the suit cannot be treated as an application under Section 47 Clause (2) with any advantage as it will then have to be' dismissed in limine as barred by limitation.

Fact	They applied Section 47 to all the 3 cases.
RatioOfTheDecision	Except on this view, Section 47 Civil Procedure Code could not well have been applie d in <NAME> case, as he would be the representative only of the judgment-debtor, <NAME> the other party to that application.
RatioOfTheDecision	I am unable to accept the suggestion that Section 47 was applied to that case by oversight.
RatioOfTheDecision	<ORG> as Iunderstand them hold that the condition in Section 47 C.P.C.
RatioOfTheDecision	that the question should be one " arising between the parties is satisfied by the question being one of a nature in which the parties to the suit are adversely interested, though the person actually raising it in any particular case against one party may not be the representative of the other party.
RatioOfTheDecision	In fact they gave an extended meaning to these words.
RatioOfTheDecision	7.
RatioOfTheDecision	Applying this view to the present case we have to see whether the question raised here is one in which the rival rights of the decree-holder and the judgment-debtor have to be decided.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the <ORG> case the application was for delivery of properties included in the decree and purchased in court sale.
RatioOfTheDecision	That question was treated as falling  within Section 47 even where the purchaser was a stranger.
RatioOfTheDecision	The present case no doubt refers to property claimed by one side to be included in the decree and denied by the other side to be so included and found by the court to be not so included.
RatioOfTheDecision	Does that make a difference?
RatioOfTheDecision	I was at first inclined to think that it did and that the decree-holder was not interested in the present controversy, as it refers to property over delivered to the purchaser.
RatioOfTheDecision	But on further consideration I have come to the conclusion that it is not correct.
RatioOfTheDecision	If the decree-holder can be held to be interested in the delivery of the property included in the decree to the purchaser as was done by <ORG> I think we must hold consistently with that view that he is also interested in the questions as to what properties that decree covered.
RatioOfTheDecision	The mistake in the boundaries in the present case is not merely in the delivery order but also in the mortgage deed and in the decree and in the sale certificate, and if the decree or the sale certifi cate had been asked to be rectified on the point, the question would clearly have arisen between the parties to the suit.
RatioOfTheDecision	The fact that it is now raised against the purchaser should not make any difference as to whether it is to be done in execution or not.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is clearly appropriate that these mistakes in execution proceedings should be rectified by the executing court itself, and we should not put too narrow a construction on Section 47 and drive parties to suits for the purpose.
RatioOfTheDecision	If plaintiff in the present suit loses his property on this view it is only due to his own laches in delaying for so many years.
RatioOfTheDecision	8.
Precedent	I would therefore hold Section 47 Civil Procedure Code applies to the present case, and as the result I will allow the Second Appeal and dismiss the suit, but, in the circumstances, without costs in any of the courts.
