Statute	Now, coming to the common question which arises for both the years, Section 11 provides, inter alia, that income derived from property held under trust wholly for charitable or religious purposes shall not be included in the total income of the person in receipt of the income.
Statute	There is no dispute that the trust in question is one wholly for charitable purposes.
Statute	Section 13 enumerates certain contingencies when Section 11 will  not apply.
Statute	We are concerned with Sub-clause (ii) of Clause (c) of Sub-section (1) of the section which provides that nothing contained in Section 11 shall operate to exempt the income of a trust for religious or charitable purposes, if any part of the income or any property of the trust is, during the previous year, used or applied directly or indirectly for the benefit of any person referred to in Sub-section (3).
Statute	Sub-section (3) refers to various persons the benefit derived by whom precludes the trust from claiming the benefit of exemption under Section 11 .
Statute	There is no dispute that the other two partners of <ORG> are those referred to in Sub-section (3).
Statute	Sub-section (2) of Section 13 mentions certain circumstances in which the income or the property of the trust will be deemed to have been used or applied for the benefit of a person referred to in Sub-section (3).
Statute	It contains eight Clauses (a) to (h) of which we are concerned with Clause (h) only.
Statute	It provides that the income or the property of  a trust shall be deemed to have been used or applied for the benefit of a person referred to in Sub-section (3), if any funds of the trust are, or continue to remain, invested for any period during the previous year in any concern in which any person referred to in Sub-section (3) has a substantial interest.
Statute	Clause (h) reads :  "13.
Statute	(2)(h) If any funds of the trust or institution are, or continue to remain, invested for any period during the previous year (not being a period before the 1st day of January, 1971) in any concern in which any person referred to in Sub-section (3) has a substantial interest."
Statute	11.
Statute	We may also refer to Sub-section (4) which provides that, notwithstanding anything contained in Clause (c) of Sub-section (1), in a case where the aggregate of the funds of the trust invested in a concern in which a person referred to in Sub-section (3) had a substantial interest did not exceed five per cent.
Fact	of the capital of that concern, the exemption under Section 11 shall not be denied in relation  to any income other than the income arising to the trust from such investment, by reason only that the funds of the trust have been invested in a concern in which such person had a substantial interest.
Fact	Since it is Section 13(2)(h) that is invoked by the <ORG>, the question for consideration is whether any funds of the assessee-trust continued to remain invested in the firm <ORG> merely by reason of the alleged delay in the withdrawal of the share of profits by a few months, after the end of the previous year.
RatioOfTheDecision	12.
RatioOfTheDecision	We may also point out that this case of the <ORG>, based on the delayed withdrawal of the profits after the close of the previous year on March 31, does not, on its face, attract the exclusionary provision of Clause (h) of Sub-section (2) even assuming that such delayed withdrawal amounted to investment.
RatioOfTheDecision	What the provision stipulates is investment, initial or continued, of the funds of the trust in the concern in question for any period during the previous year.
RatioOfTheDecision	An investment or the  continuance of the investment during any part of the previous year is the sine qua non for the applicability of this clause.
RatioOfTheDecision	Non-withdrawal of the profits or the retention thereof beyond the end of the previous year is thus outside the pale of Sub-section (h) while dealing with the assessment for the year corresponding to the aforesaid previous year.
RatioOfTheDecision	The conditions referred to in Clause (h) are cumulative all of which have to be satisfied before the deeming provision in Sub-section (2) could be attracted, and the assessee held to have used or applied the income of the trust for the benefit of a person referred to in Sub-section (3).
RatioOfTheDecision	The assessee cannot, therefore, be deprived of the exemption under Section 11 relying on Clause (h) above quoted for the years 1975-76 and 1976-77, merely because the share of profits was withdrawn from the firm only after the end of the previous year.
RatioOfTheDecision	13.
Argument	Standing counsel for the <ORG>, however, pleaded that the share of profits due to a partner accrued from day to day, an d the assessee-trust should periodically withdraw its share of profits without waiting for the end of the accounting period or for audit or settlement of accounts.
RatioOfTheDecision	This argument need not detain us long because, in our view, the crucial ingredient for Clause (h) of Sub-section (2) of Section 13 to apply, is an investment of the funds of the trust in a concern of the nature mentioned.
RatioOfTheDecision	If mere delay in withdrawal of profits by the trust does not ipso facto constitute "investment" of the funds of the trust, the question of profits accruing de die in diem, stressed by standing counsel loses its relevance.
RatioOfTheDecision	Moreover, we shall deal with the point in brief though we do not find any substance in this contention of the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	14.
RatioOfTheDecision	This question arose for consideration before <ORG> in <CITATION_1496776> [19651 56 ITR 42 ; AIR 1965 SC 1343.
Fact	<NAME>, representing a Hindu undivided family, was a partner in a firm <ORG> for which the accounting year was the calendar year.
Fact	The family was disrupted b y a deed of partition dated November 12, 1955, and its properties were divided.
Fact	<ORG> was allotted the profits of the firm for the year commencing January 1, 1955, the accounts for which were yet to be made up.
Fact	An amount of Rs.
Fact	21,051 was later received by <ORG> as the share of profits for the year 1955, and the question was whether the whole or any portion of this amount was liable to be assessed in the hands of the Hindu undivided family, as having accrued to it before November 12, 1955.
Fact	In that context, the court observed (at page 46) :  "In the gross receipts of a business day after day or from transaction to transaction lies embedded or dormant profit or loss ; on such dormant profit or loss undoubtedly taxable profits, if any, of the business will be computed.
Fact	But dormant profits cannot be equated with profits charged to tax under Sections 3 and 4 of the Income-tax Act.
Fact	The concept of accrual of profits of a business involves the determination by the method of accounting at the end of the accoun ting year or any shorter period determined by law.
Fact	If profits accrue to the assessee directly from the business the question whether they accrue de die in diem or at the close of the year of account has at best an academic significance, but when upon ascertainment of profits the right of a person to a share therein is determined, the question assumes practical importance, for it is only on the right to receive profits or income, profits accrue to that person.
Fact	If there is no right, no profits will be deemed to have accrued."
RatioOfTheDecision	15.
RatioOfTheDecision	The observations of <NAME>, in <CITATION_251775>  26 ITR 27 (SC), that it would be absurd to suggest that the profits of a company could accrue from day to day or even from month to month were referred to.
RatioOfTheDecision	The working of a company from day to day could certainly not indicate any profit or loss, even its working from month to month could not be taken as a reliable guide for the purpose.
RatioOfTheDecision	It would be only reasonable to determine the profit or loss as the case may be at the end of ev ery year.
Precedent	The court then went on to observe (at page 49) ;  "In the case of a partnership, where by a covenant binding between the partners the accounts are to be made at stated intervals, the right of a partner to demand his share of the profits does not arise until the contingency which by operation of law or under a covenant of the partnership deed gives rise to that right has arisen."
Precedent	16.
Precedent	<CITATION_1718643>  29 ITR 535 (SC), the court held that it cannot be inferred that whenever a partnership receives gross receipts in respect of a business transaction in which is embedded some profit or loss, that profit or loss results immediately on the gross receipts reaching the partnership to the individual partners in their aliquot shares.
RatioOfTheDecision	Normally, for profits to accrue or arise, there should be a right either under the statute or under a contract between the taxpayer and others which entitles the former to make a demand for those profits.
RatioOfTheDecision	17.
RatioOfTheDecision	It cannot, therefore, be laid down as an absolute proposition that t he right of a partner to receive his share of profits arises from day to day.
RatioOfTheDecision	It depends on the agreement between the parties, and as to when he can demand his share of the profits.
RatioOfTheDecision	This is for the reason that it could not be said at any given point of time during the accounting period, that there is any profit liable to be divided between the partners, the fortunes of the firm fluctuating at different times.
RatioOfTheDecision	18.
RatioOfTheDecision	What <ORG> had held in <NAME>'s case [1965] 56 ITR 42 has not in any manner been whittled down by the observation in page 376 of the decision in <CITATION_1181294> .
RatioOfTheDecision	66 ITR 373 (SC) (while referring to <NAME>'s case  56 ITR 42 (SC) or by <CITATION_317177>  66 ITR 378 (SC) on which counsel for the <ORG> laid stress.
RatioOfTheDecision	We are, therefore, of the opinion that the profits of a partnership accrue to a partner when he gets the right to demand the same either under the provisions of the agreement between the parties, or in their absence, under the provisions of the Partnership Act .
RatioOfTheDecision	If th is be the position, the contention of counsel for the <ORG> that there should be a periodical withdrawal of the profits, on pain of the unwithdrawn amounts being treated as investment in the firm cannot be accepted.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is too much to expect a partner to withdraw amounts by way of profits from day to day when there is no knowing even, whether the firm is making a profit or a loss.
RatioOfTheDecision	That matter will stand crystallised only at the end of the year when the accounts are settled between the partners.
RatioOfTheDecision	19.
RatioOfTheDecision	But, as mentioned by us earlier, this question is really not of any relevance, the crucial question being whether non-withdrawal of the share of profits from the firm by itself, can amount to "investment".
RatioOfTheDecision	At this stage, we must refer, if only for the purpose of rejection, to a contention raised by standing counsel for the <ORG> that the word "invested" in Clause (h) is not of any significance as, according to him, all that is required is that any funds of the trust continued to remain in any concern, whether i t was an investment or not.
RatioOfTheDecision	He would read the expression "or continue to remain" as qualifying the expression "in any concern", and not the word "invested".
RatioOfTheDecision	Such a reading, to say the least, does violence to the plain language of the provision and we reject the invitation of the <ORG> to read Clause (h) in this fashion.
RatioOfTheDecision	We are satisfied that Sub-clause (h) requires that the funds of the trust are or continue to remain, invested in any concern of the nature mentioned therein.
RatioOfTheDecision	So the question is, what is "investment" ?
RatioOfTheDecision	20.
RatioOfTheDecision	An investment popularly means every application of money which is intended to fetch a return by way of interest, income or profit.
RatioOfTheDecision	This only employed as capital in a business is money invested in business.
Precedent	(<NAME>,, in Tax Commissioner v. <ORG> [1902] 22 NZLR 445, as extracted in Law Lexicon by <NAME>).
Precedent	<ORG> had occasion to consider the meaning of the term "investment" in <CITATION_1391894>  138 ITR 585.
Fact	The facts of th at case are apposite.

RatioOfTheDecision	At the time of the hearing, if the assessee can satisfy the <ORG> that the grounds for decision given in the order by the Commissioner are wrong on facts or are not tenable in law, the <ORG> has no option, but to accept the appeal and to set aside the order of the Commissioner.

RatioOfTheDecision	If the <ORG> is allowed to find out the ground available to the Commissioner to pass an order under Section 263(1) of the Act, then it will amount to a sharing of the exclusive jurisdiction vested in the Commissioner, which is not warranted under the Act.

RatioOfTheDecision	Counsel, inter alia, referred us to the decisions of <ORG> in <CITATION_285521>  106 ITR 292 and <CITATION_1505286>  120 ITR, 49 and of a Full Bench of this court in <ORG> v. <NAME> (<NAME>)  179 ITR 543, to emphasise that a firm is not a legal person, distinct from its partners, but a plurality of persons, and, therefore, there can be no dealings between a partner and the firm  as such.
RatioOfTheDecision	The business of the firm is the business of the partners, and its profits their profits.
RatioOfTheDecision	The assessee contends on the strength of these decisions that the contribution of capital to a firm in which it is a partner is not hit by Section 13(2)(h) .
RatioOfTheDecision	28.
RatioOfTheDecision	We have already referred to the meaning of the term "investment" as the laying out of money with a view to fetch a return.
RatioOfTheDecision	Contribution of capital to a firm is made ordinarily with the expectation of deriving a profit from the venture.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is not laid out for any other purposes.
RatioOfTheDecision	It cannot, therefore, be termed as anything other than an investment.
RatioOfTheDecision	In fact, in common parlance, the expenditure of money for the purpose of a business is always referred to as investment in the business.
RatioOfTheDecision	We are unable to agree with counsel for the assessee that contribution of share capital to a firm is not an "investment" made by the partner.

RatioOfTheDecision	Apart from the inability of this court to read words into the section which are not there, which is part of the legislative, and not judicial process, we are of the opinion that the words "any concern" are comprehensive and embrace every concern which satisfy the qualification of being one in which any person referred to in Sub-section (3) has a substantial interest.
RatioOfTheDecision	If the concern in which the funds of the trust are invested satisf ies this condition, that is sufficient to bring it within the ambit of Clause (h).
RatioOfTheDecision	We do not find any ambiguity in the language of the section which requires us to adopt an interpretative process and read it in the way in which the assessee wants it.
RatioOfTheDecision	The question of applying liberal or strict rules of construction, as laid down in certain decisions relied on by either side, will arise only if there is any ambiguity which requires to be resolved.
RatioOfTheDecision	None such exists here.

Precedent	What Section 13(1)(c)(ii) does is to fetter the exemption provided by Section 11 and to deny it to the assessees who have used or applied their income for the benefit of any person referred to in Sub-section (3).

RatioOfTheDecision	This fiction has to be given its full effect.
RatioOfTheDecision	Clause (h) itself is clear and specific in its terms and so far as we could see, without ambiguity in it.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, we hold that "any  concern" in Clause (h) refers also to a concern in which the trust is a partner, provided it is one in which any person referred to in Sub-section (3) has a substantial interest and the funds of the trust are, or continue to remain, invested in that concern during any period of the previous year.
