Fact	The first question for consideration is whether Section 409 of the Indian Penal Code, in so far as it applies to a public servant (in this case the three appellants were admittedly public servants), has been impliedly repealed by the enactment of Sections 5(1) (c) and 5(2) of the Prevention of Corruption Act II of 1947, and if that is so, whether a prosecution of th e appellants for an offence of criminal breach of trust without the requisite sanction and without conforming to the provisions of the Prevention of Corruption Act , can be legally sustained.
Fact	Two other questions have also been urged before us and they are: Assuming that there was no such implied repeal, would the application of Section 409 of the Indian Penal Code to a public servant infringe Article 14 of the Constitution, now that the provisions of the Prevention of Corruption Act and the procedure laid down thereunder are available to deal with a breach of trust by a public servant; and next, if the appellants do not succeed on the first two points, whether the provision for sanction required by the Prevention of Corruption Act would also similarly apply to a prosecution under Section 409 of the Indian Penal Code.

RatioOfTheDecision	We now proceed to consider whether the two sections are identical in essence, import and content and in our opinion the argument on behalf of the <ORG> carries much force when it is suggested that by enacting the Amending Act of 1952 and creating Sub- section 4 to Section 5 the legislature specifically stated that the offence under Section 5(1) (c) is different from any previous existing offences under any penal statute and there can, therefore, be no scope for speculation about repeal.
RatioOfTheDecision	The words used in Sub- section 4 "any other law" made the position quite clear and explicit.
RatioOfTheDecision	Other law does not mean identical law in which case the word 'other' will have no meaning.
RatioOfTheDecision	At an earlier stage of this judgment we have already tabulated the different elements constituting the two offences and a clear comparison and contract of these elements would show that an offence under Section 405 of the Indian Penal Code is separ ate and distinct from the one under Section 5(1) (c).
RatioOfTheDecision	There are three points of difference between Section 405 of the Indian Penal Code and Section 5(1) (c).
RatioOfTheDecision	The dishonest misappropriation contemplated in Section 405 of the Indian Penal Code is different; whereas that under Section 5(1)(c) is either dishonest misappropriation or fraudulent misappropriation.
RatioOfTheDecision	The latter section is much wider in amplitude than the former.
RatioOfTheDecision	In Section 405 of the Indian Penal Code the words used are "In violation of any direction of law prescribing the mode in which such trust is to be discharged, or of any legal contract, express or implied."
RatioOfTheDecision	There are no such expressions in Section 5(1)(c) .
RatioOfTheDecision	It is clear, therefore, that whereas under Section 405 of the Indian Penal Code there are three essential ingredients to constitute the offence, each one of them being separate and distinct, in Section 5(1)(c) there are only two.
RatioOfTheDecision	Now considering Section 5(1)(c) there are certain matters in it which are absent in Section 405 of the Indian Pena l Code.
RatioOfTheDecision	The words 'dominion' and 'entrustment' connote two different things.
RatioOfTheDecision	The word 'dominion' is not in Section 5(1)(c) .
RatioOfTheDecision	We have already stated that the word 'fraudulently' is not present in Section 405 and in Section 5(1)(c) the gist of the offence can also be made out if the offender allows any person so to do, i.e., allows "any person to derogate from the law as contemplated in the earlier portion of the section.
RatioOfTheDecision	The meaning put on the word 'allows' would certainly be different from 'dishonest misappropriation' by the offender himself.
RatioOfTheDecision	It may be that the word can mean allowing by negligence or without any volition on the part of the offender.
RatioOfTheDecision	It may also mean that there is some kind of positive and tacit acquiescence necessary to bring home the offence.
RatioOfTheDecision	In any event, allowing other persons so to do does not find a place in Section 405 of the Indian Penal Code though this section also contemplates "wilfully suffering any other person so to do."
RatioOfTheDecision	There is an essential difference between "allowing" a perso n and "wilfully suffering" a person to do a certain thing.

Precedent	There is a vast difference between wilfully suffering another and allowing a person to do a particular thing and in our view the word "allows" is much wider in its import.
Precedent	Wilfully pre-supposes a conscious action, while even by negligence one can allow another to do a thing.
