Fact	A foreign vessel M. L. Anwar captained by the first petitioner <NAME> entered the Mangalore Port on 13-10-1957 from Muskat with the cargo of 341 bundles of wet dates.
Fact	The first petitioner filed the entry and other papers on 1 4-10-1957 and declared that the vessel contained only 341 bundles of wet dates and no other dutiable articles in it.
Fact	After verification the cargo of 341 bundles of wet dates was discharged by <ORG> on 16-10-1957.
Fact	The fact that only a small cargo of 341 bundles of wet dates had been brought all the way from Muskat to India roused the suspicion of the <ORG> Authorities and they subjected the vessel to a thorough rummaging for some days from 18th October 1957 hut nothing was found in it.
Fact	But the Authorities had some credible information that the members of the crew had brought gold concealed in the vessel and therefore, they kept a keen and continuous watch over the vessel.
Fact	On 31-10-1957 one of the <ORG> Officials, who was inspecting the vessel once again observed certain cavities underneath the ridge of the hatchway on the port side of the vessel covered with thin deal-wood planks.
Fact	He suspected that they may be cavities in which gold had been concealed.
Fact	He, therefore, secured some respecta ble persons to the place and in the presence of all the petitioners and another member of the crew got the deal-wood planks covered at four places removed.
Fact	On removing the planks the cavities were searched and they were found to contain gold bars in cotton cloth belts and they were taken out.
Fact	There were in all 33 belts in the four cavities.
Fact	On counting the 33 belts they were found to contain 1,390 pieces of gold bearing foreign marks weighing 13,894 1/8 tolas.
Fact	None of the petitioners had with them any permit to import gold into India.
Fact	The first petitioner, who was the Captain of the vessel had not declared that he had brought gold into India in the declaration filed by him on 14th of October 1957.
Fact	These gold bars valued at Rs.
Fact	12,50,471/25nP.

Fact	The  Collector of Central Excise of Madras and Mysore who held an enquiry under the Sea Customs Act confiscated the gold which had been brought in contravention of the Foreign Exchange Regulation Act without paying the import duty.

RatioOfTheDecision	5.
Argument	It is further urged by <NAME>, the learned counsel for the petitioners, that <NAME>, the Assistant Collector of <ORG>, who lodged the complaint, had not been au thorised by a competent authority to file the complaint and therefore, the complaint itself is incompetent.
Argument	This argument is based on the fact that no notification has been issued by <ORG> or <ORG> after Sub-sections (2) and (3) of Section 23 of the Foreign Exchange Regulation Act were amended.
Argument	Section 3 of the Act enables <ORG> to appoint a Director of Enforcement for purposes of enforcing the provisions of the Foreign Exchange Regulation Act .
RatioOfTheDecision	It is not disputed that the amendment in question had come into operation before the prosecution in this case was lodged.
RatioOfTheDecision	The procedure to be followed in taking cognizance of an offence for contravention of Section 8(1) of the Foreign Exchange Regulation Act is governed by Section 23(3)(b) of the Foreign Exchange Regulation Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	This section as it stands lays down that no Court should take cognizance of any such offence except upon any complaint in writing made by the Director of Enforcement or any officer autho rised in this he-half by <ORG> or <ORG> by general or special order.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is contended that since the Government of India or <ORG> had not issued any general or special order empowering <NAME> to file such a complaint and since no Director of Enforcement has been appointed by <ORG>, <NAME> was not competent to file a complaint.
RatioOfTheDecision	There is a fallacy in this argument.
RatioOfTheDecision	The only change made in Sub-section (3) by the amendment is that the Director of Enforcement was added as one of the officers entitled to lodge a complaint.
RatioOfTheDecision	The section as it stood originally provided that a complaint may be made in writing by any person authorised in this behalf by <ORG> or <ORG> by a general or special oider.
RatioOfTheDecision	The mere fact that the Director of Enforcement has not been appointed as contemplated by the amending Act and no fresh notification is issued does not mean that the notification that had already been issued  before the amendment which empowered the several officers named therein to file complaints has become invalid and ceased to operate.
RatioOfTheDecision	This will be abundantly clear if we bear in mind that Act 39 of 1957 was merely an unending Act and has not repealed the previous Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 23(3)(b) of the amended Act as it now stands empowers two authorities to lodge a complaint in writing in regard to the enforcement of the provisions of the Act one of them being the Director of Enforcement and the other authority is any officer authorised in that behalf by <ORG> or <ORG> by general or special order.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is not disputed that before this amendment, a notification dated 12-1-1932 had been issued by <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	<ORG> empowering the Collector and the Assistant Collector of Central excise in charge of customs preventive to file such complaints.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is not also disputed that <NAME> was competent under the said notification to lodge the complaint in question b y virtue of the notification dated 18th January 1952.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is undisputed that <ORG> has appointed the Collector of <ORG> Madras as the Chief Executive officer of <ORG> for all the ports in the State of Madras including Mangalore Port.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is the Collector of <ORG> Madras as the Chief Executive officer of <ORG> who had jurisdiction over the port of Mangalore that empowered <NAME> to lodge the complaint.
RatioOfTheDecision	It cannot therefore, reasonably be urged that <NAME> was not duly authorised by general or special order to lodge the complaint for enforcement of the provisions of the Foreign Exchange Regulation Act .
RatioOfTheDecision	I am unable to accept the contention of <NAME>, the learned counsel for the petitioners, that in the absence of a notification issued subsequent to the amendment of the Act by Act 39 of 1957 the old notification which empowered <NAME> and the other customs officers to lodge complaints became inoperative and t <NAME> <NAME> was not competent to file the complaint.
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 24 of the General Clauses Act provides that where any Central Act or Regulation is repealed and re-enacted with or without modification then unless it is otherwise expressly provided any appointment, notification, order etc., issued under the repealed Act or Regulation shall so far as they are not inconsistent with the provisions re-enacted continue in force and be deemed to have been made or issued under the provisions of the new Act unless and until it is superseded by any appointment, notification etc., issued under the provisions of the new Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is, therefore, clear that the general notification issued by <ORG>, Government of India dated 12-1-1952 empowering all the Collectors and the Assistant Collectors of <ORG> to lodge complaints continued to be in force even after the section of the Regulation was amended.
RatioOfTheDecision	There is, therefore, no substance in the second contention of the petitioners and it must fail.
