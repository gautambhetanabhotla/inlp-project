Fact	ORDER Per Kathuria - This appeal by the assessee for assessment year 1985-86 is directed against the order dated 16-9-1992 passed under section 263 of the Income-tax Act, 1961 by <ORG>, Patiala.
Fact	2.
Fact	Brief facts of the case are these : The assessee is an individual who had income for two months from 1-4-1984 to 31-5-1984 as proprietor of <ORG> dealing in manufacture and sale of poultry feeds.
Fact	The assessee also started a proprietary business in the name of <ORG>, Jagraon, which ran for two months for February and March 1985.
Fact	The accounting period of the assessee ended on 31-3-1985.
Fact	There was an income-tax search at the premises of the assessee on 14-10-1986 when certain books of accounts and documents were taken into possession by the <ORG>.
Fact	The Assessing Officer made an assessment on 27-7-1990 under section 143(3) of the Act.
Fact	Though the order is not very exhaustive, it is clear from the order that the incriminating material seized by the income-tax authorities was looked into by  the Assessing Officer before framing the assessment.
Fact	In fact, the Assessing Officer had issued three enquiry letters dated 6-2-1987, 10-2-1987 and 29-1-1988, copies of which are placed at pages 9 to 14 of the assessees compilation.
Argument	3.
Argument	The <ORG>, however examined the record of the assessee and noticed that the document seized during the income-tax search had not been considered by the Assessing Officer.

Fact	The <ORG> accordingly issued a show-cause notice dated 4-2-1992 listing as many as nine items which were not considered by the Assessing Officer at the time of original assessment.

Fact	4.
RulingByLowerCourt	The <ORG>, after enumerating the nine items mentioned the show-cause notice in the body of the order passed under section 263 of the Act, took cognizance of the reply submitted  by the assessee but held that it was neither proper nor possible for him to verify the explanation furnished by the assessees learned counsel.
RulingByLowerCourt	Since, according to him, the Assessing Officer while passing the original assessment order had failed to consider the incriminating evidence in some seized papers and also did not look into certain investments, he held that the assessment order dated 27-7-1990 was erroneous insofar as it was prejudicial to the interests of the revenue.
RulingByLowerCourt	He accordingly set aside the assessment order in question and directed the Assessing Officer to make a de novo assessment considering all the seized papers not considered earlier and also other important aspects of the case as mentioned in the impugned order.
RulingByLowerCourt	It is against this order that the assessee has come up in appeal before us.

RatioOfTheDecision	10.
RatioOfTheDecision	We have carefully considered the submissi on of both the sides.
RatioOfTheDecision	We find considerable merit in the arguments of <NAME> which have been put forth in a very clear-cut manner.
RatioOfTheDecision	We find that the assessment order framed by the Assessing Officer was passed under section 143(3) after making all the necessary enquiries.
RatioOfTheDecision	The income-tax search and taken place on 14-10-1986.
RulingByLowerCourt	The Assessing Officer issued three query letters to which a reference has been made above.
RulingByLowerCourt	The assessment was completed on 27-7-1990 which was almost four years after the seizure of documents and records by the income-tax authorities.
RulingByLowerCourt	All these clearly show that the <ORG> had made the necessary enquiries before completing the assessment.
RulingByLowerCourt	The <ORG> counsel for the assessee has demonstrated with reference to a chart that items at Sl.
RulingByLowerCourt	Nos.
RulingByLowerCourt	(i) to (vi) mentioned in the impugned order were covered by one other query letter issued by the Assessing Officer.
RulingByLowerCourt	As regards items at Sl Nos.
RulingByLowerCourt	(vii) to (ix), it was submitted that a proper and exhaustive reply had been filed before the CIT which was, howe ver, not considered by that authority.
RulingByLowerCourt	Various <ORG> have held that the power by the Commissioner under section 263 is quasi-judicial in character.
RulingByLowerCourt	The Commissioner, therefore, has not to record his subjective satisfaction but to a conclusion in an objective manner.
RulingByLowerCourt	The ld.
RulingByLowerCourt	Commissioner has also to come to a firm conclusion that the order passed by the Assessing Officer was erroneous and prejudicial to the interests of revenue.
RulingByLowerCourt	The learned Commissioner cannot sit in judgment over the discretion exercised by the Assessing Officer.
RulingByLowerCourt	He can legally interfere with the assessment order only if he finds that the order is both erroneous and prejudicial to the interests of the revenue.
RulingByLowerCourt	In the instant case, what we find is that the ld.
RulingByLowerCourt	Commissioner has neither looked into the submissions made on behalf of the assessee nor came to a firm conclusion that the assessment order was erroneous and prejudicial to the interests of the revenue.
RulingByLowerCourt	He has also not demonstrated as to how the incriminating evidence seized by the  revenue had not been utilised by the <ORG>.
RulingByLowerCourt	On the contrary, the ld.
RulingByLowerCourt	for the assessee has demonstrated before us as to how the Assessing Officer had issued detailed query letters for eliciting necessary information from the assessee.
RulingByLowerCourt	The case law cited by the assessee and referred to above makes it abundantly clear that the <ORG> must state the reasons for intervention before assuming jurisdiction under section 263 and while passing the order under section 263 must come to a conclusion that the order passed by the Assessing Officer was not only erroneous but also prejudicial to the interests of the revenue.
RulingByLowerCourt	In the case of <ORG> (supra) the facts were more or less similar to what we find in the instant case.
RulingByLowerCourt	In that case for assessment year 1979-80, the Assessing Officer completed the assessment.
RulingByLowerCourt	The <ORG> intervened under section 263 of the Act and set aside the order of assessment with a direction to make fresh assessment on the ground that business loss of the assessee amount ing to Rs.
RulingByLowerCourt	30,000 was determined after adjusting deductions under section 80J and was allowed to be carried forward and also due to certain other infirmities.
RulingByLowerCourt	The <ORG> found that no firm conclusions were arrived at by the Commissioner in his revisional jurisdiction, and therefore, it could not be said that the assessment made by the Assessing Officer was erroneous, which was one of the prerequisites for the exercise of revisional jurisdiction by the Commissioner and hence set aside the order of the Commissioner.
RulingByLowerCourt	<ORG> held that the Commissioner did not furnish his opinion or consider the cited cases or the arguments raised and merely observed that these were the points which deserved consideration and after setting aside the order of the ld.
RulingByLowerCourt	Assessing Officer, issued a direction for making assessment afresh, which was not permissible under the provisions contained in section 263 of the Act.
RulingByLowerCourt	<ORG> further held that the Commissioner had to come to a firm decision that the order of the ITO  was erroneous and prejudicial to the interests of the revenue and since no decision about the erroneous nature of the order was firmly taken.
RulingByLowerCourt	The Tribunal was held to be right in vacating the order of the Commissioner under section 263 .
RulingByLowerCourt	In our opinion, the fact in the case of <NAME> (supra), as discussed above, are akin to the in the instant case.
RulingByLowerCourt	In the present case also, the Commissioner has not come to a firm conclusion as to how the order passed by the Assessing Officer was erroneous.
RulingByLowerCourt	All the necessary enquiries as noted above already been made by the Assessing officer.
RulingByLowerCourt	The assessee had all furnished a cogent reply before the ld.
RulingByLowerCourt	Commissioner when a show-cause notice issued under section 263(1) was issued.

RulingByLowerCourt	Commissioner could not pass an order under section 263 by remarking that it was neither proper nor possible for him to verify the explanation furnished by the assessees counsel.
RulingByLowerCourt	In fact, it was the duty of the ld.
RulingByLowerCourt	Commissioner to took the explanation of t he ld.
RatioOfTheDecision	counsel for the assessee and come to a firm conclusion as to whether the Assessing Officer had committed an error framing the assessment.
RatioOfTheDecision	Setting aside an assessment is no ordinary matter.
RatioOfTheDecision	In fact, in tax laws as in other laws, certainly and finality are the prerequisites of a good tax administration.
RatioOfTheDecision	The orders of the subordinate authority should, therefore, not be cancelled or set aside on mere whims and fancies; there must be very compelling reasons for interference by the <ORG> under section 263 .
RatioOfTheDecision	In the instant case, however, the <ORG> has left the enquiries to be made to the Assessing Officer without reaching a firm conclusion that the order passed was, in fact, erroneous.
RatioOfTheDecision	We have therefore, no hesitation to say that the impugned orders stands vitiated for the reasons mentioned above and is hereby vacated.
