Fact	We have heard the counsel on both sides.
Fact	This appeal by special leave arises from the judgment of <ORG> of the Madras High Court, made on June 29, 1993 in W.A.
Fact	No.712/93.
Fact	The admitted position is that pursuant to the notification published under Section 4(1) of the Land Acquisition Act, 1894 [for short, the "Act"] in G.O.R.
Fact	No.1392 Industries dated October 17, 1962, total extent of 6 acres 41 cents of land in Madhavaram village Saidapet Taluk, Chengalpattu District in Tamil Nadu was acquired under Chapter VII of the Act for the manufacture of Synthetic Rasina by <ORG>, Madras.
Fact	The acquisition proceedings had become final and possession of the land was taken on April 30, 1964.
Fact	Pursuant to the agreement executed by the company, it was handed over to <ORG> on and <ORG> which is a subsidiary of <ORG> It would appear that at a request made by the said company, 66 cents of land out of one acre 37 cents in respect of which the appellants originally had ownership, was transferred in <ORG> dated March 24, 1971 in favour of another subsidiary company.
Fact	<ORG>, the 5th respondent which is also another subsidiary of the Company had requested from two acres 75 cents of land; the same came to be assigned on lease hold basis by the <ORG> after resumption in terms of the agreement in <ORG> dated May 10, 1985.
Fact	In <ORG> dated March 30, 1986, the same came to be approved of.
Fact	Then the appellants challenged the original <ORG> dated October 17, 1962 contending that since the regional purpose for which the land was acquired had ceased to be in operation, the appellants are entitled to restitution of the possession taken from them.
RulingByLowerCourt	The  Learned single Judge and <ORG> have held that the acquired land having already vested in the state, after receipt of the compensation by the predecessor-in-title of the appellants, they have no right to challenge the notification.
RulingByLowerCourt	Thus the writ petition and the writ appeal came to be dismissed.
RulingByLowerCourt	<NAME>, learned senior counsel appearing for the appellants, contends that when by operation of Section 44-B read with Section 40 of the Act, the public purpose ceased to be existing, the acquisition became bad and therefore, the G.O.
RulingByLowerCourt	was bad in law.
RatioOfTheDecision	We find no force in the contention.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is seen that after the notification in G.O.R.
RatioOfTheDecision	1392 dated October 17, 1962 was published, the acquisition proceeding had become final, the compensation was paid to the appellants' <NAME> and thereafter the lands stood vested in the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	In terms of the agreement as contemplated in Chapter VII of the Act, the company had delivered possession subject to the terms and conditions thereunder.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is seen that one  of the conditions was that on cessation of the public purpose, the lands acquired would be surrendered to the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	In furtherance thereof, the lands came to be surrendered to the <ORG> for resumption.
Fact	The lands then were allotted to <ORG>, 5th respondent which is also a subsidiary amalgamated company of the original company.
Fact	Therefore, the public purpose for which acquisition was made was substituted for another public purpose.
Fact	Moreover, the question stood final settled 32 years ago and hence the writ petition cannot be entertained after three decades on the ground that either original purpose was not public purpose or the land cannot be used for any other purpose.
RulingByPresentCourt	Under these circumstances, we think that <ORG> was right in refusing to entertain the writ petition.
RulingByPresentCourt	The appeal is accordingly dismissed.
RulingByPresentCourt	No costs.
