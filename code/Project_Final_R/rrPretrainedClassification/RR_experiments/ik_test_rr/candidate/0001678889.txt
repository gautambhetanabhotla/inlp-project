Fact	The crux of the matter is whether the provisions of s.24 of the Act can be construed as conferring any benefit, concession or immunity on any person other than the person making the declaration un der the provisions of the Act.
Fact	The question is whether the non-obstente clause contained in sub-s. (I) of s. 24 of the Act precludes the <ORG> from proceeding against the person to whom the income actually belonged.
RatioOfTheDecision	The contention that there was an immunity not only as regards the declarant, but there was also a finality as to the assessment under s.24 of the Act stems from a misconception of the nature and scope of the Voluntary Disclosure Scheme.
RatioOfTheDecision	859  Under sub-s. (I) of s.24, a person was required to make a voluntary disclosure in respect of the amount representing the income chargeable to tax under the Indian Income Tax Act , 1922 or the Income Tax Act , 1961 for any assessment year commencing on or before April 1, 1964.
RatioOfTheDecision	Sub s. (I) makes it clear that the declarations, which were expected to be made in the manner provided by sub-s. (2), were with regard to the income which was chargeable to tax under the Income Tax Act s of 1922 or 1961, but which was not disclosed at the proper time.
RatioOfTheDecision	Neither under  the Act of 1922 nor under the Act of 1961, was a person required to submit a return with regard to the income which was either not earned or deemed to have been earned by him.
RatioOfTheDecision	It, therefore, follows that the declarations under sub-s. (2) of s.24 had to relate to income actually earned by him.
RatioOfTheDecision	The scheme only permitted the bringing forward of income to tax it did not require investigation of the claim of the declarant.
Fact	If a person made a declaration, the Commissioner was under an obligation to assess him to tax.
Fact	In respect of the voluntary disclosures made, a declarant acquired an immunity from further investigation as to the nature and source of the income.
Fact	He also acquired certain benefits.
RatioOfTheDecision	One of the distinctive features of the scheme was that tax was chargeable on the whole of the disclosed income taken as a single block at rates prescribed for personal income or for corporate income under the Act, and not at an ad hoc concessional rate.
RatioOfTheDecision	Further, facilities were allowed to payment of tax in appropriate in stalments extending over a period not exceeding four years, subject to a down payment of not less than 10% of the tax due and furnishing a security in respect of the balance.
RatioOfTheDecision	Income which had already been detected on the material available prior to the date of disclosure, was, however, to be assessed under the regular provisions of the Income Tax Act and not under the scheme.
RatioOfTheDecision	Any admissions made by a person in the declarations filed by him under the scheme in respect of such income were not to be used in assessing that income under the Income Tax Act .
RatioOfTheDecision	Under the scheme, the disclosed income was not to be subject to any further proceedings of assessment.
RatioOfTheDecision	The identity of the declarant was not to be revealed and he was also immune from penalty and prosecution for the past concealment of the disclosed income.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is, therefore, obvious that the Act granted immunity only to the declarant alone and not to other persons to whom the income really belonged.
RatioOfTheDecision	The scheme of the Act makes it abundantly clear that it was t o protect only those who preferred to disclose the income they themselves had earned in the past and which they had failed to disclose at the appropriate time.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is undoubtedly true that the Act was brought on the statute book to unearth the unaccounted money.
RatioOfTheDecision	But there is no warrant for the proposition that by enacting the same, the legislature intended to permit, or connive at, any fraud sought to be committed by making benami declarations.
RatioOfTheDecision	If the contentions were to be accepted, it would follow that an assessee in the higher income group could, with immunity, find out a few near relatives who would oblige him by filing returns under s.24 of the Act disclosing unaccounted income of the assessee as their own and claiming that the said income was kept by them in deposit with the assessee.
RatioOfTheDecision	That takes us to the contention based on the legal fiction contained in sub-s. (3) of s.24 of the Act and the finality of the assessment, by virtue of sub-s. (8) thereof.
RatioOfTheDecision	The legal fiction contained in sub-s. (3) of s.24 o f the Act, construed in the light of the other provisions; must mean that the income voluntarily disclosed shall be deemed to be the income of the declarant.
RatioOfTheDecision	The words "as if such income were the total income of the declarant" can only mean that even though the income did not actually belong to the declarant lt would be treated to be his income for purposes of payment of income tax under the scheme.
RatioOfTheDecision	If, therefore, a person made a false declaration with regard to income not earned by him, it is difficult to comprehend how the <ORG> could be prevented from proceeding against the person to whom the income actually belonged and during the course of whose assessment the concealed income is detected.
RatioOfTheDecision	It, therefore, logically follows that on a disclosure being made, the amount was not to be charged to income tax in accordance with sub-s. (3) of s.24 of the Act, taking the disclosed income as the taxable income of the declarant.
RatioOfTheDecision	The immunity under s. 24 of the Act was conferred on the declarant only and there w as nothing to preclude an investigation into the true nature and source of the credits.
RatioOfTheDecision	The <ORG> was, therefore, justified in treating the cash credits in the books of account of the assessee in the names of the creditors as unexplained cash credits.
RatioOfTheDecision	The finality under sub-s. (8) is to the order of <ORG> under sub-s. (6).
Statute	Under sub-s. (4) the Commissioner of Income Tax was required, within thirty days, if satisfied that the whole or any part of the income declared had been detected or deemed to have been detected by the <ORG> prior to the date of declaration, to make an order in writing to that effect and forward a copy thereof to the declarant.
Statute	Any person who objected to such an order could appeal under sub- s. (5) to <ORG> stating the grounds for such an objection.
Statute	The <ORG> was empowered to pass such orders as it thought fit under sub-s. (6).
RulingByLowerCourt	This order of the <ORG> under sub-s. (6) was final and conclusive by reason of sub-s. (8).
RulingByLowerCourt	Thus, the finality under sub-s.  (8) was to the order of the <ORG> under sub-s. (6) of s. 24 and not to the assessment of tax made on the declarations furnished by the creditors under the scheme, by virtue of the legal fiction contained in sub-s. (3) of s. 24 of the Act.
Fact	The next question that calls for determination is whether the non-obstante clause contained in sub-s. (1) of s. 24 of the Act precludes the <ORG> from proceeding against the person to whom the income actually belonged.
Fact	Under sub-s. (1) of s. 24 the declaration was required to be made in respect of the amount which represented the income of the declarant.
RatioOfTheDecision	The declaration could not be made in respect of an amount which was not the income of the declarant.
RatioOfTheDecision	If, therefore, a person made a false declaration with respect to an amount which was not his income, but was the income of somebody else, then there was nothing to prevent an investigation into the true nature and sources of the said amount.
RatioOfTheDecision	There was nothing in s. 24 of the Act which prevented the <ORG>, if he was not sa tisfied with the explanation of an assessee about the genuineness or source of an amount found credited in his books, in spite of its having already been made the subject of a declaration by the creditor and then taxed under the scheme.
RatioOfTheDecision	We find no warrant for the submission that s. 24 had an overriding effect over s. 68 of the Income Tax Act , 1961, insofar as the persons other than the declarants were concerned.
RatioOfTheDecision	In our judgment, the legal fiction created by sub-s. (3) of s. 24 of the Act by virtue of which the amount declared by the declarant was to be charged to income tax "as if such amount were the total income of the declarant" was limited in its scope, and it cannot be invoked in assessment proceedings relating to any person other than the person making the declaration under the Act so as to rule out the applicability of s. 68 of the Income Tax Act, 1961.
RatioOfTheDecision	The last question that remains is whether the same income cannot be taxed twice, once in the hands of the creditors and again in the hands of the as sessee.
RatioOfTheDecision	In a case of this description, there is no question of double taxation.
RatioOfTheDecision	The situation is of the assessee's own making in getting false declarations filed in the names of the creditors with a view to avoid higher slab of taxation.
RatioOfTheDecision	Once it was found that the income declared by the creditors did not belong to them, there was nothing to prevent the same being taxed in the hands of the assessee to which it actually belonged.
RatioOfTheDecision	It follows that the decisions of <ORG> in <CITATION_382872> Court in <CITATION_443759> , and <ORG> v. Commissioner of Income Tax, Lucknow and of <ORG> in Addl.
RatioOfTheDecision	<CITATION_885513> which lay down the true scope of the Voluntary Disclosure Scheme under s. 24 of the Act must be upheld.
RatioOfTheDecision	The decisions of <ORG> in <CITATION_712559> and <ORG> & ors.
RatioOfTheDecision	v. C.I.T. and of <ORG> in <CITATION_563987> ., taking a view to the contrary, are overruled.
RatioOfTheDecision	The Income Tax officer was entitled to dete rmine whether the amount disclosed was or was not the income of the declarant, while dealing with the case of another assessee under s. 68 of the Income Tax Act , 1961.
RatioOfTheDecision	The legal fiction created by sub-s. (3) of s. 24 was restricted to <ORG> itself.
RatioOfTheDecision	The protection enjoyed by the declarant under that scheme extended only to the amounts so declared being not liable to be added, in any assessment, of the declarant.
RatioOfTheDecision	There was no absolute finality attached to the declaration especially when the nature and source of the sum declared was being determined for the purpose of its inclusion in the income of an assessee other than the declarant.
RatioOfTheDecision	There was, therefore, nothing which prevented the Income Tax officer from investigating into the nature and source of the sums credited in the books of account of an assessee and reject his explanation to the effect that the sums belonged to the persons who had made declarations about them under s. 24 of the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	Accordingly, the reference must be answe red in favour of the <ORG> and against the assessee.
RatioOfTheDecision	Our answer to the first question is that the legal fiction created by sub-s. (3) of s. 24 of the Finance (No.2) Act , 1965 by virtue of which the amounts disclosed by the declarants had to be charged to income tax "as if such amount were the total income of the declarants" was limited in its scope and could not be invoked in the assessment proceedings relating to the assessee in whose books of account the cash credits appear.
RatioOfTheDecision	The answer to the first question is sufficient to dispose of the second.
RatioOfTheDecision	On the construction placed on sub-s. (3) of s. 24 of the Act, it must also be held that the <ORG> was justified in treating the cash credits appearing in the books of account of the assessee, amounting to Rs.
RatioOfTheDecision	46,250 as the assessee's income from undisclosed sources, since the assessee failed to discharge the burden of proof placed upon him under s. 68 of the Income Tax Act, 1961.
RatioOfTheDecision	The Commissioner of Income Tax shall be entitled to his costs of the reference.
