Fact	PETITIONER: TRISUNS CHEMICAL INDUSTRY Vs. RESPONDENT: <NAME> AND OTHERS C DATE OF JUDGMENT: 17/09/1999 BENCH: <NAME> M.B.Shah JUDGMENT:  <NAME>Leave granted.
Fact	Chairman of the appellant company filed a complaint before the Judicial Magistrate of First Class, Gandhidham (Gujarat) alleging certain offences including the offence of cheating against another company located at Indore (Madhya pradesh) and its Directors.
Fact	The Magistrate forwarded the complaint to the appellant for investigation as per his order passed under Section 156(3) of the Code of Criminal Procedure (for short the Code ).
Fact	The accused Directors thereupon moved <ORG> under Section 482 of the Code for quashing the complaint.
Fact	A single Judge of <ORG> quashed the complaint as also the order passed by the Magistrate thereon.
Fact	Complainant has, therefore, filed this appeal.
Fact	The gist of the complaint is this: In the month of October 1996 the accused Directors approached him and offered to supply 5450 metric to nes of Toasted Soyabean Extractions for a price of nearly four and a half crores of rupees.
Fact	The rate quoted by the accused was higher than the market price.
Fact	Appellant had to pay the price in advance as demanded by the accused.
Fact	So the same was paid through cheques.
Fact	But the accused sent the commodity which was of the most inferior and sub-standard quality.
Argument	Complainant produced <ORG> copies of the reports obtained from the laboratory to which samples of the commodities were sent for testing purposes.
Argument	The said laboratory has remarked that the commodity was of the most inferior and sub-standard quality.
Argument	The complainant suffered a loss of 17 lakhs of rupees by the aforesaid consignment alone.
Argument	According to the appellant he was induced to pay the price on the representation that the best quality commodity would be supplied and the price was paid on such representation.
RulingByLowerCourt	But by supplying the most inferior quality the accused deceived the complainant and thereby the offence was committed.
RulingByLowerCourt	The above are the salient featu res of the allegations in the complaint.
RulingByLowerCourt	We have noted from the judgment of the learned single judge of <ORG> that appellants counsel in <ORG> did not turn up to argue the matter.
RulingByLowerCourt	Evidently learned judge was deprived of the advantage of getting appellants version projected.
RulingByLowerCourt	The deficiency is seen reflected in the impugned judgment also.
RulingByLowerCourt	Respondents counsel in <ORG> put forward mainly two contentions.
RulingByLowerCourt	First was that the dispute is purely of a civil nature and hence no prosecution should have been permitted, and the second was that the Judicial Magistrate of First Class, Gandhidham has no jurisdiction to entertain the complaint.
RulingByLowerCourt	Learned single judge has approved both the contentions and quashed the complaint and the order passed by the magistrate thereon.
RulingByLowerCourt	On the first count learned single judge pointed out that there was a specific clause in the Memorandum of Understating arrived between the parties that disputes, if any, arising between them in respect of any transaction can be resol ved through arbitration.
RulingByLowerCourt	<ORG> made the following observations: Besides supplies of processed soyabean were received by the complainant company without any objection and the same have been exported by the complainant-company.
RulingByLowerCourt	The question whether the complainant- Company did suffer the loss as alleged by it are the matters to be adjudicated by <ORG> and cannot be the subject matter of criminal prosecution."
RulingByLowerCourt	Time and again this <ORG> has been pointing out that quashment of FIR or a complaint in exercise of inherent powers of the <ORG> should be limited to very extreme exceptions [vide <CITATION_1033637> (1992 suppl.
Fact	(1) SCC 335) and <CITATION_1922701> (1999(3) SCC 259)].
Fact	In the last referred case this court also pointed out that merely because an act has a civil profile is not sufficient to denude it of its criminal outfit.
RatioOfTheDecision	We quote the following observations: It may be that the facts narrated in the present complaint would as well reveal a commercial transaction or money transaction.
RatioOfTheDecision	But t hat is hardly a reason for holding that the offence of cheating would elude from such a transaction.
RatioOfTheDecision	In fact, many a cheatings were committed in the course of commercial and also money transactions.
RatioOfTheDecision	We are unable to appreciate the reasoning that the provision incorporated in the agreement for referring the disputes to arbitration is an effective substitute for a criminal prosecution when the disputed act is an offence.
RatioOfTheDecision	Arbitration is a remedy for affording reliefs to the party affected by breach of the agreement but the arbitrator cannot conduct a trial of any act which amounted to an offence albeit the same act may be connected with the discharge of any function under the agreement.
RatioOfTheDecision	Hence, those are not good reasons for <ORG> to axe down the complaint at the threshold itself.
RatioOfTheDecision	The investigating agency should have had the freedom to go into the whole gamut of the allegations and to reach a conclusion of its own.
RatioOfTheDecision	Pre-emption of such investigation would be justified only in very extreme cases as indica ted in State of Haryana vs. <NAME> (Supra).
RatioOfTheDecision	Learned single judge has accepted the alternative contention advanced by the respondent pertaining to want of jurisdiction for the Judicial Magistrate of First Class, Gandhidham in respect of the offence alleged in the complaint.
RatioOfTheDecision	This is what <ORG> has said on that aspect: Further, there is nothing in the complaint which shows that any part of the transaction took place within the territories of the State of Gujarat.
RatioOfTheDecision	It appears that even the supply of processed soyabean was delivered to the complainant-company at the factory itself.
RatioOfTheDecision	In my view, therefore, Mr. <NAME> is right in contending that the court of the learned Judicial Magistrate, First Class, Gandhidham ought not to have taken cognizance of the matter and ought not to have directed to issue the process.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is an erroneous view that the Magistrate taking cognizance of an offence must necessarily have territorial jurisdiction to try the case as well.
Statute	Chapter XIII of the Code relates to jurisdictio n of the criminal courts in enquiries and trials.
Statute	That chapter contains provisions regarding the place where the enquiry and trial are to take place.
Statute	Section 177 says that every offence shall ordinarily be inquired into and tried by a Court within whose local jurisdiction it was committed.
Precedent	But section 179 says that when an act is an offence by reason of anything which has been done and of a consequence which has ensued, the place of enquiry and trial can as well be in a court within whose local jurisdiction such thing has been done or such consequence has ensued..
RatioOfTheDecision	It cannot be overlooked that the said provisions do not trammel the powers of any court to take cognizance of the offence.
RatioOfTheDecision	Power of the court to take cognizance of the offence is laid in Section 190 of the Code.
Statute	Sub-sections (1)& (2) read thus: (i) Subject to the provisions of this Chapter, any Magistrate of the first class, and any Magistrate of the second class specially empowered in this behalf under sub-section (2), may take cognizance of any of fence  (a) Upon receiving a complaint of facts which constitute such offence;  (b) Upon a police report of such facts;  (c) Upon information received from any person other than a police officer, or upon his own knowledge, that such offence has been committed.
Statute	(ii) The Chief Judicial Magistrate may empower any Magistrate of the second class to take cognizance under sub-section (1) of such offences as are within his competence to inquire into or try.
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 193 imposes a restriction on the court of sessions to take cognizance of any offence as a court of original jurisdiction.
RatioOfTheDecision	But any Magistrate of the First Class has the power to take cognizance of any offence, no matter that the offence was committed within his jurisdiction or not.
RatioOfTheDecision	The only restriction contained in Section 190 is that the power to take cognizance is subject to the provisions of this Chapter.
RatioOfTheDecision	There are 9 Sections in Chapter XIV most of which contain one or other restriction imposed on the power of a first class magistrate in taking cogniza nce of an offence.
RatioOfTheDecision	But none of them incorporates any curtailment on such powers in relation to territorial barrier.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the corresponding provision in the old Code of Criminal Procedure (1898) the commencing words were like these: Except as hereinafter provided.
RatioOfTheDecision	Those words are now replaced by Subject to the provisions of this chapter.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, when there is nothing in Chapter XIV of the Code to impair the power of a judicial magistrate of first class taking cognizance of the offence on the strength of any territorial reason it is impermissible to deprive such a magistrate of the power to take cognizance of an offence of course, in certain special enactments special provisions are incorporated for restricting the power of taking cognizance of offences falling under such acts.
RatioOfTheDecision	But such provisions are protected by non-obstante clauses.
RatioOfTheDecision	Any way that is a different matter.
RatioOfTheDecision	The jurisdictional aspect becomes relevant only when the question of enquiry or trial arises.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is therefore a fallacious thinking that only  a magistrate having jurisdiction to try the case has the power to take cognizance of the offence.
RatioOfTheDecision	If he is a Magistrate of the First Class his power to take cognizance of the offence is not impaired by territorial restrictions.
RatioOfTheDecision	After taking cognizance he may have to decide as to the court which has jurisdiction to enquire into or try the offence and that situation would reach only during the post cognizance stage and not earlier.
RatioOfTheDecision	Unfortunately, <ORG>, without considering any of the aforesaid legal aspects rushed to the erroneous conclusion that the judicial magistrate of first class, Gandhidham has no power to take cognizance of the offences alleged merely because such offences could have been committed outside the territorial limits of the State of Gujarat.
RatioOfTheDecision	Even otherwise, without being apprised of the fuller conspectus a decision on the question of jurisdiction should not have been taken by <ORG> at a grossly premature stage as this.
RatioOfTheDecision	For all the aforesaid reasons we are unable to concur w ith the impugned judgment.
RatioOfTheDecision	We, therefore, quash it.
RatioOfTheDecision	Learned counsel for the respondents invited our attention to the fact that all the accused persons arrayed in the complaint are residing at Indore in Madhya Pradesh and he apprehends that revival of investigation in the case would most probably embroil them in a miserable position if they are arrested.
RatioOfTheDecision	We considered that aspect in the view we now take and we also foresee such a plight for the accused.
RulingByPresentCourt	To alleviate any possible hardship for the respondents we direct that if any of the respondents is arrested in connection with the above complaint, he shall be released on bail by the arresting officer on execution of a bond to his satisfaction.
RulingByPresentCourt	However, such arrested person shall be bound to report to the investigating officer at the place and time specified for the purpose of interrogation.
RulingByPresentCourt	The appeal is disposed of in the above terms.
