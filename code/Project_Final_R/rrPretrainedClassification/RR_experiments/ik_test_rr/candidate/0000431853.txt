RatioOfTheDecision	Coming to the merits of the case, we accept the contention of the learned for the appellant that <ORG> went wrong in holding that <ORG> was a department of <ORG>.
Argument	In <NAME>'s  case (supra), <ORG> of this Court while considering a similar question held as follows:-  "We must, therefore, hold that the corporation which is <ORG> in this case is not a department of the <ORG> nor are the servants of its holding posts under the <ORG>.
Argument	It has its independent existence and by law relating to Corporations, it is distinct even from its members."
Argument	In <ORG> (supra), this Court held as follows:-  "It is contended by the Attorney General that since the appellant- companies are wholly owned by the <ORG> of India, the lands and buildings owned by the companies cannot be subjected to property tax.
Argument	The short answer to this contention is that even though the entire share capital of the appellant-<ORG> has been subscribed by the <ORG> of India, it cannot be predicted the <ORG> of India.
Argument	The companies which are incorporated under the Companies which are incorporated under the Companies Act have a corporate personality of their  own, distinct from that of <ORG>.
Argument	The lands and buildings are vested in and owned by the companies, the <ORG> only owns share capital."
RatioOfTheDecision	In the view of the above decisions of this Court, we have no hesitation to hold that <ORG> erred in thinking that <ORG> was a department of <ORG> and most of the reasons given in the judgment are based on this wrong premise.
RatioOfTheDecision	The importer in this case, it is an admitted fact, is a register exporter of steel pipes entitled to priority allotment of steel either through imports or from indigenous plants like the appellant.
RatioOfTheDecision	The allotment of steel was four manufacture of steel pipes for export against Advance Licence granted to the importer from time to tome.
RatioOfTheDecision	During the relevant period, hot rolled strips in coils manufactured by plants under <ORG> were available in sufficient quantity and hence the direct imports of the raw material were banned and the manufacturers were required to obtain the supplies of the raw materials from the  appellant against the import licences with them and/or release orders issued by the licensing authority, namely the Chief Controller of Imports and Exports.
RatioOfTheDecision	Various categories of licences are granted for the purpose of imports to the manufacturers.

RatioOfTheDecision	Bearing the above-said facts in mind, let us now consider the issue raised before us.
RatioOfTheDecision	Admittedly when the importer wanted to register the indent for supply of hot rolled strips in coils, the licences / Release Orders produced lacked in material particulars and relevant enclosures.
RatioOfTheDecision	In other words, the licence did not mention that it was an Advance Licence and no Duty Exemption Entitlement Certificate was enclosed and legal undertaking/exemption of export bone was also not enclosed.
RatioOfTheDecision	It was on this admitted position, the appellant declined to register the indent of the importer.
RatioOfTheDecision	No doubt, the mistake was committed by the licensing authority.
RatioOfTheDecision	Does that mean that the appellant can ignore the lacuna in the documents and register the indent placed by the importer contravention of the requirements of the Scheme?
RatioOfTheDecision	<ORG> held that the licensing authority and the appellant being two different wings/departments of <ORG>, the appellant on receipt of rectified documents on 26.8.1983 must register the in dent as if it was presented on 20.8.1983.
RatioOfTheDecision	We are afraid, we cannot accept the above reasoning of <ORG> as we have pointed out that the basic error committed by <ORG> was in assuming that the appellant was <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	We have already noticed that there are number of judgments of the <ORG> taking the view that a company though fully owned by <ORG> when incorporated takes its own entity/identify and cannot be considered as department of the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	Further, it is seen from the records that the importer in this case is not a new entrant to plead ignorance though that may not be an excuse.
RatioOfTheDecision	He has presented applications for registration before and after the application in question and, therefore, it must be taken that the importer new fully well about the requirements for registering the indent.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is also relevant to note that the appellant on receipts of the application for registration expressly in writing replies not only pointing out the defects but also s tated that they are not taking any action on the Letter of Credit enclosed along with the licence.
RatioOfTheDecision	it is also an admitted fact that the indent was registered only on 26.8.1983 when all the relevant documents after curing the defects was presented on that date.
RatioOfTheDecision	Under these circumstances and in the light of the Scheme published by the appellant, we hold that the import (Ambica Tubes) cannot claim as of right its order must be deemed to have been registered for supply of raw materials on 20.8.1983 when the documents with all defects were presented.
RatioOfTheDecision	We cannot agree with the contention of Mr. <NAME> that mere presentation of an irrevocable Letter of Credit covering the value of requirement of raw materials is sufficient for registering the indent.
RatioOfTheDecision	We have already set out para 2.3 Paragraph 2.3 of the paid for the indent.
RatioOfTheDecision	Mr. <NAME> placed reliance on the last part of Paragraph 2.3 to contend that the date of presentation of irrevocable Letter of Credit will be relevant for fixing the price.
RatioOfTheDecision	We have seen that it s ays price changeable shall be the price ruling on the date on which acceptable and operative, financial arrangements are made in favour of <ORG> by import licence holder eligible to get supplies under this Scheme.
RatioOfTheDecision	Mr. <NAME> wants us to ignore the portion underlined above.
RatioOfTheDecision	Mere production of Letter of Credits will not be sufficient to determined the price ruling on the date.
RatioOfTheDecision	It must be given by an import licence holder eligible to get the supplies under the Scheme.
RatioOfTheDecision	In this case, we have sen that the importer will not fall under the category of `import licence holder eligible to get supplies under the Scheme' as on the date when the Letter of Credit was presented, the licence / release order was defective.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, we cannot agree with Mr. <NAME> that the importer having produced the Letter of Credit well before 25.8.1983, the price payable for the supplies must be pre-revised one.
RatioOfTheDecision	In view of our above conclusion, it is not necessary for us to consider and decide the other points raised by learned counsel fo r the appellant.
RatioOfTheDecision	We have seen that <ORG> has not decided but left open the question relating to the right of the appellant to fix the price from the time to time even though that was the main issue raised in the writ petition before <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	As we are not in agreement with the view expressed by <ORG> on other issues, it is now necessary for <ORG> to consider the issue relating to the right of the appellant to fix the price from time to time.
