Fact	In exercise of the power under Rule 8, <ORG> by a notification dated July 31, 1959' granted exemption to "cotton fabrics produced by <ORG>/formed of owners of cotton powerlooms which is .registered or which may be registered on or before March 31, 1961" subject to certain conditions set out in the notification.
Fact	A subsequent notification dated April 30, 1960 granted exemption to "cotton fabrics produced on powerlooms owned by <ORG> or owned by or allotted to the members of the <ORG> which is registered on or before March 31, 1961".
Fact	On the strength of these notifications the appellant sought exemption from excise duty in respect of' the cotton fabrics which were manufactured for it on powerlooms by the Cooperative <ORG>.
Fact	The excise authorities d id not accept the claim for exemption and in a writ petition filed by the appellant, <ORG> gave only partial relief.
Fact	In appeal before this Court the question was whether the exemption granted under the; notifications in question could be claimed only when the cotton fabrics were manufactured by <ORG> 'for itself.
Fact	HELD: On a true construction of the language of the notifications dated July 31, 1959 and April 30, 1960, it is clear that all that is required for claiming exemption is that the cotton fabrics must be produced on powerlooms owned by <ORG>.
Fact	There is no further requirement under the two notifications that the cotton fabrics must be produced by the cooperative society on powerlooms "for itself'.
RatioOfTheDecision	The appellant was therefore entitled to the exemption claimed.
RatioOfTheDecision	[259 D-E] It is well established that in a taxing statute there is no room for any intendment but regard must be had to the dear meaning of words.
RatioOfTheDecision	A statutory notification may not be extended so as to meet  a casus omissus.
RatioOfTheDecision	It could be that the object behind the two notifications in question was to encourage the actual manufacturers of handloom cloth to switch over, to powerlooms by constituting themselves into Cooperative Societies.
RatioOfTheDecision	But, the.
RatioOfTheDecision	operation of the notifications had to be judged not by the object which 254 the rule, making authority had in mind but by the words which it had employed to effectuate the legislative intent.
RatioOfTheDecision	Applying this principle, the case of the appellant was covered by the language of the: two notifications and the appellant was entitled to exemption from excise duty for the cotton fabrics.

RatioOfTheDecision	the appellant supplied raw material and the contractor i.e., the <ORG> produced the goods.

RatioOfTheDecision	were produced on  power-looms owned by the co-operative society and there is nothing in the notifications to suggest that the cotton fabrics should be produced by <ORG> "for itself" and not for a third party before it was entitled to claim exemption from excise duty.
RatioOfTheDecision	It was contended on behalf of the respondent that the object of granting exemption was to encourage the formation of co-operative societies which not only produced cotton fabrics but which also consisted of members, not only owning but having actually operated not more than four power-looms during the three years immediately preceding their having joined the society.
RatioOfTheDecision	The policy was that instead of each such member operating his looms on his own, he should combine with others by forming a society which, through the cooperative effort should produce cloth.
RatioOfTheDecision	The intention was that the goods produced for which exemption could be claimed must be goods produced on its own behalf by the society.
RatioOfTheDecision	We are unable to accept the contention put forward on beha lf of the respondents as correct.
RatioOfTheDecision	On a true construction of the language of the notifications, dated July 31, 1959 and April 30, 1960 it is clear that all that is.
RatioOfTheDecision	required for claiming exemption is that the cotton fabrics must be produced on power-looms owned by the cooperative society.
RatioOfTheDecision	There is no further requirement under the two notifications that the cotton fabrics must be produced by <ORG> on the powerlooms "for itself".
RatioOfTheDecision	It is well established that in a taxing statute there is no room for any intendment but regard must be had to the clear meaning of the words.
RatioOfTheDecision	The entire matter is governed wholly by the language of the notificatlon.
RatioOfTheDecision	If the tax-payer is within the plain terms of the exemption it cannot be denied its benefit by calling in aid any supposed intention of the exempting authority.
RatioOfTheDecision	If such intention can be gathered from the construction of the words of the notification or by necessary implication therefrom, the matter is different, but that is not the case here.
RatioOfTheDecision	In this conne ction we may refer to the observations of Lord <NAME> in <NAME> v. <NAME> & Co.(1):  "Intentlon of the legislature is a common but very slippery phrase, which, popularly understood may signify anything from intention embodied in positive enactment to speculative opinion as to what the legislature probably would have meant, although there has been an omission to enact it.
RatioOfTheDecision	In <ORG> or Equity, what the <ORG> intended to be done or not to be done can only be legitimately ascertained from that which it has chosen to enact, either in express words o.r by reasonable and necessary implication."
RatioOfTheDecision	(1) [1897] A.C. 22, 38.

RatioOfTheDecision	As appears in the judgment of <ORG> in <NAME> v. Spooner(1).
RatioOfTheDecision	"....we cannot aid the legislature's defective phrasing of the Act, we cannot add, and mend, and, by construction, make up deficiencies which are left there."
RatioOfTheDecision	Learned <ORG> <NAME> is possibly right in his submission that the object behind the two notifications is to encourage the actual manufacturers of handloom cloth to switch over to power-looms by constituting themselves into <ORG>.
