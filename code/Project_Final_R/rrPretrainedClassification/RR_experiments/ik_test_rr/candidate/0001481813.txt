Argument	5.
Fact	We propose to discuss first the validity of conviction under Section 306 of the Indian Penal Code because that is the more serious offence alleged against <NAME> and <NAME>.
Fact	Section 306 of the Indian Penal Code states inter alia that whoever abets the commission of suicide shall be punished with imprisonment and fine as set out in the said section.
Fact	Section  307 of the Indian Penal Code defines abetment.
Fact	Reading Sections 306 and 307 together it is clear that if any person instigates any other person to commit suicide and as a result of such instigation the other person commits suicide, the person causing the instigation is liable to be punished under Section 306 of the Indian Penal Code for abetting the commission of suicide.
RatioOfTheDecision	A plain reading of this provision shows that before a person can be convicted of abetting the suicide of any other person, it must be established that such other person committed suicide.
RatioOfTheDecision	We may mention here that commission of dowry death is now punishable under Section 304B of the Indian Penal Code and certain presumptions are prescribed under Sub-section (1) of that section, but it is pointed out to us that that section was not enacted at the time when the incident in question took place and hence can have no application to the case before us.
RatioOfTheDecision	We must, therefore, first examine whether the evidence on record establishes with the degree of  certainty required in criminal law that <NAME> committed suicide.
RatioOfTheDecision	In this regard it is pointed out by Mr. <NAME> that, in the present case, there is no such clear piece of evidence like a suicide note or statement had been made by <NAME> after the incident to the effect that she had taken her own life or set herself on fire.
RatioOfTheDecision	Further there is no evidence that her clothes gave out a smell of kerosene which would indicate of suicide.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is true that the post-mortem was performed after several hours and such smell might not have been detectable from <NAME>'s clothes at that time but it is significant that Dr. <NAME>, who treated <NAME> at <ORG> has not been asked any question regarding any smell given out by <NAME>'s clothes.
RatioOfTheDecision	As far as the question of improbability of sustaining burns accidentally from the stove lighted for preparing tea is concerned, evidence was led by the prosecution to show that the stove was placed on the raised slab which would make it unlikely that burns were caused to <NAME>  by accident.
RatioOfTheDecision	The evidence in this regard, however, is by no means satisfactory.
RatioOfTheDecision	The evidence of the Investigating Officer, <NAME> undoubtedly is to the effect that when he went to the residence of <NAME> for the first time for investigation, the stove was lying on the slab.
RatioOfTheDecision	However, the evidence of the photographer who went to the residence of <NAME> along with the investigating officer is to the effect that when he went there, the said stove was lying on the kitchen floor.
RatioOfTheDecision	In view of this contradiction, it is not possible to hold that the stove was placed on the club when <NAME> was preparing tea for the family and hence an accident was highly unlikely.

RulingByLowerCourt	Coming next to conviction under Section 498A of the Indian Penal Code, a reading of the said section shows that whoever, being the <NAME> or relative of the <NAME> of a woman subjects such wom an to cruelty shall be punished with imprisonment for a term which may extend to three years and shall also be liable to fine.
RulingByLowerCourt	Clause (b) of the Explanation to that section shows that the harassment of the woman where such harassment is with a view to coercing her or any person related to her to meet any unlawful demand for property or valuable security or is on account of the failure by her or any person related to her to meet such demand would amount to cruelty for the purposes of Section 498A .
RulingByLowerCourt	In the present case, as pointed out by the learned Additional Sessions Judge as well as the learned Judge of <ORG>, there is an ample evidence that repeated demands were made inter alia by <NAME> and <NAME> on <NAME>, her parents and her brother <NAME> for articles of dowry and money.
RulingByLowerCourt	There is also evidence that <NAME> made statements after her marriage and right up to the time when she died that she and her parents were being harassed by <NAME>, <NAME> and his fa mily members for various dowry articles and also for money.
RulingByLowerCourt	As pointed out by Mr. <NAME> that there are some inconsistencies regarding the precise nature of the articles demanded but we are of the view that these inconsistencies are of little importance, considering the volume of evidence showing that repeated demands were made for various articles of dowry and money on <NAME> and her parents.
RulingByLowerCourt	In fact, what is the most telling circumstance is that after the death of <NAME> a large number of dowry articles seem to have been taken back by <NAME>'s family members from <NAME> residence which were admittedly being given as dowry.
RulingByLowerCourt	There is also substantial evidence to show that an amount of Rs.
RulingByLowerCourt	20,000 to 25,000 was demanded from <NAME>, the brother of <NAME> and her mother by <NAME> for setting up <NAME> in business and they were unable to satisfy these demands.
RulingByLowerCourt	We, therefore, agree with the conclusion reached by the Additional Sessions Judge and confirmed by <ORG> in this connection an d confirm the conviction and sentence imposed on <NAME> and <NAME> under Section 498A of the Indian Penal Code.
