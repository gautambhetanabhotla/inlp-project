Fact	JUDGMENT <NAME>, J.
Fact	1.
Fact	The limited question that arises in this application, preferred by the Government Under Section 12-B of the Madras General Sales-tax Act is whether the arecanuts sold by the respondent in the assessment year 1951-52 constituted "agricultural or horticultural produce grown by (the assessee) himself or grown on any land which he had an interest, whether as owner, usufructuary mortgagee, tenant or otherwise" within the meaning of the proviso to Section 2 (1) of the Act.
Fact	2.
Fact	The Deputy Commercial Tax Officer rejected the accounts of the petitioner and estimated the turnover of the cured nuts' sold by the petitioner, during 1951-52 at Rs.
Fact	40,736 and assessed him to a tax of Rs.
Fact	636-8-0 on that turnover.
Fact	An appeal to the Commercial Tax Officer failed.
Fact	The assessee took the matter up on further appeal to <ORG>.
Fact	The <ORG> rejected the assessee's contention that he was not a "dealer" at all as defined by the Act.
Fact	The <ORG>, however, accepted the contentions of the as sessee : (1) that what the assessee sold was horticultural produce within the meaning of the proviso to Section 2 (i) of the Act, and (2) that the assessee had an interest in the land in which that produce was grown, within the meaning of Section 2 (i) of the Act.
Fact	It was only the correctnees of the finding of the <ORG>, that what was sold by the assessee in 1951-52 was horticultural produce, that the Government challenged in the proceedings before us.
Argument	3.
Argument	The assessee's appeal was heard by the Appellate <ORG> along with appeals preferred by others similarly situate.
Argument	They were all persons who grew and marketed arecanuts in Coimbatore District in accordance with the practice that prevailed in that district.
Argument	It was not disputed at any stage that there was no market for the arecanuts' as gathered from the tree.
RatioOfTheDecision	While the practice in South Kanara District is to gather the nuts after they are ripe, the practice in Coimbatore district to gather them while they are still raw.
RatioOfTheDecision	After gathering the produce, the ar eca-nuts are peeled, and the kernels are then sliced, boiled and dried.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the course of boiling, the tannin content of the nuts gets minimised, and that Itself colours the nuts, and that colour persist after the nuts are dried.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is only after they are dried that they are fit to be marketed.
RatioOfTheDecision	So much was common ground.
RatioOfTheDecision	While the assesses contended that what was sold was still horticultural produce, the contention of the <ORG> was that it was produce subjected to a manufacturing process that was sold, and that what was sold had ceased to be horticultural produce as such before it was sold.
RatioOfTheDecision	4.
RatioOfTheDecision	The <ORG> disposed of the question before it on a very natrow basis.
RatioOfTheDecision	In an earlier batch of appeals of 1952 preferred by assessees of Coimbatore District, the <ORG> discussed the question at some length and came to the conclusion that despite the processing to which the raw arecanuts had been subjected, what was sold was still horticultural produce.
Fact	The respondent before us was one of the appellants in that  batch of 1952 appeals.
Fact	In that batch of appeals the question whether the assessees had any interest in the land on which the arecsnut was grown, within the meaning of Section 2 (i), had also to be considered.
Fact	Against the decisions of the <ORG> in the 1952 appeals, petitions were preferred to this Court; under Section 12-B of the Act, and the Judgment in that batch of petitions was reported in <CITATION_1789611> ).
RatioOfTheDecision	In that batch of cases, this Court had no occasion to go into the question whether what was sold was agricultural or horticultural produce.
RatioOfTheDecision	In that judgment It was recorded "The finding of the Appellate <ORG>, which differed from the Departmental authorities on that point, was that the cured arecanut sold by the assessee was agricultural or horticultural produce.
RatioOfTheDecision	The correctness of that finding was not challenged, and the argument before us proceeded on the assumption that the arecanut sold by the assessee was horticultural produce within the meaning of Section 2 (i) of the Act."
RatioOfTheDecision	The Tribuna l referred to this, aspect in its judgment in the batch of 1954 appeals, of which the respondent assessee's was one; and held that what was sold was horticultural produce.
RatioOfTheDecision	We have had to set out all this only to show that the reasons for the view taken by the <ORG>, that what was sold was horticultural produce, will have to be sought not in its judgment in the 1954. batch of appeals now before us but in the 1952 hatch of appeals.
RatioOfTheDecision	5.
RatioOfTheDecision	We cannot, of course, treat the question at issue as concluded by authority, because the question was certainly not decided in (A).
RatioOfTheDecision	6.
Statute	What the proviso to Section 2 (i) of the Act excludes from the turnover of a dealer is "the proceeds of the sale by a person of agricultural or horticultural produce grown by himself or grown on any land in which he has an interest.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is true, no specific provision has been made in the Sales-tax Act to correspond to "the process ordinarily employed by a cultivator or receiver of rent in kind to render the produce raised or received by him fi t to be taken to market,"  which the Income-tax Act specifies in Section 2 (i) (b) (ii) of that Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	If the principle of that test can be applied in deciding whether what was sold by the assessee was still horticultural produce, it should be obvious that the question at issue should be answered in favour of the assessee.
RatioOfTheDecision	7.
RatioOfTheDecision	As we have pointed out, it was common ground that there is no market in Coimbatore or elsewhere for arecanuts as they are when plucked from the trees, and it should be remembered they are gathered when they are still unripe.
RatioOfTheDecision	The proviso to Section 2 (i) of the Act is obviously conceived in the interests of agriculturists.
RatioOfTheDecision	It excludes from any tax liability under the Act sale of agricultural and horticultural produce, the primary condition to be satisfied being that it must be produce of the land which either belongs to the seller or of the land in which he has an interest as specified by Section 2 (i).
RatioOfTheDecision	To restrict that concession to sale of areca-nuts, for instance, only if those arecanu ts are sold in the state in which they are immediately on being gathered from the trees, would render the statutory exclusion meaningless.
RatioOfTheDecision	The person who grew that produce could not sell it in that form.
RatioOfTheDecision	He could not use it.
RatioOfTheDecision	It was not, of course, the contention of the learned Government Pleader that the arecanuts could retain their character as horticultural produce only in the state in which they were immediately after being gathered from the trees.
RatioOfTheDecision	That what was horticultural produce ceases to be horticultural produce when it is subjected to a manufacturing process, and what is sold is that manufactured produce, does not admit of any doubt.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the present case, however, it could hardly be said that the marketed commodity was the product of a manufacturing process, to which the arecanuts as horticultural produce had been subjected.
RatioOfTheDecision	No doubt, the arecanuts gathered have to be processed before they could be sold at all.
RatioOfTheDecision	But that processing falls far short of what is normally understood as a manufacturing pro cess.
RatioOfTheDecision	That was not seriously denied.
RatioOfTheDecision	The processing is just to preserve the commodity against its eventual sale.
RatioOfTheDecision	The difficulty which the learned Government Pleader experienced was in drawing a line where arecanuts ceased to be horticultural produce within the meaning of Section 2 (i) of the Act, especially when he was asked to consider the comparable cases of paddy, rice and particularly boiled rice.
RatioOfTheDecision	8.
RatioOfTheDecision	Under the Act , tax liability is imposed only on sales by a dealer, and the agriculturist who sold his agricultural produce has to satisfy the statutory requirements of the definition of a "dealer" before any question of tax liability or exclusion therefrom could arise.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the present case that the assessee was a "dealer" as defined by the Act did not admit of any controversy, at any rate before us.
RatioOfTheDecision	The limitations the court can impose in cons tilling the statutory expression "horticultural produce" must conform to the scheme of the Act, particularly the scheme that underlies Section 2 (1) of the Act the obj ect of the scheme, of course, being gathered in the first instance from the language of the statutory provision itself.
RatioOfTheDecision	We are of opinion, that, where any agricultural produce has to be subjected to a minimum processing before that produce can be marketed at all.
RatioOfTheDecision	it will still retain its character as agricultural produce or horticultural produce, despite that minimum processing.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the case of arecanuts, We have pointed out earlier that what the goods sold by the assessee were subjected to was that minimum processing, absolutely necessary before his horticultural produce could be sold at all.
RatioOfTheDecision	We have confined ourselves only to the case of arecanuts, because what the minimum processing should be for any other kind of horticultural produce may not determine the question, what is the minimum processing to which the arecanuts have to be subjected before they are sold.
RatioOfTheDecision	9.
RatioOfTheDecision	The processing to which the arecanut was subjected, which we have described above, did not really change the character of the produce.
RatioOfTheDecision	There w as no process of manufacture.
RatioOfTheDecision	Something was no doubt excluded, for example, tannin.
RatioOfTheDecision	The size shrunk by the process of boiling and drying.
RatioOfTheDecision	But otherwise it was the same produce.
RatioOfTheDecision	That the produce coloured itself during the process of boiling and drying still left in tact the real nature of the commodity, the nut that was eventually Sold, collected from the green nut that had been gathered from the tree.
RatioOfTheDecision	The process to "cure" the arecanuts was only to preserve the commodity.
RatioOfTheDecision	Once again, we have to point out that the process the assessee had subjected the produce as he gathered it from the tree was in accord wish the established practice of such agriculturists in that locality, Coimbatore district.
RatioOfTheDecision	We are, therefore, of opinion, that despite the process, the minimum necessary, that the goods sold had been subjected to, what the assessee sold still retained its character as horticultural produce within the meaning of the proviso to Section 2 (i) of the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	As we said, any narrow construction of the term "hortic ultural produce'' would tend to defeat the very object of the <ORG> with which the proviso to Section 2 (1) was enacted.
RatioOfTheDecision	The view taken by the <ORG> is correct.
RatioOfTheDecision	10.
RatioOfTheDecision	The learned Government Pleader referred to the decision in <CITATION_1423213> for India, ILR 48 Cal 161: (AIR 1921 Cal 40) (B).
Fact	That case dealt with tea and the decision was under the Indian Income-tax Act .
Fact	At page 171 (of ILR Cal): (at p. 42 of.
Fact	AIR) the learned Judges pointed out the tea that was sold had been subjected to a manufacturing process.
RatioOfTheDecision	We fail to see any scope for applying the tent laid down in that decision to the question now before us.
RatioOfTheDecision	11.
RulingByPresentCourt	The petition fails and is dismissed with costs.
