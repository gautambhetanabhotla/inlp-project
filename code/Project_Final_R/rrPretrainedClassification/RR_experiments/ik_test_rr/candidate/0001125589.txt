RatioOfTheDecision	A Primary Health Centre cannot be established in vacuum; it must be established in some place.

RatioOfTheDecision	The Rules also confer a power on the District Medical Officer and the District Health Officer in the matter of location of the Centre and give the Gover nment the final voice, if there is any conflict between -those officers and <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	Even in regard to shifting of <ORG>, the <ORG>'s voice is final under the Rules.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is one thing to say that the exercise of the power by <ORG> is regulated by the Rules, but another thing to deprive it of that power in the matter of location of <ORG> and confer the said power on the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	We, therefore, hold that the Rules, in so far as they make <ORG> a mere recommendatory body, are inconsistent with the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	This may be the reason why in <ORG> the <ORG> did not think fit to sustain the order under the authority of the Rules.
RulingByPresentCourt	The next question is whether the order dated April 18, 1963, can be sustained under s. 72 of the Act.
Statute	Section 72 of the Act reads :  Power of revision and review by <ORG>:  (1) The <ORG> may either suo motu or on an application from any person interested, call for and examine the rec ord of <ORG> or <ORG> or of their <ORG> in respect of any proceeding to satisfy themselves as to the regularity of such proceeding or the correctness, legality or propriety of any decision or order passed therein; and, if, in any case, it appears to the Government that any such decision or order should be modified, annulled or reversed or remitted for reconsideration, they may pass orders accordingly Provided that the Government shall not pass any order prejudicial to any party unless such party has had an opportunity of making a representation.
Statute	(2)  (3) The Government may suo motu at any time or on an application received from any person interested within ninety days of the passing of an order under subsection (1), review any such order if it was passed by them under any mistake, whether of fact or of law, or in ignorance of any material fact.
Statute	The provisions contained in the proviso to sub- section (1) and in sub-section (2) shall apply in respect of any proceeding und er this sub- section as they apply to a proceeding under subsection (1)."
Statute	Sub-section (1) of S. 72 of the Act confers a wide power on the <ORG> to revise any decision or order passed in any proceeding under the Act.
Statute	Sub-section (3) thereof confers a power on the <ORG> to review the order made under sub- s. (1) thereof if it was passed by the <ORG> under any mistake, whether of fact or of law, or in ignorance of any material fact.
Statute	To attract sub-s. (3), the order sought to be reviewed should have been made under sub-s. (1).
Statute	To appreciate the scope of S. 72(1) of the Act, it is necessary to compare the said sub-:section with S. 62 of the Act.
Statute	Under S. 62(1) , the <ORG> may, by order in writing, cancel any resolution passed by <NAME>, if in its opinion such resolution is not legally passed or is in excess or abuse of the powers conferred by or under the Act or for any other reasons mentioned therein.
Statute	Under sub-s. (2) of S. 62 , the <ORG> shall, before taking action under su b-s. (1) thereof shall give <NAME> or <NAME>, as the case may be, an opportunity for explanation.
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 72 confers a general power on the <ORG>; and on its terms, if there was no other section, it can cancel a resolution of a Panchayat Samithi.
RatioOfTheDecision	But, S. 62 of the Act confers a special power on the <ORG> to cancel a resolution passed by a Panchayat Samithi in the cir-  185  cumstances mentioned therein.
RatioOfTheDecision	The principle generalities specialities non derogant compels us to exclude from the operation of S. 72 the case provided for under s. 62 .
RatioOfTheDecision	If so construed, it follows that if the order reviewed fell under the scope of s. 62 , it could not be reviewed under s. 72 , for s. 72(3) enables the <ORG> only to review an order made under sub-s. (1) -of S. 72 .
RatioOfTheDecision	So, the learned counsel for the <ORG> as well as for the 4th respondent made a serious effort to bring the order of the <ORG> dated March 7, 1962, within the terms of s. 72(1) of the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	As the argument turns upon t he terms of the said order, it may conveniently be read at this stage :  <ORG>.

RulingByLowerCourt	We, therefore, hold that the order of the <ORG> dated March 7, 1962, was one made under s. 62 of the Act and, therefore, it could not be reviewed under s. 72 thereof.
Argument	The learned counsel for the <ORG> then contended that the order dated April 18, 1963, could itself be sustained under S. 62 of the Act.
Argument	Reliance is placed upon s. 13 of the Madras General Clauses Act, 1891, whereunder if any power is conferred on the <ORG>, that power may be exercised from time to time as occasion requires.
RatioOfTheDecision	But that section cannot apply to an order made in exercise of a quasi- judicial power.
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 62 of the Act confers a power on the <ORG> to cancel or suspend the resolution of <NAME>, in the circumstances mentioned therein, after giving an opportunity for explanation to <NAME>.
RatioOfTheDecision	If the <ORG> in exercise of that power cancels or confirms a resolution of <NAME>, qua that or der it becomes functus officio.
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 62 , unlike s. 72, of the Act does not confer a power on the <ORG> to review its orders.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, there are no merits in this contention.
RatioOfTheDecision	Before we leave s. 62 of the Act, it may be noticed that the order dated March 7, 1962, was passed by the <ORG> without giving notice to the Panchayat Samithi.
Argument	It was in violation of the mandatory provision of sub-s. (2) of s. 62 which says that the <ORG> shall, before taking action under sub-s. (1), give the Panchayat Samithi an opportunity for explanation.
Argument	This opportunity was not given and, therefore, that order was not legal.
RatioOfTheDecision	Now let us assume that the said order was made under sub-s. (1) of s. 72 of the Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	Two objections were raised against the validity of the order reviewing the previous order, namely, (i) there was no mistake of fact or law, and (ii) the said order, which was prejudicial to Dharmajigudem village, was made without giving an opportunity to the representatives of the said village of making a r epresentation.
RatioOfTheDecision	The order gives in extension the history of the dispute between Dharmajigudem and Lingapalem in the matter of location of the Primary Health Centre.
RatioOfTheDecision	It points out that all the earlier resolutions of <ORG> were cancelled and Sup .C/66-13 the only outstanding resolution was that of May 29, 1961, whereunder the said Centre was directed to be located permanently at Lingapalem.
RatioOfTheDecision	Then it proceeds to say that the order dated March 7, 1962, was passed on a mistaken impression that it was a case of shifting the Primary Health Centre from one place where it was permanently located to another, while the correct position was that the place where the Primary Health Centre was to be located permanently had not till then been decided by the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	In that view, in supersession of the order issued by it on March 7, 1962, it directed that the said Centre should be located permanently at Lingapalem as per the resolution of <ORG> dated May 29, 1961.
RatioOfTheDecision	No doubt the statement in th at order, namely, that the place where the Primary Health Centre was to be located permanently had not so far been decided by the <ORG>, if taken out of context, may appear to be an incorrect statement, for the <ORG> by it-, order dated July 6, 1960, approved the proposal of <ORG>, Chintalapudi, to locate the Primary Health Centre permanently at Dharmajigudem.
RatioOfTheDecision	But an analysis of the various orders passed by <ORG> and the <ORG> discloses, as we have already indicated, that the Primary Health Centre was never permanently located at Dharmajigudem, that before the Act it was located therein subject to certain conditions which were not fulfilled, that after the Act <ORG>, though it passed a resolution on May 28, 1960, approving the location of the said Centre permanently at Dharmajigudem and though it was approved by the <ORG> by its order dated July 6, 1960, cancelled its earlier resolution in accordance with law on May 29, 1961 and voted for loc ating the <ORG> at Lingapalem.
RatioOfTheDecision	Therefore, the <ORG> was right when it said in its order that it made a mistake of fact in passing its earlier order on March 7, 1962, on a misapprehension that there was a permanent location of the <ORG> at Dhar- majigudem.
RatioOfTheDecision	But there is another flaw in the order of the <ORG> dated April 18, 1963, i.e., it made the order without giving an opportunity to the representatives of Dhartnajigudem who were prejudicially affected by the said order.
Argument	Learned counsel for the <ORG> said that the appellant could not be considered to be a party prejudicially affected by that order.
Argument	But, as we have stated earlier, the appellant was the President of the <ORG> which collected the amount, he was representing the village all through and he also deposited the prescribed amount with the <ORG> officer.
Argument	The <ORG> should have, therefore, given notice either to him or to the <ORG>, which was representing the village all through for the purpose of securing the locati on of <ORG> in their village.
RatioOfTheDecision	The order made in derogation of the proviso to sub-s. (1) of s. 72 of the Act is also bad.
RatioOfTheDecision	The result of the discussion may be stated thus : <ORG> was not permanently located at Dharmajigudem.
RatioOfTheDecision	The representatives of the said village did not comply with the necessary conditions for such location.
RatioOfTheDecision	<ORG> finally cancelled its earlier resolutions which they were entitled to do and passed a resolution for locating <ORG> permanently at Lingapalem.
RatioOfTheDecision	Both the orders of the <ORG>, namely, the order dated March 7, 1962, and that dated April 18, 1963, were not legally passed : the former, because it was made without giving notice to the Panchayat Samithi, and the latter, because the <ORG> had no power under s. 72 of the Act to review an order made under s. 62 of the Act and also because it did not give notice to the representatives of Dharmajigudem village.
RatioOfTheDecision	In those circumstances, was it a case for the Hig h Court to interfere in its discretion and quash the order of the Government dated April 18, 1963 ?
RatioOfTheDecision	If <ORG> had quashed the said order, it would have restored an illegal order-it would have given <ORG> to a village contrary to the valid resolutions passed by the Panchayat Samithi.
RatioOfTheDecision	<ORG>, therefore, in our view, rightly refused to exercise its extraordinary discretionary power in the circumstances of the case.
