RatioOfTheDecision	According to the learned <ORG> "manufacture" is complete as soon.
RatioOfTheDecision	as* by the application of one or more processes, the raw material undergoes some change.
RatioOfTheDecision	To say this is to equate "'processing" to "manufacture" and for this we can find no warrant in law.
RatioOfTheDecision	The word "manufacture" used as a verb is generally understood to mean as "bringing into existence a new substance" and does not mean merely "to produce some change in a substance", however minor in consequence the change may be.
Precedent	This distinction is well  brought about in a passage thus quoted in Permanent Edition of Words and Phrases, Vol.
Precedent	26, from an American judgment.
Precedent	The passage runs thus :-  'Manufacture' implies a change, but every change is not manufacture and yet every change of an article is the result of treatment, labour and manipulation.
Precedent	But something more is necessary and there must be transformation; a new and different article must emerge having a distinctive name, character or use."
RatioOfTheDecision	It is helpful to consider also in this connection the ordinary meaning of the word "'goods".
RatioOfTheDecision	For, by the very words of the: Central Excises and Salt Act , 1944, excise duty is leviable on "'goods".
RatioOfTheDecision	The Act itself does not define "goods" but defines "excitable goods" as meaning "goods specified in 'the First Schedule as being, subject to a duty of excise and includes salt."
RatioOfTheDecision	On the meaning of the word "goods" an interesting passage is quoted in the Words and Phrases, Permanent Edition, Vol.
RatioOfTheDecision	18, from a judgment of <ORG> thus:  "The first exposition I have  found of the word 'goods' is in <ORG>, which defines it simply 'Merchandise'-, and by <NAME>, who followed as the next lexicographer, it is defined to be movables in a house; personal or immovable estates; warn,; freight; merchandise,,"  <ORG> defines the word "'goods" thus :- "Goods, noun' plural, (1) movables; household, furniture; (2) Personal or movable estate, as horses, cattle, utensils, etc., (3) Wares; merchandise; commodities bought and sold by merchants and traders."
RatioOfTheDecision	These definitions make it clear that to become "goods" an.
RatioOfTheDecision	article must be something which can ordinarily come to the market to be bought and Sold.
RatioOfTheDecision	This consideration of the meaning of the word "goods" an provides strong support for the view that "manufacture" which is liable to excise duty under the Central, Excises and Salt Act , 1944, must be the "bringing into existence of a new substance known, to the market."
RatioOfTheDecision	"But," says the learned Counsel, "look at the definition of 'manufacture' in the definitio n clause of the Act and you will find that 'manufacture' is defined thus: 'Manufacture' includes any process incidental or ancillary to the completion of a manufactured product (s.2(f)".
RatioOfTheDecision	We are unable.
RatioOfTheDecision	to agree with the learned Counsel that by inserting this definition of the word ',manufacture,, in s. 2 (f) the legislature intended to equate "processing" to "manufacture" and intended 'to make mere "Processing" as distinct from "manufacture" in the: same, sense of bringing into existence of a new substance known to the market, liable to duty.
RatioOfTheDecision	The sole purpose of inserting this definition is to make it clear that at certain places in the Act the word 'manufacture' has been used to mean a process incidental to the manufacture of the article.
RatioOfTheDecision	Thus in the very item under which the excise duty is claimed in these cases, we find the words : "in or in relation to the manufacture of which any process is ordinarily carried on with the aid of power".
RatioOfTheDecision	The definition of "manufacture' as in s. 2(f) puts it beyond any poss ibility of controversy that if power is used for any of the numerous processes that are required to turn the raw material into a finished article known to the market the clause will be applicable; and an argument that power is not used in the whole process of manufacture using the word in its ordinary sense, will not be available.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is only with this limited purpose that the legislature, in our opinion, inserted this definition of the word 'manufacture' in the definition section and not with a view to make the 'mere "processing" of goods as liable to excise duty.
RatioOfTheDecision	Mr. <NAME> wanted to derive some assistance, for his argument from the words "all sorts" as used in the clause.
RatioOfTheDecision	According to him, the words "all sorts" will be superfluous unless interpreted to mean "whether bringing into existence a new substances or not."
RatioOfTheDecision	The reasoning is clearly fallacious.
RatioOfTheDecision	The words "all sorts" have been used to make it clear: that "vegetable non-essential oils" whether raw or refined and from whatever raw material produced will  be liable to excise duty.
RatioOfTheDecision	Refined oil is one sort; raw oil is another sort.
RatioOfTheDecision	But as the duty is on the manufacture of goods, that is, on the bringing into existence a new substance known to the market, the raw oil or the refined oil must be some substance known to the, market before it can be subjected to duty.
