Fact	In a fatal accident action, the accepted measure of damages awarded to the dependants is the pecuniary loss suffered by them as a result of the death.
Fact	"How much has the widow and family lost by the <NAME>'s death?"
Fact	The answer to this lies in the oft quoted passage from the opinion of Lord <NAME> in <NAME> v. <ORG> (1942 AC 601) which says:  "The starting point is the amount of wages which the deceased was earning, the ascertainment of which to some extent may depend on the regul arity of his employment.
Fact	Then there is an estimate of how much was required or expended for his own personal and living expenses.
Fact	The balance will give a datum or basic figure which will generally be turned into a lump sum by taking a certain number of years' purchase.
Fact	That sum, however, has to be taxed down by having due regard to uncertainties, for instance, that the widow might have again married and thus ceased to be dependent, and other like matters of speculation and doubt."
Fact	The rule in common law in <NAME> v. <NAME> (1979 (1) All ER 774) enunciated by Lord <NAME> was that "in <ORG>, the death of a human being could not be complained of as a injury,".
RatioOfTheDecision	Indeed, the maxim action personalis moritur cum persona, had the effect that all actions in tort, with very few exceptions, also became extinguished with that person.
RatioOfTheDecision	Great changes were brought about by the Fatal Accidents Act , 1846 (now Fatal Accidents Act , 1976) and the Law Reforms (Miscellaneous Provisions) Act, 1934.
RatioOfTheDecision	Under the statute,  as indeed under the Indian Statute as well, there are two separate and distinct cause of action, which are maintainable in consequence of a person's death.
RatioOfTheDecision	There were the dependant's claim for the financial loss suffered and acclaim for injury, loss or damage, which the deceased would have had, had he lived, and which survives for the benefit of his estate.
RatioOfTheDecision	The measure of damage is the pecuniary loss suffered and is likely to be suffered by each dependant.
RatioOfTheDecision	Thus "except where there is express statutory direction to the contrary, the damages to be awarded to a dependant of a deceased person under the Fatal Accidents Act s must take into account any pecuniary benefit accruing to that dependant in consequence of the death of the deceased.
Argument	It is the net loss on balance which constitutes the measure of damages."
Argument	Lord <NAME> in the <NAME>'s case (supra) said, "The actual pecuniary loss of each individual entitled to sue can only be ascertained by balancing on the one hand the loss to him of the future pecuniary ben efit, and on the other any pecuniary advantage which from whatever sources comes to him by reason of the death."
Precedent	These words of Lord <NAME> were adopted as the principle applicable also under the Indian Act in <CITATION_947881> (1962 (1) SCR 929) where this <ORG> stated that the general principle is that the actual pecuniary loss can be ascertained only by balancing on the one hand the loss to the claimant of the future pecuniary benefit and on the other any pecuniary advantage which from whatever sources comes to them by reason of the death, that is, the balance of loss and gain to a dependant by the death, must be ascertained.
Precedent	The assessment of damages to compensate the dependants is beset with difficulties because from the nature of things, it has to take into account many imponderables, e.g., the life expectancy of the deceased and the dependants, the amount that the deceased would have earned during the remainder of his life, the amount that he would have contributed to the dependants during that period , the chances that the deceased may not have lived or the dependants may not live up to the estimated remaining period of their life expectancy, the chances that the deceased might have got better employment or income or might have lost his employment or income together.
Precedent	The manner of arriving at the damages is to ascertain the net income of the deceased available for the support of himself and his dependants, and to deduct therefrom such part of his income as the deceased was accustomed to spend upon himself, as regards both self- maintenance and pleasure, and to ascertain what part of his net income the deceased was accustomed to spend for the benefit of the dependants.
RatioOfTheDecision	Then that should be capitalized by multiplying it by a figure representing the proper number of year's purchase.
RatioOfTheDecision	Much of the calculation necessarily remains in the realm of hypothesis "and in that region arithmetic is a good servant but a bad master" since there are so often many imponderables.
RatioOfTheDecision	In every case "it is the overall picture that  matters", and the court must try to assess as best as it can the loss suffered.
RatioOfTheDecision	There were two methods adopted to determine and for calculation of compensation in fatal accident actions, the first the multiplier mentioned in <NAME> case (supra) and the second in <NAME> v. <ORG> (1951 (2) All ER 448) .
RatioOfTheDecision	The multiplier method involves the ascertainment of the loss of dependency or the multiplicand having regard to the circumstances of the case and capitalizing the multiplicand by an appropriate multiplier.
RatioOfTheDecision	The choice of the multiplier is determined by the age of the deceased (or that of the claimants whichever is higher) and by the calculation as to what capital sum, if invested at a rate of interest appropriate to a stable economy, would yield the multiplicand by way of annual interest.
RatioOfTheDecision	In ascertaining this, regard should also be had to the fact that ultimately the capital sum should also be consumed-up over the period for which the dependency is expected to last.
Argument	The con siderations generally relevant in the selection of multiplicand and multiplier were adverted to by Lord <NAME> in his speech in Mallett v. Mc Mongle (1969 (2) All ER 178) where the deceased was aged 25 and left behind his widow of about the same age and three minor children.
Argument	On the question of selection of multiplicand Lord <NAME> observed:  "The starting point in any estimate of the amount of the 'dependency' is the annual value of the material benefits provided for the dependants out of the earnings of the deceased at the date of his death.
Argument	But....there are many factors which might have led to variations up or down in the future.
Argument	His earnings might have increased and with them the amount provided by him for his dependants.
Argument	They might have diminished with a recession in trade or he might have had spells of unemployment.
Argument	As his children grew up and became independent the proportion of his earnings spent on his dependants would have been likely to fall.
Argument	But in considering the effect to be given in the award  of damages to possible variations in the dependency there are two factors to be borne in mind.
Argument	The first is that the more remote in the future is the anticipated change the less confidence there can be in the chances of its occurring and the smaller the allowance to be made for it in the assessment.
RatioOfTheDecision	The second is that as a matter of the arithmetic of the calculation of present value, the later the change takes place the less will be its effect upon the total award of damages.
RatioOfTheDecision	Thus at interest rates of 4- 1/2% the present value of an annuity for 20 years of which the first ten years are at $ 100 per annum and the second ten years at $ 200 per annum, is about 12 years' purchase of the arithmetical average annuity of $ 150 per annum, whereas if the first ten years are at $200 per annum and the second ten years at $ 100 per annum the present value is about 14 years' purchase of the arithmetical mean of $ 150 per annum.
RatioOfTheDecision	If therefore the chances of variations in the 'dependency' are to be reflected in the multiplic and of which the years' purchase is the multiplier, variations in the dependency which are not expected to take place until after ten years should have only a relatively small effect in increasing or diminishing the 'dependency' used for the purpose of assessing the damages."
