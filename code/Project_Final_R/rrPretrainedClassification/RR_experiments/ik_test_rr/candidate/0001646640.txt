RatioOfTheDecision	Legislation is the process of formulating a general rule of conduct without reference to particular cases and usually operating in future; administration is the process of performing particu- lar acts, of issuing particular orders or of making deci- sions which apply general rules to particular cases.

RatioOfTheDecision	But, where the legislature has not chosen to provide for any notice or hearing, no one can insist upon it and it will not be permissible to read natural justice into such legislative activity.
RatioOfTheDecision	Occasionally, the legislature directs the subordinate legislating body to make 'such enquiry as it thinks fit' before making the subordinate legislation.
RatioOfTheDecision	In such a situa- tion, while such enquiry by the subordinate legislating body as it deems fit is a condition precedent to the subordinate legislation, the nature and the extent of the enquiry is in the di scretion of the subordinate legislating body and the subordinate legislation is not open to question on the ground that the enquiry was not as full as it might have been.
RatioOfTheDecision	The provision for 'such enquiry as it thinks fit' is generally an enabling provision, intended to facilitate the subordinate legislating body to obtain relevant information from all and whatever source and not intended to vest any right in any one other than the subordinate-legislating body.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is the sort of enquiry which the legislature itself may cause to be made before legislating, an enquiry which will not confer any right on anyone.
RatioOfTheDecision	The third observation we wish to make is, price fixation is more in the nature of a legislative activity than any other.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is true that, with the proliferation of delegated legislation, there is a tendency for the line between legis- lation and administration to vanish into an illusion.
RatioOfTheDecision	Admin- istrative, quasi-judicial decisions tend to merge in legis- lative activity and, conversely, legislative activity  tends to fade into and present an appearance of an administrative or quasi-judicial activity.
RatioOfTheDecision	Any attempt to draw a distinct line between legislative and administrative functions, it has been said, is 'difficult in theory and impossible in practice'.
RatioOfTheDecision	Though difficult, it is necessary that the line must sometimes be drawn as different legal fights and conse- quences may ensue.
RatioOfTheDecision	The distinction between the two has usually been expressed as 'one between the general and the particular'.
RatioOfTheDecision	'A legislative act is the creation and promulgation of a general rule of conduct without refer- ence to particular cases; an administrative act is the making and issue of a specific direction or the application of a general rule to a particular case in accordance with the requirements of policy'.
RatioOfTheDecision	'Legislation is the process of formulating a general rule of conduct without reference to particular cases and usually operating in future; adminis- tration is the process of performing particular acts, of issuing particular orders or of  making decisions which apply general rules to particular cases.'
RatioOfTheDecision	It has also been said "Rule making is normally directed toward the formulation of requirements having a general application to all members of a broadly identifiable class" while, "an adjudication, on the other hand, applies to specific individuals or situa- tions".
RatioOfTheDecision	But, this is only a bread distinction, not neces- sarily always true.
RatioOfTheDecision	Administration and administrative adju- dication may also be of general application and there may be legislation of particular application only.
RatioOfTheDecision	That is not ruled out.
RatioOfTheDecision	Again, adjudication determines past and present facts and declares rights and liabilities while legislation indicates the future course of action.
RatioOfTheDecision	Adjudication is determinative of the past and the present while legislation is indicative of the future.
RatioOfTheDecision	The object of the rule, the reach of its application, the rights and obligations arising out of it, its intended effect on past, present and future events, its form, the manner of its promulgation are so me factors which may help in drawing the line between legisla- tive and non-legislative acts.
RatioOfTheDecision	A price fixation measure does not concern itself with the interests of an individual manufacturer or producer.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is generally in relation to a particular commodity or class of commodities or transac- tions.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is a direction of a general character, not direct- ed against a particular situation.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is intended to operate in the future.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is conceived in the interests of the general consumer public.
RatioOfTheDecision	The right of the citizen to obtain essential articles at fair prices and the duty of the <ORG> to so provide them are transformed into the power of the <ORG> to fix prices and the obligation of the producer to charge n6 more than the price fixed.
RatioOfTheDecision	Viewed from whatever angle, the angle of general application the prospectivity of its effect, the public interest served, and the rights and obligations flowing therefrom, there can be no question that price fixation is ordinarily a legislative activity.
RatioOfTheDecision	Price- fixation may occas ionally assume an administrative or quasi-judicial character when it relates to acquisition or requisition of goods or property from individuals and it becomes necessary to fix the price separately in relation to such individuals.

RatioOfTheDecision	Otherwise, price fixation is generally a legislative activity.
RatioOfTheDecision	We also wish to clear a misapprehension which appears to prevail in certain circles that price-fixation affects the manufacturer or producer primarily and therefore fairness requires that he be given an opportunity and that fair opportunity to the manufacturer or producer must be read into the procedure for price-fixa- tion.

RatioOfTheDecision	We are unable to agree with the submissions of the learned counsel for the respondents either with regard to the applicability of the principles of natural justice or with regard to the nature and the scope of the enquiry and review contemplated by paragraphs 3 and 27 while making our preliminary observations, we pointed out that price fixation is essentially a legislative activity though in rare circum- stances, as in the case of a compulsory sale to <ORG> or its nominee, it may assume the character 'of an administrative or quasijudicial activity.
RatioOfTheDecision	Nothing in the scheme of the Drugs (Prices Control) Order induces us to hold that price fixation under the Drugs (Prices Control ) Order is not a legislative activity, but a quasi-judicial activity which would attract the observance of the princi- ples of natural justice.
RatioOfTheDecision	872  Nor is there anything in the scheme or the provisions of the Drugs (Prices Control) Order which otherwise contemplates the observance of any principle of natural justice or kin- dred rule, the non-observance of which would give rise to a cause of action to a suitor.
RatioOfTheDecision	What the order does contemplate however is 'such enquiry' by the <ORG> 'as it thinks fit'.
RatioOfTheDecision	A provision for 'such enquiry' as it thinks fit' by a subordinate legislating body, we have explained earlier, is generally an enabling provision to facilitate the subordi- nate legislating body to obtain relevant information from any source and it is not intended to vest any right in any body other than the subordinate legislating body.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the present case, the enquiry contemplated by paragraph 3 of Drugs (Prices Control) Order is to be made for the purposes of fixing the maximum price at which a bulk dru g may be sold, with a view to regulating its equitable distribution and making it available at a fair price.
RatioOfTheDecision	The primary object of the enquiry is to secure the bulk drug at a fair price for the benefit of the ultimate consumer an object designed to fulfil the mandate of Art.
RatioOfTheDecision	39(b) of the Constitution.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is primarily from the consumer public's point of view that the <ORG> is expected to make its enquiry.
RatioOfTheDecision	The need of the consumer public is to be ascertained and making the drug available to them at a fair price is what it is all about.
RatioOfTheDecision	The enquiry is to be made from that angle and directed towards that end.
RatioOfTheDecision	So, information may be gathered from whatever source considered desirable by the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	The enquiry, obviously is not to be confined to obtaining infor- mation from the manufacturers only and indeed must go be- yond.
RatioOfTheDecision	However, the interests of the manufacturers are not to be ignored.
RatioOfTheDecision	In fixing the price of a bulk drug, <ORG> is expressly required by the Order to take into account the aver age cost of production of such bulk drug manufac- tured by 'an efficient manufacturer' and allow a reasonable return on 'net worth'.
RatioOfTheDecision	For this purpose too, the Government may gather information from any source including the manu- facturers.
RatioOfTheDecision	Here again the enquiry by the Government need not be restricted to 'an efficient manufacturer' or some manu- facturers; nor need it be extended to all manufacturers.
RatioOfTheDecision	What is necessary is that the average cost of production by 'an efficient manufacturer' must be ascertained and a rea- sonable return allowed on 'net worth'.
RatioOfTheDecision	Such enquiry as it thinks fit is an enquiry in which information is sought from whatever source considered necessary by the enquiring body and is different from an enquiry in which an opportunity is required to be given to persons likely to be affected.
RatioOfTheDecision	The former is an enquiry leading to a legislative activity while the latter is an enquiry which ends in an administrative or quasi-judicial decision.
RatioOfTheDecision	The enquiry contemplated by para- graph 3 of the Drug (P rices Control) Order is an enquiry of the former charac-  873  ter.
RatioOfTheDecision	The legislative activity being a subordinate or dele- gated legislative activity, it must necessarily comply with the statutory conditions if any, no more and no less, and no implications of natural justice can be read into it unless it is a statutory condition.
RatioOfTheDecision	Notwithstanding that the price fixation is a legislative activity, the subordinate legisla- tion had taken care here to provide for a review.
RatioOfTheDecision	The review provided by paragraph 27 of the order is akin to a post decisional hearing which is sometimes afforded after the making of some administrative orders, but not truly so.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is a curious amalgam of a hearing which occasionally precedes a subordinate legislative activity such as the fixing of municipal rates etc.
RatioOfTheDecision	that we mentioned earlier and a post-decision hearing after the making of an administra- tive or quasi-judicial order.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is a hearing which follows a subordinate legislative activity intended to provide an opportunity to affect ed persons such as the manufacturers, the industry and the consumer public to bring to the notice of the subordinate legislating body the difficulties or problems experienced or likely to be experienced by them consequent on the price fixation, whereupon the <ORG> may make appropriate orders.
RatioOfTheDecision	Any decision taken by the <ORG> cannot be confined to the individual manufacturer seeking review but must necessarily affect all manufacturers of the bulk drug as well as the consumer public.
RatioOfTheDecision	Since the maximum price of a bulk drug is required by paragraph 3 to be notified any fresh decision taken in the proceeding for review by way of modification of the maximum price has to be made by a fresh notification fixing the new maximum price of the bulk drug.
RatioOfTheDecision	In other words, the review if it is fruitful must result in fresh subordinate legislative activity.
RatioOfTheDecision	The true nature of the review provided by paragraph 27 in so far as it relates to the fixation of maximum price of bulk drugs under paragraph 3 leader price and pr ices of formulations under paragraphs 12 and 13 is hard to define.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is diffi- cult to give it a label and to fit it into a pigeon-hole, legislative, administrative or quasi-judicial.
RatioOfTheDecision	Nor is it desirable to seek analogies and look to distant cousins for guidance.
RatioOfTheDecision	From the scheme of the Control Order and the context and content of paragraph 27, the <ORG> in so far as it concerns the orders under paragraph 3, 12 and 13 appears to be in the nature of a legislative review of legislation, or more precisely a review of subordinate legislation by a subordinate legislating body at the instance of an aggrieved person.
RatioOfTheDecision	Once we have ascertained the nature and character of the review, the further question regarding the scope and extent of the review is not very difficult to answer.
RatioOfTheDecision	The reviewing authority has the fullest freedom and discretion to prescribe its own procedure and con-  874  sider the matter brought before it so long as it does not travel beyond the parameters prescribed by paragraph 3 in the case of a re view against an order under paragraph 3 and the respective other paragraphs in the case of other orders.
RatioOfTheDecision	But whatever procedure is adopted, it must be a procedure tuned to the situation.
RatioOfTheDecision	Manufacturers of any bulk drug are either one or a few in number and generally they may be presumed to be well informed persons, well able to take care of themselves, who have the assistance of Accountants, Advocates and experts to advise and espouse their cause.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the context of the Drug industry with which we are concerned and in regard to which the Control Order is made we must proceed on the basis that the manufacturers of bulk drugs are generally persons who know all that is to be known about the price fixed by the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	From the legislative nature of the activity of the <ORG>, it is clear that the <ORG> is under no obligation to make any disclosure of any information received and considered by it in making the order but in order to render effective the right to seek a review given to an aggrieved person  we think that the <ORG>, if so requested by the aggrieved manufacturer is under an obligation to disclose any relevant information which may reasonably be disclosed pertaining to 'the average cost of production of the bulk drug manufactured by an efficient manufacturer' and 'the reasonable return on net worth'.

RatioOfTheDecision	We think that it is the necessary duty of the <ORG> to proceed to fix the retail price of a formulation as soon as the price of the parent bulk drug is fixed.
RatioOfTheDecision	Price fixation of a formulation is no doubt de- pendant on the price of the bulk drug, but it is not to await the result of a review app lication which in the end may turn out to be entirely without substance.
RatioOfTheDecision	If a review application is allowed and the price of the bulk drug is raised and if in the meanwhile, the formulation had been ordered to be sold at a low price, it may result in consid- erable loss to the manufacturer.
RatioOfTheDecision	But on the other hand, if the review application turns out to be entirely without substance and has to be rejected and if in the meanwhile the formulation is allowed to be sold at a higher price, the consumer public suffers.
RatioOfTheDecision	Thus, the ups and downs of commerce are inevitable and it is not possible to devise a fool proof system to take care of every possible defect and objection.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is certainly not a matter at which the court could take a hand.
RatioOfTheDecision	All that the court may do is to direct the Government to dispose of the review application expeditiously according to a time-bound programme.
RatioOfTheDecision	All that the Government may do is to dispose of the review application with the utmost expedi- tion.
RatioOfTheDecision	But as we perceive the public interest,  it is neces- sary that the price of formulation should be fixed close on the heels of the fixation of bulk drug price.
RatioOfTheDecision	Another submission of <NAME> was that there was considerable delay in the disposal of the review applica- tions by <ORG> and that even now no orders had been passed in several cases.
RatioOfTheDecision	Accordingly to the learned counsel, the very delay in the disposal of review applications was sufficient to viti- ate the entire proceeding and scheme of price fixation.
RatioOfTheDecision	According to the learned counsel, the price of a bulk drug is dependant on many variable factors which keep changing very fast.
RatioOfTheDecision	If time is allowed to lapse whatever price is fixed, it soon becomes out of date.
RatioOfTheDecision	If review applications are not disposed of expeditiously the notifications fixing the prices must be struck down as having become obsolete.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is difficult to agree with these propositions.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is true that the price of a bulk drug is dependent on innumerable variables.
RatioOfTheDecision	But it does not follow that the notification fixing  the maximum price must necessarily be struck down as obsolete by the mere passage of time.
RatioOfTheDecision	We agree that applica- tions for review must be dealt with expeditiously and when- ever they are not so dealt with, the aggrieved person may seek a mandamus from the court to direct the <ORG> to deal with the review application within a time framework.
RatioOfTheDecision	We notice that in all these matters, <ORG> granted stay of implementation of the notifications fixing the maximum prices of bulk drugs and the retail prices of formulations.
RatioOfTheDecision	We think that in matter of this nature, where prices of essential commodities are fixed in order to main- tain or increase supply of the commodities or for securing the equitable distribution and availability at fair prices of the commodity, it is not right that the court should make any interim order staying the implementation of the notifi- cation fixing the prices.
RatioOfTheDecision	We consider that such orders are against the public interest and ought not to be made by a court unless the court is sat isfied that no public interest is going to be served.
RulingByLowerCourt	In the present case, on ex-parte interim order was made on April 20, 1981 in the following terms:  "In the meanwhile on the petitioners' giving an undertakings to maintain prices both for bulk and formulation, as were prevailing prior to the impugned notification we stay implemen- tation of the impugned bulk drug prices as well as formulation prices."

RatioOfTheDecision	Their interests have in no way been taken care of.
RatioOfTheDecision	In matters of fixation of price, it is the interest of the consumer public that must come first and any interim order must take care of that interest.
RatioOfTheDecision	It was argued by the learned counsel that the undertaking gi ven by the parties lapsed with the disposal of the writ petition by <ORG> and that it could no longer be enforced.
RatioOfTheDecision	We do not agree with this submission.
RulingByPresentCourt	Apart from the fact that an appeal is ordinarily considered to be a continuation of the original proceeding, in the present case, we notice that further orders of <ORG> were also in contempla- tion and such further orders could only be if appeals were preferred to <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	We do not think that there was any doubt in anyone's mind that the matter would be taken up in appeal to <ORG> whichever way the writ petitions were decided.
RatioOfTheDecision	We are of the view that the undertakings given by the parties in the present cases were intended to and do continue to subsist.
RatioOfTheDecision	On the conclusions arrived at by us we have no doubt that the appeal must be allowed and the writ petition in <ORG> dismissed.
RatioOfTheDecision	However, we think that it is necessary to give a direction to the Government to dispose of the review applications after giving a n otice of hearing to the manufacturer.
RatioOfTheDecision	The hearing may be given within two months from today and the review application disposed of within two weeks after the conclusion of the hearing.
RatioOfTheDecision	Any information sought by the manufacturer may be given to him at the hearing in terms of what we have said in the judgment.
Fact	<ORG> is entitled to the costs of the appeal and the writ petition in <ORG>.
Argument	It appears that although several writ petitions filed by different manufacturers were disposed of by <ORG> by a common judgment, <ORG> filed an appeal within the prescribed period of limitation against one of the manufacturers, <ORG> only.
Argument	This was apparently done under some misapprehension that it would be enough if a single appeal was filed.
Argument	Later when it was realized that separate appeals were necessary, <ORG> filed petitions for special leave to appeal against the other manufacturers also.
Argument	As these petitions were filed beyond the prescribed period of l imitation, petitions for condoning the delay in filing the petitions for special leave to appeal had to be and were filed.
Argument	These applications are strenuously opposed by the manufacturers who contend the ordinary rule which is enforced in cases of delay namely that everyday's delay must be properly explained should also be rigorously enforced against the <ORG>.
Argument	It is con- tended that the <ORG> is a well verse litigant as compared with private litigants and even if there is justi- fication of adopting a liberal approach in condoning delay in the case of private litigants there was no need to adopt such approach in the case of the <ORG>.
Argument	In cases like the present where parties have acted on the assumption that no appeals had been filed against them and have proceeded to arrange their affairs accordingly it would be unjust to condone the delay in filing the appeals at the instance of the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	Though we see considerable force in the submission of <NAME>, we think that the circumstances of  the instant cases do justify the exercise of our discretion to condone the delay.
RatioOfTheDecision	Two important features have weighed with us in condoning the delay.
RulingByLowerCourt	One is that all the writ petitions were disposed of by a common judgment and an appeal had been filed in the principal case.
RulingByLowerCourt	The other is that it is a 'matter of serious concern to the public inter- est.
RulingByLowerCourt	We, therefore, condone the delay, grant special leave in all the petitions for special leave and direct the ap- peals to be listed for hearing on May 1, 1987.
