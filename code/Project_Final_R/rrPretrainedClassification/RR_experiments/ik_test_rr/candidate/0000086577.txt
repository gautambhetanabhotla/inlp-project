Fact	JUDGMENT <NAME>, C.J.
Argument	1.
Argument	This is an application by the State of Rajasthan for leave to appeal to <ORG> under Articles 132 and 133 of the Constitution of India.
Argument	2.
Argument	The application has been opposed on behalf of the opposite party on two technical grounds, namely,  1. that it is barred by limitation, and  2. that it has not been properly presented.
Argument	Learned counsel for the opposite party agrees that but for these two technical matters, the case is a fit one for granting leave under Articles 132(1) and 133(1)(c) of the Constitution.
Argument	3.
Fact	The case arose on an application under Article 226 of the Constitution by <NAME> and <NAME>.
Fact	That application was allowed on the 19th October, 1951.
Fact	The present application was filed on the 30th of January, 1952.
Argument	It is contended on behalf of the opposite party that the application is barred by limitation for two reasons  1. that the applicant is not entitled to any deduction of time taken in obtaining a copy of the judgment to be appealed from under Section 12 of  the Limitation Act, and  2. that, in any case, <NAME> having been brought on the record on an application, dated 5th March, 1952, the application was barred against him.
Argument	4.
Argument	Articles 132 and 133 of the Constitution provide for appeal to <ORG> from any judgment, decree or final order of <ORG>.
Statute	The relevant provisions of Section 12 of the Limitation Act in this connection are these :  "2.
Precedent	In computing the period of limitation prescribed for an appeal, an application for leave to appeal and an application for a review of judgment, the day on which the judgment complained of was pronounced, and the time requisite for obtaining a copy of the decree, sentence or order appealed from or sought to be reviewed, shall be excluded.
Precedent	3.
Precedent	Where a decree is appealed from or sought to be reviewed, the time requisite for obtaining a copy of the judgment on which it is founded shall also be excluded."
Argument	5.
Argument	The contention on behalf of the opposite party is that this case is covered by Section 12 (2), arid the  time taken in obtaining a copy of the judgment cannot be excluded.
RatioOfTheDecision	We have already pointed out that the judgment was delivered on the 19th October, 1951.
RatioOfTheDecision	Application for copy was made on the 15th January, 1952, and copy was delivered on the 30th January.
RatioOfTheDecision	1952.
RatioOfTheDecision	The application for leave to appeal was presented on the same day, namely, the 30th of January, 1952.
RatioOfTheDecision	Now if the period spent in obtaining the copy is excluded", the application would be within time, on the 30th of January.
Fact	The question is whether this period can be excluded under Sections 12 (2) or 12 (3) of the Limitation Act .
Argument	6.
Argument	There is difference of opinion among the various <ORG> as to whether the time taken in obtaining copy of the judgment can be excluded in cases of application for leave to appeal.
Precedent	Some <ORG>, as for example Allahabad, have held that as Sub-section (2) expressly contains the words 'application for leave to appeal', while Sub-section (3) does not, the time spent in obtaining copy of the Judgment cannot be allowed  in cases of applications for leave.
Precedent	Vide -- 'Gulabchand v. Peary Lal', A.I.R.
Precedent	1935 All.
Precedent	99.
Precedent	Other <ORG> have held that time taken in obtaining a copy of the judgment can be allowed under Sub-section (3) Vide -- 'In re, Secy, of State', A.I.R.
Precedent	1925 Mad.
Precedent	1241.
RatioOfTheDecision	We do not think it necessary to decide in the present case whether time for obtaining a copy of the judgment can be excluded in a case where there is a judgment as well as a decree following it.
RatioOfTheDecision	The present is a case, in our opinion, where there is no decree following the judgment of this Court.
RatioOfTheDecision	The application of the opposite party was under Article 226 of the Constitution of India, and was disposed of by the judgment dated the 19th October, 1951.
RatioOfTheDecision	Both Articles 132 and 133 allow appeals from a judgment, decree or final order of <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	It may be mentioned that in Section 109 of the Code of Civil Procedure, the words used were 'decree' or 'final order', and the word 'judgment' has been added by Articles 132 and 133 of the Constitution.
RatioOfTheDecision	It see ms to us that the word 'judgment' has been added to provide for cases coming to <ORG> under Article 226 of the Constitution of India.
RatioOfTheDecision	In cases under Article 226 , there is no decree.
RatioOfTheDecision	The application is disposed of by a judgment of the court, and it is that judgment, in our opinion, which is liable to appeal under Articles 132 and 133.
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 12(2) of the Limitation Act allows exclusion of time for copy of a decree, sentence or order.
RatioOfTheDecision	The word 'decree' is well understood, while the word 'sentence' refers to criminal proceedings.
RatioOfTheDecision	The word 'order' used in Sub-section (2), in our opinion, must cover judgments in cases under Article 226 of the Constitution of India, otherwise it is difficult to understand what is the order a copy of which has to be filed in cases under Article 226.
RatioOfTheDecision	We are, therefore, of opinion that in cases covered by Article 226 , the Judgment itself is the order to be appealed from, and the time taken in obtaining copy of the judgment has to be excluded.
RatioOfTheDecision	Once the time taken in obtaini ng the copy of the judgment is excluded, it is admitted that the application for leave to appeal was filed within time.
RatioOfTheDecision	7.
RatioOfTheDecision	As to the contention that, in any case, the application was barred by time as against <NAME>-lal, it is enough to say that we accept the explanation that the name of <NAME>lal was left out by oversight from the application which was filed on the 30th January, 1952.
RatioOfTheDecision	We, therefore, condone the delay, under the circumstances, under Section 5 of the Limitation Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	8.
RatioOfTheDecision	The next point that is urged that the application was not presented properly in so far as though it was signed by the Government Advocate, it was presented before the Registrar by his clerk and not by the Government Advocate himself.
RatioOfTheDecision	Under Order 3, Rule 1, C. P. C., any act in any court required or authorised by law to be made or done by a party may, except where otherwise expressly provided, be made or done by the party in person, or by his recognised agent, or by a pleader on his behalf.
RatioOfTheDecision	In this case, the act that was done was  the act of presentation of the application for leave to ap-  peal to <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	This act should have been done by the party in person, or by his recognised agent, or pleader.
RatioOfTheDecision	The Government Advocate naturally represents the <ORG>, but his clerk can neither be called the recognised agent of the <ORG>, nor a pleader acting for the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	The presentation, therefore, was not according to law in this case.
RatioOfTheDecision	9.
RatioOfTheDecision	It appears however that there has been a practice in this Court and in <ORG> of the former <ORG> of Jodhpur that Government Advocate's clerk presents applications on behalf of the Government Advocate.
RatioOfTheDecision	That this is so also appears from the fact that in this very case, the application for amendment of the original application praying for bringing <NAME> on the record was also presented by <NAME>, clerk of the Government Advocate, and not by the Government Advocate himself.
RulingByPresentCourt	We are of opinion that this practice, unsupported as it is by any rule, must stop in future.
RulingByPresentCourt	At the same time, it would,  in our opinion, be unfair to Government that it should suffer because of a wrong practice having grown up.
RulingByPresentCourt	The result of the improper presentation is that the application would not be deemed to have been presented on the 30th of January when it was actually presented.
RulingByPresentCourt	It may be taken to have been presented on the 3rd March, 1952, when the Government Advocate appeared in support of it at the preliminary hearing.
RulingByPresentCourt	Considering the peculiar circumstances of this case, we extend the time for making the presentation up to the 3rd of March, 1952, (on which date, at any rate, the application must be taken to have been properly presented,) under Section 5 of the Limitation Act.
RulingByPresentCourt	10.
RulingByPresentCourt	We, therefore, reject the technical objections raised on behalf of the opposite party, and order that a certificate be granted as prayed under Articles 132(1) and 133(1)(c) of the Constitution.
RulingByPresentCourt	In view of the technical defects in the case, we order parties to bear their own costs of the present application.
