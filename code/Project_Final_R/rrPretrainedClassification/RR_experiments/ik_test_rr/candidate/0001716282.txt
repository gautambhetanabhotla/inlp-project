It is, therefore, clear that in view of this clear intention of the legislature there can be no room for any argument that the State Act was in any way repugnant to the Central Act s. We have already pointed out from the decisions of <ORG> and this <ORG> that one of the important tests to find out as to whether or not there is repugnancy is to ascertain the intention of the legislature regarding the fact that the dominant legislature allowed the subordinate legislature to operate in the same field pari passu the State Act .
RatioOfTheDecision	<NAME> in his Interpretation on Statute Law 6th Ed.
RatioOfTheDecision	p. 369 observes as follows:-  "Many earlier statutes contain clauses similar in effect t o the general rule, but without the confusing words as to contrary intention.
RatioOfTheDecision	These statutes, of some of which a list is given below, seem not to be affected by the above rule, save so far as it enables the revisers of the statute-book to excise the particular clauses.
RatioOfTheDecision	In accordance with this rule, penalties imposed by statute for offences already punishable under a prior statute are regarded as cumulative or alternative and not as replacing the penalty to which the offender was previously liable."
RatioOfTheDecision	Such an intention is clearly discernible from the provisions of section 29 of the State Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	Mr. <NAME> tried to rebut this argument on the ground that section 29 would have no application where the inconsistency between the dominant statute and the subordinate statute is direct and complete.
RatioOfTheDecision	We have already found on a discussion of the various provisions of the State Act that there is no direct inconsistency at all between the State Act and the Central Act s, and this affords a sufficient answer to the argume nt of Mr. <NAME>.
RatioOfTheDecision	Having, therefore, given our anxious consideration to the import and ambit of section 29 it seems to us that the provisions of section 29 would be presumptive proof of the fact that there is no repugnancy between the State Act and the Central Act s nor did either the legislature or the President intend to create any repugnancy between these Acts as a result of which the criticism regarding the repugnancy is completely obliterated in the instant case and we, therefore, hold that the State legislature never intended to occupy the same field covered by the Central Act s.  It was also contended by Mr. <NAME> that if the Central Act s being repugnant to the State Act are pressed into service even after the repeal of the State Act , the Central Act s would stand repealed hence the prosecution of the appellant would be hit by Article 20(3) of the Constitution, i.e.
RatioOfTheDecision	the appellant cannot be prosecuted for an ex post facto offence.
RatioOfTheDecision	On our findings in this case that there is no inconsistency be tween the State Act and the Central Act s the application of Article 20(3) of the Constitution to the facts of this case does not arise at all.
RatioOfTheDecision	We, therefore, find ourselves in complete agreement with the view taken by <ORG> that the State Act creates new and distinct offences and is not in any way repugnant to any provisions of the Central Act s and consequently overruled the first limb of the argument of counsel for the appellant.
RatioOfTheDecision	Similarly the contention of Mr. <NAME> as to whether or not the prosecution of the appellant would be violative of Article 14 of the Constitution is not available to the appellant, and consequently the learned counsel gave up this point and in our opinion very rightly because since the State Act has now been repealed the question of the prosecution of the appellant hereafter under the State Act does not arise at all, and, therefore, the question of two remedies being open to the prosecution which they may elect at their own option does not arise in this case.
RatioOfTheDecision	The app ellant can be prosecuted only under the Corruption Act and the Penal Code and under no other Act at the moment.
RatioOfTheDecision	Moreover, it was obviously wrong to say that the earlier Central Law became violative of Article 14 as soon as the State law was enacted.
RatioOfTheDecision	This brings us to the second limb of the argument of the learned counsel for the appellant which relates to the import and connotation of the term 'public servant' appearing in section 21(12) of the Indian Penal Code .
RatioOfTheDecision	Clause 12 of section 21 which is the relevant provision so far as the present case is concerned runs thus :-  "21.
Argument	The words 'public servant' denote a person falling under any of the descriptions hereinafter following namely:-  X X X Twelfth-Every person-  (a) in the service or pay of the <ORG> or remunerated by fees or commission for the performance of any public duty by the <ORG>:  X X X It was vehemently contended by Mr. <NAME> that having regard to the constitutional and public duties of a Chief Minister or a Minister he cannot be  deemed to be a public servant in any sense of the term.
Argument	He further contended that the entire clause (12) (a) should be read as a whole and cannot be severed into two limbs in as much as the words 'in the service or pay of the <ORG>' are used as synonyms.
Argument	It was further contended that the words 'in the service or pay of the <ORG>' clearly connote the relationship of master and servant-a relationship which is completely beyond the concept of the position of a Minister or a Chief Minister.
RatioOfTheDecision	We, however, agree that so far as the first part of clause (12) (a) is concerned, namely "in the service of the <ORG>' undoubtedly signifies a relationship of master and servant where the employer employs the employee on the basis of a salary or remuneration.
RatioOfTheDecision	But we are of the opinion that so far as the second limb 'in the pay of the <ORG>' is concerned, that appears to be of a much wider amplitude-so as to include within its ambit even public servant who may not be a regular employee receiving salary from  his master.
RatioOfTheDecision	In other words, we think that even a Minister or a Chief Minister will be clearly covered by the expression 'person in the pay of the Government'.
RatioOfTheDecision	Mr. <NAME>, however, relied on the meaning of the words "in the pay of' as appearing in the various dictionaries.
RatioOfTheDecision	In Shorter Oxford English Dictionary the expression 'in the pay of' is defined thus-  "To give money, etc., in return for something or in discharge of an obligation.
RatioOfTheDecision	Of a thing or action.
RatioOfTheDecision	To yield an adequate return.
RatioOfTheDecision	To give money or other equivalent value for".
RatioOfTheDecision	Similarly 'Payer' is defined thus:  "One who pays a sum of money".

Statute	"Wages, salary remuneration".

RatioOfTheDecision	A careful analysis of the meanings assigned to the word 'pay' in the various dictio naries and the texts referred to above would clearly reveal that the expression 'in the pay of' connotes that a person is getting salary, compensation, wages or any amount of money.
RatioOfTheDecision	This by itself however does not lead to the inference that a relationship of master and servant must necessarily exist in all cases where a person is paid salary.
RatioOfTheDecision	This aspect of the matter would become crystal clear if we examine the nature and the constitutional position and status of a Minister or a Chief Minister.
RatioOfTheDecision	284  Article 164 of the Constitution runs thus:-  "Other provisions as to Ministers: (1) The Chief Minister shall be appointed by the Governor and the other Ministers shall be appointed by the Governor on the advice of the Chief Minister, and the Ministers shall hold office during the pleasure of the Governor: Provided that in the States of Bihar, Madhya Pradesh and Orissa, there shall be a Minister in Charge of tribal welfare who may in addition be in charge of the welfare of the Scheduled Castes and backward classe s or any other work".
RatioOfTheDecision	This Article clearly shows that a Chief Minister is appointed by the Governor and having been appointed by the Governor it is manifest that he is subordinate to the Governor.
RatioOfTheDecision	Even in section 52 (1) of the Government of India Act, 1935 which preceded our Constitution the provision was worded thus:-  "52 (1) The Governor of a Governor's province may, by notification, appoint ministers, not being members of his executive council or other officials to administer transferred subjects, and any ministers so appointed shall hold office during his pleasure: There may be paid to any minister so appointed in any province the same salary as is payable to a member of the executive council in that province, unless a smaller salary is provided by vote of the legislative council of the province".
RatioOfTheDecision	In this section also it was the Governor who alone had the power to choose the ministers.
RatioOfTheDecision	In fact, in Article 164 the word 'appointment' is much higher than the concept of a person being chosen.
RatioOfTheDecision	Article 164(5 ) provides for the salary and allowances of Ministers and runs thus:-  "164 (5) The salaries and allowances of Ministers shall be such as <ORG> may from time to time by law determine and, until <ORG> so determines, shall be as specified in the Second Schedule".
RatioOfTheDecision	Under this provision the Ministers on being appointed by the Governor are entitled to such salaries and allowances as <ORG> may determine from time to time and until this is done, the emoluments will be such as are specified in the Second Schedule.
RatioOfTheDecision	As however all the Legislatures of the States as also <ORG> have already passed Acts providing for the salaries and emoluments of the Chief Minister and the Ministers the specification of their emoluments in the Second Schedule to the Constitution have been deleted.
RatioOfTheDecision	Article 167 lays down the duties of the Chief Minister and runs thus:-  "167.
RatioOfTheDecision	Duties of Chief Minister as respects the furnishing of information to Governor etc.
RatioOfTheDecision	It shal l be the duty of the Chief Minister of each State  (a) to communicate to the Governor of the State all decisions of <ORG> relating to the administration of the affairs of the State and proposals for legislation;  (b) to furnish such information relating to the administration of affairs of the State and proposals for legislation as the Governor may call for;  (c) if the Governor so requires, to submit for the consideration of <ORG> any matter on which a decision has been taken by a Minister but which has not been considered by the <ORG>".
RatioOfTheDecision	It is, therefore, clear that by virtue of the provisions contained in Article 167 , the Chief Minister undoubtedly performs a public duty of the nature as enjoined by clauses (a) to (c) of Article 167.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is also clearly provided in the Constitution that the Chief Minister or the Ministers are entitled to salaries or allowances obviously in lieu of public duties that they perform.
RatioOfTheDecision	The salaries given to the Chief Minister or the Ministe rs are given from the <ORG> funds, and therefore, there will be no difficulty in holding that the Ministers are in the pay of the <ORG> inasmuch as they receive their salaries, remunerations or wages from the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	Mr. <NAME>, however, submitted that no analogy can be drawn between the constitutional provisions and the provisions contained in the <ORG> of India Act because the constitutional position of a Chief Minister under the Constitution was not the same as under the <ORG> of India Act where the Governor enjoyed vast and plenary powers and was not bound by the advice of <ORG> as the Governor is under our Constitution.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is not necessary to probe into this aspect of the matter, because the Constitution clearly lays down that the Governor appoints the Chief Minister and being the appoint-  286  ing authority he is also the dismissing authority.
RatioOfTheDecision	We are not at all concerned in the instant case as to the circumstances under which the Governor can appoint or di smiss the Chief Minister.
RatioOfTheDecision	Once it is conceded that the Governor appoints the Chief Minister who is paid a salary according to a statute made by the legislature from the <ORG> funds, the Chief Minister becomes a person in the pay of the <ORG> so as to fall squarely within clause (12) of section 21 of the Penal Code.
RatioOfTheDecision	There is another circumstance to show that a Chief Minister or a Minister is undoubtedly a public servant which was relied upon by <ORG> in repelling the argument of Mr. <NAME>.
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 199 of the Code runs thus:-  "199 (2) Notwithstanding anything contained in this Code, when any offence falling under Chapter XXI of the Indian Penal Code is alleged to have been committed against a person who, at the time of such commission is the President of India, the Vice President of India, the Governor of a State, the Administrator of a Union Territory or a Minister of the Union or of a State or of a Union territory, or any other public servant employed in connection with the affairs o f the <ORG> or of a <ORG> in respect of his conduct in the discharge of his public functions <ORG> may take cognizance of such offence, without the case being committed to it, upon a complaint in writing made by the Public Prosecutor".
