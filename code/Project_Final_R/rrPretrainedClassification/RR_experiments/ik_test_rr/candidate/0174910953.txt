RatioOfTheDecision	12.
RatioOfTheDecision	The validity of the levy in question was challenged on the following grounds :  (1) That no fresh levy can be imposed by a retrospective legislation;  (2) That t he legislature cannot in case of legislation of the nature with which we are concerned, separate into independent commodities split and unsplit pulses or processed or unprocessed pulses and on that footing seek to impose tax twice over on the same commodity in respect of the goods liable to be taxed at a single point;  (3) That the newly added Explanation to Section 3-D read with Section 7 of the Amending Act amounts to an unlawful usurpation of judicial power by the legislature;  (4) The newly added Explanation II to Section 3-D is violative of Article 14 of the Constitution.
RatioOfTheDecision	There is no rational basis for separating split or processed pulses from unsplit or unprocessed pulses;  (5) On a true construction of Explanation II to Section 3-D no fresh charge can be held to have been imposed:  (6) No levy of purchase tax can be made without a fresh Notification Under Section 3-D read with Explanation II showing therein separately foodgrains unsplit or unprocessed as well as foodgrains split or processed; and (7) T hat the power conferred on the <ORG> Under Section 3-D amounts to an excessive delegation of legislative power and consequently void.

RatioOfTheDecision	The source of the legislative power to levy sales or purchase tax on goods is Entry 54 of the List II of.
RatioOfTheDecision	the Constitution.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is well settled that subject to Constitutional restrictions a power to legislate includes a power to legislate prospectively as well as retrospectively.

Precedent	14.
RatioOfTheDecision	We see no force in the second contention  advanced on behalf of the appellants.
RatioOfTheDecision	As seen earlier the general rule as enunciated in Section 3 is multi-point tax-sales tax or purchase tax; but power is conferred on the Government to select any transaction in respect of such goods or class of goods as the Government may choose to levy a single point sales tax or purchase tax.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is open to the legislature to define the nature of the goods, the sale or purchase of which should be brought to tax.
RatioOfTheDecision	Legislature was not incompetent to separate the processed or split pulses from the unsplit or unprocessed pulses and treat the two as separate and independent goods.
Precedent	15.
Precedent	<CITATION_436116> , .
Precedent	question arose for decision whether it was open to the legislature to impose separate excise duty on tobacco leaf as well as on broken leaf of tobacco.
Precedent	This Court overruled the contention that such a levy was invalid.
RatioOfTheDecision	It held that it was open for the legislature to separate the two items.
RatioOfTheDecision	We see no basis for the contention that the legislature cannot for the purpose of tax  under the Act separate the split or processed pulses from the unsplit or unprocessed.
RatioOfTheDecision	The power of the legislature to specify the nature of the goods the sale or purchase of which, it will bring to tax is very wide.
RatioOfTheDecision	16.
RatioOfTheDecision	Now coming to point No.
RatioOfTheDecision	3, there is no justification for the contention that the legislature has usurped any judicial power.
RatioOfTheDecision	The legislature has not purported either directly or by necessary implication to overrule the decisions of <ORG> in <NAME> case (supra).
RatioOfTheDecision	On the other hand it has accepted that decision as correct; but has sought to remove the basis of that decision by retrospectively changing the law.
RatioOfTheDecision	This <ORG> has pointed out in several cases the distinction between the encroachment on the judicial power and the nullification of the effect of a judicial decision by changing the law retrospectively.
RatioOfTheDecision	The former is outside the competence of the legislature but the latter is within its permissible limits.
RatioOfTheDecision	From the statement of objects and reasons, it  appears that in the principal Act, the legislative intent was not clearly brought out.
RatioOfTheDecision	By means of the Amending Act the legislature wanted to make clear its intent.
RatioOfTheDecision	17.
RatioOfTheDecision	The fourth contention also appears to be without any basis.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is true that the taxing statutes are not outside the scope of Article 14 of the Constitution.
RatioOfTheDecision	But the legislature has wide powers of classification in the case of taxing statutes.
RatioOfTheDecision	18.
Precedent	In <NAME>'s case (supra), this <ORG> ruled that there was no unconstitutional discrimination in the imposition of the excise duty on tobacco in the broken leaf form.
Precedent	Therein it was observed that tobacco in the broken leaf form was capable of being used in the manufacture of bidis while tobacco in the whole leaf form could not be so used economically; the two forms of tobacco were different by the test of capability of user; the tariff is not based either wholly or even primarily by reference to the use of tobacco and there was a clear and unambiguous distinction between tobacco in the whole leaf  form covered by item 5 and tobacco in the broken leaf form covered by item 6 which had a reasonable relation to the object intended by the imposition of the tariff.
Precedent	19.
Precedent	<CITATION_1048632> , .
Precedent	this Court laid down the tests to find out whether there are discriminatory provisions in a taxing statute.
Precedent	Therein this Court observed that in order to judge whether a law was discriminatory what had primarily to be looked into was not its phraseology but its real effect.
RatioOfTheDecision	If there was equality and uniformity within each group, the law could not be discriminatory, though due to fortuitous circumstances in a peculiar situation some included in a class might get some advantage over others, so long as they were not sought out for special treatment.
RatioOfTheDecision	Although taxation laws could be no exception to this rule, the courts would, in view of the inherent complexity of fiscal adjustment of diverse elements, permit a larger discretion to the legislature in the matter of classification so long as there was no transgression of the f undamental principles underlying the doctrine of classification.
RatioOfTheDecision	The power of the legislature to classify must necessarily be wide and flexible so as to enable it to adjust its system of taxation in all proper and reasonable ways.
RatioOfTheDecision	20.
RatioOfTheDecision	It must be noticed that generally speaking the primary purpose of the levy of all taxes is to raise funds for public good.
RatioOfTheDecision	Which person should be taxed, what transaction should be taxed or what goods should be taxed, depends upon social, economic and administrative considerations.
RatioOfTheDecision	In a democratic set up it is for the legislature to decide what economic or social policy it should pursue or what administrative consideration it should bear in mind.
RatioOfTheDecision	The classification between the processed or split pulses and unprocessed or unsplit pulses is a reasonable classification.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is based on the use to which those goods can be put.
RatioOfTheDecision	Hence, in our opinion, the impugned classification is not violative of Article 14.
RatioOfTheDecision	21.
RatioOfTheDecision	A feeble attempt was made to show that the retrospective levy made und er the Act is violative of Article 19(1)(f) and (g).
RatioOfTheDecision	But we see no substance in that contention.

RatioOfTheDecision	The contention that the retrospective levy did not afford any opportunity to the dealers to pass on the tax payable to the consumers has not much validity.
RatioOfTheDecision	The tax is levied on the dealer: the fact that he is allowed to pass on the tax to the consumers or he is generally in a position to pass on the same to the consumer has no relevance when we consider the legislative competence.
RatioOfTheDecision	22.
RatioOfTheDecision	It was next urged that on a true contribution of Explanation II to Section 3-D , no charge can be said to have been created on the purchases of split or processed pulses.
RatioOfTheDecision	It was firstly c ontended that an Explanation cannot extend the scope of the main section; it can only explain that section.
RatioOfTheDecision	In construing a statutory provision, the first and and the foremost rule of construction is the literary construction.
RatioOfTheDecision	All that we have to see at the very outset is what does that provision say ?
RatioOfTheDecision	If the provision is unambiguous and if from that provision, the legislative intent is clear, we need not call into aid the other rules of construction of statutes.
RatioOfTheDecision	The other rules of construction of statutes are called into aid only when the legislative intention is not clear.
RatioOfTheDecision	Ordinarily a proviso to a section is intended to take out a part of the main section for special treatment.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is not expected to enlarge the scope of the main section.
RatioOfTheDecision	But cases have arisen in which this <ORG> has held that despite the fact that a provision is called proviso, it is really a separate provision and the so called proviso has substantially altered the main section.
Precedent	<CITATION_1359356> , .
Precedent	this <ORG> held that by the fiction i n Section 10(2) (vii) second proviso read with Section 2 (6C) of the Indian Income-tax Act, 1922 what is really not income is, for the purpose of computation of assessable income, made taxable income.
Precedent	23.
Precedent	<CITATION_1669028> ., this Court observed :  The primary purpose of the proviso now under consideration is, it is apparent, to provide a substitute or an alternative remedy to that which is prohibited by the main part of Section 4(1) .
RatioOfTheDecision	There is, therefore, no question of the proviso carving out any portion out of the area covered by the main part and leaving the other part unaffected.
RatioOfTheDecision	What we have stated earlier should suffice to establish that the proviso now before us is really not a proviso in the accepted sense but an independent legislative provision by which to a remedy which is prohibited by the main part of the section, an alternative is provided.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is further obvious to us that the proviso is not coextensive with but covers a field wider than the main part of Section 4(1) .
Precedent	24.

Precedent	26.
RatioOfTheDecision	We are unable to accept the contention that Explanation II to Section 3-D did not widen the scope of Section 3-D .
RatioOfTheDecision	Sectio n 3-D as it originally stood dealt with foodgrains and pulses.

RatioOfTheDecision	We next come to the contention that no levy of purchase tax can be made on split or unprocessed pulses without a fresh notification Under Section 3-D read with Explanation II showing therein separately foodgrains unsplit or unprocessed as well as foodgrains split or processed.

RatioOfTheDecision	31.
RatioOfTheDecision	The only remaining contention is that the delegation  made to the executive Under Section 3-D is an excessive delegation.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is true that the legislature cannot delegate its legislative functions to any other body.
RatioOfTheDecision	But subject to that qualification, it is permissible for the legislature to delegate the power to select the persons on whom the tax is to be levied or the goods or the transactions on which the tax is to be levied.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the Act , Under Section 3 the legislature has sought to impose multi-point tax on all sales and purchases.
RatioOfTheDecision	After having done that it has given power to the executive, a high authority and which is presumed to command the majority support in the legislature, to select for special treatment dealings in certain class of goods.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the very nature of things, it is impossible for the legislature to enumerate goods, dealings in which sales tax or purchase tax should be imposed.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is also impossible for the legislature to select the goods which should be subjected to a single point sales or purchase tax.
RatioOfTheDecision	Before making such selections several  aspects such as the impact of the levy on the society, economic consequences and the administrative convenience will have to be considered.
RatioOfTheDecision	These factors may change from time to time.
RatioOfTheDecision	Hence in the very nature of things, these details have got to be left to the executive.
