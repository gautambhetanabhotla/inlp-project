RatioOfTheDecision	25 and those reasons did charge each worker with misconduct inasmuch as he had taken part in the illegal strike and had refused to resume duty inspite of repeated demands made by the <ORG> in that behalf.
RatioOfTheDecision	All the same, the <ORG> made it clear that inspite of such misconduct it had no intention of punishing the workers who were given not only the benefit of an order of discharge simpliciter but also the option to come back to work within a specified period in which case they would be reinstated with full benefits.
RatioOfTheDecision	An intention not to punish could not be expressed in clearer terms and is further made out from the fact that more than 400 workers who resumed duty were reinstated without break in service.
RatioOfTheDecision	In passing the orders of discharge, therefore, the <ORG> did nothing more than act under M.S.O.
RatioOfTheDecision	23 and its action cannot be regarded as am ounting to dismissal in the case of any of the workers.
RatioOfTheDecision	They had the right to choose between a discharge simpliciter and a dismissal and, in the interests of the factory and the members of the <ORG> and perhaps on compassionate grounds also, they chose the former in unequivocal terms.
RatioOfTheDecision	The intention to punish being absent, the finding of <ORG> that the order of discharge amounted to one of dismissal cannot be sustained.
RatioOfTheDecision	10.
RatioOfTheDecision	I now turn to the interpretation of sub-section (2) of section 11A of the 1947 Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is a well settled canon of interpretation of statutes that the language used by the legislature must be regarded as the only source of its intention unless such language is ambiguous, in which situation the preamble to the Act the Statement of Objects of and Reasons for bringing it on the Statute book and the purpose underlying the legislation may be taken into consideration for ascertaining such intention.
RatioOfTheDecision	That the purpose of the legislation is to fulfil a socio-economic need, or the express ob ject underlying it, does not come into the picture till an ambiguity is detected in the language and the court must steer clear of the temptation to mould the written word according to its own concept of what should have been enacted.
RatioOfTheDecision	That is how I propose to approach the exercise in hand.
RatioOfTheDecision	11.
RatioOfTheDecision	For the sake of convenience of reference I may set out the provisions of clauses (aa) and (r) of section 2 , of sub-sections (1) and (2) and the opening clause of sub-section (3) of section 11 , and of the whole of section 11A of the 1947 Act:  "2.
Statute	(aa) `arbitrator' includes an umpire;" "2.
Statute	(r) `<ORG>' means an Industrial <ORG> constituted under section 7A and includes an Industrial <ORG> constituted before the 10th of March, 1957, under this Act;"  "11.
Statute	(1) Subject to any rules that may be made in this behalf, an arbitrator, a <ORG>, <ORG>, Labour <ORG>, <ORG> or National <ORG> shall follow such procedure as the arbitrator or other authority concerned may think fit.
Statute	"(2) A conciliation officer or a mem ber of a <ORG>, or <ORG> or the presiding officer of a Labour <ORG>, <ORG> or National <ORG> may for the purpose of inquiry into any existing or apprehended industrial dispute, after giving reasonable notice, enter the premises occupied by any establishment to which the dispute relates.
Statute	"(3) Every <ORG>, <ORG>, Labour <ORG>, <ORG> and National <ORG> shall have the same powers as are vested in a Civil <ORG> under the Code of Civil Procedure, 1908. when trying a suit, in respect of the following matters, namely:- .........."  "11A.
Statute	Where an industrial dispute, relating to the discharge or dismissal of a workman has been referred to a Labour <ORG>, <ORG> or National <ORG> for adjudication and, in the course of the adjudication proceedings, the Labour <ORG>, <ORG> or National <ORG>, as the case may be, is satisfied that the order of discharge or dismissal was not justified, it may, by its award, set aside the order of discharge or dismissal and direct reinstatement of the workman on such  terms and conditions, if any, as it thinks fit, or give such other relief to the workman including the award of any lesser punishment in lieu of discharge or dismissal as the circumstances of the case may require:  Provided that in any proceeding under this section <ORG>, <ORG> or National <ORG>, as the case may be, shall rely only on the materials on record and shall not take any fresh evidence in relation to the matter."
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 2 of the Act specifically lays down that unless there is anything repugnant in the subject or context, the expressions defined therein would have the meanings attributed to them.
RatioOfTheDecision	Throughout the Act therefore, while 'arbitrator' would include an umpire, a '<ORG>' would not include an arbitrator but would mean only an Industrial <ORG> constituted under the Act, unless the context makes it necessary to give the word a different connotation.
RatioOfTheDecision	In sub- section (1) of section 11 , it is conceded, the word '<ORG>' has been used in accordance with the definition ap pearing in clause (r) of section 2 because an arbitrator is separately mentioned in that sub-section.
RatioOfTheDecision	In sub-sections (2) and (3) of that section a <ORG>, <ORG>, a <ORG> and a National <ORG> have been invested with certain powers.
RatioOfTheDecision	Would a <ORG> as contemplated by sub- sections (2) and (3) then include an arbitrator ?
RatioOfTheDecision	My reply to the question is all emphatic 'no'.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is well settled that if a term or expression is used in a particular piece of legislation in one sense at one place, the same sense will pervade the entire legislation wherever the term is used unless an intention to the contrary is expressed.
RatioOfTheDecision	Here the word '<ORG>' has been used in three sub-sections of the same section and no reason at all is fathomable for the proposition that it means one thing in sub-section (1) and something different in sub-sections (2) and (3).
RatioOfTheDecision	It may also be mentioned here that in all the three sub-sections the word '<ORG>' has a capital 'T' which is also part of the expression '<ORG>' as occurri ng in clause (r) of section 2 and thus connotes a proper noun rather than the generic word 'tribunal' as embracing all institutions adjudicating upon rights of contending parties.
RatioOfTheDecision	A third and perhaps a clinching reason for this interpretation is available in the use of the expression "National <ORG>" along with the word "<ORG>" in all the three sub-sections which militates against the argument that the word "<ORG>" as used in sub-sections (2) and (3) means an institution of that type.
RatioOfTheDecision	If the word "<ORG>" as used in sub-sections (2) and (3) means such an institution, then the use of the expression "National <ORG>" would be redundant and redundancy is not one of the qualities easily attributable to a legislative product.
RatioOfTheDecision	In that case, in fact, other words used in the two sub-sections last mentioned, namely, '<ORG>' and 'Labour <ORG>' would also become redundant.
RatioOfTheDecision	In this view of the matter, the word "<ORG>" as used in all the first three sub-sections of section 11 must be held to have been used  in the sense of the definition occurring in clause (r) of section 2 .
RatioOfTheDecision	12.
RatioOfTheDecision	Section 11A is just the next succeeding section and therein a part of the arrangement adopted is the same as in sub-sections (2) and (3) of section 11 so that powers are conferred by it on <ORG>, <ORG> or National <ORG>" which arrangement is repeated in the section thrice over.
RatioOfTheDecision	That the word "<ORG>" as used in section 11A has the same meaning as it carries in the three sub-sections of section 11 is obvious and I need not repeat the reasons in that behalf; for, they are practically the same as have been set out by me in relation to section 11 .
RatioOfTheDecision	13.
RatioOfTheDecision	In my opinion the language employed in section 11A sufferers from no ambiguity whatever and is capable only of one meaning, i.e., that the word '<ORG>' occurring therein is used in the sense of the definition given in clause (r) of section 2 .
RatioOfTheDecision	It is thus not permissible for this Court to take the Statement of Objects and Reasons or the purpose underlying t he enactment into consideration while interpreting section 11A .
RatioOfTheDecision	I may mention here however that a perusal of the Statement of Objects and Reasons forming the background to the enactment of section 11A leads me to the same conclusion.
RatioOfTheDecision	In that Statement a reference was specifically made to tribunals as well as arbitrators in, terms of the recommendations of <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	But inspite of that the word 'arbitrator' is conspicuous by its absence from the section.
RatioOfTheDecision	What is the reason for the omission ?
RatioOfTheDecision	Was it consciously and deliberately made or was it due to carelessness on the part of the draftsmen and a consequent failure on the part of the legislature ?
RatioOfTheDecision	In my opinion the <ORG> would step beyond the field of interpretation and enter upon the area of legislation if it resorts to guess work (however intelligently the same may be carried out) and attributes the omission to the latter cause in a situation like this which postulates that the pointed attention of the legislature was drawn to t he desirability of clothing an arbitrator with the same powers as were sought to be conferred on certain courts and tribunals by section 11A and it did not accept the recommendation.
RatioOfTheDecision	I would hold, in the circumstances, that the omission was deliberately made.
RatioOfTheDecision	It follows that the powers given to a <ORG> under section 11A are not exercisable by an arbitrator who, therefore, cannot interfere with the punishment (awarded by the employer) in case he finds misconduct proved.
RatioOfTheDecision	14.
RatioOfTheDecision	The last point on which I differ with the finding of my learned brother relates to the exercise by <ORG> of its powers under article 227 of the Constitution of India.
RatioOfTheDecision	As pointed out by him <ORG>, while discharging its functions as envisaged by that article, does not sit as a court of appeal over the award of the arbitrator but exercises limited jurisdiction which extends only to seeing that the arbitrator has functioned within the scope of his legal authority.
RatioOfTheDecision	This proposition finds full support from <CITATION_568069>  , Assam and Others(1), <CITATION_794955> , Calcutta(2), state of <CITATION_1763592> (3) and <CITATION_5947> .
RatioOfTheDecision	(4), all of which have ben discussed at length by him and require no further consideration at my hands.
RatioOfTheDecision	In this view of the matter it was not open to <ORG> to revise the punishment (if the discharge is regarded as such) meted out by the <ORG> to the delinquent workmen and left in tact by the arbitrator whose authority in doing so has not been shown to have been exercised beyond the limits of his jurisdiction.
RatioOfTheDecision	15.
RatioOfTheDecision	I need not go into the other aspects of the case.
RatioOfTheDecision	In view of my findings-  (a) that the orders of discharge of the workmen could not be regarded as orders of their dismissal and were, on the other hand, orders of discharge simpliciter properly passed under M.S.O.
RulingByPresentCourt	23;  (b) that the arbitrator could not exercise the powers conferred on a <ORG> under section 11A of the 1947 Act and could not therefore interfere with the punishment awarded by the <ORG> to the workmen (even  if the discharge could be regarded a punishment), and  (c) that in any case <ORG> exceeded the limits of its jurisdiction in interfering with the said punishment purporting to act in the exercise of its powers under article 227 of the Constitution of India, the judgment of <ORG> must be reversed and the order of the arbitrator restored.
RulingByPresentCourt	The three appeals are decided accordingly, the parties being left to bear their own costs throughout.
