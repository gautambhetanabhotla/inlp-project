Fact	The appellant was working as a Ticket Examiner in <ORG> at its Nagpur Depot.
Fact	On March 21, 1964 a bus belonging to the <ORG> was checked by <ORG> when nine and half passengers out of 26 were found travelling without tickets.
Fact	The appellant was on the bus in the discharge of his duties as a Ticket Examiner.
Fact	<ORG> prepared a Panchnama on the spot obtaining thereon the signatures of the appellant, the driver  and the ticked conductor.
Fact	It was found that the conductor had collected the fare from all the 26 passengers who were travelling in the bus but had not issued tickets to 9 1/2 passengers.
Fact	Since it was the duty of the appellant as a Ticket Examiner to check whether the conductor had collected fare from all the passengers and in token thereof had issued tickets to them, a chargesheet was served on the appellant for breach of clauses 12(b) and (d) of the Madhya Pradesh Standard Standing Orders which govern the matter by reason of Rule 7 of the Madhya Pradesh Industrial Employment (Standing Orders) Rules, 1963.
Fact	A Domestic inquiry was thereafter held into the charges and the appellant having been found guilty the <ORG>, through its Depot Manager, dismissed him by an order dated August 14, 1964.
Fact	On December 9, 1964 the appellant filed an application in <ORG>, Jabalpur, under section 31 of the Madhya Pradesh Industrial Relations Act challenging the validity of the inquiry on various grounds and pray ing that the order of dismissal be set aside and that an order of reinstatement be passed with back wages.
Fact	By a preliminary order dated December 7, 1966 <ORG> held that the Domestic tribunal did not hold a proper inquiry into the charges levelled against the appellant but that it was open to the parties to lead evidence before it on the merits of the case and to satisfy it whether the appellant was guilty of the charges and further whether the conduct of the appellant was such as to call for an order of dismissal.
RulingByLowerCourt	Parties thereafter led evidence before <ORG>, on a consideration of which it held by an order dated August 18, 1967 that the appellant was guilty of the charges levelled against him and that in the circumstances of the case the punishment of dismissal was neither harsh nor unjust.
RulingByLowerCourt	Feeling aggrieved by the aforesaid order of <ORG> the appellant preferred a revision application to <ORG>, Indore, under section 66 of the Madhya Pradesh Industrial Relation s Act.
RulingByLowerCourt	<ORG> confirmed the findings of fact recorded by <ORG> and upheld the order of dismissal.
RulingByLowerCourt	As regards back wages, <ORG> held that the order of <ORG> dated August 18, 1967 would relate back to the date when the appellant was dismissed by the <ORG> and therefore the appellant was not entitled to back wages till the date when <ORG> passed its final order.
Fact	The appellant thereafter filed a petition in <ORG> under articles 226 and 227 of the Constitution challenging the order of <ORG>.
Fact	<ORG> by its order dated March 6, 1968 dismissed the petition in limine, though with a speaking order.

Precedent	The Madhya Pradesh Industrial Employment (Standing Orders) Rules, 1963 provide by Rule 7, to the extent material, that the Standard Standing Orders for all undertakings to which the Ma dhya Pradesh Industrial Employment (Standing Orders) Act, 26 of 1961, applies shall be those set out in the Annexures to the Rules.
Precedent	We are concerned in this case with Standard Standing Order 12(b) which provides that "theft, fraud, or dishonesty in connection with the business or property of the undertakings" shall amount to a major misconduct on the part of an employee.
RatioOfTheDecision	The appellant had evidently entered the particular bus in order to check it in his capacity as a Ticket Examiner and it is undisputed that it was his duty to check whether the passengers travelling in the bus had paid the fare and whether tickets were issued to them by the conductor on collecting the fare from them.
RatioOfTheDecision	The bus was carrying a complement of 26 passengers out of whom 9 1/2 passengers did not hold tickets.
RatioOfTheDecision	The Panchnama which was drawn on the spot shows that the conductor had collected the fare from the passengers but had not issued any tickets to them.
RatioOfTheDecision	There cannot be the least doubt that if the appellant were so minded, he could h ave easily detected the misconduct of the conductor.
RatioOfTheDecision	Obviously, the appellant had colluded with the conductor in depriving the <ORG> of its legitimate earnings.
RatioOfTheDecision	The only explanation offered by the appellant was that he was not travelling in the bus at the relevant time and that explanation has been found to be false consistently by all the courts.
RatioOfTheDecision	The appellant having acted dishonestly in connection with the business of the <ORG>, he was clearly guilty of a major misconduct.

Precedent	(p. 487) We will consider the impact and implication of the concluding portion of this passage but before we do so, we must draw attention to a 5-Judge Bench decision of this <ORG> in <CITATION_794955> (4), which is directly in point and concludes the question under consideration.
Precedent	In that case an inquiry was held against an employee of <ORG> by the Station Manager, on whose findings the employee was dismissed by the Regional Representative of the Company on payment of one month's wages.
Precedent	The employee challenged the order of dismissal by filing an application under section 33A of the Industrial Disputes Act, 1947 on the ground that the Station Manager was biased against him and therefore the inquiry was vitiated as being contrary to the principles of natu ral justice.
Precedent	<ORG> took the view that the employee's contention that the Station Manager was biased against him could not be brushed aside lightly but it went on to hold that even if there was some violation the principles of natural justice on account of the bias of the Inquiry Officer, the Company was entitled to adduce, as it did, all the evidence before it in support of its action and therefore it was open to <ORG> to decide on that evidence whether the action was justified and whether the approval to the order of dismissal should be granted.
Precedent	On going into that evidence, <ORG> held that the breaches committed by the employee were of a serious nature and the order of dismissal passed by the employer was therefore justified.
Precedent	In an appeal by special leave against the decision of <ORG>, it was contended in this Court on behalf of the employee that since the Domestic inquiry was found defective, <ORG>, even if it was of the opinion that the dismissal was j ustified, should have ordered the dismissal from the date of its award and not from the date when the Regional Representative passed the order of dismissal.
Precedent	Rejecting this contention, this Court held:  "The present is a case where the employer has held an inquiry though it was defective and has passed an order of dismissal and seeks approval of that order.
RatioOfTheDecision	If the inquiry is not defective, <ORG> has only to see whether there was a prima facie case for dismissal and whether the employer had come to the bona fide conclusion that the employee was guilty of misconduct.
RatioOfTheDecision	Thereafter on coming to the conclusion that the employer had bona fide come to the conclusion that the employee was guilty i.e.
RatioOfTheDecision	there was no unfair labour practice and no victimisation, <ORG> would grant the approval which would relate back to the date from which the employer had ordered the dismissal.
RatioOfTheDecision	If the inquiry is defective for any reason, <ORG> would also have to consider for itself on the evidence adduced bef ore it whether the dismissal was justified.
RatioOfTheDecision	However, on coming to the conclusion on its own appraisal of evidence adduced before it that the dismissal was justified its approval of the order of dismissal made by the employer in a defective inquiry would still relate back to the date when the order was made."
RatioOfTheDecision	These observations directly cover the case before us because though <ORG>, in the instant case, found that the inquiry was defective as it infringed the principles of natural justice, it came to the conclusion after considering the evidence adduced before it, that the dismissal was justified.
RatioOfTheDecision	The award of <ORG> must therefore relate back to the date when the order of dismissal was passed on the termination of the <ORG> inquiry.
RatioOfTheDecision	The observations extracted earlier from the judgment of <ORG> in <ORG> case (p. 487 of the Report), on which the appellant relied strongly prima facie support the appellant's contention that if an inquiry is found to be defective, the  employer can make good the defect by producing the necessary evidence before <ORG> but that in such a case he will have to pay wages up to date of the decision of <ORG> even if that decision went in his favour.
RatioOfTheDecision	The particular observations purport to summarize what was decided by the same <ORG> a fortnight earlier in <CITATION_35363> (1).
RatioOfTheDecision	Learned counsel for the respondent took us closely through the judgment in <ORG> but we are unable to find anything in that judgment showing that whenever there is a defect in a Domestic inquiry, the employer would have to pay wages up to the date of the award of <ORG> or <ORG> even if the order passed in the Domestic inquiry was ultimately upheld by <ORG> or the <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	In <ORG> (supra), the domestic inquiry was in gross violation of the fundamental principles of natural justice and was therefore vitiated.
RatioOfTheDecision	The employers did not lead proper evidence before the <ORG> to justify the  order of dismissal and were content merely to produce before the <ORG> the statements which were recorded during the inquiry.
RatioOfTheDecision	The employee therefore had no opportunity to cross- examine the witnesses before the <ORG>.
RulingByLowerCourt	Since the inquiry was bad and the <ORG> had no evidence before it to sustain the order of dismissal it set aside that order but held that in the peculiar circumstances of the case, the employee may be granted the alternative relief of compensation instead of an order of reinstatement.
RulingByLowerCourt	The <ORG> accordingly granted to the employee pay and allowance from the date of his suspension till payment.
RulingByLowerCourt	The award of the <ORG> was upheld is appeal by this Court.
RatioOfTheDecision	It shall have been seen that in the case of <ORG> (supra) the employers made no attempt to make good the defect in the inquiry by producing necessary evidence before the <ORG> and by affording an opportunity to the employee to cross-examine their witnesses.
RatioOfTheDecision	"This left the matters where they were", as observed by <ORG> <NAME> who spoke on behalf of the <ORG>, with the result that the <ORG> which found that the inquiry was vitiated had no evidence before it to examine the legality and propriety of the order of dismissal.
RatioOfTheDecision	In the instant case, the <ORG> inquiry was held to be in violation of the principles of natural justice but the employer led evidence before the Labour <ORG> in support of the order of dismissal and on a fresh appraisal of that evidence, the Labour <ORG> found that the order of dismissal was justified.
RatioOfTheDecision	The ratio of <NAME> case would therefore govern the case and the judgment of the Labour <ORG> must relate back to the date on which the order of dismissal was passed.

Precedent	As pointed out by this <ORG> in <NAME> case, <NAME> was a case where an application had been made under section 33(1) of the Industrial Disputes Act for permission to dismiss the employees and such permission was asked for, though no inquiry whatsoever was held by the employer and no decision was taken that the employees be dismissed.

RatioOfTheDecision	The second contention must also therefore fail.
RatioOfTheDecision	We would, however, like to add that the decision in <NAME> case is not to be construed as a charter for employers to dismiss employees after the pretence of an inquiry.
RatioOfTheDecision	The inquiry in the instant case does not suffer from defects so serious or fundamental as to make it non- est.
RatioOfTheDecision	On on appropriate occasion, it may become necessary to carve an exception to the ratio of <NAME>'s case so as to exclude from its operation at least that class of cases in which under the facade of a domestic inquiry, the employer passes an order gravely detrimental to the employee' s interest like an order of dismissal.
RatioOfTheDecision	An inquiry blatantly and consciously violating principles of natural justice may well be equated with the total absence of an inquiry so as to exclude the application of the 'relation-back' doctrine.
RatioOfTheDecision	But we will not pursue the point beyond this as the facts before us do not warrant a closer consideration thereof.
