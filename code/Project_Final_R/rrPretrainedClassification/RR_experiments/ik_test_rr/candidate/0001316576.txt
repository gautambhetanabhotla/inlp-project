Fact	HEADNOTE: The appellant filed an application under s. 26(f) of the Bengal Tenancy Act claiming right of preemption over the land purchased by the respondent.
Fact	The respondent took up the position that the appellant was not a co-sharer in the land which had been purchased by her and that he along with his uncle had acted as brokers in the transaction and received brokerage; the appellant had thus waived his right of preemption.
Fact	The trial Judge allowed the application.
Fact	<ORG> held that the appellant's claim was barred owing to waiver on his part.
Fact	A revision to <ORG> was unsuccessful.
Argument	In appeal to this <ORG> it was urged that waiver could be brought about only by a contract and since no consideration had passed it could not be said that there had been any waiver.
RatioOfTheDecision	HELD, : Under the Indian Law  neither consideration nor an agreement would be necessary to constitute waiver.
RatioOfTheDecision	A waiver signifies nothing- more than an intention not to insist upon the right.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is well known that in the law of preemption the general principle which can be said to have been uniformly adopted by the Indian courts is that acquiescence in the sale by any positive act amounting to relinquishment of a preemptive right has the effect of forfeiture of such a right.
RatioOfTheDecision	So far as the law of preemption is concerned the principle of waiver is based mainly on Mohammedan Jurisprudence.
RatioOfTheDecision	The contention that the waiver of the appellant's right under s. 26F of the Bengal Tenancy Act must be founded on contrast or agreement cannot therefore be acceded to.
