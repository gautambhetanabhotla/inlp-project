Argument	1.
Fact	These cases were tried before a Judge and Jury.
Fact	The Judge agreeing with a majority of the Jury, four to one, convicted the prisoners of an offence under Section 302 read with Section 149 of the Indian Penal Code, and sentenced them to transportation for life.
Fact	The appeals, therefore, cannot succeed, unless the appellants can satisfy us that there was some misdirection by the learned Judge in his charge to the Jury.
Fact	The first criticism upon the action of the Judge is that, whilst the verdict was delivered on the 29th May 1908, and the sentence was passed on the 5th June following, his charge to the Jury was not written out until the 29th June.
Fact	Reference has been made to Section 367 of the Code of Criminal Procedure.
Fact	That section does not assist the appellants, for there is a proviso that "in trials by Jury, the Court need not write a judgment, but <ORG> should record the heads of the charge to the Jury."
RatioOfTheDecision	There is nothing there as to when it must be writt en, as in the case of a judgment by the <ORG> dealt with in a preceding part of the section.
RatioOfTheDecision	If we refer to the Circular Orders of this <ORG>, Chapter I, Order 59, we find an express order to the effect that it is not necessary that the direction to the Jury should be reduced to writing before delivery, but it is essential that the "heads of charge" ( Section 367 ) placed upon the record should represent with absolute accuracy the substance of the charge, and be such as to enable the High <ORG>, in the event of an appeal, to see distinctly, whether the case was fairly and properly placed before the Jury.
RatioOfTheDecision	Whilst, therefore, there is nothing in the point to assist the appellants, we think it is very unsatisfactory that the charge to the Jury was not, in the case before us, written out until the 29th June, nearly three weeks after the sentence.
RatioOfTheDecision	We are strongly of opinion that in these cases such charge ought to be written out as soon as possible after the charge to the Jury has been actually delivered, and when  the facts of the case are fresh in the mind of the Judge.
RatioOfTheDecision	2.
RatioOfTheDecision	The first point of misdirection is that the Judge did not read the medical evidence to the Jury, but one has only to look at the charge to see that there were frequent references to the medical evidence.
RatioOfTheDecision	There is nothing that makes it incumbent upon any Judge to read the whole of the depositions of the witnesses to the Jury, and, we think, in the present case the medical evidence was sufficiently attracted to their attention.
RatioOfTheDecision	3.
RatioOfTheDecision	Then it is said that the Judge did not sufficiently warn the Jury that the omission of the prosecution to call certain witnesses, and particularly the palki-bearers, raised a presumption that their evidence would be unfavourable to the prosecution, and reference is made to Section 114 Ill. (g) of the Evidence Act .
RatioOfTheDecision	It is perfectly true that in his charge we do not find the word "presumption," but again and again the Judge has pointed out to the Jury that they might properly draw any inferences they pleased from the fact th at these witnesses were not called.
RatioOfTheDecision	There is no substance in this point.
RatioOfTheDecision	4.
RatioOfTheDecision	Then it is suggested that the Judge did not sufficiently explain to the Jury the provisions of Section 141 of the Indian Penal Code.
Precedent	But it appears from a note made by the Judge that in the charge to the Jury they were told that, if the five persons went in a body with the common object of murdering <NAME>, and, if he was killed in the prosecution of that common object, then, no matter which of them struck the blow or blows, which caused death, all would be equally guilty of murder under Section 302 , read with Section 149 of the Indian Penal Code.
Precedent	We scarcely think we should be justified in saying, if the Judge, as appears, so addressed the Jury, that there was any real misdirection on this point.

Precedent	But the Code then under consideration was the Code of 1882; the language of the corresponding Section 162 of the present Code is materially different.

Precedent	6.
Precedent	The language of the respective Codes of 1872, 1882 and 1898 are different.
Precedent	In that of 1872 Section 119 enacted: "No statement so reduced into writing shall be signed by the person making it, nor shall it be treated as part of the record or used as evidence."
Precedent	Section 1 62 of the Code of 1882, on the other hand, was thus expressed: "No statement, other than a dying declaration, made by any person to a police officer in the course of an investigation under this Chapter shall, if reduced to writing, be signed by the person making it or be used as evidence against the accused."
RatioOfTheDecision	The present Section 162 provides that: "No statement made by any person to a police officer in the course of an investigation under this Chapter shall, if taken down in writing, be signed by the person making it, nor shall such writing be used as evidence."

Precedent	In the case of Reg.

Precedent	H.C. 120 it was held that the provisions of Section 155 of the Indian Evidence Act of 1872 were not controlled by Section 119 of the Act of 1872, the Criminal Procedure Code then in force.
Precedent	This view was approved of by <NAME> in <CITATION_629026> (1881) I.L.R.
Precedent	8 Calc.

Precedent	The point may be shortly summed up thus.
Precedent	Section 157 of the Evidence Act allows the statement by way of corroborati on to be proved.
Precedent	Section 162 of the Criminal Procedure Code, now in force, enacts that, if any such statement as is now under consideration is taken down in writing, the writing cannot be used as evidence.
Precedent	If it be said that it is a refinement to hold that the writing cannot be admitted, but that the statement, if not reduced to writing, can, the answer is that the <ORG> has chosen to alter its language in Section 162 of the present Criminal Procedure Code , drawing a distinction between the statement and the writing.

RatioOfTheDecision	We may add that we have not overlooked the proviso to the present section, but that is confined to and for the benefit of the defence, as the prosecution have free access to all the police papers.
RatioOfTheDecision	9.
RatioOfTheDecision	There is nothing, therefore, in the special provision of the existing Code to over-ride the general provisions of the Evidence Act as to the proof by oral evidence of former statements; consequently the oral evidence here objected to was rightly admitted by the lower <ORG>.
