Fact	For the purpose of attaining this object, it shall be  competent....  (c) To purchase silk yarn and other raw materials and advance the same to the weaver members who shall convert them into finished goods and deliver them to the society, to pay wages for such conversion....  (e) To act as an agent for marketing the finished products of weaver members to their best advantage.

Fact	The assessing officer did not accept this contention and held that the society is a "dealer" and assessed it to tax.

Argument	5.
Argument	To bring a "transaction" within the purview of the Act, there must be a "sale" and such sale must be made by a "dealer" in the course of his business.
Statute	The expression "dealer" is defined in Section 2(c) of the Act as follows :  2.
Statute	Definitions.-In this Act, unless there is anything repugnant in the subject or context....  (c) 'Dealer' means any person who carries on the business of purchasing or selling or supplying goods in Orissa, whether for commission, remuneration or otherwise and includes a department of Government which carries on such business and any firm or Hindu joint family, and any society, club or association which purchases goods from or sells or supplies goods to its members and also includes a casual dealer as hereinbefore denned.
Statute	Explanation.-The manager or agent of a dealer who resides outside Orissa and who carries on the business of purchasing or selling or supplying good s in Orissa shall, in respect of such business, be deemed to be a dealer for the purpose of this Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	As the definition itself shows, a co-operative society can also be a dealer provided the other conditions are satisfied.
RatioOfTheDecision	Carrying on own business of buying or selling goods is essential for a person to be a dealer.
RatioOfTheDecision	It may be that he is selling somebody else's goods, but the business which he carries on must be his own.
RatioOfTheDecision	The fact that such a person would be receiving commission or remuneration for selling somebody else's goods would not make any difference provided that the business of selling belongs to him.
RatioOfTheDecision	Unless, therefore, the assessee can be said to have carried on such business of selling goods, whether as principal or as agent, he cannot be termed as a "dealer".
RatioOfTheDecision	Two questions, therefore, arise for determination in this case, namely, (1) whether the goods that are sold by the petitioner are its own, and (2) assuming that the goods do not belong to it whether the business of selling belongs to it.
RatioOfTheDecision	6.
RatioOfTheDecision	Th e admitted facts in this case are these : The petitioner-society purchases the yarn and other raw materials and supplies the same to the weaver members for conversion into finished goods.
RatioOfTheDecision	The society purchases the same on its own responsibility.
RatioOfTheDecision	The raw materials after purchase by the society are given to the members for conversion into finished goods and it is not disputed that this transfer does not constitute a "sale" to the members.
RatioOfTheDecision	As such the raw materials, namely, the yarn so long as they are in possession of the weavers legally remain the goods of the society.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is not disputed that wages are paid to the weaver members for converting the yarn into finished goods.
RatioOfTheDecision	If title in the yarn had ever passed to the weavers and the legal title of the finished goods vested with these members, the question of paying any wages to them for converting the yarn into finished goods would never have arisen.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is conceded that after the finished products are handed over to the society, the weavers concerned exercise n o further control over the disposal thereof; they have no voice in the fixation of the price; they do not select the buyers, and there is no contact between them and the buyers.
RatioOfTheDecision	The mere fact that in the receipts given to the buyers at the time of purchase by them of the fabrics the name of the weaver who manufactured that particular fabric is mentioned does not in the circumstances of the case, alter the situation or constitute that particular weaver the seller of the goods.
RatioOfTheDecision	The main plank of Mr. <NAME>'s argument is that the society itself does not derive any profit by the transaction of sale and whatever profits are made are diverted to the weaver members.
RatioOfTheDecision	We are not concerned here with the fact whether the society retains such profits out of the transaction or whether it diverts the entire profit to the members.
RatioOfTheDecision	The transaction of sale with which we are concerned is the one between the society and the purchasers.
RatioOfTheDecision	In that transaction, the society undoubtedly aims to make a profit and that is what determines t he nature of the transaction.
RatioOfTheDecision	We thus find in this case that the yarn before it is made over to the members remains the property of the society and after the yarn is converted into finished goods, these finished goods remain the property of the society, and it is the society which has absolute control over the goods till they are sold.
RatioOfTheDecision	The entire transaction of sale is arranged and carried on by the society.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is the society which makes the profit on these sales.
RatioOfTheDecision	How it deals with the profit thereafter is not relevant.
RatioOfTheDecision	On a consideration, therefore, of the objects of the society and the manner in which the transactions take place, we feel that the courts below were right in holding that the petitioner is a dealer.

RatioOfTheDecision	Their Lordships were of the view that the position of the assessee was somewhat akin to that of an ordinary agent or employee who was remunerated on a commission basis.
