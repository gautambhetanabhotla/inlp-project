Argument	The narrative of facts, as aforesaid, is ample manifestation of the conduct of the petitioner as noticed at the commencement of this order.
Argument	Mr. <NAME> is correct in his contention that being a licensee in respect of the suit property, as claimed by the opposite party, or a tenant as claimed by him, the petitioner ought to know which property is the subject matter of the two suits.
Precedent	Reliance placed by him on the decision of <ORG> reported in XXIII CWN 77 ( <CITATION_167180869> .)
RatioOfTheDecision	appears to be apt.
RatioOfTheDecision	It was ruled therein that the notices to quit, issued under the Transfer of Property Act , though not strictly accurate or consistent in the statements embodied in them may still be good and effective in law.
RatioOfTheDecision	The test of their sufficiency is not what they would mean to a stranger ignorant of all the facts and circumstances touching the holding to which they purport to refer but what they would mean to tenants presumably conversant with all those facts and circumstan ces.
RatioOfTheDecision	They are to be construed not with a desire to find faults in them which would render them defective, but to be construed ut res magis valeat quam pereat.
RatioOfTheDecision	Applying the principle here, it is too late in the day for the petitioner to voice a grievance that the suit property not having been properly described in the plaint, the decree is a nullity and not executable.
RatioOfTheDecision	In its decision reported in AIR 1970 SC 1475 ( <CITATION_1024124> ), <ORG> was considering the 6 scope of objection under Section 47 of the Code in relation to the executability of a decree and it was laid down that only such a decree can be the subject- matter of objection which is a nullity, and not a decree that is erroneous either in law or on facts.
RatioOfTheDecision	Hon'ble <NAME> (as His Lordship then was), speaking for himself and on behalf of Hon'ble <NAME> and Hon'ble <NAME>, JJ.
RatioOfTheDecision	(as Their Lordships then were), laid down the law which runs thus:  "6.
RatioOfTheDecision	A court executing a decree cannot go behind the decree between the parties  or their representatives it must take the decree according to its tenor, and cannot entertain any objection that the decree was incorrect in law or on facts.
RatioOfTheDecision	Until it is set aside by an appropriate proceeding in appeal or revision, a decree even if it be erroneous is still binding between the parties.
RatioOfTheDecision	7.
RatioOfTheDecision	When a decree which is a nullity, for instance, where it is passed without bringing the legal representatives on the record of a person who was dead at the date of the decree, or against a ruling prince without a certificate, is sought to be executed an objection in that behalf may be raised in a proceeding for execution.
RatioOfTheDecision	Again, when the decree is made by a court which has no inherent jurisdiction to make it, objection as to its validity may be raised in an execution proceeding if the objection appears on the face of the record: where the objection as to the jurisdiction of the court to pass the decree does not appear on the face of the record and requires examination of the questions raised and decided at t he trial or which could have been but have not been raised, the executing court will have no jurisdiction to entertain an objection as to the validity of the decree even on the ground of absence of jurisdiction."
RatioOfTheDecision	As noticed above, after suffering the appellate decree decree passed in Title Appeal No.102 of 2004, the petitioner did not leave any stone unturned to pursue all the remedies the Code provided to him, albeit unsuccessfully.
Argument	With the dismissal of the first appeal, the remedy open to him was to take recourse to Section 100 of the Code.
Argument	Although second appeal was filed, the petitioner did not risk taking steps for admission thereof under Order XLI Rule 11 of the Code, lest it is also dismissed.
Argument	Like a drowning man latching on to the last straw, he did what was expected of him.
Argument	The Section 47 petition was presented before the executing <ORG> after Misc Case No.11 of 2007 was dismissed, wherein a factual issue was raised in respect of improper description of the suit property.
Argument	It was not the plea of t he petitioner that <ORG> lacked inherent jurisdiction to decide the contentious issues and, therefore, the decree passed by it was a nullity and thus not executable.
Argument	It was open to the petitioner to set up a plea before <ORG> as well as the superior <ORG> that the suit property has been inappropriately described and, therefore, no decree for eviction could have been passed against him at the instance of the decree-holder/opposite party.
RatioOfTheDecision	Such contention does not appear to have been raised.
RatioOfTheDecision	That the suit property has not been appropriately described is of little consequence now.
RatioOfTheDecision	The executing <ORG> has rightly observed that reopening of this issue would amount to going behind the decree, which is impermissible in law.
RatioOfTheDecision	In this connection, it would be pertinent to refer to a decision reported in AIR 1993 Orissa 251 ( <CITATION_1270372> ).
RatioOfTheDecision	Hon'ble <NAME>, C.J.
RatioOfTheDecision	(as His Lordship then was) while considering a somewhat similar plea, had the occasion to observe that the plea of the judgment -debtors in their petition under Section 47 of the Code that the property had vested in <ORG> was not really available on the principle of constructive res judicata.
RatioOfTheDecision	Relevant extract from the decision reads as under :  "*****This point was thus not taken not only before the trial <ORG>, but in the first appellate <ORG> as well as before this <ORG> in Civil Revision No.252 of 1974.
RatioOfTheDecision	To allow this point to be taken for the first time in the executing <ORG> could almost be a travesty of justice and would give a very long scope to judgment-debtors who after failing at all levels during trial put up pleas of non-executability which was very much available at the time of trial, which would be totally against larger interest of the society, because a litigation fought to the end at the trial stage would be set at naught by taking pleas available earlier and not taken either knowingly or even unknowingly.
RatioOfTheDecision	If constructive res judicata were not to be applied in such a case, very few decrees would attain f inality and decrees would remain paper tigers, which cannot be allowed to happen."
RatioOfTheDecision	I am convinced that the petitioner has been successful in prolonging litigation and thereby remaining in unlawful possession of the suit property.
RulingByPresentCourt	The decree that the opposite party has obtained cannot be allowed to be defeated at the execution stage by going behind the decree.
RulingByPresentCourt	There is no worthy reason to upset the order of the executing Court.
RulingByPresentCourt	The application stands dismissed.
