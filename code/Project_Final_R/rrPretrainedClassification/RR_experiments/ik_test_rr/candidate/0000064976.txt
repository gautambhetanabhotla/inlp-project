RatioOfTheDecision	The 'market yard' in the Karnataka Act is what corresponds to a 'market' in the Maharashtra Act.
RatioOfTheDecision	Unlike the Maharashtra Act, the Karnatka Act itself [S. 8(2)] expressly provides that no place in the Market or the sub-market, except the market-yard, sub-yard or the sub-market yard as the case may be, shall be used for the purchase or sale of notified agricultural produce.
RatioOfTheDecision	Originally, after the words "purchase or sale of notified agricultural words" occurred the words "belonging to a producer" in Section 8(2) .
RatioOfTheDecision	The words "belonging to a producer" were omitted by a 1976 amendment and this makes the provisions of S. 8(2) applicable to transactions between trader and trader too.
RatioOfTheDecision	The shifting of market yard from one place to another and the application of the Act to transactions between traders and traders are what were principally questioned in the Karnataka cases.
RatioOfTheDecision	Substantially the same submissions as in the Maharashtra cases were made and we have already dealt with them.
RatioOfTheDecision	We my now turn to the Bihar cases.
RatioOfTheDecision	The  Bihar Agricultural Produce Markets Act, 1960, follows roughly the same pattern as the other Acts.
RatioOfTheDecision	A market area has to be first declared within which the marketing of specified agricultural produce is proposed to be regulated.
RatioOfTheDecision	For every market area there is to be a principal market yard and one or more sub market yards.
Precedent	In between the market area and the market yard there is to be a market but market does not seem to play any part in the scheme of the Act as it now stands after the 1974 amendments.
Precedent	However it should be mentioned here that Rule 80 which is still on the Statute Book, provides that a market shall be established for a market area and that after the establishment of a market, a notification under Sec.
Precedent	5 (declaring market yards) shall be issued.
Precedent	Sec.
Precedent	15 of the Act provides that no specified agricultural produce shall be bought or sold at any place within the market area other than the principal market yard or sub-market yard established therein except such quantity as may be prescribed for retail  sale or personal consumption.
Precedent	The arguments advanced in the Maharashtra and Karnataka cases were advanced in the Bihar cases also.
RatioOfTheDecision	For the reasons already mentioned we reject the submission.
Precedent	In one of the Bihar cases it was further submitted that when a market yard was disestablished at one place and established at another place, it was the duty of the concerned authority to invite and hear objections.
RatioOfTheDecision	Failure to do so was a violation of the principles of natural justice and the notification disestablishing the market yard at one place and establishing it elsewhere was therefore, bad.
RatioOfTheDecision	It was said that even as there was express provision for inviting and hearing objections before a "market area" was declared under the Act, so should objections be invited and heard before a 'market yard' was established at any particular place.
RatioOfTheDecision	The principles of nature justice demanded it.
RatioOfTheDecision	We are unable to agree.
RatioOfTheDecision	We are here not concerned with the exercise of a judicial or quasi-judicial function where the very nature of the fu nction involves the application of the rules of natural justice, or of an administrative function affecting the rights of persons, wherefore, a duty to act fairly.
RatioOfTheDecision	We are concerned with legislative activity; we are concerned with the making of a legislative instrument, the declaration by notification of the Government that a certain place shall be a principal market yard for a market area, upon which declaration certain statutory provisions at once spring into action and certain consequences prescribed by statute follow forthwith.
RatioOfTheDecision	The making of the declaration, in the context, is certainly an act legislative in character and does not oblige the observance of the rules of natural justice.
Precedent	In <NAME> v. Lord <NAME>, <NAME> pointed out that the rules of natural justice do not run in the sphere of legislation, primary or delegated, and in <CITATION_451059> , our brothers <NAME> and <NAME> approved what was said by <NAME> and applied it to the field of conditional legislation too.
Precedent	In <NAME> on's Natural Justice (Second Edn.
Precedent	), it has been pointed out (at p.169):  "There is no doubt that a Minister, or any other body, in making legislation, for example, by statutory instrument or by-law, is not subject to the rules of natural justice- <NAME> v. Lord <NAME> of St. Mayleborne (1972) 1 W.L.R.
RatioOfTheDecision	1373-any more than is <ORG> itself; Edinburgh and <ORG> v. <NAME> (1842) 8 Cl.
RatioOfTheDecision	& F. 710, 720 per Lord <NAME>; <ORG> v. <NAME> (1974) A.C. 765".
RatioOfTheDecision	Prof. <NAME> has similarly pointed in his Administrative Law (4th Edn.
RatioOfTheDecision	): "There is no right to be heard before the making of legislation, whether primary or delegated, unless it is provided by statutes".
RatioOfTheDecision	There is, therefore, no substance in the invocation of the rules of natural justice.
Precedent	One of the submissions in the Bihar cases was that the declaration of places as market yards was made in such an erratic fashion that the exercise of the power could only be termed as an arbitrary misuse of power.
Precedent	The facts in Civil Appeal No.
RatioOfTheDecision	1 507 of 1980 were that on September 16, 1964, a certain area was declared as a principal market yard and Amgola, Chandwara, Sarai Said Ali and Brahmpura were declared as Sub market yards.
RatioOfTheDecision	On February 23, 1978 instead of the principal market yard declared by the notification of September 16, 1964, Muradpur Dulla was declared as principal market yard.
RatioOfTheDecision	The sub-market yards were abolished.
RatioOfTheDecision	By another notification dated April 9, 1979, all the market yards notified on September 16, 1964 were allowed to continue as before, but it was also simultaneously made known that such market yards would be closed on specified dates and merchants were advised to move their business into the Muradpur Dulla principal market yard as early as possible.
RatioOfTheDecision	Finally by a notification dated July 3, 1979, the previous notification dated April 9, 1979 was cancelled and Muradpur Dulla market yard was alone notified as the principal market yard.
RatioOfTheDecision	The facts in the other two appeals were that on September 19, 1963, Gaya town was declared as a mar ket area.
RatioOfTheDecision	On April 6, 1964, Chandauti was declared as the market proper under Sec.5(2)(ii) of the Bihar Act .
RatioOfTheDecision	By a notification dated April 7, 1964, Mohalla Purani Godown was declared as principal market yard and Kedarnath Market was declared as the sub-market yard for the market area.
RatioOfTheDecision	On October 19, 1973, Mohalla Purani Godown was once again declared as the Principal Market Yard.
RatioOfTheDecision	Subsequently on February 28, 1978, Chandauti was declared as the Principal Market Yard.
Fact	This meant that Mohalla Purani Godown ceased to be a market yard and Kedarnath Market ceased to be a sub- market yard.
Fact	But, again on April 9, 1979, another notification was issued, to the effect that Mohalla Purani Godown would continue as the market yard as before.
Fact	Finally on June 27, 1979, Chandauti was declared as the Principal Market yard once more.
Fact	This was questioned in Writ Petitions filed in <ORG>.
Fact	<ORG> rejected all but one of the contentions raised.
Fact	The only contention which was accepted was that the proced ure prescribed by Rule 80 was not followed before Chandauti was declared as the principal market yard by the notification dated February 28, 1978.
Fact	Rule 80, as already mentioned by us provides that a market shall be established for a market area and that after the establishment of a market a notification declaring the market yard shall be issued.
Fact	The contention which was accepted was that a market had not been established before a market yard was declared.
Fact	Against the judgment of <ORG> the merchants have filed Civil Appeal No.
Fact	1715 of 1980 and the State of Bihar has filed Civil Appeal No.
Fact	36 of 1980.
Fact	Not-withstanding the filing of the appeal, the State of Bihar chose to issue a fresh notification after observing the procedure prescribed by Rule 80.
Fact	This was again questioned in <ORG>.
Fact	<ORG> upheld the notification.
Argument	The merchants have preferred Civil Appeal No.
Argument	1716 of 1980 against the judgment of <ORG>.
RatioOfTheDecision	From the history of events it may appear as if declarations regarding mar ket yards have been made in a most erratic fashion but as pointed out by the learned Attorney General who appeared for the State of Bihar it was not madness.
RatioOfTheDecision	There was a method.
RatioOfTheDecision	The old markets had existed from ancient days and it had become necessary to establish modern market yards with conveniences and facilities.
RatioOfTheDecision	When this was sought to be done there were representations by the traders and the Government appears to have thought that it was advisable to give the traders sufficient time to enable them to prepare themselves to move into the new market yards.
RatioOfTheDecision	The notifications establishing new market yards were therefore, cancelled and the old markets were allowed to function for some time.
RatioOfTheDecision	Later when the time was thought to be ripe, notifications establishing new market yards were once again issued.
RatioOfTheDecision	It is, therefore seen that the seeming confusion was not the result of any arbitrary or erratic action on the part of the Government but was the result of a desire to accommodate the traders as much as possible.
RatioOfTheDecision	We, therefore, see no force in any of the submissions made on behalf of the petitioners.
RulingByPresentCourt	All the Writ Petitions and Civil Appeals are therefore, dismissed with costs.
