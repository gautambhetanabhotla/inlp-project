Fact	JUDGMENT <NAME>, J.
Fact	1.
Fact	This is an appeal from a decree of <ORG> which confirmed a decree of the Subordinate Judge of Murshidabad.
Fact	2.
Fact	The suit was brought in September 1902 by the respondent <NAME> against the appellant for a declaration that the purchase by the appellant of a house at an auction sale, held under the order of <ORG>, in certain execution proceedings, was invalid on the ground that it was brought about by the fraud of the appellant and the other defendants <NAME>, the judgment creditor, and <NAME>, the judgment debtor, in those proceedings.
Fact	3.
Fact	The respondent <NAME> was a creditor of <NAME> and held a decree for the amount of his debt, Rs.
Fact	10,000.
Fact	He applied to <ORG> for execution against <NAME>'s properties and accordingly in September 1900 the house in question was attached and ordered to be sold on the 15th January 1901.
Fact	The debtor procured a postponement of the sale unti l the 21st March 1901 by a part payment of Rs.
Fact	4,000.
Fact	In January and February 1901 suits were begun by the debtor's two sons against the respondent, alleging that the house was family property; that they were entitled to shares therein according to the Mitakshara law, and asking for injunctions restraining the sale of their shares.
Fact	The sale was accordingly stayed in February 1901, on terms that security for the whole amount of <NAME> debt should be furnished.
Fact	This was done on the 8th March 1901, and the sale on behalf of the respondent thus indefinitely stayed.
Fact	4.
Fact	In the meantime proceedings were also being taken against <NAME> by the defendant <NAME>, who had obtained a decree in <ORG> for the amount of his debt, Rs.
Fact	1,800, as far back as September 1896.
Fact	That decree was transmitted to <ORG> for execution, and on the 22nd February 1901 the house in question was duly attached, and the sale fixed for the 15th May 1901.
Fact	On that day the ju dgment debtor petitioned the <ORG> to postpone the sale for seven days.
Fact	On his promise that the debt should be paid in that time, <NAME> consented, and this delay was granted by the <ORG>.
Fact	The promise was not kept, and the sale accordingly took place on the 22nd May 1901, when the appellant <NAME> became the purchaser at the price of Rs.
Fact	12,000.
Fact	That sum was paid by him, and has since been distributed among the general body 'of creditors.
Fact	It is now alleged that the real purchaser was the judgment debtor himself, and that the appellant was only a "benamidar" or mere nominee.
Fact	5.
Fact	The charges of fraud made by the respondent in his settlement of claim were of a sweeping character.
Fact	It was alleged that <NAME>'s decree was false, and obtained by collusion with the judgment debtor; that there was similar fraud in relation to the attachment and the sale proclamation; that the suits by the sons were really instituted by the judgment debtor in order to obtain a stay of the respondent's suit, and s o enable a sham sale to be effected in the collusive suit of <NAME>; that the sale fixed for the 15th May 1901 was then adjourned with the fraudulent consent of <NAME> because it was known that effective bidders would be present; that on the 22nd May the judgment debtor's pleader, one <NAME>, fraudulently told the respondent's agent that the sale was again to be adjourned, and thus prevented him from attending, and that only dependants and followers of the judgment debtor were present at the sale.
Fact	The charges against <NAME> in relation to his debt and decree were conclusively disproved.
Fact	There can be no doubt that <NAME>'s decree, which was of long standing, was genuine, and that he sought and obtained execution under it in perfect good faith.
Argument	Both the Subordinate Judge and <ORG> have found in the appellant's favour on this very important part of the case.
Argument	6.
Argument	The learned Subordinate Judge has also found that the purchase money for the house was found by the appellant himself and not , (as the respondent alleges), by the judgment debtor.
Argument	<ORG> do not dissent from this view, so that on the evidence it cannot be said that the appellant was a mere " benamidar," Further, it was found that the respondent's allegations of fraudulent non-service of process of attachment and sale were not correct.
Argument	7.
Argument	These findings materially attenuate the general case of fraud set up by the respondent and, in what remains of the case, their Lordships are unable to see any sufficient or substantial evidence of fraud on the part of the appellant.
Argument	8.
Argument	The genuineness of <NAME> debt and decree being now admitted, it becomes necessary to scrutinize carefully the charges brought against him in relation to the execution.
Argument	His consent to the postponement of the sale for seven days on the debtor's promise of payment was necessarily fraudulent.
Argument	No doubt it fitted in well with the specific charges of fraud originally brought against him but, when those charges are eliminated from the case, there is nothing t o show that this particular act may not have been quite innocent.
Argument	The respondent however, can make no case unless it be treated as a step in the alleged conspiracy.
Argument	Nor can much importance be attached to the fact that <NAME> pleader had intimate business connections with the judgment debtor's pleader, <NAME>.
Argument	The fact is that <NAME> agent, <NAME>, desired to retain <NAME> in the proceedings, but <NAME>, being already retained for the judgment debtor, recommended his friend <NAME>, who thereupon acted for <NAME>.
Argument	9.
Argument	The appellant is a money lender and a man of substance.
Argument	When cross-examined as to his means he staled his income tax and offered to produce the receipts, so the suggestion that he was merely an impecunious nominee was not sustained.
Argument	10.
Argument	He said he had heard of the proposed sale at the house of his pleader, <NAME>, who had also acted occasionally for <ORG>.
Argument	<NAME> told him the property would be sold cheap, owing to the litigation of the sons which, he thought  would come to nothing in the end--an opinion which proved correct.
Argument	The appellant thereupon instructed him to arrange for a representative to attend the sale on his behalf and to bid up to Rs.
Argument	10,000 or Rs.
Argument	12,000.
Argument	He forthwith paid Rs.
Fact	3,000 to <NAME> to meet the deposit that might be required and afterwards sent, out of his own funds, the balance of Rs.
Fact	9,000.
Fact	Other bidders competed for the property.
Fact	They are said, perhaps truly, to have been friends or associates of the judgment debtor, but it is not correct to say they were "followers and dependants;" nor is there anything in the evidence or the circumstances to establish the suggestion that they were acting in collusion with the appellant.
Argument	11.
Argument	The respondent says he was willing to give Rs.
Argument	60,000 for the house, but was prevented from attending by <NAME> telling his agent on the day of the sale that he was instructed to apply for another postponement.
Argument	<NAME> was called by the respondent, and would only say that he had no recollection of this; but, a ssuming that he said it, there is nothing to show that the appellant should be held responsible for any misrepresentation by <NAME>.
Argument	12.
Argument	On the 20th June 1908 the respondent preferred a claim under Section 311 of the Civil Procedure Code, in <ORG>, praying that the sale might be set aside, but his petition was dismissed, and the sale duly confirmed after evidence had been heard on both sides in relation to the points raised by the respondent.
Argument	13.
Argument	Litigation also arose after the sale between the appellant and the sons of the judgment debtor, who set up against him their claim to shares in the house under the Mitakshara law.
Argument	That claim was dismissed and the appellant obtained possession in January 1902.
Argument	It is said that he has made no use of the property and still holds it for the benefit of the judgment debtor, but this suit was commenced in September 1902 so that the appellant has not been able to deal effectively with what was probably a speculative purchase.
Argument	14.
Argument	Their Lordshi ps are of opinion that the allegations of fraud and conspiracy made against the appellant have not been brought home to him and that, under all the circumstances, there is no sufficient ground for setting aside a sale, confirmed by the <ORG> after prompt local inquiry, and for inflicting on the appellant a forfeiture of the considerable purchase money paid by him out of his own funds.
Argument	15.
RulingByPresentCourt	Their Lordships will therefore humbly advise His Majesty that the decrees of the High <ORG> and of the <ORG> of the Subordinate Judge ought to be reversed and this appeal allowed, with costs here and in the <ORG>s below.
