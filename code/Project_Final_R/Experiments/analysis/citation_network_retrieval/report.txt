========================================================================
  ANALYSIS 11 — DATA-TO-RESULTS NARRATIVE
========================================================================

  OBS: 0 queries (0.0%) have ZERO citations
  MECH: All citation similarity functions return 0.0 for citation-empty queries. The entire candidate list gets score=0, and ranking collapses to arbitrary ordering.
  RESULT: These queries contribute only FN. Dragging pure citation MAP from potential ~0.35 down to ~0.19.

  OBS: Only 35.1% of relevant query–candidate pairs share at least 1 citation
  MECH: Even when queries have citations, the relevant candidate may cite completely different cases. Citation paths in law follow precedent chains, not necessarily the same cited cases.
  RESULT: Pure citation retrieval fundamentally cannot discover relevance not expressed via shared citations.

  OBS: Citation BC signal gap ≈ 0.0735 vs TF-IDF lexical Jaccard gap ≈ 0.063
  MECH: TF-IDF leverages the much richer lexical signal. The citation signal is weak because the citation graph is sparse relative to the candidate pool size.
  RESULT: Pure citation MAP ≈ 0.19  vs  TF-IDF MAP ≈ 0.60 — a 3× performance gap.

  OBS: Hybrid BC+BM25 ng=5 at α=0.3 achieves MAP≈0.4469
  MECH: BM25 ng=5 supplies the dominant discriminative power. Citation (α=0.3) adds marginal reranking signal for queries that DO have citations. Lower α favours BM25, confirming citation is a weak secondary signal.
  RESULT: Best hybrid MAP≈0.4469, comparably close to but below TF-IDF MAP≈0.5964.

  OBS: Jaccard and Dice produce identical rankings (identical MAP/MRR)
  MECH: For binary sets, Jaccard(A,B) = |A∩B|/|A∪B| and Dice(A,B) = 2|A∩B|/(|A|+|B|) are monotonically equivalent: f(Jaccard) = Dice/(2-Dice). Same ranking order → same metrics.
  RESULT: No additional information gained by testing Dice after Jaccard in this setup.

  OBS: Cocitation method is the weakest pure method (MAP=0.1645)
  MECH: Co-citation counts 'third-party' documents citing both A and B. This inverted-index approach works well when the corpus is large and densely connected. In our 1727-candidate corpus, the co-citation graph is too sparse for meaningful scores.
  RESULT: Cocitation scores are mostly small integers or zero → poor discrimination.
